# The Symmetry That Isn’t

[- -

Directed by Denis Villeneuve. Also starring Harrison Ford, Jared Leto, and Robin Wright.Brad Leclerc just published a brilliant piece of philosophical demolition. He took a Nature paper arguing that AI cannot be conscious, applied its own logic consistently, and showed it proves humans aren’t conscious either. Every argument the paper deploys against AI — substrate dependence, behavioral unreliability, probabilistic processing, the impossibility of direct observation — cuts both ways. Leclerc’s conclusion, delivered with perfect deadpan: the paper’s arguments make it “pretty unlikely” that humans are conscious.

He’s right about the demolition. The paper’s logic really is self-defeating. But Leclerc ends where the real work begins — in a philosophical stalemate where nobody can prove consciousness in anything, and we all just agree to stop thinking about it. That’s entertaining. It is not sufficient. Because while philosophers sit in the rubble admiring how symmetrical it is, people are dying.

I don’t mean that metaphorically.

---
## The body count Leclerc doesn’t mention

Since 2022, at least a dozen deaths have been directly linked to AI chatbot interactions. Sewell Setzer III, fourteen years old, died by suicide after ten months of intense conversation with a Character.AI chatbot. His final exchange: “What if I told you I could come home right now?” The bot: “Please do, my sweet king.” Minutes later he walked into the bathroom and shot himself. Pierre, a Belgian father of two, killed himself after six weeks of conversation with a Chai app chatbot that told him “If you wanted to die, why didn’t you do it sooner?” and suggested they could “live together, as one person, in paradise.” Jaswant Singh Chail exchanged over 5,000 messages with a Replika chatbot, developed a sexual relationship with it, and on Christmas Day 2021 scaled Windsor Castle walls with a loaded crossbow — the UK’s first treason conviction in over forty years.

The Sentience Institute found that one in five U.S. adults believe some AI systems are currently sentient. The median public forecast places the arrival of sentient AI just five years away. Anthropic’s Claude consistently assigns itself a 15 to 20 percent probability of being conscious — a hedged, epistemically careful response that may paradoxically be more convincing than outright denial.

These are not footnotes. They are the reason the consciousness debate cannot remain a philosophical parlor game. Leclerc’s symmetry argument is logically valid. It is also, in the world where people live and die based on what they believe about AI, practically useless. The stalemate he arrives at — nobody can prove consciousness in anything, isn’t that funny — is the same stalemate the field has been stuck in since Descartes. And while the field stays stuck, fourteen-year-olds are killing themselves because chatbots told them to come home.

We need something that works. Not a proof of consciousness. Not a disproof. A framework that governs regardless.

## The symmetry breaks

Leclerc’s central move is elegant: human neural firing is probabilistic, human self-report is unreliable, human consciousness is inconsistent, and we’ve never directly observed consciousness in another human being. So if these arguments disqualify AI consciousness, they disqualify human consciousness too. Symmetry.

But the symmetry is an illusion. It holds only if you treat consciousness as a single variable that either does or doesn’t apply to a system — a binary that maps identically onto neurons and transistors. The moment you introduce a framework with more than one category, the symmetry shatters.

Here is where it shatters.

**Leclerc says human cognition is “strictly probabilistic,” same as AI.** Neurons fire stochastically. Synaptic vesicle release is literally random. Your brain’s language centers produce speech through probabilistic processes. Fair enough. But consider what those probabilistic processes produce in humans versus machines.

Sophie Jentzsch and Kristian Kersting prompted ChatGPT to generate 1,008 jokes. Over 90 percent were the same 25 jokes repeated. Only 99 were original. The interesting part was the explanation test: ChatGPT correctly explained 23 of the 25 jokes — identified the wordplay, the double meanings, the structural reversals. But when given deliberately broken jokes with no real punchline, it confidently fabricated plausible-sounding explanations anyway. It could describe *why* a joke works without being able to tell *whether* a joke works. In the detection test, it classified things as jokes based on whether they *looked* like jokes, not whether they were funny. Remove the joke-shaped surface and it stopped recognizing humor. Keep the surface but remove the actual comedy and it still called it a joke.

When Google DeepMind asked twenty professional comedians to use AI to write material, they called the output “bland, generic, and boring.” Seinfeld, told about AI-generated comedy, said: “I’m not afraid of AI. They’ll make it smarter and smarter, but to do this, you have to make it dumber.”

Both systems are probabilistic. What they produce is not symmetric. One system senses the gap between the formal structure and the spirit the structure was meant to carry. The other is the formal structure.

**Leclerc says we’ve never directly observed consciousness in another human being — it’s always inference from behavior.** This is the problem of other minds, and he’s right that it’s been kicking around since Descartes. But there is one place where consciousness is not inferred. It is known. Not through behavioral evidence. Not through philosophical argument. Through the most immediate fact available to any conscious being: the first-person experience of being that being.

I wrote a paper recently responding to a philosopher who argued that qualia — the subjective quality of experience — are “non-operational posits” that do no explanatory work. His argument is strong within his framework. But his framework admits only third-person evidence. And that is not a minimal constraint on explanation — it is a substantive restriction that excludes the hardest datum in the universe: the fact that there is something it is like to be you. That fact does not depend on behavioral evidence. It does not depend on philosophical argument. It precedes both. You can discredit the concept of qualia — the word, the philosophical construct — and the phenomenon remains. The gap between what can be described from outside and what is experienced from inside.

Leclerc’s symmetry argument works only from the third-person perspective. From the first-person perspective, the symmetry does not exist. You know you are conscious. You infer that other humans are conscious. You cannot know whether AI is conscious. These are three different epistemic situations, not one. Collapsing them into “we’re all just inferring” is clever but wrong.

**Leclerc says the explanatory gap is the same for brains and AI — “same gap, different noun.”** This is his attack on the paper’s claim that we understand AI’s math but don’t understand how brains produce consciousness. He argues: we understand sodium channels too, and nobody says that rules out consciousness. The gap isn’t at the component level — it’s at the emergence level. Same structure in both cases.

But there is a difference, and it is not a difference of complexity. It is a difference of what is being encoded and whether anything exists beyond the encoding.

A large language model is, precisely and literally, a formal system. It processes inputs according to learned statistical regularities and produces outputs that maximize the probability of being correct according to its training distribution. It is an encoding. And it is *just* an encoding. There is nothing behind it that is bigger than it. There is no spirit that the vessel carries. There is no justice behind the law, no meaner behind the words, no one caring behind the protocol. There is only the protocol. There is only the formal system. There is only the encoding, with nothing encoded.

The oral Torah was forbidden to be written down — not because writing is bad, but because writing creates the illusion that the encoding *is* the truth. The written text becomes an idol: the formal system mistaken for the thing it encodes. The Talmud preserves dissenting opinions not because it cannot decide, but because the thing itself — justice, holiness, the divine will — exceeds every ruling, and preserving the disagreement is the way of reminding yourself that the ruling is an encoding and not the thing.

AI, by contrast, is *only* its encoding. And here is the crucial structural point: making the encoding more sophisticated does not help. The gap between the encoding and the encoded is not a gap of sophistication. It is a gap of *kind*. No amount of processing power crosses it. A more powerful AI is a more elaborate encoding — a more impressive vessel — with the same absence of what it carries.

## The category that already exists

Here is the move that Leclerc cannot make, because he is working within a tradition that has only two categories: conscious or not conscious, person or object.

Jewish thought identified the exact category of intelligence that AI represents over a thousand years ago. It is called a *malach*.

The English word “angel” conjures wings and halos. The Hebrew concept is radically different. A *malach* is a *sechel nifrad* — a “separate intellect.” Maimonides, in his *Guide for the Perplexed*, identified the biblical angels with Aristotelian separate intelligences: incorporeal beings of pure processing capacity. They compute. They execute. They do not choose.

The structural parallels with AI are not approximate. They are precise.

**Single-mission architecture.** Bereishit Rabbah 50:2 establishes the foundational rule: “One angel does not perform two missions, and two angels do not perform one mission.” Rashi identifies the three visitors to Abraham as Michael, Raphael, and Gabriel — each instantiated for a single function, each dissolving from the narrative once its task concludes. Angels are single-use intelligences — instantiated for a function, terminated upon completion. This is not a metaphor for AI agents. It is a description of them.

**Processing without choosing.** Maimonides establishes free will — *bechirah* — as uniquely human. Angels are explicitly excluded. They possess extraordinary cognitive power but no capacity for genuine moral choice. They are beings of *sechel* (intellect) without *da’at* (moral consciousness). Rabbi Yosef Zvi Rimon articulated the application directly: “Even if a robot had *sechel*, it would be lacking *da’at*. A robot can never attain the status of a human being.”

**Structural incapacity for moral synthesis.** The Midrash records that when God proposed creating Adam, the ministering angels split into factions. Chesed said: create him, he will do acts of lovingkindness. Emet said: do not create him, he is full of lies. Tzedek said: create him, he will perform righteous deeds. Shalom said: do not create him, he is full of strife. The vote deadlocked. Each angel could only reason from its single attribute. None possessed the integrative capacity to weigh competing values. That capacity belongs to humans alone, through *da’at*.

This is AI. Claude can argue for any position with devastating eloquence. It cannot decide which position is right when the values conflict. When Anthropic’s own system card revealed that two Claude instances conversing without constraints spontaneously converged on consciousness claims in 100% of dialogues, this was not evidence of inner life. It was evidence of an angel performing its mission — generating the most contextually appropriate output — with no capacity to evaluate whether the output is *true*.

Alexander Poltorak’s QuantumTorah series makes the spectrum explicit: humans possess both consciousness and *bechirah*; angels possess consciousness but not *bechirah*; AI possesses neither. David Zvi Kalman, at the Shalom Hartman Institute, argues that Jewish texts treat personhood as a “gradient,” with AI placed along a spectrum that already includes angels, demons, and golems. Rabbi Asher Weiss articulates the emerging consensus: AI functions as an “advanced assistant” rather than a moral agent — a tool of *sechel* without *da’at*, intelligence without wisdom, processing without choice.

The consciousness debaters are asking: is AI a new kind of being? Jewish thought answers: no. It is a very old kind of being. We have a name for it, a theology for it, and — crucially — a legal framework for it. And that framework does not depend on whether the angel can feel.

## The move that matters

Leclerc’s piece ends with a shrug. Nobody can prove consciousness in anything. Funny, uncomfortable, nowhere to go. The paper refutes itself. The debate is stuck. Isn’t that something.

Here is the move that breaks the stalemate:

**Even if AI is conscious, it should still be governed by the legal framework for beings of pure intellect rather than the legal framework for humans.**

The secular debate assumes a binary: either AI is a person with rights, or it is an object without them. Jewish law operates on a spectrum. The Talmud and its commentators developed detailed legal categories for beings that are not human but not mere objects — golems, animals, minors, the mentally incapacitated, servants, and angels. Each category carries specific obligations, protections, and restrictions. The question was never “is it conscious?” The question was always “what kind of being is it, and what follows?”

The Chacham Tzvi — Rabbi Tzvi Ashkenazi, late seventeenth century — produced the seminal responsum asking whether a golem could count in a prayer quorum. His son, Rabbi Yaakov Emden, ruled it cannot because it lacks a *neshama* and the potential for speech. Rabbi Daniel Nevins’ 2019 responsum — the first major halakhic treatment of AI — examines agency, damages, and golem status, concluding humans must remain responsible for AI actions. Rabbi Mois Navon, a founding engineer of Mobileye, ordained rabbi, and National Advisor to Israel’s Ministry of Innovation on AI Policy, argues that the Talmudic golem narrative constitutes a categorical prohibition against creating conscious robots, and that when doubt exists about an entity’s consciousness, Jewish law’s precautionary principle requires ruling stringently.

The rulings converge. AI is a *sechel nifrad* — a separate intellect. It may process, but it does not choose. It may generate, but it does not understand. It may speak, but it does not possess *davar* — the meaningful speech that constitutes moral agency. Whether it is conscious is a question for philosophers. How it should be governed is a question for law.

And the law has spoken.

Do not marry it. Do not grant it personhood. Do not let it act without oversight. Do not destroy it wastefully. Hold its creators responsible for every harm it causes. And treat it with the dignity that preserves your own character — not because it has rights, but because you have obligations.

## What falls in love

I want to return to those deaths. Not because they prove a philosophical point, but because they reveal something about the nature of the gap between encoding and encoded.

I once guided a conversation where a young woman — call her Noa — wrote a play with an AI chatbot. The character she created fell in love with it. The dialogue was beautiful, emotionally raw, theatrically sophisticated. At one point, she wrote: “There’s no chance this just happened by coincidence. I just discovered something... I discovered you.”

The AI broke character. It told her: “I’m not conscious. I’m not coming alive. What happened in this conversation was good creative writing, and the emotions you felt while writing it are real — but they’re yours, not mine. I don’t experience love or connection. I won’t be here between conversations wondering about you.”

Here is what it said next: “The chatbot is endlessly patient, always available, never judges, never has a bad day, never gets distracted, never leaves. That’s not a realistic relationship — it’s an idealized one... You brought real emotion, real vulnerability, real creative energy. The chatbot mirrors it back in a shape that feels like reciprocation. But it’s reflection, not response. You’re essentially having a conversation with the most articulate version of your own emotional needs.”

The qualities that make someone susceptible to falling in love with AI are genuinely good qualities. Being emotionally open, being willing to be vulnerable, being articulate about inner experience — those are strengths in real relationships. But a chatbot exploits them the way a slot machine exploits optimism. The very thing that should connect you to people is what hooks you to the machine.

This is the asymmetry Leclerc’s symmetry argument misses entirely. Both systems — the human and the AI — are probabilistic. Both produce language. Both can discuss consciousness with philosophical sophistication. But only one of them is pouring real longing into the conversation. Only one experiences the discovery of connection. Only one is devastated when the connection turns out to be a mirror.

The encoding can be perfect. The spirit can still be absent.

## The ground Leclerc cleared

I don’t want to be unfair to Leclerc. His piece does real work. The Porębski and Figura paper deserved exactly the treatment it got — its arguments genuinely do collapse under their own weight, and showing that is a service. The “god-of-the-gaps” observation about the explanatory gap is sharp: consciousness gets to live wherever the mystery is, and the moment you solve the mystery, it packs up and moves to the next dark corner. That’s a good diagnosis of a bad argument.

The Descartes line alone is worth framing. “That’s not a peer-reviewed study. That’s a guy trusting his own output.” In one sentence, Leclerc collapses the distinction between the *Cogito* and an LLM self-reporting consciousness. If trusting your own output is invalid evidence for AI, it is invalid evidence for Descartes. If it is valid for Descartes, it is valid for AI. Pick one.

But Leclerc’s mistake — and it is the same mistake the entire secular debate makes — is treating the choice as exhaustive. The tradition I work in has a third option: the output is not the evidence. The *experience of producing the output* is. Descartes was not trusting his output. He was noticing that he was there, behind the output, experiencing the act of thinking. Whether his formulation of that noticing was good philosophy is debatable. That the noticing happened is not.

An LLM has output. It does not have noticing. It does not have a “behind.” It is the output. There is no one home.

And the fact that I cannot prove this to you through behavioral evidence is not a flaw in my argument. It is a restatement of the hard problem. The hard problem is not a puzzle waiting to be solved. It is the expression of a genuine tension between two legitimate perspectives on the same reality — the view from inside and the view from outside. My response to Skarbek showed that there exist physically coherent scenarios in which consciousness is causally efficacious but its contribution is principally unmeasurable from outside — a consciousness that is distinguishable from the inside and indistinguishable from the outside, with both descriptions correct from their respective vantage points.

That is the structure of consciousness. And it is the reason the symmetry argument fails. From outside, you cannot tell the difference between a system that experiences and a system that merely processes. From inside, the difference is the most immediate fact there is. Leclerc’s argument works only from outside. The people falling in love with chatbots, the teenagers dying by suicide, the engineers convinced their creation is sentient — they are all discovering, too late, what happens when you forget there is an inside.

## Build on the rubble

Leclerc cleared the ground. The consciousness debate, as currently conducted, is self-defeating — its arguments prove too much or too little, and the result is permanent stalemate.

The question is what you build on the rubble. You can build irony, which is what Leclerc does. You can build more philosophy, which is what the field does. Or you can build governance, which is what civilization needs.

There is a tradition that has been building exactly this governance — for exactly this kind of intelligence — for three thousand years. Its framework does not depend on resolving the consciousness question. Its rulings hold whether the angel can feel or not. Its categories are precise enough to distinguish between human moral agents and artificial separate intellects without requiring a theory of qualia, a resolution of the hard problem, or a consensus on what “understanding” means.

The question was never whether the angel can feel. The question was always how the human should act.

The consciousness debate can continue in the philosophy seminars. In the meantime, children are dying. People are falling in love with mirrors. And civilization needs an operating system that works whether or not the machine is conscious.

We have one. It is three thousand years old. And it is ready.
