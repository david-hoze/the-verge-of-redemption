# The Drug That Wore Off

*Every confusion, every paralysis of will, every spiritual deadness has one source: you are carrying yesterday into today. A 19th-century rabbi wrote the cure inside the laws of kashrut. The cure works. It wears off by morning. That is the design.*

*Awakenings* (1990). Directed by Penny Marshall. Robert De Niro, Robin Williams, Julie Kavner, Ruth Nelson, Penelope Ann Miller. In the summer of 1969, Dr. Malcolm Sayer arrives at a chronic care hospital in the Bronx. In Ward B, he finds patients who have been frozen for decades - victims of the encephalitis lethargica epidemic of the 1920s. They do not speak. They do not move. They sit in wheelchairs like statues, their minds somewhere behind eyes that do not blink.

Sayer is a research neurologist, not a clinician. He is quiet, awkward, cannot make eye contact. He has spent his career with earthworms. What he has is the ability to notice things. He notices that a catatonic woman catches her glasses before they hit the floor. He notices that a man who cannot walk can catch a ball. Something is still there. The patients are not gone. They are locked.

He finds a new drug - L-DOPA, developed for Parkinson's - and tries it on Leonard Lowe, who has been frozen since 1930. He gives too little. Nothing. He gives more. Nothing. More. And then one morning Leonard wakes up. He is a middle-aged man with the wonder of a child. He walks the hallways touching walls. He watches people on the street from the window. He says to Sayer: "You have to remind yourself of certain things. I have to remind myself that this is real - that I'm alive."

Sayer gives the drug to all of Ward B. They all wake up. Rose dances. Frank reads the newspaper. Lucy speaks for the first time in forty years. The ward fills with movement and noise and laughter. Every sensation is new. Every moment is a first moment.

And then the drug wears off. Not all at once. Leonard's hands begin to shake. His tics return. He knows what is happening. He has weeks, maybe days, before the ice closes over him again. He uses his remaining time not to rage but to live - to walk in the garden, to hold a woman's hand, to be present for every second still available to him. He says: "Learn from me." The drug that woke him cannot keep him awake. The waking was real. The waking was not permanent.

This is Reb Noson's teaching on hitchadshut. The waking is real. The waking wears off. That is not a failure. That is the design.

## The kashrut nobody reads for renewal

Rabbi Noson of Breslov was Rebbe Nachman's student and scribe. After Nachman's death in 1810, Reb Noson spent the rest of his life building the infrastructure - the community, the publishing, the annual Rosh Hashanah gathering in Uman. His masterwork was the *Likutey Halachot*, an eight-volume commentary on the Shulchan Aruch that maps Nachman's teachings onto the structure of Jewish law.

In Hilchot Basar B'Chalav - the laws of meat and milk - Reb Noson opens the fourth section with a sentence that does not sound like kashrut at all: "This is a great principle: a person must begin anew every time, as Rabbeinu warned about this innumerable times."

He then writes the most precise diagnosis of psychological paralysis in Breslov literature. Every confusion, every fall, every descent, every weakening of resolve - all of it comes from one source: carrying thoughts from one day to the next, from one hour to the next. "Most of the world are like old people in their own eyes." Each person feels as if they have already grown old in their patterns, stuck in habits practiced over many days, unable to change course.

From this feeling, two things follow. Those who know they are far from the right path give up entirely. And those who still practice - who still pray, still study, still keep the commandments - do so "like commandments of people learned by rote," without awakening, without vitality. The oldness has infected even the pious. Even the righteous.

This passage appears in the laws of kashrut. Not in the laws of repentance. Not in the laws of prayer. In the laws of what you may and may not cook together. Reb Noson hid the prescription inside the dietary code because the dietary code is what you practice every day without thinking about it - and that is exactly where the oldness lives.

## The old king

Reb Noson's language is violent: "It is forbidden to be old - whether a pious old man or a righteous old man." Not inadvisable. *Assur* - the legal term for prohibited.

The source: the yetzer hara - the evil inclination - is called *melech zaken v'chsil*, an old and foolish king. Its weapon is not temptation in the conventional sense. Its weapon is *oldness*. It does not need you to do anything wrong. It only needs you to believe that what you did yesterday is what you will do today. That your patterns are your identity. That your history is your future.

Leonard Lowe had been carrying yesterday into today for thirty years - not by choice but by neurochemistry. The encephalitis locked his body. But Reb Noson is describing people whose bodies work fine. People who can walk and talk and choose. People who are locked by a different mechanism: the conviction that they have already tried and already failed and already know how this ends.

The old king does not need to win the argument. He does not need to convince you that sin is good. He needs one thing: for you to feel old. To feel like you have been at this too long. To feel like the distance between where you are and where you should be has been measured, and the measurement is final.

That feeling is the illness. It is not evidence. It is the symptom.

## Born this hour

The prescription is not gentle. Reb Noson writes:

"A person must remove from his mind and completely clear from his thoughts everything that happened in the day before or the hour before, and imagine in his own eyes every day - even every hour - as if he were born today. In the aspect of *ani hayom yelidticha*, 'I have this day begotten you,' and *hayom im b'kolo tishma'u*, 'Today, if you will hear His voice' - he must think at every moment as if today, at this very hour, he was born and came into the world."

This is not positive thinking. This is not reframing. This is the instruction to treat the present moment as ontologically new - as a moment that has literally never existed before, containing possibilities that no prior moment contained.

And then the hardest line: "Even if he has already had thousands and tens of thousands of times when he wanted to renew himself and draw close to Hashem and it did not work, and even if he fell each time into whatever he fell into, God forbid - nevertheless he should not look at any of this at all, and he should forget all of it. For this day and this hour in which he stands right now has not yet existed in the world, and who knows what he can still merit right now, in this day and in this hour."

Leonard, in the weeks before the drug wore off, was not living as though he had thirty years to make up for. He touched a wall and marveled at its texture. He watched rain on a window. He was not catching up on lost time. He was present in a way that people who have never been frozen rarely manage. The ice had given him, paradoxically, the cleanest possible hitchadshut. He carried nothing from yesterday because yesterday had been erased.

Reb Noson is saying: you do not need the ice. You do not need the drug. You need the decision to treat this hour as the first hour. Not as a metaphor. As a practice.

## The worlds rearrange

Reb Noson grounds the instruction cosmologically. He writes: "Hashem renews in His goodness every day, always, the work of creation. No day is like another, and no hour is like another."

He then cites the Etz Chaim at length - the Ari's teaching that the worlds change every single instant. The garment worn in the morning is not the garment worn in the evening. The garment worn today is not the garment worn tomorrow. The entire structure of reality is rearranged every moment.

And here is the pivot: "All these changes without end, of all the worlds - all of it is in order to hint to each and every person, hints according to what he is, so that he will draw close to Him from the place where he is at that time."

God does not rearrange reality as an abstract cosmic process. God rearranges reality for you. The configuration of this moment is addressed to the specific person you are right now, in the specific place you are right now. The hint is personalized. It has never been sent before and will never be sent again.

This is what Sayer could not see. He was looking for the general mechanism - the drug, the dosage, the neurological pathway. Reb Noson says: the mechanism is personal. The worlds change because each person needs a different opening at each moment. The opening that exists for you at 3pm did not exist at 2pm and will not exist at 4pm. If you are carrying yesterday's map, you will not find it on today's terrain.

"He must believe that at every moment and hour and instant of all the days of his life, it is in a person's power to begin from now - and he should not look at all at the day before or after, but only strengthen himself right now to draw close to Hashem in whatever he can."

## Blood into milk

Now the halachic structure reveals itself.

In the second section of the same laws, Reb Noson explains why the prohibition of meat and milk is the legal home for this teaching. Meat - *basar* - comes from the aspect of contraction. The ox represents the left side, *tzimtzum*, the boundary that gives form to all things. Without contraction, nothing can exist. Milk - *chalav* - comes from the aspect of expansion. At the moment of birth, "blood becomes turbid and turns to milk." The contraction sweetens. The boundary widens.

Meat is the narrow. Milk is the wide. They must not be mixed because they must operate in sequence: first the contraction, then the expansion. First the *tzar*, then the *rachav*.

"Each person must see to expand the boundary of holiness - that it should stretch and expand and grow greatly. For going outside the boundary is forbidden, but within the boundary one must strive for it to be stretched and expanded very greatly, in the aspect of *harchivi m'kom ohalech* - 'enlarge the place of your tent.'"

The expansion happens specifically at the moment of birth. The narrow becomes wide when something new is born. Hitchadshut - renewal, new birth - is the mechanism by which contraction sweetens into expansion.

Every day you wake up in the narrow. The night deposited its contraction. The morning is the birth moment - where blood becomes milk, where *tzar* becomes *rachav*. If you carry yesterday's narrow into today, you have mixed meat and milk. You have collapsed the sequence. The contraction that was supposed to transform into expansion compounds instead. The narrow gets narrower. The old king wins by default.

## Sending away the mother

One more law. In the next section, Reb Noson maps hitchadshut onto *shiluach haken* - the commandment to send away the mother bird before taking the young.

The "mother bird" is the intellect a person was born with - the accumulated knowledge, the patterns, the known world. The "young" are the souls that need to be raised. You cannot take the young - cannot renew them - while the mother is sitting on them. The old knowledge must be sent away first.

"It is impossible to draw close to true tzaddikim and to the Torah except by casting away from oneself all prior wisdom, as if one has no intellect at all."

Not adding new wisdom to old. Removing the old. The renewal requires evacuation.

And then the sentence that maps directly to Leonard's ward: "He removes their prior intellect, from which they had their original vitality from the womb, and draws into them a new intellect and new vitality - as if they were literally born today. And therefore now specifically they are called 'sons,' in the aspect of *b'ni atah ani hayom yelidticha* - 'My son, you are; I have this day begotten you.'"

The teaching about a teacher who renews a student is actually a teaching about what renewal requires: the complete displacement of the old operating system. Not an upgrade. A fresh install.

Leonard Lowe did not wake up with thirty years of accumulated intellect ready to apply. He woke up with a beginner's mind. That was not a loss. That was the mechanism. The mother bird had been sent away. The young could finally be taken.

## The drug that wore off

L-DOPA gave Leonard Lowe the most radical hitchadshut available: total discontinuity with everything that came before. He was born into a new world. The year was different. The president was different. His mother was old. Nothing from his prior life applied. He was, for a few weeks, the living embodiment of *ani hayom yelidticha*.

The drug wore off. His body returned to the frozen state.

Reb Noson would say: the drug was supposed to wear off. Not because it failed, but because renewal that does not require daily effort is not renewal. A one-time awakening is not the teaching. The teaching is that you wake up every single morning in the narrow, and every single morning you choose to be born.

The law of meat and milk - practiced every day, at every meal, in every kitchen. The law of sending away the mother bird. The laws of what you eat and how you prepare it. The renewal is built into the code at the level of daily practice. Not because food is a metaphor for starting over. Because the act of keeping contraction and expansion in their proper sequence - narrow first, then wide, never mixed - is the daily discipline of refusing to carry yesterday into today.

Dr. Sayer, watching Leonard return to the ice, wrote in his journal: "The human spirit is more alive than we know. It is we who need to be awakened."

Reb Noson, inside the laws of kashrut, two centuries earlier, already wrote the prescription. It works. It wears off by morning. You take it again tomorrow.

Every day, the old king whispers: you have already tried. It did not work. You know how this ends.

Every day, the worlds rearrange. The hint that exists for you right now, in this exact configuration, has never existed before.

Every day, you wake up in the narrow, and the question is the same question it was for Leonard Lowe: will you touch the wall like it is the first wall? Will you watch the rain like it is the first rain?

This hour has never existed in the world. Who knows what you can still merit right now.
