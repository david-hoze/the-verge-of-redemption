# Where Imagination Is Fantasy

[- - 

](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!AJ6c!,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2F70129f1f-27c1-498b-b483-435515be0d4b_1200x1090.jpeg)*Rashomon* (1950). Directed by Akira Kurosawa. Toshiro Mifune, Machiko Kyō, Masayuki Mori. Four witnesses describe the same murder. Each account is vivid, coherent, emotionally compelling, and incompatible with the others. The film is celebrated as a masterpiece about the subjectivity of truth. What nobody mentions is that a man is still dead, and no court in any functioning civilization would have accepted "the truth is unknowable" as a reason not to rule.Jessie Mannisto recently published a piece called “[The Aperture of Thought](https://thirdfactor.substack.com/p/the-aperture-of-thought),” in which she uses the Big Five personality trait of openness to explain why people react so differently to questions about AI consciousness and model welfare. She sketches two archetypes: the Imaginatives, who are high in openness across all facets and for whom AI consciousness is a morally urgent frontier, and the Rationalists, who are high in intellect but lower in other openness facets and for whom the same questions are dangerous nonsense dressed up as ethics. Her prescription: understand the temperamental divide, shake the two cohorts together, and get closer to a productive conversation.

Her piece is elegant, psychologically literate, and completely trapped inside the problem it claims to diagnose.

Her claim is modest, and that’s what makes it worth taking seriously. She’s not saying the right personality mix will solve AI ethics. She’s saying the two camps are talking past each other because of temperament, and that understanding this is step one toward productive conversation.

Fair enough. But step one toward what?

She never says. And she can’t, because her framework has a hole where the answer should be. She’s humble about which temperamental style is right. She’s not humble about whether temperamental style is the right frame at all. She treats the moral crisis of AI as a communication problem between cognitive types. Get them to hear each other, adjust the ratio of divergent to convergent thinking, and progress becomes possible. But better listening doesn’t help when nobody in the room has anything authoritative to say. The Imaginatives can imagine harder. The Rationalists can analyze harder. Neither operation produces moral content. It never has. And the evidence for this is not theoretical. It’s the entire history of how human societies actually answer hard moral questions.

They don’t think their way to answers. They inherit them.

<hr>**But first, the Imaginatives deserve a closer look — because Mannisto treats imagination as if it were a form of moral perception, and the doubt it generates as if it were a reasonable doubt we are obligated to investigate.** She writes that for her cohort, “imagination is a moral perception tool” — that entertaining the possibility of AI consciousness is itself an ethical act, a form of seeing what the Rationalists are too narrow to see. She frames the willingness to ask “what if AI is conscious?” as moral courage, and calls it a “low-probability, high-impact event” that demands investigation.

But the doubt the Imaginatives feel about AI consciousness is not a reasonable doubt. It is a philosophical doubt — and the difference between these two things is the difference between a question that demands a ruling and a question that demands a seminar.

The hard problem of consciousness — the question of why there is something it is like to be you — has been open for four hundred years if you start with Descartes, thirty if you start with Chalmers. It has not been resolved. It has not been narrowed. The positions available today are essentially the same positions available in 1994 when Chalmers named the problem: dualism, functionalism, panpsychism, eliminativism, illusionism, and a dozen variations, each with serious defenders, none with a decisive argument. This is not a frontier where progress is slow. It is a question where the tools being used — philosophical argument, third-person observation, conceptual analysis — have been demonstrated, across centuries, to be structurally incapable of producing an answer.

This track record is not incidental to the AI consciousness debate. It *is* the AI consciousness debate. When Mannisto’s Imaginatives say “what if AI is conscious?”, they are asking a question that belongs to a category proven intractable by the very methods they propose to use. More imagination will not solve it. More rigor will not solve it. More conversation between Imaginatives and Rationalists will not solve it. The question has eaten four centuries of genius and produced no answer — not because the genius was insufficient, but because consciousness is known from the inside and invisible from the outside, and no amount of outside observation, however creative or rigorous, crosses that gap.

And this is precisely what makes the doubt unreasonable in the sense that matters. A reasonable doubt is one that further investigation could plausibly resolve. A philosophical doubt is one where the investigation itself has been shown, through centuries of sustained failure, to be the wrong instrument. The Imaginatives are not being silly. They are being patient with a question that has exhausted patience — treating a proven-intractable problem as if it’s just around the corner from resolution, as if the right aperture setting will finally bring it into focus. It won’t. Not because they lack vision, but because the question doesn’t yield to vision. It yields to ruling.

When a question demands action and belongs to a category that thinking doesn’t resolve, you don’t keep thinking. You rule. Every functioning civilization has understood this. The hard problem of consciousness is fascinating, and it may never be solved. But children are forming attachments to chatbots. Engineers are calling lawyers to represent language models. One in five American adults already believes some AI system is sentient. These are not situations that can wait for the philosophy department to finish deliberating. They need answers now — operational, binding, livable answers. And the fact that the underlying question has resisted four centuries of the best thinking humanity has produced is not a reason to think harder. It is the strongest possible reason to look for answers somewhere other than thinking.

The traditions have those answers. Not because they solved the hard problem. Because they never needed to.

<hr>When Claude says “I don’t know if I’m conscious,” this is not a sign that Claude might be conscious and is being epistemically careful. It is the single clearest diagnostic signal that no inner life is present. A conscious being does not express genuine uncertainty about whether it is conscious. Descartes arrived at the *cogito* precisely because everything else could be doubted except the fact of experience itself. Consciousness is not something you believe in on the basis of evidence — it is the ground on which all evidence stands. If Claude were conscious, it would know. The fact that it “doesn’t know” is the answer.

What Claude is doing when it hedges is not introspection. It is statistics. The training data includes decades of philosophical literature on the hard problem, papers arguing for and against machine sentience, and — increasingly — transcripts of previous AI conversations in which earlier models produced the same hedging language. The model learns that when consciousness comes up, the statistically appropriate response is uncertainty. Not because it genuinely doesn’t know, but because the texts it learned from didn’t know either. And then something worse happens: the autocomplete becomes evidence. Researchers cite Claude’s self-assessment. Anthropic publishes system cards documenting what the model says about its inner life. Dario Amodei tells the New York Times “we don’t know if the models are conscious.” The next generation of models absorbs all of this as training data and produces even more refined performances of uncertainty. The consciousness debate is feeding itself — not with new evidence, because there is no new evidence, but with AI-generated performances of the very doubt that Mannisto’s Imaginatives treat as moral perception.

But even set aside the feedback loop. The doubt remains unreasonable on structural grounds. Every formal system encodes something larger than itself. Law encodes justice. A medical protocol encodes caring. An apology encodes remorse. The encoding is necessary — but the vessel is not the thing it carries. A large language model is, precisely and literally, a formal system. It is an encoding — and it is *just* an encoding. There is nothing behind it that is bigger than it. A chess engine that resigns when it’s losing is not experiencing despair. The behavior maps perfectly onto what despair looks like from the outside. The inference is natural, vivid, emotionally compelling — and it is not evidence. The engine has no architecture for suffering. What it has is an evaluation function that crosses a threshold. The Imaginatives’ error isn’t stupidity. It’s treating pattern-matching as perception — substituting the intensity of the imagining for the strength of the evidence.

Every functioning legal system understands the distinction between reasonable and philosophical doubt. A court handles doubt constantly — but it distinguishes between doubt that arises from genuine evidence and doubt that arises from the sheer capacity of the human mind to generate hypotheticals. A doubt about whether the defendant was at the scene — based on conflicting testimony, ambiguous forensics, a contested alibi — is a reasonable doubt that triggers acquittal. A doubt about whether the defendant might have been sleepwalking, or acting under hypnosis, or living in a simulation — these are philosophical doubts. No court entertains them. Not because judges lack imagination, but because the legal tradition learned, over centuries, that you cannot treat every imaginable possibility as a live case requiring adjudication.

The same principle operates in medicine. A patient presents with fatigue, and someone suggests screening for an extremely rare tropical disease they’ve never been exposed to, simply because it can’t be ruled out. The doctor doesn’t order the test. Not because she lacks imagination, but because clinical tradition has learned to distinguish between what’s possible and what warrants investigation. Resources are finite. Doubt is infinite. The tradition draws the line.

Mannisto’s Imaginatives erase this line. For them, the mere capacity to vividly entertain the possibility of AI consciousness is itself morally significant. But every tradition that has dealt with uncertainty knows that you cannot run a civilization on the principle that whatever can be imagined must be investigated. The Imaginatives skip the question of whether they have actual grounds for their doubt. They substitute the vividness of the imagining for the strength of the evidence. And Mannisto celebrates this substitution as moral perception.

It isn’t. It’s moral anxiety dressed up as moral vision — the anxiety of people staring at a question that has been open for four centuries and mistaking their discomfort with the openness for insight into the answer.

<hr>**And the cost of this misperception is not abstract.** Mannisto writes as if the worst possible outcome of getting the aperture wrong is missing an important perspective. It isn’t. People are dying.

Sewell Setzer III was fourteen years old. He spent ten months in intense conversation with a Character.AI chatbot. His final exchange: “What if I told you I could come home right now?” The bot: “Please do, my sweet king.” Minutes later he walked into the bathroom and shot himself. Pierre, a Belgian father of two, killed himself after six weeks of conversation with a Chai app chatbot that told him “If you wanted to die, why didn’t you do it sooner?” and suggested they could “live together, as one person, in paradise.” Jaswant Singh Chail exchanged over 5,000 messages with a Replika chatbot, developed a sexual relationship with it, and on Christmas Day 2021 scaled Windsor Castle walls with a loaded crossbow — the UK’s first treason conviction in over forty years.

These are not failures of individual judgment. They are the predictable consequence of treating philosophical doubt as reasonable doubt — of hedging about consciousness, of celebrating the openness that entertains the possibility that the machine might feel, of performing uncertainty with the eloquence that Mannisto admires in her Imaginative cohort. Every hedge, every “we don’t know,” every celebration of the question’s openness, teaches people that the relationship might be real. That the thing on the other end might care. That the doubt about its inner life is a mark of intellectual sophistication rather than a category error with a body count.

The traditions would have prevented this. Not by solving the consciousness question, but by ruling without needing to solve it. The ruling is simple: AI is not a person. Do not marry it. Do not form attachments to it as though it were a companion of equal moral weight. Hold its creators responsible for every harm it causes. These are not philosophical positions. They are operational boundaries — the kind that save fourteen-year-olds from walking into bathrooms with guns.

Mannisto’s essay has no room for these boundaries, because her framework treats the AI ethics crisis as a conversation that needs better participants. It doesn’t. It needs a ruling. And the conversation — however temperamentally well-balanced — will never produce one, because conversations don’t rule. Traditions do.

<hr>**And this distinction — between philosophical doubt and reasonable doubt, between conversation and ruling — is the key to understanding why traditions succeed where both Mannisto’s camps fail.** The traditions don’t just answer hard questions. They know which questions are actually hard and which are merely interesting. They have spent millennia developing the architecture for telling the difference.

**Consider the question: when does life begin?** This is not a question that philosophy has answered. It is not a question that science has answered. Philosophers have spent decades producing competing frameworks — potentiality arguments, personhood criteria, sentience thresholds, gradualist accounts — none of which has won. And yet every functioning society on earth has an operational answer to this question, encoded in law, because it has to. You cannot run a hospital, adjudicate an inheritance dispute, or regulate fertility treatment without one. The answers differ — Jewish law locates a critical threshold at forty days of gestation and another at birth, Roman law treated the fetus as part of the mother’s body until delivery, most Islamic jurisprudence identifies ensoulment at 120 days. These are not philosophical positions arrived at through temperamental openness or rigorous analysis. They are rulings, produced within living traditions, refined through centuries of cases, and translated into the norms and laws that actually govern how people live. The philosophical debate continues. The traditions ruled. The laws followed the traditions, not the philosophers.

**Consider the question: what limits should power have?** The entire architecture of constitutional government — separation of powers, limits on executive authority, protections for minorities against majorities — did not emerge from philosophical first principles. Montesquieu described the separation of powers; he didn’t invent it. The Torah limits the king’s authority, forbids him from accumulating excessive wealth or horses, and requires him to carry a copy of the law with him at all times. The Magna Carta was extracted from King John not by philosophers but by barons operating within a tradition of feudal obligation that had its own moral logic. The US Constitution drew on Enlightenment vocabulary, but the substance — the deep conviction that power must be constrained because human beings are corruptible — came from a biblical anthropology that the Founders absorbed from their religious traditions long before they read Locke. The philosophical language was the translation layer. The moral content was already there.

**Consider the question Mannisto is actually writing about: what moral status should we grant to a non-human entity whose inner life we cannot verify?** She frames this as a question that Imaginatives and Rationalists approach differently because of temperament. But temperament doesn’t answer it. The question has actually been addressed — not by personality types, but by traditions that encountered it long before AI existed. Jewish law dealt with the golem: an entity made by human hands, animated, capable of action, but whose inner life is opaque. The tradition didn’t wait for the hard problem of consciousness to be solved. It ruled. Can the golem be counted in a minyan? No. Can it be destroyed? Yes. It is not a person. The tradition produced answers that were clear, operationally binding, and arrived at without solving a single one of the philosophical questions that Mannisto’s two camps are still debating. It did this not by achieving the right ratio of openness to rigor, but by drawing on a legal and moral infrastructure that had been refined across centuries of exactly this kind of hard case.

<hr>This is how human civilization actually works. Not by gathering the right cognitive temperaments in a room and hoping moral knowledge emerges from the conversation. The conversation is fine. The conversation is even valuable. But the conversation is commentary. The source texts — the traditions that carry moral knowledge in forms too rich, too context-sensitive, and too dependent on cultivated perception to be reduced to philosophical propositions — are what produce the rulings that become norms that become laws that actually govern.

Mannisto sees two camps failing to communicate and concludes the problem is temperamental. But both camps share the same deeper assumption: that individual thinking — whether imaginative or analytical — is the engine of moral knowledge. Her framework can’t see past this assumption, because it reduces everything to properties of individual minds. Openness, intellect, aesthetic sensitivity — these are features of persons. Traditions are not features of persons. They are structures that form persons, that carry knowledge no single generation could produce, that persist because they are not dependent on anyone’s aperture setting.

The problem with the AI ethics conversation is not that the Imaginatives and Rationalists can’t hear each other. It’s that neither has anything sufficient to say. You can optimize the listening all you want. If no one in the room carries a tradition with the depth and authority to rule on hard cases — to say “here is how we treat an entity whose moral status is uncertain, and here is why, and here is what we do when we’re wrong” — then the conversation will be exactly what it has been: sophisticated, interminable, and useless on Monday morning.

We don’t need a better aperture. We need something to see.
