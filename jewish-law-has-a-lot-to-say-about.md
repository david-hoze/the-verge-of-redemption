# Jewish law has a lot to say about AI training and the plagiarism double standard

[- - 

](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!iMqS!,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2F685834ef-7a1c-49ff-8c92-38f0e0f86737_2048x1152.jpeg)**Jewish law offers a devastating critique of the claim that “the only difference is the legal budget.”** It not only prohibits the kind of unattributed copying at the heart of AI training — it explicitly forbids applying different standards to the powerful and the powerless for the same act. Across Talmudic, medieval, and contemporary sources, the tradition provides robust frameworks for intellectual property, mandatory attribution, equal justice, and ethical obligations that transcend mere legal compliance. The emerging consensus among Jewish legal scholars is that even if secular law permits AI training on copyrighted data through doctrines like “fair use,” Jewish law imposes independent and often stricter obligations that companies cannot simply budget their way around.

## Jewish law recognized intellectual property centuries before copyright existed

The framework for intellectual property rests on several interlocking prohibitions, each addressing a different dimension of the problem AI training raises.

The prohibition against **encroachment on boundaries**, rooted in Deuteronomy 19:14’s command not to move a neighbor’s landmark, was extended by rabbinic authorities to cover economic encroachment and unfair competition. The Talmud (Bava Batra 21b) uses a striking analogy: fishing nets must be kept away from another fisherman’s bait “the full length of the fish’s swim.” Even though uncaught fish are technically ownerless, the first fisherman’s *effort* in baiting his traps creates a protected interest. Rabbi Moshe Sofer (the Chatam Sofer, 1762–1839) drew a direct line from this principle to intellectual property in multiple responsa, arguing that an author’s creative effort grants protected economic rights — and that without such protection, “which fool would risk a heavy financial loss? Publication will cease, God forbid, and Torah study will be weakened.”

The foundational case of copyright in Jewish law arose in 1550 when Rabbi Meir Katzenellenbogen invested heavily in a careful critical edition of Maimonides’ legal code, only to have a rival printer copy his annotations and sell the edition at a lower price. Rabbi Moshe Isserles (the Rema), in Responsa no. 10, invoked boundary encroachment and issued a ban of excommunication against purchasing the pirated edition. This established the template for **over 3,600 rabbinic printing approbations** issued between 1499 and 1850 — a Jewish proto-copyright system centuries before the Berne Convention.

Classical Jewish law defines theft in terms of physical objects with minimum value. Yet the overwhelming modern consensus, as documented by Rabbi Nachum Menasha Weisfish and confirmed by Rabbi Moshe Feinstein, Rabbi Yosef Shalom Elyashiv, and Rabbi Asher Weiss, holds that copying copyrighted materials without consent constitutes actual theft. Rabbi Shimon Shkop offers an ingenious proof: the Torah assigns liability to the pit-digger for the intangible vapor inside his pit that harms animals (Bava Kama 3a–b), demonstrating that ownership can attach to non-physical creations. The Talmud in Gittin 59b–60a provides another precedent: a poor person who shakes olives from an ownerless tree acquires a protected interest through effort alone — taking those olives constitutes rabbinic theft. The parallel to content creators whose work feeds AI training is direct.

**Deception** (literally “stealing the mind”) rounds out the framework. The Talmud (Chullin 94a) records Shmuel’s ruling that “it is forbidden to steal the mind of anyone, even a non-Jew.” The Midrash calls this **the worst of the seven types of theft** because it attacks the person’s judgment directly. Rabbi Yeshayahu Horowitz (the Shlah) goes further: “The theft of Torah ideas is worse than the theft of money. The soul of a Jew and the Torah are one; thus stealing Torah ideas is tantamount to wounding the soul.” When AI systems present synthesized output without disclosing that it derives from identifiable human authors, this creates precisely the kind of false impression this prohibition targets.

## “Whoever quotes a source by name brings redemption to the world” — and AI strips that attribution

The Talmud’s most powerful statement on attribution appears in **Megillah 15a**: “Rabbi Elazar said in the name of Rabbi Chanina: Whoever reports a saying in the name of the one who said it brings redemption to the world, as it states: ‘And Esther told the king in the name of Mordechai’ (Esther 2:22).” This teaching also appears in Ethics of the Fathers 6:6 as the **48th and final quality** by which Torah is acquired — saying a thing in the name of the one who said it.

The Esther narrative gives this principle dramatic force. Mordechai discovered a plot to assassinate King Ahasuerus and reported it to Esther, who told the king **explicitly crediting Mordechai**. This act of attribution was recorded in the royal chronicles. When Haman later plotted genocide against the Jews, the king’s sleepless reading of those chronicles — and his discovery of his unpaid debt to Mordechai — set in motion the reversal that saved the Jewish people. Attribution literally brought redemption. The Maharal explains the deeper logic: a person who credits the true source demonstrates the character of giving credit where due rather than seeking undeserved glory, and only such a person merits bringing sanctification of God’s name.

The debate over whether attribution is a legal obligation or merely a virtue has significant implications for AI. The Magen Avraham rules that failing to name one’s source transgresses a rabbinic negative commandment. The Sefer Chassidim bases the obligation on “Do not steal your friend’s boundary” (Deuteronomy 19:14). The Midrash Tanchuma states explicitly: “whoever does not name his source transgresses a negative command,” citing Proverbs 22:22: “Do not steal from the poor.” The Shach goes so far as to say that presenting another’s teaching without attribution is “wearing a garment that is not his.”

There are recognized exceptions — if the source does not care to be named, if the information has been so internalized through independent research that it has become one’s own, or if naming the source would embarrass them. This last exception raises an interesting question about AI: do large language models “internalize” their training data sufficiently to constitute independent knowledge? The answer is almost certainly no. As Rabbi Aaron Levine argues in *Moral Issues of the Marketplace in Jewish Law*, the obligation of attribution is also rooted in **gratitude**. The Midrash teaches that Moses did not initiate the first plagues against Egypt because the Nile had protected him as an infant — the law requires gratitude even to inanimate objects. AI systems that ingest human creativity and produce output competing with those very creators represent an inversion of this principle.

## “Hear the small and the great alike”: Jewish law’s prohibition on the legal-budget defense

The claim that “the only difference is the legal budget” directly implicates one of the tradition’s most fundamental principles. **Deuteronomy 1:17** commands: “Do not show favoritism in judgment; hear the small and the great alike; do not fear any man, for judgment belongs to God.” **Leviticus 19:15** sharpens the prohibition from both directions: “Do not favor the poor, and do not honor the great.”

The Talmud took these principles to extraordinary procedural lengths. Shevuot 31a records that when one litigant appeared in rags and the other in expensive clothing, the court would instruct the wealthy party: “Dress like the poor person, or dress the poor person in garments like yours.” Rava bar Rav Huna required litigants to remove their fine shoes before appearing in court. The Talmud recognized that **visible power disparity itself corrupts justice**, even when the judge intends to be fair.

The most iconic biblical illustration is Nathan confronting King David (2 Samuel 12:1–13) with the parable of a rich man who takes a poor man’s single lamb. David, the most powerful person in Israel, is told *“You are that man!”* — and is held fully accountable. The principle is clear: power creates no exemption from moral law.

More remarkably, Jewish law holds the powerful to a **higher** standard, not a lower one. In the landmark case of Bava Metzia 83a, porters negligently broke Rabbah bar bar Channah’s wine barrel. By strict law, the porters owed damages, and Rabbah seized their garments. When the porters complained to Rav, he ordered Rabbah to return the garments *and* pay the porters’ wages — not because the law required it, but because Rabbah, as a Torah scholar and public figure, was held to the standard of acting **beyond the letter of the law**. The commentators are explicit: *greater status creates greater ethical obligation, not lesser*. Applied to the AI context, companies with enormous resources and influence bear a heavier ethical burden than a student submitting a paper — the precise opposite of the current reality.

## Contemporary analysis treats AI as a tool, not a person — and its owners bear full responsibility

The most authoritative treatment of AI’s legal status is **Rabbi Danny Nevins’ 2019 responsum** for the Conservative Movement’s Committee on Jewish Law and Standards, titled “Artificial Intelligence, Moral Machines and Halakhah.” Nevins examines whether AI can serve as a legal agent and concludes it cannot, because agency requires a conscious, morally obligated being. AI functions more like a sophisticated tool — analogous to property rather than a person — whose outputs belong to and remain the responsibility of the owner.

This analysis invokes the Talmudic principle that **“the hand of the servant is like the hand of the master,”** which holds that what a servant acquires belongs to the master. Applied to AI, this means the companies deploying these systems cannot disclaim responsibility for what their tools produce or consume. As Rabbi Gil Student wrote on Torah Musings in 2025: “AI is not a person to whom you owe gratitude. It is an object, a stick or a stone.” This classification cuts both ways — AI cannot hold rights, but its operators bear full liability.

The **Israeli Patent Office’s DABUS ruling** reinforced this framework in secular law. When Dr. Stephen Thaler attempted to list his AI system “DABUS” as the inventor on patent applications, the Israeli Patent Commissioner rejected the claim, and the Tel Aviv District Court affirmed in December 2025 that an AI system cannot be an inventor under Israeli law. The court held that the “natural and ordinary” meaning of “inventor” is intrinsically linked to human agency. Israel joined a near-universal global consensus — only South Africa has accepted AI inventorship.

Among contemporary authorities, several have addressed AI and copyright directly. The Chabad.org analysis presents three historical approaches: Rabbi Yosef Shaul Nathanson holds that authors have *inherent* intellectual property rights from the Torah itself; Rabbi Yitzchok Shmelkes holds that copyright is enforceable only through the principle that the law of the land is binding; and a third view sees rabbinic bans as merely encouraging publication. Even under the most lenient reading, the Chabad analysis notes that if AI output “contains significant changes that make it barely recognizable compared to the original, it might not be considered the same product” — but this requires genuine transformation, not mere relabeling.

## The principle that “the law of the land is the law” does not let companies off the ethical hook

The principle that the law of the land is binding, first articulated by the Babylonian sage Shmuel and appearing in four Talmudic passages, is often cited as grounds for following secular copyright law. But its application to the AI training question is more complex than it appears.

The Rashbam grounds the principle in social contract theory — law binds because citizens accept the sovereign’s authority. Rambam similarly roots it in consent. Both frameworks support compliance with democratic copyright law. But the principle has critical limits: it applies only to monetary and civil matters, not ritual law; the law must apply equitably to all inhabitants; it cannot override Torah prohibitions; and it is a one-directional principle — secular law can add obligations, but secular permissions do not automatically become permissions under Jewish law.

This last point is decisive. If secular copyright law has a “fair use” exception that AI companies rely on, **Jewish law does not automatically accept that exception**. Under Nathanson’s view that intellectual property rights are inherent in Torah law, the analysis is entirely independent of secular frameworks — rights may be violated even when secular law permits the action. Under Shmelkes’ approach, one follows secular law — but only once courts definitively establish what that law permits, and ongoing litigation suggests the question is far from settled.

Moreover, Jewish law imposes ethical obligations that go well beyond legal compliance. **Bava Metzia 30b** contains what may be the most relevant teaching for the entire debate: “Rabbi Yochanan said: Jerusalem was destroyed only because they based their judgments strictly on Torah law.” The Gemara asks in astonishment — should they have judged corruptly? No: “The problem was that they insisted on the strict letter of the law and did not go beyond it.” Tosafot connects this to the destruction’s other attributed cause, baseless hatred, explaining that both are the same phenomenon — insisting on maximal legal rights breeds social collapse. The Ramban codifies this: the Torah commands doing “the straight and the good” precisely because it is impossible for written law to regulate every human interaction. Companies that exploit the gap between what is technically legal and what is substantively just are, in this framework, reenacting the very behavior that the Talmud warns leads to civilizational destruction.

The prohibition against **placing a stumbling block before the blind** (Leviticus 19:14) adds another dimension. The Talmud extends this beyond literal blindness to prohibit facilitating wrongdoing — creating conditions that enable others to transgress when they otherwise could not. When companies build legal and technical architectures that reframe copying as “training,” they potentially place stumbling blocks before an entire industry.

## Conclusion: Jewish law sees through the relabeling

The tradition converges from multiple directions on a single verdict: **renaming an action does not change its moral character, and the powerful bear greater — not lesser — responsibility for ethical conduct**. The Chatam Sofer’s extension of boundary encroachment to creative works, the Talmud’s insistence on attribution as a path to redemption, the procedural requirement that litigants remove markers of wealth before facing judgment, the explicit teaching that Torah scholars and powerful figures must exceed the legal minimum — these are not peripheral teachings. They represent core commitments of the system.

Three insights emerge that secular copyright discourse has largely missed. First, Jewish law’s concept of intellectual property is in some respects **broader** than secular copyright — under the Nathanson view, it is inherent and perpetual, not time-limited or dependent on government grant. Second, the requirement to go beyond the letter of the law means that legal compliance is the ethical **floor**, not the ceiling — and that insisting on bare legality while causing harm is itself a form of moral failure the Talmud associates with civilizational collapse. Third, the principle that the servant’s hand is the master’s hand ensures that AI’s status as a non-person does not absolve its operators — to the contrary, it concentrates all moral responsibility on the humans and corporations who deploy these systems.

The original claim — that the only difference between “training” and “plagiarism” is the legal budget — finds in Jewish law not just agreement but amplification. The tradition would add: the difference in legal budget is itself the injustice, and the refusal to apply the same standard to the powerful and the powerless is precisely the corruption that the command “do not show favoritism in judgment” was given to prevent.
