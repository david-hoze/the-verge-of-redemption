And what does the Western intellectual tradition — the one that was supposed to have moved past this — offer in response?

More questions.

<hr>Philosophy is diagnostics without a pharmacy. It can identify a disease with extraordinary precision. It just can’t treat it. And at some point, you have to ask whether a doctor who has been diagnosing the same condition for three centuries without ever writing a prescription is still practicing medicine — or just billing for visits.

The trolley problem is sixty years old. Unsettled. The hard problem of consciousness — four hundred years if you start with Descartes, thirty if you start with Chalmers. Unsettled. The is-ought gap — two hundred and eighty years since Hume. Unsettled. The fairness impossibility theorem — mathematically proven, no resolution. Philosophy tells you *that* you have a problem. It cannot tell you what to do Monday morning.

The traditions can. When the Sanhedrin encountered a case with no precedent, they didn’t write a paper. They ruled. And the minority opinion was preserved — not as a footnote, not as a curiosity, but as a live option for when the circumstances changed. That’s not philosophy. That’s governance. Operational, binding, lived. The trolley problem, reframed as a question about which lives to prioritize in a specific set of circumstances, has been adjudicated in Jewish law — case by case, context by context — for longer than the discipline of philosophy has existed.

<hr>Someone will object: philosophy gave us logic, empiricism, political rights, ethics. These are real. Nobody should dismiss them. But look more carefully at what philosophy actually did with each of these, and a pattern emerges.

It described what was already happening.

Empiricism — people were observing, testing, and refining based on results for thousands of years before anyone called it a method. Egyptian physicians documented treatments and outcomes. Babylonian astronomers tracked celestial patterns with extraordinary precision. Ibn al-Haytham conducted rigorous optical experiments in the 11th century — controlled variables, reproducible results, peer review — seven hundred years before the Western philosophy of science got around to formalizing what he was already doing. The Talmud is relentlessly empirical; half its reasoning begins with “what do we observe?” Farmers have been running controlled experiments since the invention of agriculture. Nobody needed Francis Bacon’s permission to notice what works.

Logic — people reason validly without studying Aristotle. Mathematics flourished in Babylon and Egypt long before the Greeks formalized the syllogism. Aristotle organized what was already operational and gave it technical vocabulary. The contribution was real — naming and systematizing things is useful. But it’s curation, not creation.

Political rights — the Torah limits the king’s power, protects the stranger, mandates rest for workers and animals, and redistributes land every fifty years. Jubilee is a functioning economic reset mechanism with built-in protections for the vulnerable, operating millennia before Locke put pen to paper. The concept of rights did not begin with the Enlightenment. The Enlightenment gave it a secular vocabulary.

So what did philosophy actually add? One thing, genuinely: the ability to discuss ethics, knowledge, and governance in a tradition-neutral language. A translation layer. It lets people from different backgrounds reason together without first agreeing on revelation.

That’s valuable. But it’s a service role, not a foundational one. And for a service role, it comes with extraordinary overhead — entire university departments, centuries of literature, and an implicit claim to be the *source* of ideas it was merely *describing*. You don’t need Kant to explain the golden rule to someone who learned it from Hillel rather than from Jesus. You need a sentence.

<hr>And alongside the useful parts — the description, the translation — philosophy has produced entire fields that generate no consequences at all.

Philosophy of mind is the starkest example. Descartes split mind from body in 1641. The field has been trying to put them back together ever since. We now have dualism, property dualism, functionalism, eliminativism, panpsychism, illusionism, and a dozen other positions, each with serious defenders, none of which has won. I spent months responding to a philosophy of mind paper — a sophisticated argument that qualia are “non-operational.” The author relocated the mystery with impressive precision. He didn’t dissolve it. Nobody has. Nobody will, through philosophy alone, because you cannot arrive at first-person experience through third-person analysis. The tools are wrong for the job. But philosophy keeps trying, because third-person analysis is the only tool it has.

And here’s what should disturb everyone about this: the traditions never needed to solve the hard problem of consciousness to know how to treat people. The prohibition against murder didn’t wait for a peer-reviewed theory of subjective experience. “Love your neighbor as yourself” doesn’t require resolving the explanatory gap first. A mother doesn’t need Chalmers to know her child is in pain. A judge doesn’t need Dennett to know that destroying a person is wrong. The knowing was never in question. Philosophy was a four-century conversation about whether the knowing *counts.*

The traditions built functioning moral systems on the premise that some questions — however fascinating — are not prerequisites for moral action. You act first. You keep thinking about it. You never stop thinking about it. But you don’t wait for the thinking to finish before you act, because the thinking will never finish, and people are waiting.

<hr>Then there’s postmodernism, which is worse than useless — it’s actively destructive.

Derrida, Foucault, Baudrillard. What’s the application? The systematic deconstruction of every stable framework for meaning, truth, and authority, with nothing offered to replace them. Postmodernism didn’t just fail to answer the questions philosophy left open. It argued that the questions themselves were illegitimate — that meaning is constructed, truth is power, and any claim to moral authority is a disguised act of domination.

The result is measurable. Deaths of despair tripled in twenty-five years among middle-aged Americans. Half of US adults report measurable loneliness — with health effects equivalent to smoking fifteen cigarettes a day. Fifty-eight percent of young adults say they lack meaning or purpose. Real incomes tripled over seven decades and happiness flatlined. A civilization that systematically dismantled every framework for answering “what is this all for?” is now surprised that nobody can answer the question.

Postmodernism didn’t solve a problem. It *is* the problem. It is the philosophy of the void. And voids don’t stay empty.

If you’re not opinionated about life’s purpose, someone else will be. Velayat-e Faqih in Iran. Dispensationalist premillennialism in the US military. Bad theology rushes into the space that good theology vacated. The commanders citing the Book of Revelation to their troops aren’t aberrations. They’re the predictable consequence of a civilization that spent fifty years teaching its brightest minds that meaning is a construct — and then acted surprised when people went looking for meaning in the worst possible places.

The vacuum doesn’t wait for your next journal article.

<hr>Someone will raise the obvious counterargument: traditions have caused harm too. The Crusades. The Inquisition. Religious wars. Forced conversions. This is true, and it matters. But notice what the counterargument actually proves.

The bloodiest regimes of the 20th century — Stalin’s USSR, Mao’s China, Pol Pot’s Cambodia — were explicitly secular. They didn’t lack tribalism. They relocated it from religious identity to ideological identity. The 20th century’s greatest atrocities were committed not by people who believed too much, but by people who believed in the wrong things — or in nothing at all. Tribalism is the constant. Religion is one possible vehicle, and secular ideology is another. Strip away the religious framework, and what remains is not neutral rationality. It is a different set of unexamined commitments — often more dangerous, because they don’t know they’re commitments.

And the traditions that caused harm? They also contained the tools for diagnosing the harm. Judaism’s prophets reserved their harshest words not for foreign empires, but for their own people. The tradition that produced the Crusades also produced Francis of Assisi, Dietrich Bonhoeffer, and the Quakers. The tradition that produced forced conversions also produced the concept of *teshuvah* — return, self-correction, the institutional capacity to say “we were wrong.”

Philosophy has no equivalent mechanism. It can critique from outside. It cannot correct from within. A philosophical tradition doesn’t *repent*. It just publishes a new paper.

<hr>This matters now more than it ever has, because the AI alignment crisis is philosophy’s failure made operational.

The field has spent years trying to build ethical AI from philosophical first principles — and keeps collapsing. “Align with human values” falls apart the moment you ask *whose* values. Google’s AI ethics board lasted less than a week. OpenAI’s board crisis was a war between competing philosophical commitments — effective altruism versus commercial acceleration — with no adjudication mechanism. The fairness impossibility theorem proves mathematically that you cannot satisfy multiple reasonable definitions of fairness simultaneously. Philosophy identified the problem with extraordinary clarity. It has no solution. It was never going to have a solution. Because philosophy doesn’t *rule*. It *deliberates*. And deliberation without decision is just sophisticated paralysis.

Jewish law has been adjudicating between competing values for three thousand years. Not by resolving the tension philosophically — by ruling. Case by case. Context by context. With the humility to preserve the dissent and the authority to act anyway. With graduated liability — not all harms are equal. With affirmative obligation — not just “do no harm” but “you must prevent harm.” With the parapet principle — if you build a roof, you must build a railing, *before* someone falls. The duty is anticipatory, not reactive.

These are not philosophical positions. These are operational governance structures, tested across centuries, refined through lived cases, carrying binding authority. The AI alignment field doesn’t need another paper on value pluralism. It needs a legal tradition that has already survived the challenge of pluralism — and produced workable answers that people actually live by.

<hr>A recent note on Substack observed that prior to the mid-20th century, it was nearly impossible to access good translations of important texts from distant traditions. Today we have no excuse for failing to take world philosophy seriously.

I agree. But I’d push further. The traditions aren’t philosophy. Philosophy describes. Traditions prescribe. And the crisis we’re in isn’t that we failed to read enough philosophy. It’s that we read philosophy *instead of* the traditions — and philosophy gave us better questions without answers, while the traditions had answers the whole time.

Philosophy described what traditions had built. Then it stripped out the binding authority. Then it left the questions permanently open. Then postmodernism said the questions themselves were meaningless. And now we wonder why people are dying of despair amid unprecedented abundance, why bad theology fills the space that good theology vacated, why a civilization with the most sophisticated intellectual tradition in human history cannot tell its own children what life is for.

Philosophy is a commentary tradition that forgot it was a commentary — and started thinking it was the source text.

The source texts are still there. They’ve been there the whole time. They fell out of fashion because they came with obligations, and obligations are uncomfortable. They ask you to be *bound* by something — not just to discuss it, not just to analyze it, not just to deconstruct it, but to live inside it and let it shape how you act on Monday morning, when the questions are still open and someone is waiting for an answer.

The traditions have answers. Not perfect ones. Not final ones. But operational ones — tested across millennia, refined through lived experience, preserved in their full complexity including the disagreements. That’s not philosophy. That’s wisdom. And wisdom is what we’re dying without.
