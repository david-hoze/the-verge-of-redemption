# The Sword and the Parapet: What Jewish Law Says About the Anthropic-Pentagon Dispute That Neither Side Is Saying

[](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!83bz!,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2Ffa474b50-c284-479e-9a78-f0cdc449bd1c_350x234.jpeg)
### Ancient legal reasoning resolves a modern AI standoff — but not the way you’d expect

There is a post circulating that frames the Anthropic-Pentagon confrontation as a courage narrative: brave company says no to mass surveillance and autonomous weapons, gets blacklisted, industry rallies to its defense. Remember who stood alone.

It’s a compelling story. It’s also the wrong framework. It turns a structural question into a character test — and character tests teach nothing usable for the next time this happens.

Jewish halakhic and Talmudic sources offer something better: a legal architecture, refined across millennia, that holds both the obligation to defend and the obligation to constrain as simultaneously binding — and identifies the specific failure mode on each side of the dispute. The tradition that commands “rise up and kill” the pursuer is the same tradition that demands 23 judges deliberate overnight before imposing a death sentence. The law that prohibits standing idly by your neighbor’s blood is the law that prohibits placing a stumbling block before the blind.

This is not a story about who was brave. It is a story about two genuine obligations colliding — and the only legal tradition in history that was designed to hold both at once.

## What actually happened

In early 2026, the Pentagon [demanded that Anthropic remove contractual restrictions](https://www.cnn.com/2026/02/26/tech/anthropic-rejects-pentagon-offer) on how its AI model Claude could be used by the military. Anthropic maintained two red lines: no mass domestic surveillance, and no fully autonomous weapons. The Pentagon insisted on “all lawful use.” [Anthropic refused](https://www.anthropic.com/news/statement-department-of-war). The government [designated the company a supply-chain risk](https://www.npr.org/2026/02/27/nx-s1-5729118/trump-anthropic-pentagon-openai-ai-weapons-ban) — a classification normally reserved for foreign adversaries — canceled its $200 million contract, and ordered all federal agencies to stop using Claude.

Within hours, [OpenAI swept in to sign a Pentagon deal of its own](https://fortune.com/2026/02/28/openai-pentagon-deal-anthropic-designated-supply-chain-risk-unprecedented-action-damage-its-growth/). The backlash was immediate: ChatGPT uninstalls surged, Claude downloads spiked, and an OpenAI executive quit over the rush. [Microsoft filed an amicus brief supporting Anthropic](https://federalnewsnetwork.com/artificial-intelligence/2026/03/microsoft-backs-anthropic-urging-a-judge-to-halt-pentagons-actions-against-ai-company/). [Over 30 employees from Google and OpenAI filed their own](https://fortune.com/2026/03/10/google-openai-employees-back-anthropic-legal-fight-military-use-of-ai/). [Tech industry groups representing hundreds of companies — including Meta, Nvidia, Adobe, and Deloitte — joined the coalition](https://www.axios.com/2026/03/16/tech-industry-rallies-anthropic-pentagon-fight). [Nearly 150 retired judges added their names](https://www.cnn.com/2026/03/17/tech/former-judges-support-anthropic). [Catholic ethicists filed a brief invoking just war doctrine](https://www.ncregister.com/news/catholic-amicus-brief-backing-anthropic-in-pentagon).

The near-total unanimity of support is striking. It is also, if you’re paying attention to the incentives, suspicious.

## The Sanhedrin unanimity principle: when everyone agrees, ask harder questions

The Talmud records a remarkable legal principle in [Sanhedrin 17a](https://www.sefaria.org/Sanhedrin.17a): if all seventy-one judges of the Sanhedrin vote unanimously to convict in a capital case, the defendant is *acquitted*. Not convicted — acquitted. Because unanimous agreement in a matter of life and death signals that the court has not tried hard enough to find merit in the defense. It suggests groupthink, not truth.

Apply that here: Microsoft has [$5 billion invested in Anthropic](https://www.cnbc.com/2026/03/10/microsoft-says-court-should-temporarily-block-pentagon-ban-anthropic.html). Google, Amazon, and Apple all run Anthropic’s models on their cloud platforms. The industry groups aren’t defending Anthropic’s *ethics* — they’re defending the principle that the government can’t weaponize a supply-chain designation to punish a contractor. That’s a procurement precedent question, not a moral one. Every company in that coalition is thinking: *if they do this to Anthropic today, they can do it to us tomorrow.*

The Sanhedrin principle says: when the unanimity is this complete, look harder at what’s driving it. Self-interest doesn’t invalidate the position. But it should make you less confident this is a story about ethics — and more confident it’s a story about power.

So let’s set aside the hero narrative and ask the real question: does either side have the law — not the secular law, but the deeper law — on its side?

## The obligation to defend: three pillars that cannot be dismissed

The halakhic tradition does not permit passivity in the face of existential threat. The obligation to defend rests on three independent sources, each sufficient on its own.

**First, defensive war is *****milchemet mitzvah***** — an obligatory war.** [Maimonides codifies](https://www.sefaria.org/Mishneh_Torah%2C_Kings_and_Wars.5.1) three categories of obligatory war, including *ezrat Yisrael mi-yad tzar* — assisting Israel against an attacking enemy. Unlike discretionary war, which requires authorization from a Sanhedrin of seventy-one, defensive war carries no procedural prerequisites. The [Mishnah in Sotah 44b](https://www.sefaria.org/Sotah.44b) establishes that in obligatory war, “even a groom from his chamber and a bride from her canopy” must participate. No exemptions. [Rabbi J. David Bleich’s analysis in ](https://www.jstor.org/stable/23259450)*[Tradition](https://www.jstor.org/stable/23259450)* carefully maps the halakhic distinction between preemptive and preventive war, concluding that defense against clearly imminent threats qualifies as obligatory.

**Second, the *****rodef***** (pursuer) principle creates a duty — not merely a right — to use lethal force against those pursuing the destruction of others.** [Sanhedrin 73a](https://www.sefaria.org/Sanhedrin.73a) establishes that bystanders must save a potential murder victim “even at the cost of the pursuer’s life.” Maimonides [codifies in ](https://www.sefaria.org/Mishneh_Torah%2C_Murderer_and_the_Preservation_of_Life.1.6)*[Hilchot Rotze’ach](https://www.sefaria.org/Mishneh_Torah%2C_Murderer_and_the_Preservation_of_Life.1.6)*[ 1:6](https://www.sefaria.org/Mishneh_Torah%2C_Murderer_and_the_Preservation_of_Life.1.6): “Every Jewish person is commanded to attempt to save the person being pursued, even if it is necessary to kill the pursuer.” A nuclear-armed regime that has openly declared its intent to destroy other nations fits the *rodef* category with precision.

**Third, the prohibition of *****lo ta’amod al dam re’ekha***** — “do not stand idly by the blood of your neighbor” ([Leviticus 19:16](https://www.sefaria.org/Leviticus.19.16)) — imposes direct liability on those who withhold life-saving capabilities.** [Maimonides specifies](https://www.sefaria.org/Mishneh_Torah%2C_Murderer_and_the_Preservation_of_Life.1.14) that “whoever is able to save another and does not endeavor to do so violates this prohibition.”

The combined force of these three pillars creates an affirmative obligation. An entity that possesses defensive capabilities and categorically refuses to deploy them may violate Torah law. This is not a metaphor. It is halakhic reasoning applied to the most consequential question of our time.

## The tool-maker who arms without safeguards also violates Torah law

But here is what the Pentagon’s supporters don’t want to hear: the same legal tradition that commands defense also constrains it — and the constraints are not optional.

**The Talmud explicitly prohibits selling weapons without safeguards.** *[Avodah Zarah](https://www.sefaria.org/Avodah_Zarah.15b)*[ 15b–16a](https://www.sefaria.org/Avodah_Zarah.15b) establishes a categorical prohibition on weapons sales to those suspected of potential misuse. [Maimonides codifies this](https://www.sefaria.org/Mishneh_Torah%2C_Murderer_and_the_Preservation_of_Life.12.12): one may not sell weapons or weapon accessories to those who may use them to harm others. Critically, the Talmud’s discussion extends beyond obvious bad actors. Even when the buyer has no history of violence, if there exists a heightened propensity for misuse, the sale is prohibited. Rashi explains that even if one is certain the buyer won’t kill, the weapon may be used to threaten — making the seller an accessory. The halakhic principle: **when screening for misuse is feasible, it is obligatory.**

**The prohibition of *****lifnei iver***** — [placing a stumbling block before the blind](https://www.sefaria.org/Leviticus.19.14) — creates a biblical-level prohibition on enabling harm.** The Talmud (*[Avodah Zarah](https://www.sefaria.org/Avodah_Zarah.6b)*[ 6b](https://www.sefaria.org/Avodah_Zarah.6b)) establishes that when you are the sole enabler of a harmful act — when the person could not have committed it without you — the prohibition is biblical. For technology providers, this distinction matters: providing an AI system that enables *novel* capabilities — surveillance at a scale that was previously impossible, or autonomous targeting that could not otherwise be done — may constitute the biblical *lifnei iver*, because the provider is creating a capacity that did not previously exist.

**The Golem tradition embeds creator responsibility into Jewish metaphysics.** [Sanhedrin 65b](https://www.sefaria.org/Sanhedrin.65b) records that Rava created a humanoid being and sent it to Rabbi Zeira, who immediately destroyed it upon discovering it could not speak. The tradition’s teaching is that the creator never surrenders moral authority over the creation. The [Golem of Prague](https://www.thenewatlantis.com/publications/the-golem-and-the-limits-of-artifice) reinforces this: when the Golem rampaged, the creator bore the obligation to deactivate it — to erase the first letter of *emet* (truth) to produce *met* (death). The Pentagon’s demand that Anthropic remove all restrictions is, in Golem terms, a demand to remove the aleph. To surrender the kill switch.

**And the *****ma’akeh***** (parapet) obligation makes safeguards a commandment, not a preference.** [Deuteronomy 22:8](https://www.sefaria.org/Deuteronomy.22.8) requires building a guardrail on a roof to prevent falls. [Maimonides generalizes](https://www.sefaria.org/Mishneh_Torah%2C_Murderer_and_the_Preservation_of_Life.11.4): any hazard with potential to endanger life must be guarded against. A powerful AI system deployed in military contexts without restraints violates the digital equivalent of a missing parapet. Safeguards are not corporate virtue-signaling. They are a biblical commandment.

## Three absolute limits that survival cannot override

Jewish law goes further. It establishes categories of acts that remain prohibited regardless of the stakes — including national survival.

**The doctrine of *****yehareg v’al ya’avor***** — one must die rather than transgress — applies to murder of innocents.** The Talmud in [Sanhedrin 74a](https://www.sefaria.org/Sanhedrin.74a) records that for three sins — murder, sexual immorality, and idolatry — one may not violate the prohibition even to save one’s own life. The reasoning for murder is devastatingly simple: “Who says your blood is redder than his?” One cannot kill an innocent person to preserve oneself. By extension, neither can a nation claim that its survival justifies the killing of innocents through tools deployed without human moral deliberation.

**The Sanhedrin’s capital punishment procedures demonstrate that halakha demands extraordinary human judgment before taking life.** The requirements are staggering: [23 judges](https://www.sefaria.org/Mishneh_Torah%2C_Sanhedrin_and_Penalties.5.2), two eyewitnesses who issued a prior warning, deliberations that must open with arguments for acquittal, overnight delay before sentencing, and — as noted above — unanimous conviction results in acquittal. The famous passage in [Makkot 1:10](https://www.sefaria.org/Mishnah_Makkot.1.10) records that Rabbi Tarfon and Rabbi Akiva said: had we sat on the Sanhedrin, no one would ever have been executed. This framework is fundamentally incompatible with autonomous lethal decision-making. As [Rabbi Shlomo Brody wrote in ](https://sapirjournal.org/technology/2023/12/jewish-ethics-in-an-age-of-autonomous-warfare/)*[SAPIR Journal](https://sapirjournal.org/technology/2023/12/jewish-ethics-in-an-age-of-autonomous-warfare/)*: “Autonomous weapons systems are not so much immoral as **amoral**. They don’t allow for the type of moral deliberation and reflection necessary to pass ethical judgment.”

**Jewish privacy law treats surveillance as real harm — not a policy preference.** The Talmud in *[Bava Batra](https://www.sefaria.org/Bava_Batra.60a)*[ 60a](https://www.sefaria.org/Bava_Batra.60a) establishes *hezek re’iyah* — “damage by seeing” — as genuine legal harm analogous to physical injury. Rabbeinu Gershom’s universally binding decree prohibits reading private correspondence. Rabbi Shneur Zalman of Liadi ruled that “it is forbidden to watch your friend without his prior knowledge while he is conducting his activities.” The tradition reserves omniscience for God alone.

When the Pentagon’s undersecretary [told CBS News](https://www.cbsnews.com/news/pentagon-anthropic-feud-ai-military-says-it-made-compromises/) that “you have to trust your military to do the right thing,” he was asking for exactly the kind of trust the Talmud warns against. [Mishnah Avot 2:3](https://www.sefaria.org/Pirkei_Avot.2.3) says: “Avoid a close relationship with the government... they draw people close only for their own needs.”

## The Netziv’s warning: sanctioned violence corrupts even the righteous

[The Netziv](https://etzion.org.il/en/philosophy/issues-jewish-thought/rabbinic-thought/netziv-dangers-legitimate-violence) — Rabbi Naftali Tzvi Yehuda Berlin (1816–1893) — identified something in his commentary that speaks directly to the question of autonomous AI weapons. On the episode of Pinchas in Numbers 25:12, where God grants a covenant of peace to the man who performed a justified killing, the Netziv observed that *aggressive acts leave a lasting impression on a human being*. Even justified violence damages the one who carries it out. The Torah’s grant of peace was not a reward — it was a remedy. A correction for the spiritual damage that even righteous violence inflicts.

On the destruction of an idolatrous city in Deuteronomy 13:18, the Netziv identified three dangers: those who carry out sanctioned violence may lose their capacity for mercy, it may create societal discord, and it damages the polity itself. The Torah’s response — “God may show you mercy” — is conditional on purity of motivation.

Applied to autonomous weapons, the Netziv’s teaching is devastating. When killing becomes frictionless — when no human being deliberates on the specific decision to take a particular life — the corrupting effect is not concentrated in a person who can receive healing. It is diffused across a system that feels nothing and learns nothing. The moral reckoning that the Netziv identified as essential to maintaining human dignity in warfare is eliminated entirely.

This is not an argument against defense. It is an argument that **the manner of defense matters** — that a nation which automates killing without human judgment does not become more secure but less human.

## The framework: five principles that operate simultaneously

What emerges from the sources is not a compromise between the Anthropic and Pentagon positions. It is a genuine legal architecture in which both obligations — to defend and to constrain — operate at the same time.

**1. Affirmative defense obligation.** *Pikuach nefesh*, *lo ta’amod*, and the *rodef* principle create genuine obligations to participate in national defense. An entity that categorically refuses to provide defensive capabilities may violate Torah law. Anthropic’s willingness to deploy on classified military networks while maintaining its two red lines more closely tracks this obligation than either blanket refusal or unconditional surrender.

**2. Creator responsibility.** The *ma’akeh* obligation, the Golem tradition, and the weapons-sale prohibition in *Avodah Zarah* 15b establish that a tool-maker retains independent moral obligations that are not transferred to the buyer. The creator must build in safeguards, screen for misuse, and be prepared to deactivate a dangerous creation. The Pentagon’s demand to remove all restrictions asks the creator to abandon the relationship with the creation — which the halakhic tradition explicitly forbids.

**3. Human moral agency in lethal decisions.** The Sanhedrin’s procedural requirements, the requirement for *da’at* (understanding) in life-and-death decisions, and the emerging scholarly consensus — [Rabbi Daniel Nevins’ responsum](https://rabbinevins.com/jewish-law/artificial-intelligence/) for the Conservative movement, [Nadav Berman Shifman’s analysis in ](https://www.academia.edu/41516786/_Jewish_Law_Techno_Ethics_and_Autonomous_Weapon_Systems_Ethical_Halakhic_Perspectives_Jewish_Law_Association_Studies_29_2020_pp_91_124)*[Jewish Law Association Studies](https://www.academia.edu/41516786/_Jewish_Law_Techno_Ethics_and_Autonomous_Weapon_Systems_Ethical_Halakhic_Perspectives_Jewish_Law_Association_Studies_29_2020_pp_91_124)*, and Brody’s SAPIR article — establish that fully autonomous killing is incompatible with Jewish law’s requirement for moral deliberation in lethal acts.

**4. Privacy as substantive right.** The framework of *hezek re’iyah* establishes that mass data collection and analysis constitutes genuine harm regardless of technical legality. When the [MIT Technology Review noted](https://www.technologyreview.com/2026/03/02/1133850/openais-compromise-with-the-pentagon-is-what-anthropic-feared/) that OpenAI’s “compromise” essentially trusts the government not to break the law — while acknowledging that surveillance programs exposed by Edward Snowden had been deemed legal by internal agencies — the halakhic response is clear: legal compliance is necessary but insufficient.

**5. Absolute limits that survival cannot override.** *Yehareg v’al ya’avor* establishes that murder of innocents remains prohibited even to preserve one’s own life. The Netziv’s warning about the corruption inherent in sanctioned violence reinforces that neither individuals nor nations may claim existential threat as grounds for dissolving all moral constraints.

## What Rabbi Lichtenstein would say about “all lawful use”

[Rabbi Aharon Lichtenstein’s landmark essay](https://merrimackvalleyhavurah.wordpress.com/2019/07/30/does-jewish-tradition-recognize-an-ethic-independent-of-halakha/) “Does Jewish Tradition Recognize an Ethic Independent of Halakha?” (1975) argued that Jewish law recognizes moral obligations that go *beyond* strict legal requirements — through *lifnim mishurat ha-din*, beyond the letter of the law. His key insight: “if we mean that everything can be looked up, every moral dilemma resolved by reference to code or canon, the notion is both palpably naïve and patently false.”

The Pentagon’s position — that existing law sufficiently constrains military AI use and no additional restrictions are needed — is precisely the kind of legalistic minimalism Lichtenstein warned against. The Talmud itself cautions (*[Bava Metzia](https://www.sefaria.org/Bava_Metzia.30b)*[ 30b](https://www.sefaria.org/Bava_Metzia.30b)) that “Jerusalem was destroyed only because they judged according to Torah law and did not go beyond the letter of the law.”

“All lawful use” is the letter of the law. What Anthropic demanded — explicit contractual limits on surveillance and autonomous weapons — is the *spirit* beyond the letter. Jewish law holds that the spirit is not optional. It is the difference between a civilization that survives and one that deserves to.

## The real framework: not who was brave, but what is owed

The hero narrative says: remember who stood alone.

The halakhic framework says something harder: **every entity that builds powerful tools bears permanent, graduated responsibility for how those tools are used.** This obligation is not contingent on courage. It does not depend on whether Dario Amodei wakes up feeling bold on a Tuesday morning. It is structural — encoded in the relationship between maker and creation, between the one who builds the parapet and the roof it protects.

The Pentagon’s demand to remove the parapet violates the builder’s obligation. A blanket refusal to build the house at all — to withdraw from defense entirely — violates the obligation to the people who would live in it. The halakhic path is narrow but clearly marked: **participate in defense. Impose safeguards. Maintain human agency in lethal decisions. Refuse to enable surveillance that treats human beings as objects of scrutiny rather than bearers of divine dignity.**

This framework does not depend on any single company’s courage. It creates obligations that apply to everyone building AI, regardless of whether they feel brave that day. That is the difference between a values system and a hero story. Hero stories depend on someone being willing to sacrifice. Values systems make the sacrifice unnecessary by encoding the obligation in advance.

The tradition that spent millennia debating whether a speechless artificial being can join a prayer quorum has been preparing — in ways its authors could not have imagined — for exactly this moment.
