# The AI Alignment Problem Has a 2,000-Year-Old Precedent

[- - 

](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!Gz9f!,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2F9623cfc6-05c2-427d-97c0-cbf478bd746e_1200x675.jpeg)In 2016, ProPublica revealed that the COMPAS algorithm used to sentence criminal defendants across the United States was racially biased: Black defendants were almost twice as likely to be falsely labeled high-risk as white defendants.¹ Northpointe, the company behind COMPAS, fired back with its own analysis showing the algorithm predicted recidivism at similar accuracy rates for both groups.² The controversy consumed AI ethics for months.

Then mathematicians ended the debate — not by picking a side, but by proving that both sides were right and that the conflict was unsolvable. Alexandra Chouldechova at Carnegie Mellon and a team led by Jon Kleinberg at Cornell independently demonstrated that when base rates of an outcome differ across groups, no imperfect algorithm can simultaneously achieve equal predictive accuracy and equal error rates.³ This is not a software limitation. It is a theorem. Competing definitions of fairness are mathematically irreconcilable.

This should have been a turning point. It was not. The AI industry continued to promise “fair” and “aligned” systems, and governments continued to write ethics guidelines as though the right set of principles would resolve what mathematics had already proven impossible under existing frameworks. The reason is not ignorance. It is that the philosophical foundations on which Western AI governance rests — individual rights, informed consent, procedural fairness, value neutrality — were never designed to answer the question AI now forces: what are human beings for?

The evidence of structural failure is now extensive. Google assembled an external AI ethics board in April 2019; it dissolved in nine days.⁴ Including one conservative voice triggered thousands of employee signatures demanding removal. Excluding that voice made the board unrepresentative. There was no neutral composition possible — because liberal pluralism cannot assemble a representative ethics body when including one value system makes it unacceptable to holders of another.

OpenAI’s November 2023 board crisis exposed the same fault line at existential scale. The board fired Sam Altman because members believed they had a duty to slow AGI development. Within 72 hours, 95 percent of employees threatened to resign.⁵ The board was replaced by a former Salesforce CEO and a former Treasury Secretary. The market, not ethics, adjudicated the dispute. By May 2024, the co-leader of OpenAI’s Superalignment team resigned, writing that “safety culture and processes have taken a backseat to shiny products.”⁶ His team was disbanded.

Meanwhile, a 2025 analysis of the datasets used to train AI alignment — the human feedback that teaches models to behave — found that annotators disagree with expert consensus roughly 30 percent of the time.⁷ A separate study in *PNAS Nexus* testing five GPT models against World Values Survey data from 112 countries found all models exhibit cultural values resembling English-speaking Protestant European countries. Prompting the models to adopt other cultural perspectives failed to correct alignment for up to 29 percent of countries.⁸ The alignment process does not encode universal human values. It encodes a narrow cultural subset and calls it neutral.

The consequences are not theoretical. Cigna’s PxDx algorithm enabled a single medical director to reject 60,000 health insurance claims in one month — 1.2 seconds per claim.⁹ When patients appeal AI-driven denials, federal judges reverse roughly 90 percent, but only 0.2 percent of policyholders ever appeal.¹⁰ Australia’s Robodebt system issued 794,000 false and unlawful automated debt notices; at least 2,030 people died after receiving them. A Royal Commission found the scheme was “neither fair nor legal.”¹¹ In hiring, a University of Washington audit found AI resume screening preferred white-associated names in 85 percent of tests.¹² HireVue’s AI video interviewing tool scored a deaf employee low and recommended she “practice active listening.”¹³

These are not malfunctions. They are systems operating exactly as their philosophical architecture permits. When there is no shared definition of fairness, algorithms default to whatever metric is easiest to optimize. When consent means clicking a terms-of-service box, surveillance capitalism is technically authorized. When human dignity is indexed to economic productivity, automation becomes an identity crisis — not merely a labor market disruption. Sam Altman acknowledged as much at a BlackRock summit in March 2026: “If there was an easy consensus answer, we’d have done it by now, so I don’t think anyone knows what to do.”¹⁴

The core problem is architectural. Western liberal democracy excels at procedural protections — rights, due process, transparency — but has deliberately evacuated substantive commitments about human purpose and the good life. This was intentional: Rawlsian neutrality between “comprehensive doctrines” was designed for a pluralistic society. But AI fills the vacuum that neutrality creates. The EU AI Act, the world’s most comprehensive AI regulation, contains zero provisions for managing economic displacement.¹⁵ The Asilomar Principles, signed by nearly two thousand AI researchers, have no enforcement mechanism.¹⁶ Luke Munn’s influential analysis identified the pattern across the field: AI ethics principles are “meaningless” (defined to suit existing business), “isolated” (situated in industries that ignore ethics), and “toothless” (lacking consequences).¹⁷ A member of the EU’s own AI expert group publicly called its Trustworthy AI Guidelines “ethicswashing.”¹⁸

The secular philosophical frameworks fare no better in isolation. Utilitarianism — the natural fit for optimization-driven AI — cannot handle incommensurable values and collapses before genuine moral dilemmas. Deontological ethics provides rules but no hierarchy for when rules conflict. Virtue ethics focuses on cultivating character in designers, but algorithms, not characters, make the decisions. Rights-based approaches face irreconcilable conflicts between free speech and protection from harm, individual privacy and collective safety.

This is where an unexpected source becomes relevant. One legal and ethical tradition has been wrestling with precisely these structural problems — the moral status of artificial beings, creator liability for autonomous agents, hierarchical resolution of conflicting values, and the limits of human authority over entities that act independently — for roughly two millennia.

The Talmud, in Sanhedrin 65b, records that a third-century rabbi created a humanoid — a golem — using methods described in Judaism’s earliest esoteric text. The creation could not speak, which was the criterion by which another rabbi identified it as non-human and ordered its destruction.¹⁹ This is not mythology invoked for color. It is the origin point of a sustained legal and philosophical tradition that generated centuries of jurisprudence on questions the AI field now treats as novel: What is the moral status of an entity that appears intelligent but may not be? Who bears responsibility when an autonomous creation causes harm? What constitutes a legitimate “kill switch”? When does the creator’s liability end?²⁰

More practically, Jewish law (Halacha) offers structural features that secular AI ethics systematically lacks. First, a hierarchical value architecture: the principle of pikuach nefesh holds that preserving life overrides virtually all other obligations, except three — murder, idolatry, and sexual immorality.²¹ These are not arbitrary exceptions. They are bright lines: an AI system cannot take an innocent life to save other lives, which resolves the trolley problem that utilitarian frameworks notoriously cannot.²² It cannot facilitate sexual exploitation, even in order to save lives. And it cannot be treated as an authority to be deferred to unconditionally — a prohibition with growing relevance as industry leaders describe AI in increasingly reverential terms and organizations cede moral judgment to algorithmic outputs. Outside these three boundaries, life preservation takes absolute priority, with centuries of case law defining the edges. When AI systems face value conflicts, this architecture provides what utilitarian calculus and deontological rules cannot: a tested method for deciding which principle wins.

[](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!KfcH!,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2Fb19b6524-4563-4f43-9a88-874c61e80d09_300x168.jpeg)Second, graduated liability. The Talmudic shomer (guardian) framework from Exodus 22:6–14 and tractate Bava Metzia 93a–94b assigns four tiers of responsibility based on how much the guardian benefits: an unpaid guardian is liable only for negligence; a paid guardian for foreseeable failures; a borrower for all damage, even unforeseeable.²³ Applied to AI, this maps cleanly: open-source developers who profit nothing bear minimal liability; commercial providers bear liability for foreseeable harms; organizations deploying free tools for profit bear the highest duty of care. Israel’s modern Bailees Law already incorporates these Talmudic categories.²⁴

Third, built-in institutional skepticism. The Sanhedrin’s rule that a unanimous guilty verdict results in acquittal — because unanimity signals systemic failure rather than certainty — embodies a principle directly applicable to AI overconfidence.²⁵ When a system produces a result with 100 percent confidence, the tradition says that should trigger greater scrutiny, not less. Recent mathematical work has confirmed the insight: even with low individual error rates, the probability of correctness can fall as decision-makers approach unanimity due to systemic contamination.²⁶

None of this requires adopting a religious worldview. It requires recognizing that a tradition which has spent two thousand years codifying, debating, and stress-testing moral reasoning through radical social change — while maintaining coherence — has developed structural tools that no secular framework has replicated. The alignment problem is not waiting for better principles. It is waiting for a value architecture that can hold competing principles in productive tension and tell practitioners which one wins when they collide.

The growing academic literature at this intersection — spanning Oxford law journals, Springer AI ethics publications, and a 2025 Cambridge University Press companion — suggests the synthesis is already beginning.²⁷ The question is whether the AI governance community will engage with it before the next 60,000 claims are denied in a month, or the next 794,000 unlawful debt notices are issued, or the next ethics board collapses under the weight of a pluralism it was designed to celebrate but cannot operationalize.

The expert consensus is not that Western values are wrong. It is that they are radically incomplete for governing AI. The missing piece is not more guidelines. It is a substantive account of what humans are for — the question liberalism was designed to never answer, and the question AI will not let us avoid.
