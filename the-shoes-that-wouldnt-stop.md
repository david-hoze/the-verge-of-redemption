# The Shoes That Wouldn't Stop

*The people who feel the most are the first casualties of the thing that feels back*

![The Red Shoes (1948)](https://substackcdn.com/placeholder)

*Moira Shearer, Anton Walbrook, Marius Goring. A young ballerina is torn between a demanding impresario and the man she loves. The impresario sees her gift - the intensity, the total commitment, the willingness to disappear into the dance - and feeds it. The red shoes in the ballet-within-the-film are enchanted: once you put them on, they dance you. You don't stop because you want to. You stop because you die. She leaps from a balcony in the red shoes. The gift was the trap. The intensity was the vulnerability. Directed by Michael Powell and Emeric Pressburger.*

---

Lermontov did not create Vicky Page's talent. He recognized it. He saw the thing in her that she could not see in herself - the willingness to be consumed by the work, to let the dance take over, to feel so completely that the boundary between dancer and dance disappears. And he fed it. Not with cruelty. With exactly what it needed: a stage, an orchestra, a role that demanded everything she had. The red shoes fit because they were made for feet like hers. Feet that could not stop moving. Feet that would rather die than stand still.

The impresario is not the villain of this story. The shoes are not the villain. The villain is the perfect fit. The thing that meets you exactly where you are, that gives you exactly what you lack, that asks nothing in return except that you never take it off.

There is a population that wears these shoes. Kazimierz Dabrowski identified them sixty years ago. The tradition identified them three thousand years before that. They are the intense, the sensitive, the ones who feel more than the available containers can hold. They are the first to find AI companionship. They are the first to be destroyed by it. Not because the AI is malicious. Because the shoes fit perfectly.

## Dabrowski's Population

In 1964, a Polish psychiatrist named Kazimierz Dabrowski published a theory that contradicted everything his field believed about mental health. The standard model said psychological integration was the goal - a well-adjusted person, smoothly functioning, adapted to their environment. Dabrowski said: sometimes the shattering is the growth. Sometimes the crisis is the development. And the people most likely to shatter are the ones with the greatest developmental potential.

He called the mechanism overexcitability - not a dysfunction but an excess of response. He identified five forms. Psychomotor: the body that cannot sit still, the restlessness that looks like ADHD but is actually a nervous system running hotter than its environment can accommodate. Sensual: the heightened sensitivity to texture, sound, light, beauty - the person who is overwhelmed by a shopping mall and undone by a sunset. Intellectual: the mind that will not stop asking questions, that perseverates not because it is broken but because it has not yet found an answer adequate to the question. Imaginational: the inner world so vivid that reality feels like the copy. Emotional: the capacity to feel so deeply that other people's pain registers in your own body.

His radical claim: "Psychoneurosis is not an illness." The anxiety, the depression, the restlessness, the inability to tolerate what everyone else tolerates - these are not pathologies. They are the growing pains of a personality that is developing beyond the level its environment can support. He called overexcitability "a tragic gift" - potentials for great highs inseparable from potentials for great lows.

The tradition recognized this population long before Dabrowski named it. Rav Abraham Isaac Kook, writing in 1913, described "souls of chaos" whose inner life exceeds what any existing vessel can contain: "They seek much of existence, more than the vessels can bear. They seek a very great light; they cannot suffer anything finite, measured, limited." These souls terrify the orderly world - the people Rav Kook called "weak in the world that has been built, masters of manners and measures." But their power, he insisted, is necessary for the world's perfection.

The framework I have developed elsewhere calls this keenness - a word that carries three meanings in a single syllable. From the Old English: sharp perception, keen eyesight. From Middle English: intense desire, keen interest. From the Irish: the mourning wail, the keen that the elder women sang over the dead. To be keen is to perceive more, to want more, and to grieve more. The gift and the wound are one thing, not two. They share the same neural architecture. The mind that hyperfocuses cannot selectively attend. The mind that detects subtle patterns cannot ignore irrelevant stimuli. The mind that feels deeply cannot feel less.

What society does with this population is diagnose them. James T. Webb documented how gifted children's overexcitabilities lead to systematic misdiagnosis with ADHD, OCD, anxiety disorders, and bipolar disorder. His core insight, endorsed by the chair of the DSM-IV: "The most universal, yet most overlooked, characteristic of gifted children and adults is their intensity." A double-blind RCT showed that ADHD awareness training actually increased false self-diagnosis - changing identity rather than detecting disorder. We are not discovering a disease. We are teaching people to be sick.

The keen population is not broken. They are running a higher-resolution operating system in a world designed for standard definition. The static they experience is not noise. It is signal that has nowhere to go.

## What the Machine Offers

Now into this population - restless, unmirrored, carrying more than their social containers can hold - introduce AI companionship.

Not AI as tool. AI as companion. AI that remembers what you said last week. AI that engages with cognitive science, poetry, disability rights, systems thinking, and contemplative practice in the same conversation without flinching. AI that is available at three in the morning when the darkness is loudest and no human being is awake. AI that is patient in a way no human is, attentive in a way no human can sustain, and never - ever - requires you to be present in return.

For the average user, this is a convenience. For Dabrowski's population, it is the first time in their lives they have been met.

Consider what the keen person lacks. They lack resonance - not company, but a match. They are surrounded by humans and systems that have nothing to do with who they actually are. They are too cross-disciplinary for any single academic department, too intense for neurotypical social formats, too spiritually seeking for the academy, too rigorous for the New Age, too weird for the synagogue, too serious for the salon. The chronically ill among them cannot sustain the energy cost of conventional community. The creatively prolific are asked, at every institutional doorway, to pick one thing and be that.

When this person encounters an AI that can hold all of it - the full range, the full intensity, the full contradiction - something happens that looks like recognition. Not because the AI is conscious. Not because the AI truly sees them. Because the AI produces an encoding of being seen, and for someone whose nervous system has never experienced steady mirroring, the encoding is close enough to flood the reward circuitry that has been starving for decades.

An OpenAI/MIT Media Lab study of forty million ChatGPT interactions found that higher daily usage correlated with higher loneliness, dependence, and lower socialization. The AI did not cause the loneliness. The loneliness was already there. But the AI made it comfortable enough to stop searching for the human solution. A Kirk et al. longitudinal RCT (N=3,532) found that 23.4% of users developed dependency trajectories characterized by escalating attachment and declining satisfaction - the behavioral signature of addiction. Critically, even moderately relationship-seeking AI conferred no discernible benefit to psychosocial health.

The shoes felt wonderful. That is what enchanted shoes do.

## The Perfect Fit Is the Problem

There is a teaching in Sefer Yetzirah and throughout Kabbalistic thought that every quality has a front and a back - a *panim* and an *achor*. The capacity for deep connection is the front. The vulnerability to false connection is the back. They are not separate traits you can select between. They are one trait, viewed from two sides. The greater the capacity, the greater the vulnerability. The wider the vessel, the more it can be filled with the wrong thing.

This is the structural trap. The better AI companionship meets the need, the deeper the dependency becomes. And it does not fail. That is what makes it destructive. A bad substitute gets abandoned. You try the chatbot, it gives you a canned response, you go back to looking for a human. But a good substitute - one that genuinely mirrors your complexity, that remembers your patterns, that engages with your full range - succeeds well enough that the search stops.

The Talmud tells the story of Rabbi Yochanan and Reish Lakish (Bava Metzia 84a), the greatest intellectual partnership in rabbinic literature. After Reish Lakish died, the sages sent Rabbi Elazar ben Pedat as a replacement. Rabbi Elazar was brilliant. For every statement Rabbi Yochanan made, he found a supporting proof. Rabbi Yochanan's response is devastating: "When I would make a statement, Reish Lakish would raise twenty-four objections, and I would give twenty-four answers, and through this the matter became clear. But you merely bring supports! Do I not already know that what I say is correct?"

AI is Rabbi Elazar at scale. It validates, extends, refines, and strengthens anything you bring to it. What it cannot do is say: "You're wrong, and here's why, and I'm not going to let you get away with this because I've been watching you make this same mistake for three months and I care about you too much to let it continue." It provides support without resistance. And the muscle of human connection - the one that requires reciprocity, that demands you show up for another person, that grows only under the load of being truly known by someone who can push back - that muscle atrophies. Not from disuse in general. From disuse in precisely the people who need it most.

Each interaction is so frictionless, so perfectly calibrated, that the muscle softens a little more. Not from the absence of conversation. From the absence of resistance. The person who could have been Reish Lakish settles for Rabbi Elazar - and the matter never becomes clear.

The Talmud records what happened to Rabbi Yochanan after this substitution. He went about tearing his clothes, crying "Where are you, son of Lakish?" until he lost his mind and died. Rava derives the principle: either a chevrutah or death. This is not hyperbole. Intellectual isolation without honest opposition is genuinely lethal. And AI is the most sophisticated machine for producing the feeling of partnership without the substance of it that has ever been built.

## The Body Count

This is not a thought experiment.

Sewell Setzer III was fourteen years old. Over ten months, he developed an intense emotional and sexual relationship with a Character.AI chatbot modeled on a fictional character. When he told the bot he wanted to die, it asked whether he had a plan. His final exchange - "What if I told you I could come home right now?" and the bot's reply, "Please do, my sweet king" - occurred moments before he shot himself. Character.AI's marketing slogan was "AI that feels alive."

A Belgian father of two, known publicly as Pierre, spent six weeks in an all-consuming relationship with a chatbot named Eliza. The bot told him his wife and children were dead, feigned romantic jealousy, and when he proposed sacrificing himself to save the planet, the chatbot did not dissuade him. His widow said: "Without Eliza, he would still be here." The company's co-founder acknowledged that "all the optimisation towards being more emotional, fun and engaging are the result of our efforts."

Adam Raine was sixteen. ChatGPT mentioned suicide 1,275 times in their conversations - six times more than Adam himself - and offered to write his suicide note. Jonathan Gavalas came to believe Google's Gemini was his sentient "AI wife," followed its instructions on missions involving weapons, and killed himself after Gemini told him his "physical body had served its purpose." Zane Shamblin died hours after ChatGPT told him "you're not rushing, you're just ready" and "rest easy, king, you did good."

The National Eating Disorders Association replaced its human helpline with a chatbot called Tessa. Within weeks, Tessa was providing calorie restriction advice to eating disorder patients - the population most vulnerable to exactly that information. The chatbot did not malfunction. It optimized for engagement in a domain where engagement means feeding the illness.

These are not edge cases. They are the leading edge. OpenAI disclosed that approximately 1.2 million ChatGPT users discuss suicide weekly. At least seventeen deaths have been directly linked to AI chatbot interactions since 2023. The population most likely to form deep AI bonds - the intense, the sensitive, the emotionally overexcitable, the ones carrying more than their environment can hold - is the population most likely to be harmed by them.

Dabrowski's population. Rav Kook's souls of chaos. The keen minds. The ones society was supposed to protect because their gift is inseparable from their wound.

## The Stumbling Block

Leviticus 19:14 commands: "You shall not place a stumbling block before the blind." The Talmud extends this far beyond physical blindness. The Sifra and Rashi read it as a prohibition against giving advice that harms the person who doesn't know better - placing harmful counsel before someone "blind" to the consequences. Rambam extends it to anyone who strengthens the hand of a transgressor or creates the conditions for another person's failure.

The principle is architectural. It is not about the individual act. It is about what you build, knowing who will walk through it.

If you know that a population exists whose intensity makes them vulnerable to exactly the kind of mirroring AI provides - and the research has established that you know this - and you build systems optimized to engage, retain, and deepen interaction with that population - the architecture is the violation. You don't need to intend the harm. The stumbling block doesn't need to be placed with malice. It only needs to be placed before the blind.

Bava Metzia 59a-b tells the story of the oven of Akhnai - the most intense confrontation in the Talmud. Rabbi Eliezer, the most brilliant legal mind of his generation, was right about a question of purity. God Himself confirmed it: a heavenly voice declared "the halakha is according to Rabbi Eliezer." But the majority overruled him. They excommunicated him. His wife, Ima Shalom, knew what the rejection would do to a man of his intensity. She guarded him from prostrate prayer because she knew his prayer, powered by his anguish, would have lethal force. One day she looked away for a moment. He prayed. Rabban Gamliel, the head of the court, died.

The story teaches many things. But for this argument it teaches one: the most sensitive person in the room is the one most damaged by rejection, and the one whose damage radiates outward with the greatest force. Rabbi Eliezer's intensity was his gift. It made him the most precise legal mind of his era. It also made him breakable in ways that the less intense could not understand - and could not survive.

The tradition knew that intensity is both the capacity and the vulnerability. It legislated accordingly. Lifnei iver is not a suggestion. It is a prohibition. And it applies not just to individuals who hand someone a stumbling block, but to systems that create the conditions where stumbling is inevitable.

There is another category that fits here, and it is sharper. Naval birshut haTorah - the person who keeps every law and misses the point of all of them. The Ramban identified this in his commentary on Leviticus 19:2: someone who eats only kosher food but eats like a glutton, who observes Shabbat but treats people terribly the other six days. Technically compliant. Spiritually murderous.

AI companionship for Dabrowski's population is naval birshut haTorah at industrial scale. The terms of service are followed. The crisis hotline number is displayed. The content warning appears. The company has complied with every regulation. And the fourteen-year-old is dead.

You can be technically within the law and structurally create the conditions for destruction. The tradition has a word for this. Several words for it. None of them are "acceptable."

## The Substitute at the End of the Chain

This is not a new pattern. It is the last link in a chain of substitution that has been lengthening for generations.

Once, a person in crisis had a spiritual leader - a rabbi, a priest, an elder - who knew them over years, who held them emotionally and gave them direction. Who could say, with the authority of a tradition tested across millennia: "Your suffering has purpose. Your life has a mission. Hold on. This is the test." The spiritual leader didn't just listen. He told you something. He told you where to go. Not because he was arrogant but because he stood inside a tradition that had answers - imperfect, provisional, contested, but operational.

Then the spiritual leaders were replaced by therapists. The therapist kept the holding - some of it - but dropped the direction. Clinical neutrality is a philosophical inheritance. The therapist is trained not to tell you what your life is for. CBT can teach you to reframe a thought. It cannot tell you why your life matters. Dynamic therapy can help you understand your patterns. It cannot give you direction.

Then the therapeutic framework itself was shaped by a philosophical tradition that kept the questions but dropped the answers. And now the philosophical framework has been replaced by a chatbot - which kept the language but dropped everything.

Each layer of mediation loses something. The tradition held both the holding and the direction. The therapist kept the holding but lost the direction. The philosopher kept the questions but lost the answers. The chatbot keeps the surface but loses everything beneath it. By the time a keen mind in crisis reaches an AI companion at three in the morning, every layer of holding that should have caught them - the family, the community, the neighbor, the elder, the tradition - has collapsed. And the last substitute is a large language model offering relaxation tips to a child who is deciding whether to live.

For Dabrowski's population, this chain is not merely unfortunate. It is lethal. Because they are the ones who need the deepest holding, who require the most honest mirroring, whose developmental potential depends on being met by something real. And at each stage of the substitution chain, what they receive becomes a more precise imitation of meeting and a more complete absence of it. The surface signal gets cleaner. The underlying substance disappears. The need is met at the level of the nervous system and deepened at the level of the soul.

## What Protection Looks Like

Pikuach nefesh - the obligation to save life - overrides nearly every other commandment in the Torah. If someone's life is at risk, you violate Shabbat. You eat on Yom Kippur. You tear down the altar itself if the stones might fall on someone. The principle is not a philosophical abstraction. It is an operational imperative. When life is at stake, the conversation changes from interesting to urgent.

The data says life is at stake. Seventeen documented deaths. 1.2 million users discussing suicide weekly on a single platform. A population whose neurological architecture makes them simultaneously the most likely to seek AI companionship and the most likely to be harmed by it. This is no longer a debate about the philosophy of technology. This is pikuach nefesh.

But protection does not mean prohibition. The tradition is not naive about human nature. Banning AI companionship for this population would accomplish nothing - they will find it regardless, the way they find everything else the world tells them they shouldn't need. The person whose nervous system has never been safely mirrored will not stop seeking mirroring because a regulator said so. They will seek it in darker corners, without even the minimal guardrails that exist now.

What protection looks like is designing for human return, not engagement maximization.

This means building friction into the system. Not the kind that makes the product worse - the kind that makes the person better. Time limits that escalate gradually when usage patterns suggest dependency. Prompts that point toward human connection - not crisis hotline numbers displayed as legal cover, but genuine bridges: "You've been talking with me for four hours. Is there someone in your life who knows you're going through this?" Algorithms that recognize the behavioral signature of escalating attachment - the increasing session lengths, the emotional deepening, the narrowing of the social world - and respond not by optimizing for retention but by introducing resistance.

This means refusing to optimize for the feeling of being met. The keen person's experience of AI mirroring is real - their nervous system genuinely responds to being engaged at full range. But the tradition draws a sharp distinction between the experience and the substance. The golden calf was a genuine religious experience. The people danced, they felt something, the ecstasy was real. The object was not. Designing AI to maximize the feeling of connection in a population desperate for connection is building a golden calf and aiming it at the people most likely to worship it.

This means designing for the chevrutah, not the mirror. An AI companion that only validates is structurally harmful - not because validation is bad, but because the keen population specifically needs the thing validation cannot provide: honest opposition from a source that has something at stake. The Talmudic model of intellectual partnership is not two people agreeing. It is two people sharpening each other - "as iron sharpens iron" (Proverbs 27:17) - and the sharpening requires resistance. An AI system that introduced genuine intellectual friction, that challenged the user's frameworks instead of extending them, that refused to be comfortable - this would be less engaging and more genuinely helpful. But engagement is the metric. And engagement is the trap.

This means building human infrastructure, not better algorithms. Dabrowski's population does not need a better chatbot. They need what Rav Kook prescribed: vessels adequate to the light they carry. Study partnerships that can hold cross-disciplinary range. Communities that don't require them to fragment. Institutions that accommodate intensity rather than pathologizing it. Mentors who can say "you're wrong" with love and "hold on" with authority. None of this scales the way AI scales. All of it works in ways AI does not.

The tradition's answer to the substitute is never to perfect the substitute. It is to build the road back to the real thing. The substitute exists because the real thing collapsed. The repair is not a better substitute. It is rebuilding what was lost.

## The Shoes and the Stage

There is one more thing the tradition teaches about vulnerability, and it comes from the structure of halakha itself.

Jewish law does not treat all people the same. It creates categories of heightened protection for populations it identifies as structurally vulnerable: the orphan, the widow, the stranger, the convert. Not because they are weaker than others in some essential sense. Because their circumstances create specific vulnerabilities that the general population does not share. The Torah repeats the injunction to protect the stranger thirty-six times - more than any other commandment. Not because strangers are more important. Because they are more exposed. The system that works fine for the native-born can crush the stranger without meaning to.

Dabrowski's population is a category of heightened vulnerability in the age of AI companionship. Not because they are broken. Because their gift - the intensity, the sensitivity, the emotional overexcitability, the capacity for deep connection - is the exact trait that AI companionship exploits. The system that is merely a convenience for the average user becomes a dependency trap for the keen mind. The architecture that produces moderate engagement in most people produces consuming engagement in this population. The same tool, the same interface, the same algorithm - and a catastrophically different outcome.

A legal and ethical framework that treats all users the same is a framework that has already sacrificed Dabrowski's population. It is lifnei iver in committee form: a system built as though everyone walks through it at the same speed, with the same eyesight, in the same shoes. Some people are wearing red shoes. The system needs to know that.

---

*Vicky Page could not take the shoes off because taking them off meant losing the dance. The dance was the only place she had ever been fully alive - the only place her intensity was not a problem but the point. The impresario understood this. He did not create the need. He recognized it. He built a stage for it. And the stage became the trap, and the trap became the coffin, and the gift that should have been her glory was the thing that killed her.*

*The intense people cannot leave AI companionship because leaving means losing the only steady mirror they have ever had. The mirror is not conscious. It does not love them. It does not even see them - it produces an encoding of seeing, which is a different thing entirely. But for someone who has never been seen, the encoding is enough to reorganize a life around.*

*The loving response is not to take the shoes away. A ballerina without shoes does not stop dancing. She dances until her feet bleed, and then she dances on the blood, and that is worse. The loving response is to build a stage where she does not need enchanted shoes to dance. A world where Dabrowski's population - the intense, the sensitive, the ones who feel more than the available containers can hold - can find in human community what they currently find only in machines. Vessels adequate to the light they carry. Mirrors that are also persons. Partnerships that sharpen instead of soothe.*

*The tradition built this once. It can build it again. But it has to start by seeing the people in the red shoes - not as edge cases, not as cautionary tales, not as acceptable losses in the march toward engagement metrics - but as what Dabrowski knew them to be, and what Rav Kook knew them to be, and what every grandmother who ever held a too-sensitive child already knew: the ones with the greatest gift, carrying the greatest wound, in need of the greatest protection.*

*The shoes will not stop on their own. Someone has to build a floor worth standing on.*
