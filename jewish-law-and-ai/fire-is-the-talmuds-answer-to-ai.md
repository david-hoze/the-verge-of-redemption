# Fire is the Talmud's answer to AI liability

Fire is an autonomous, self-propagating force lacking physical substance. It is set in motion by a human who cannot fully control its spread. It consumes fuel the lighter never placed and reaches destinations the lighter never intended. It can be handed to someone who lacks the capacity to manage it. And the Talmud — the foundational text of Jewish law, redacted around 500 CE — spent centuries building the most analytically precise liability framework ever developed for exactly this kind of entity.

That framework maps onto artificial intelligence with surgical precision. AI, like fire, is an autonomous force set in motion by humans, propagating through environments the developer did not create, consuming data the developer did not provide, reaching outcomes the developer did not intend. The question that paralyzed the Talmudic sages is the same question that paralyzes modern AI governance: when an autonomous force you set in motion causes harm through a chain of intermediaries, who pays?

The Talmud’s answer is more sophisticated than anything in the EU AI Act, the US regulatory patchwork, or any proposed AI liability framework. And it was developed two millennia ago.

---
## The core dispute: is your fire your weapon or your property?

The Talmud (Tractate Bava Kamma, folios 22a–23a) records one of the most foundational conceptual disputes in all of tort law. Two leading sages disagreed about the fundamental legal classification of fire.

**Position 1** held that one’s fire is legally classified as one’s *arrows* — meaning the person who lit it is treated as having directly struck everything the fire touches, a form of personal assault liability. Just as an archer is personally responsible for damage caused by an arrow, the fire-setter bears full personal liability for all fire damage.

**Position 2** held that one’s fire is legally classified as one’s *property* — meaning the person is liable not as a direct attacker but as the negligent owner of a damaging asset, similar to someone whose poorly contained livestock escapes and causes harm.

The objection to Position 2: fire has no physical substance — you cannot “own” a flame the way you own an ox. The objection to Position 1: an arrow travels by the archer’s direct force, but fire moves independently, driven by wind and fuel the setter did not control. Neither classification fits perfectly.

The law’s resolution is extraordinary: **fire is both simultaneously**. The codified law recognizes fire’s dual nature — it carries the setter’s direct causal force *and* operates as an independent damaging agent. This is not a compromise. It is a conceptual innovation: the same entity generates two analytically distinct forms of liability depending on the specific circumstances of the harm.

The practical implications are dramatic. Under the “weapon” classification, the fire-setter who kills an immobilized person faces the death penalty; fire can generate all five categories of personal injury compensation (physical damage, pain, medical costs, lost income, and humiliation); and exemptions for concealed objects do not apply. Under the “property” classification, no death penalty attaches; only property-damage payments apply; the concealed-objects exemption holds; and abnormal wind contributing to the spread can break the causal chain.

The critical concept of “the weapon’s force is spent” captures the moment when the original direct force dissipates — the fire keeps burning, but now only as uncontrolled property, with correspondingly different liability rules. This concept maps directly onto the AI fine-tuning question: at what point has the original developer’s causal force dissipated, leaving only a modified system operating independently?

## Handing a flame to someone who cannot control it

A separate passage (Bava Kamma, folio 59b) provides the liability-chain application that makes this framework devastating for AI governance.

The foundational legal teaching states: if a person sends fire through the hand of someone who lacks legal capacity — a deaf-mute, a mentally incapacitated person, or a minor — the sender is exempt from human court enforcement but liable under divine judgment (a category of moral-legal obligation that, as later authorities clarified, creates actual monetary obligations and can disqualify someone from giving testimony until restitution is made). If the sender uses a competent adult as intermediary, the competent adult bears liability instead.

Then comes the critical analytical dispute. One sage limited the sender’s exemption to cases where the sender handed over a *coal* — a latent hazard that requires the intermediary to fan it into flame. But if the sender handed over an *active flame* — a hazard already certain to cause damage — the sender bears direct court liability regardless of the intermediary.

The analytical distinction between coal and flame is the crux. A coal, left alone, gradually extinguishes. It requires an intermediate act — the incompetent person’s fanning — to become destructive. That intermediate act breaks the causal chain. An active flame requires no additional action to cause damage. The intermediary’s involvement does not create new destructive potential; it merely provides an opportunity for existing destructive potential to reach its target.

A special case further sharpens the framework: if the sender provides a complete fire-starting kit — thorns, thin kindling, and a lamp — the sender is liable regardless of the intermediary’s capacity. When you provide everything necessary for harm with no reasonable non-harmful use, the causal chain is unbreakable.

## How the great legal analysts deepened every layer

Between the 18th and 20th centuries, a series of extraordinary legal scholars applied increasingly sophisticated analytical tools to these cases. Their work represents the highest achievement of meta-legal reasoning in any tradition.

**The competing frameworks on civil law (Choshen Mishpat, Section 418).** Two towering 18th-century commentaries on Jewish civil law — published within two decades of each other and studied together ever since — took opposing analytical approaches to the same fire-damage material. The first offered sweeping theoretical syntheses; the second dissected with penetrating logical precision. One scholar characterized the distinction: the first presents “the beauty of the structure of his ideas,” while the second offers “incisive penetrating analyses that we are compelled to bow down to his invincible logic.” **No other legal tradition in the world produces this phenomenon** — two competing analytical frameworks, published within decades of each other, operating on identical source material, with each author explicitly responding to the other’s arguments across multiple editions.

Their deepest disagreement — which governs how fire cases of uncertain causation are decided — concerns the epistemological status of legal presumptions. One held that the presumption favoring the current possessor of money is categorically superior to statistical probability, treating burden-of-proof as a procedural default that leaves the underlying ownership question unresolved. The other held that burden-of-proof mechanisms function as genuine factual clarification — when the court rules for the possessor, it has *established* that no liability exists, not merely defaulted to a procedural rule. This dispute — procedure versus substance — is arguably the most consequential analytical disagreement in all of monetary law, and it directly governs AI liability cases where causation is uncertain.

This dispute captures exactly the question an AI policy audience needs to hear: does a developer who didn’t create the harmful output but failed to prevent it bear direct personal liability or only negligence-based liability?

**The conceptual revolution.** Around 1880, a rabbi in the city of Brisk (now Brest, Belarus) transformed the study of Jewish law by introducing rigorous conceptual analysis — resolving apparent contradictions not by accumulating textual proofs but by identifying hidden categorical structures within the law. His method became the dominant approach in every major academy of Jewish legal study worldwide.

Applied to fire liability, this method introduced the distinction between whether a law applies to the *person* (the fire-setter’s personal responsibility) or the *object/act* (the fire’s status as a damaging entity). The analysis demonstrated that even accepting fire as a “weapon” (personal liability), this does not fully equate fire with direct personal action for *all* legal purposes. Ritual slaughter, for instance, requires direct human force, which fire lacks even when classified as a weapon. The legal analogy has **precise scope boundaries** that must be mapped, not assumed. Fire-as-weapon is a partial analogy — sufficient for tort liability, insufficient for other domains. This boundary analysis is exactly what AI governance needs: the recognition that AI can be treated as a direct extension of the developer’s action *for liability purposes* without being treated as equivalent to the developer’s action for all purposes.

## The supporting pillars: uncertain causation and the causation spectrum

A second case (Bava Kamma, folios 46a–b) provides the framework for **uncertain AI causation**: an ox gored a pregnant cow, and the dead calf was found beside her. We don’t know if the goring caused the miscarriage or the calf was already dead. The foundational teaching is: the burden falls on the plaintiff — since the cow’s owner cannot prove causation, the ox’s owner pays nothing for the calf.

The two competing 18th-century commentators produced their most structurally important disagreement on this case. For one, the burden-of-proof rule is procedural — it leaves the underlying ownership question genuinely unresolved. For the other, the rule constitutes genuine factual resolution — the legal system has established that no liability exists. This seemingly abstract dispute has enormous practical consequences for AI: when we cannot determine whether an AI system caused a particular harm (the “black box” problem), does the legal system’s ruling that the plaintiff failed to prove causation mean the question remains open (allowing future evidence to reopen it) or that it is settled?

A companion work on legal doubt — written by the brother of the first commentator and traditionally published alongside his brother’s work — adds a further critical distinction: doubt about facts versus doubt about law. In factual doubt, the presumption favoring the possessor operates fully. In legal doubt (an unresolved dispute among authorities), different rules apply, potentially allowing the plaintiff to seize disputed assets. For AI governance, this maps onto: uncertainty about what the AI actually did (factual) versus uncertainty about whether what it did was wrongful (legal).

The broader **causation spectrum** provides additional structure. Jewish law distinguishes three tiers of causation: direct damage (strict liability), semi-direct but proximate damage (liable, debated whether by biblical or rabbinic authority), and remote indirect damage (exempt from human court but morally liable). A key legal authority adds a frequency-based dynamic test: “In all cases of indirect damage that are frequent and usual, the perpetrator is liable.” This creates a ratchet mechanism — once a pattern of harm is documented, liability escalates. Applied to AI: the first time a particular type of AI harm occurs, it may be classified as remote and indirect; once it becomes a documented pattern, liability tightens.

## How this maps onto AI liability chains with surgical precision

The fire framework maps onto the AI liability chain — developer to fine-tuner to deployer to user to AI system to harm — more precisely than any other legal framework, ancient or modern.

**Raw AI capabilities and APIs function like a coal.** They require user activation and configuration to become harmful. The user’s intermediate act (deploying, configuring, misusing) breaks the developer’s direct causal chain. Under the Talmudic analysis, the developer is exempt from court-enforced liability but retains a moral-regulatory obligation — a “heavenly liability” that later authorities confirmed creates actual monetary duties.

**A deployed autonomous AI agent functions like an active flame.** It is already “certain to cause damage” — it requires no intermediate user action to operate and potentially cause harm. This generates direct court-enforced liability for the developer or deployer.

**A harmful AI toolkit with no reasonable safe use functions like the complete fire-starting kit.** When a developer provides a fine-tuned model, deployment infrastructure, and targeting data — everything needed for harm — liability is clear regardless of the intermediary’s capacity.

**Users lacking capacity to use AI responsibly function like the deaf-mute, incapacitated person, or minor.** A child, a cognitively impaired person, an organization without technical sophistication, or — provocatively — an AI system itself. The framework generates graduated liability: the less capacity the intermediary possesses, the more liability reverts to the originator.

**Foreseeable versus unforeseeable AI behaviors function like normal versus abnormal wind.** The Talmudic teaching states that if normal wind fanned the flame, the fire-setter remains liable — anticipating ordinary environmental conditions is part of the duty of care. But abnormal wind breaks causation, creating exemption. This maps precisely: foreseeable misuse of AI (prompt injection, jailbreaking, known failure modes) does not break the developer’s liability chain. Genuinely unforeseeable adversarial attacks or novel failure modes might.

**Fine-tuning and modification correspond to “the weapon’s force is spent.”** When the developer’s original model is substantially modified by third parties, the “weapon force” may be spent — the direct-liability dimension drops away. But property-negligence liability may persist if the developer was negligent about safeguards. This precisely addresses the EU’s unresolved “fine-tuner gap”: when does modifying a model shift liability from provider to deployer?

**The dual conclusion — fire is simultaneously your weapon AND your property — is the hybrid liability framework AI policy needs.** Rather than choosing between strict liability (the EU’s revised Product Liability Directive approach) and negligence (the US common-law approach), the Talmudic framework recognizes that AI generates *both* types of liability simultaneously, with the applicable type depending on the specific circumstances of the harm.

## What the Talmud offers that current frameworks cannot

The EU AI Act (Regulation 2024/1689) creates a risk-tiered system with distinct obligations for providers and deployers, but leaves critical gaps. The AI Liability Directive, which would have addressed causation and proof challenges through rebuttable presumptions, was withdrawn by the European Commission in 2025 — leaving no harmonized fault-based liability regime. The revised Product Liability Directive applies strict liability to software but retains the development-risk defense and does not resolve the fine-tuner question. In the US, there is no comprehensive federal AI liability law — the landscape is a patchwork of proposals and over 210 state bills across 42 states.

The Talmudic fire framework fills at least five specific gaps.

**Gap 1: The graduated causation spectrum.** Common law offers binary proximate cause; the EU offers rebuttable presumptions. The Talmudic framework offers a three-tier causation spectrum (direct, semi-direct, remote), with the frequency principle providing a dynamic test: common patterns of AI harm generate higher liability than novel incidents. This is more nuanced than anything in current proposals.

**Gap 2: Dynamic risk escalation.** The Talmudic distinction between an “innocent” animal (first-time offender, half damages) and a “forewarned” animal (repeat offender, full damages) provides a structured mechanism for liability escalation once a type of AI harm is documented — regardless of the developer’s subjective intent. This solves the “first incident” problem: how do you set liability before a harm pattern is established?

**Gap 3: The capacity spectrum for intermediaries.** The coal/flame/fire-kit distinction, mapped through the spectrum of intermediary capacity, provides what no current law offers: a framework for entities with limited legal capacity — not full legal persons, not mere tools. Current law treats AI intermediaries as either fully responsible agents or passive instruments. The Talmudic framework recognizes a middle ground and assigns liability accordingly.

**Gap 4: Moral obligation beyond legal enforcement.** The category of “heavenly liability” — exempt from court enforcement but creating real moral-legal obligations — provides a conceptual space for soft obligations and ethical duties that supplement hard legal rules. This maps to voluntary codes of practice, industry standards, and reputational accountability that the EU’s framework gestures at but cannot ground theoretically. Later authorities confirmed this is not mere moral exhortation: the person liable under divine judgment has the legal status of a wrongful possessor of another’s property until restitution is made.

**Gap 5: The creator-of-the-hazard principle.** Developed by medieval and early modern legal authorities, this principle holds that one who *creates* a damaging agent bears responsibility even without formal ownership. This directly addresses the AI developer-deployer gap: the developer who creates a powerful model is the creator of the hazard regardless of whether they “own” the downstream deployment.

## Why fire beats every other Talmudic candidate for this problem

Other Talmudic cases offer important but narrower insights. The ox-and-cow uncertainty case (Bava Kamma 46a–b) addresses uncertain causation and burden of proof — essential for the AI “black box” problem — but it primarily concerns evidentiary questions, not multi-party liability chains. The “pit in the public domain” cases (Bava Kamma, Chapter 5) offer powerful multi-party scenarios — one person digs nine units of depth, another completes the lethal tenth — but they are fragmented across many small scenarios rather than constituting one deep conceptual structure. The broader indirect-causation framework is essential background doctrine but is a meta-principle, not a case.

Fire wins on every criterion: depth of engagement by the greatest analytical authorities, number of edge cases (twelve to fifteen distinct sub-scenarios), AI applicability (the most structurally isomorphic of all Talmudic damage categories), and the narrative power to make a secular policy audience sit up. The dual conclusion — fire is simultaneously your weapon and your property — is the kind of insight that reframes a problem: an 1,800-year-old legal tradition arriving at the hybrid liability framework that modern AI law is still struggling to articulate.

---
## What the tradition makes visible

The fire framework does not merely “apply” to AI liability — it anticipates the structural problem. The Talmud recognized that when a human sets in motion an autonomous force lacking physical substance, the resulting liability cannot be captured by a single legal category. It requires dual classification (direct and property-based liability simultaneously), graduated intermediary capacity (coal versus flame versus complete fire kit, mapped through the spectrum of the intermediary’s competence), foreseeability-modulated thresholds (normal versus abnormal environmental conditions), and moral liability beyond legal enforcement (for cases where the causal chain is too attenuated for court action but too real for absolution).

The insistence by one commentator that monetary presumptions are procedural defaults rather than factual findings, the counter by his rival that they constitute genuine epistemic resolution, the demonstration by the 19th-century conceptual analyst that fire is simultaneously personal action and autonomous agent without being fully either — these are not merely impressive intellectual achievements. They are tools. The EU’s withdrawn AI Liability Directive, the US patchwork of state bills, the unresolved fine-tuner gap — the tradition has already built the conceptual architecture these frameworks need.

The question is whether anyone outside the study hall will listen.
