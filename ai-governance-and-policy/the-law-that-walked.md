# The Law That Walked: How authority survives the collapse of every institution that held it

A farming village learns that bandits will return after the harvest. They have no army, no lord, no institutional protection. They hire seven ronin - masterless samurai with no institutional authority, no land, no wealth. The samurai defend the village not because they are paid well (they are paid in rice) but because the defense is worth doing. Four of the seven die. The village survives. Every AI governance proposal published in the last five years begins with an assumption so foundational that nobody bothers to state it: you need an institution. The EU needs a Commission. The UN needs an advisory panel. The United States needs a federal agency, or at the very least a framework published by one. The entire conversation assumes that governance begins when an institution claims jurisdiction, writes rules, and enforces compliance. Without the institution, there is no governance. Without the building, there is no law.

The Jewish legal tradition spent two thousand years proving that assumption wrong. Not in theory. In practice - across continents, across centuries, across every form of persecution a civilization can devise. The law kept walking after every institution that held it was destroyed. And the mechanisms that kept it walking are not historical curiosities. They are a working model for exactly the kind of distributed governance that AI needs and that every centralized proposal is failing to provide.

## The Institutional Assumption

The landscape of AI governance in 2026 is a catalogue of institutional failure.

The EU AI Act - the most ambitious regulatory effort in history - took four years to negotiate and was outdated before it was enacted. It classifies systems by risk tier but provides no mechanism for reclassifying them when capabilities change overnight. It requires transparency documentation but leaves the substantive choice of which fairness definition to apply to the companies deploying the systems. It has jurisdictional reach only within the EU, while the systems it regulates operate globally. And it moves at legislative speed in a domain where the technology shifts quarterly. As Arvind Narayanan, who co-authored the field’s definitive textbook on algorithmic fairness before abandoning the area entirely, observed: algorithmic fairness is “a bandage for a bandage” - two levels removed from what needs fixing.

The NIST AI Risk Management Framework is voluntary. No company is required to adopt it. No penalty attaches to ignoring it. It is a suggestion dressed in the language of governance - useful as a reference document, toothless as a regulatory instrument. When I cite NIST to people who build AI systems for a living, they nod politely. They are nodding at a pamphlet.

The UN’s AI advisory body released its interim report in December 2023. It recommended “an international AI governance regime.” It did not specify what this regime would do, who would run it, or how it would enforce anything. The UN cannot enforce climate agreements that took thirty years to negotiate and that the largest emitters routinely ignore. The notion that it will govern a technology that evolves faster than its member states can draft position papers is not serious. It is institutional reflex - the instinct to form a committee in response to a crisis, regardless of whether committees are the right tool.

The structural failures are not incidental. They are built into the institutional model itself.

Regulatory capture: the companies being regulated are the only entities with the technical expertise to draft the regulations. The EU AI Act’s lobbying disclosures show that the largest AI companies spent more on influencing the Act’s provisions than any civil society organization could match. The fox is not guarding the henhouse. The fox is writing the building code for the henhouse.

Jurisdictional arbitrage: any company that finds one jurisdiction’s rules too restrictive can relocate operations, route data, or restructure subsidiaries to fall under a friendlier regime. This is not a theoretical concern. It is the business model of the global technology industry. Regulation that applies only within borders is regulation that applies only to companies that choose not to leave.

Speed mismatch: GPT-3 to GPT-4 took sixteen months. GPT-4 to Claude 3.5 to Gemini Ultra happened in a blur. Legislative cycles run in years. Regulatory rulemaking runs in months at best. The technology moves in weeks. By the time a regulation is drafted, commented on, revised, and enacted, the system it was designed to regulate has been deprecated and replaced by something the regulators have not yet seen.

Sam Altman said it plainly at a March 2026 summit: “If there was an easy consensus answer, we’d have done it by now, so I don’t think anyone knows what to do.” That is not humility from a man running one of the most powerful technology companies on earth. It is an accurate description of a governance paradigm that has hit its structural limit.

The question nobody in the room was asking: what if the institution is the wrong unit of governance?

## The Chain That Kept Walking

In 70 CE, the Romans destroyed the Second Temple. The Sanhedrin - the supreme Jewish court of seventy-one judges - lost its seat. The sacrificial system, which had been the central organizing institution of Jewish religious life for a thousand years, ended overnight. The priesthood lost its function. The political sovereignty that had given Jewish law its enforcement mechanism was gone. By any institutional measure, the legal system should have died with the state.

It did not die. It walked.

The mechanism that carried it was semikhah - ordination. Not ordination in the modern sense of a seminary granting a credential, but a specific, physical act: a teacher places his hands on the head of a student and says, in effect, “I vouch for this person.” The chain ran from Moses to Joshua (Numbers 27:18-23 - “Take Joshua son of Nun, a man in whom there is spirit, and lay your hand upon him”), from Joshua to the elders, from the elders through the generations of judges and prophets and scholars. Each link was personal. Each was a human being looking at another human being and staking his reputation on the judgment: this person is fit to rule.

The formal chain broke around 358 CE, when Hillel II published the fixed calendar and the last holders of the original semikhah died without successors. The Roman persecutions had made ordination dangerous - the Talmud records (Sanhedrin 14a) that the Romans decreed death on anyone who ordained and anyone who was ordained, and destroyed any city where the ceremony took place. Rabbi Yehuda ben Bava was martyred for ordaining five students between two mountains, outside any city’s boundaries, so that the decree would not destroy a community. He was pierced with three hundred iron spears. The students survived. The chain continued.

When it could not continue formally, it continued functionally. Communities recognized authorities not because a chain of physical hand-placement connected them to Moses, but because the markers of genuine authority were visible: depth of learning, quality of judgment, consistency of character, communal trust earned over decades. The title changed - from *musmakh* to *rav* to *moreh hora’ah* (teacher of legal rulings) - but the substance held. Authority lived in persons, not in institutions. When the building burned, the people who carried the law walked out.

This is not a metaphor for AI governance. It is a structural model. The EU AI Act lives in Brussels. If the political will shifts, if the Commission is captured, if the institutional culture degrades - the governance dies with the institution. The NIST framework lives in a federal agency. Administrations change. Priorities shift. Frameworks gather dust. Every institutional governance mechanism is exactly as durable as the institution that houses it, and institutions - as the last decade has demonstrated with brutal clarity - are not durable.

Semikhah offers a different architecture: authority that travels in persons. Not in buildings, not in charters, not in jurisdictions. In people whose competence is vouched for by other people whose competence is known. The chain is resilient because it is distributed. You cannot destroy it by destroying a building. You cannot capture it by capturing a board. You would have to find and silence every qualified person in the chain - and the Romans tried that, and failed.

## The Question and the Answer

After the destruction of the Temple and the dissolution of the Sanhedrin, Jewish communities were scattered across Babylon, North Africa, Spain, France, Germany, and eventually across every continent. They had no legislature. They had no enforcement agency. They had no army, no police, no prison system. They had questions.

A merchant in eleventh-century Cairo wants to know whether a new financial instrument is permissible under Jewish commercial law. A community in fifteenth-century Poland needs to decide whether a specific technological innovation can be used on the Sabbath. A physician in eighteenth-century Prague is asked whether a particular medical procedure violates the prohibition against wounding. A family in twenty-first-century New York wants to know whether an AI-generated document can serve as a valid contract.

In each case, the process is the same. The community writes to an authority - a rabbi whose learning, judgment, and character are recognized, whose qualifications are vouched for by the chain of transmission. The authority responds. The response is called a *teshuvah* - literally, “an answer,” from the same root as *teshuvah* meaning “return” or “repentance.” The question returns to its source. The answer carries the weight of the tradition.

The responsa literature is staggering in scope. From the Geonim - the heads of the Babylonian academies from roughly the seventh to the eleventh century - through the Rishonim, the Acharonim, and into the present day, rabbis have issued hundreds of thousands of binding legal opinions on every conceivable topic. Rav Sherira Gaon (906-1006 CE) and his son Rav Hai Gaon issued responsa on commercial partnerships, inheritance disputes, and the boundaries of judicial authority. The Rosh - Rabbenu Asher ben Yechiel (1250-1327) - fled Germany for Spain and brought an entire legal tradition with him in his head, issuing responsa that synthesized Ashkenazic and Sephardic rulings. Rabbi Moshe Feinstein (1895-1986), the preeminent American posek (legal decisor), issued over a thousand responsa collected in the *Igrot Moshe*, covering everything from electricity on the Sabbath to the permissibility of artificial insemination to the status of a heart transplant under Jewish law.

The mechanism has three features that current AI governance lacks entirely.

First, it is case-based. Every responsum addresses a specific situation brought by a specific community. The ruling binds that community. Other communities may adopt it, distinguish it, or reject it with reasoned argument. This is not top-down regulation imposed uniformly on diverse contexts. It is bottom-up governance that respects local conditions while maintaining systemic coherence through shared principles and accumulated precedent.

Second, it is expertise-driven and consent-based. No one forces a community to consult a particular rabbi. No enforcement mechanism compels compliance with the answer. The authority rests on two pillars: the community asked because it recognized the rabbi’s competence, and the answer is better than anything the community could produce on its own. This is governance by demonstrated superiority of reasoning, not governance by monopoly on violence. The community can reject the ruling - but then it bears the burden of explaining why its own analysis is stronger. This almost never happens, because the whole point of asking was that the local analysis was insufficient.

Third, it builds precedent. Every responsum is recorded. Every argument is preserved. The next question on a similar topic does not start from nothing. It starts from a body of accumulated reasoning that grows richer with every case. The system gets smarter over time - not through algorithmic optimization, but through the compounding of human judgment applied to real problems.

Imagine this applied to AI governance. A hospital in Kenya deploys a diagnostic AI and encounters a case where the system’s recommendation conflicts with the physician’s clinical judgment. Instead of waiting for the EU to update a regulation that was written without Kenyan healthcare infrastructure in mind, the hospital writes to a recognized authority - someone whose expertise in both AI systems and medical ethics has been vouched for by a chain of qualified people. The authority responds with a reasoned ruling grounded in principles and precedent. The ruling is recorded. The next hospital that faces a similar conflict does not start from zero.

This is not utopia. It is the system that has been running for fourteen hundred years.

## When Practice Becomes Law

There is a third mechanism, and it is the one that most directly addresses the gap between where AI governance is and where it needs to be.

*Minhag* - communal custom. The principle is stated with striking force in the Talmud (Yerushalmi Pesachim 4:1) and codified by the Rema (Rabbi Moshe Isserles, 1530-1572) and others: *Minhag Yisrael Torah hi* - the custom of Israel is Torah. When a community consistently practices something, that practice hardens into binding law for that community.

This is bottom-up law-making. No legislature votes on it. No executive signs it. No court imposes it. The community does something. It keeps doing it. It becomes normative. The law recognizes what the community has already decided through sustained, consistent practice.

The Talmud (Bava Metzia 7:1) establishes that in commercial matters, local custom governs. If workers in a particular city customarily eat lunch at their employer’s expense, that custom is binding even without a contract. If merchants in a particular region customarily extend thirty-day credit, that custom governs transactions between them. The operative principle is that communal practice reflects communal wisdom - the accumulated knowledge of people living inside a situation, not the theoretical projections of people regulating from outside.

Maimonides codifies (Hilchot Mekhirah 26:8) that commercial custom overrides even explicit contractual terms when the custom is well-established. The Rashba (Rabbi Shlomo ben Adret, 1235-1310) rules that a custom established by the community’s consent has the force of a formal enactment. The custom is not just a default that applies in the absence of a rule. It is itself a source of law, because the community’s consistent practice is treated as a form of communal legislation.

AI governance needs this mechanism badly. Because AI communities are already developing customs - and no governance framework is treating those customs as normative.

Open-source AI communities have developed consistent practices around model documentation. They write model cards. They disclose training data. They flag known limitations. These practices emerged organically, through community consensus, not through regulation. The EU AI Act eventually required some of these disclosures - but the practice preceded the regulation by years. The community had already decided what responsible disclosure looked like. The institution caught up.

AI safety researchers have developed customs around responsible disclosure of vulnerabilities. When a research team discovers a jailbreak or a safety failure, there is an emerging norm about disclosure timelines, coordination with the model developer, and publication practices. These norms are not codified anywhere. They live in community practice.

Medical AI deployment is developing customs around human-in-the-loop requirements. Clinicians who use diagnostic AI systems are developing informal but consistent practices about when to defer to the system and when to override it, how to document disagreements between human and machine judgment, and when to escalate. These practices are emerging from the ground - from people who actually use the systems in actual clinical settings.

Under the minhag principle, these customs should be recognized as normative. Not because a legislature enacted them, but because they represent the accumulated wisdom of communities living inside the problem. A governance framework that ignores community practice in favor of top-down regulation is not just practically inadequate. It is epistemologically arrogant. It assumes that people in Brussels or Washington know more about how AI should be governed in a Nairobi hospital or a Sao Paulo courtroom than the people who work there.

The minhag model does not replace formal governance. The Talmud recognizes that custom can be overridden by explicit legislation when the stakes warrant it. But it establishes a presumption: start with what the community already does. If you want to override it, you bear the burden of explaining why your regulation is better than the accumulated practice of the people living closest to the problem.

## Pluralism with Spine

Every AI governance conversation I have observed in the last three years follows the same pattern. Smart people present their positions. Other smart people present different positions. Everyone acknowledges the complexity. Nobody decides.

The tradition has a name for this. Half of a name, actually.

*Eilu v’eilu divrei Elohim chayyim* - “these and these are the words of the living God” (Eruvin 13b). The schools of Hillel and Shammai disagree on virtually everything - how to light Hanukkah candles, when evening begins, what constitutes a valid marriage contract. The Talmud declares that both positions are the words of the living God. Both are valid. Both reflect genuine aspects of the truth.

The AI governance world loves this half. Multiple stakeholder perspectives. Diverse viewpoints. Inclusive dialogue. Eilu v’eilu at every conference, every panel, every multi-stakeholder framework. Everyone’s position is valid. Everyone’s concerns are heard. The complexity is acknowledged. The multiplicity is celebrated.

But the tradition does not stop there. The sentence continues: *v’halacha k’Beit Hillel* - “and the law follows the school of Hillel.” Both are the words of the living God, *and the law follows one of them*. The declaration of multiple validity is immediately followed by a binding decision. Not because Hillel was smarter than Shammai - the Talmud suggests Shammai’s students were actually sharper. But because Hillel’s students were humble, because they studied Shammai’s positions before presenting their own, and because a functioning legal system requires decisions, not endless deliberation.

The Talmud (Eruvin 13b) explains: the school of Hillel was favored because they were gentle and patient, and because they would study the words of Beit Shammai alongside their own - and even mention Beit Shammai’s position first. Humility and engagement with the opposing view are prerequisites for earning the authority to decide. You do not get to rule if you have not genuinely reckoned with the alternative.

The Mishnah in Eduyot 1:5 asks: why does the Talmud record the minority opinion at all, if the law does not follow it? The answer: so that a future court, finding the minority’s reasoning more compelling in changed circumstances, can rely on it. The dissent is preserved not as an academic exercise but as a live legal option for a future the current court cannot predict.

This is what AI governance is missing. Not the plurality - we have plenty of plurality. What is missing is the decision. The *v’halacha*. The moment when someone says: we have heard all positions, we have studied the dissent, we have weighed the arguments, and the law follows this position. Not because the other positions are wrong. Because a system that validates every position equally and decides nothing is not pluralism. It is paralysis.

Remove the *v’halacha* from *eilu v’eilu* and you do not get a richer discourse. You get John Stuart Mill’s marketplace of ideas with no closing bell - positions circulating endlessly, each equally weighted, none binding, while the technology deploys without governance and the harms accumulate without remedy.

The Sanhedrin provides the structural model for how this works. The great court required a majority to rule, but not unanimity - indeed, as I have written elsewhere, unanimity in a capital case resulted in acquittal, on the suspicion that the court had failed to genuinely consider the defense. The court was required to include members capable of arguing for acquittal. Structural dissent was built into the institution. You could not constitute a valid court without someone whose job was to disagree. But after the disagreement, the court ruled. And the ruling was binding.

The Rambam codifies in Hilchot Sanhedrin 10:1 that once the court has ruled by majority, the minority is bound by the decision - but the minority opinion is preserved in the record. This is not suppression of dissent. It is the opposite: dissent is required, recorded, and preserved for future use. But it does not prevent the system from functioning. The law moves forward. The dissent walks alongside it, waiting for its moment.

AI governance needs exactly this architecture. A body that hears all positions, that requires structural dissent, that preserves minority views - and that decides. Not a panel that issues recommendations. Not a framework that documents considerations. A court that rules.

## The Formation Nobody Is Talking About

There is one more mechanism the tradition provides, and the current governance conversation has not even begun to consider it.

Maimonides opens the Mishneh Torah not with laws of commerce or governance or even worship. He opens with Hilchot De’ot - the laws of character formation. Before you govern, before you judge, before you rule on the permissibility of a medical procedure or the validity of a contract or the deployment of a military system - you must be formed. You must have cultivated patience, humility, honesty, courage, and the capacity to hold two valid positions in mind without collapsing into either one.

The Talmud (Sanhedrin 17a) lists the qualifications for membership on the Sanhedrin. A judge must be wise, humble, God-fearing, indifferent to money, a lover of truth, loved by people, and possessed of a good reputation. These are not nice-to-haves appended to the technical requirements. They are the technical requirements. Brilliance without humility disqualifies you. Expertise without character disqualifies you. You do not get to govern if you have not been formed.

Contrast this with the current AI governance landscape. Who sits on AI ethics boards? People with the right credentials, the right publications, the right affiliations. Nobody asks whether they have been formed. Nobody asks whether they can hold two valid positions without collapsing. Nobody asks whether they are humble. The Google ATEAC collapsed in nine days not because the members lacked expertise but because the selection process had no concept of the character prerequisites for governance. You cannot assemble a body capable of adjudicating value conflicts if the members have never been trained to hold value conflicts without resolving them prematurely.

The Jewish tradition’s insistence on formation is not soft. It is structural. A judge who lacks the requisite character is not merely a bad judge. He is an invalid judge. His rulings do not bind. The court he sits on is not a court. Formation is a legal prerequisite, not a personal aspiration.

This maps onto AI governance with uncomfortable precision. The people making decisions about how AI systems affect billions of lives have, in many cases, no training in moral reasoning, no exposure to the disciplines of holding competing obligations simultaneously, and no formation in the specific kind of humility that governance demands - the humility to decide while knowing you might be wrong, and to record your reasoning so that those who come after you can correct you.

A Beit Din - a rabbinic court - requires only three judges. Three people whose competence, character, and judgment have been vouched for by the chain of transmission. No state backing. No enforcement mechanism beyond communal consent and the moral authority of demonstrated wisdom. This model has operated continuously for two thousand years. It still operates today - resolving commercial disputes, adjudicating religious matters, and increasingly serving as a mediation framework in contexts that have nothing to do with Jewish law per se. The structure works because the people in it were formed before they were authorized.

## What This Means for the Machines

The argument is not that AI governance should be governed by Jewish law. The argument is that Jewish law has solved - operationally, not theoretically - the exact structural problems that AI governance is currently failing to solve. And it solved them using mechanisms that do not require a state, a central institution, or an enforcement monopoly.

Semikhah: authority transmitted through personal chains of vouched competence. AI governance needs recognized authorities whose qualification rests not on institutional affiliation but on demonstrated expertise validated by other recognized experts. Not self-appointed thought leaders. Not corporate ethics officers reporting to the CEO. People whose competence has been tested by people whose competence is known.

Responsa: case-based, expertise-driven governance by consent. AI governance needs a mechanism for specific communities facing specific problems to consult recognized authorities and receive reasoned rulings grounded in principles and precedent. Not one-size-fits-all regulation. Not voluntary frameworks. Binding rulings that the community accepts because it asked and because the answer is sound.

Minhag: bottom-up norm formation through community practice. AI governance needs to recognize and codify the customs that AI communities are already developing. Model documentation practices, responsible disclosure norms, human-in-the-loop standards - these are already emerging from practice. The governance framework should start there, not in a committee room.

Eilu v’eilu with v’halacha: structured pluralism that decides. AI governance needs bodies that hear all positions, require structural dissent, preserve minority views - and rule. The alternative is the permanent conference, the eternal multi-stakeholder dialogue, the governance conversation that never becomes governance.

Hilchot De’ot: character formation as a prerequisite for authority. AI governance needs to ask not just whether decision-makers have the right credentials but whether they have the right character. Can they hold competing obligations? Can they decide while remaining humble about their decisions? Have they been formed?

These are not exotic imports from an ancient tradition. They are the load-bearing structures that every governance system needs and that the institutional model is failing to provide. The tradition figured this out because it had no choice. When every institution you depend on is destroyed - the Temple, the Sanhedrin, the state, the homeland - you either develop governance mechanisms that survive institutional collapse, or you disappear.

The tradition did not disappear. It walked for two thousand years. And the mechanisms it developed are available to anyone willing to examine them.

## The Objection That Matters

The sharpest counter is not that this is romantic or impractical. The sharpest counter is that the Jewish legal system worked because it operated within a shared normative framework - the Torah, the Mishnah, the Gemara. Everyone in the system accepted the same canon. Semikhah meant something because the chain connected to a shared source. Responsa carried weight because the principles they invoked were accepted by everyone who asked the question. Minhag hardened into law because the community shared a substantive account of what law was for.

AI governance has no such shared canon. There is no Torah of AI. There is no accepted body of first principles that everyone in the system recognizes as authoritative. Without that shared foundation, the mechanisms are empty forms. You can have chains of transmission, but transmission of what? You can have responsa, but grounded in what principles? You can have minhag, but custom of what kind of community?

This objection is serious. And the honest answer is that it is partially right. The full architecture of the halakhic system cannot be transplanted into a context that lacks its normative foundations. You cannot run the software without the operating system.

But partially right is not entirely right. Three responses.

First, the shared canon does not need to be theological. The halakhic system’s structural features - case-based reasoning, precedent accumulation, hierarchical value priorities, preservation of dissent, character prerequisites for authority - are methodological, not theological. You do not need to accept the divine origin of the Torah to adopt case-based reasoning. You do not need to accept the Sinaitic revelation to build a system that preserves minority opinions. The methodology can run on different foundations. It has, in fact, already done so: secular arbitration, common law, and international commercial law all borrow structural features from systems like this. The question is whether AI governance will borrow them deliberately and systematically or continue to reinvent them poorly.

Second, shared prohibitions are easier to establish than shared aspirations. The AI governance conversation cannot agree on what human flourishing looks like. It can, with somewhat less difficulty, agree on what it will not tolerate. *Lo ta’amod al dam re’echa* - do not stand idly by the blood of your neighbor - does not require theological agreement. It requires agreement that when someone is drowning, you do not walk past. The network that activated for Annie Ramos, as I wrote in “The Network That Already Exists,” shared no theology. It shared a prohibition: you do not cage a woman for something that happened to her when she was twenty-two months old. That shared prohibition was thick enough to generate real action. AI governance can start with shared prohibitions - we will not tolerate autonomous weapons that kill without human judgment, we will not tolerate algorithmic discrimination that the deployer knows about and does not correct, we will not tolerate surveillance systems that treat entire populations as suspects - and build the positive canon over time, case by case, responsum by responsum.

Third, the Torah itself was not given all at once. The written Torah was given at Sinai. The oral Torah - the Mishnah, the Gemara, the responsa, the codes - developed over centuries through the very mechanisms I have described. The canon grew through use. The same process is available to AI governance: start with the thin agreement you have, build precedent through cases, let the canon thicken with practice. The first generation of responsa will be tentative. The tenth generation will be thick. The tradition teaches (Pirkei Avot 5:26) that Torah study deepens with effort - “turn it and turn it, for everything is in it.” The canon is not a starting condition. It is an outcome of the process.

<hr>*The surviving samurai stand before the graves. The farmers wade back into their rice paddies. Kambei looks at the mounds of earth where his friends are buried and says the truest thing anyone in the film says: the victory belongs to the farmers, not to the warriors. The samurai had no institution. They had competence vouched for by other competent people, commitment to a specific community facing a specific threat, and the willingness to serve without owning what they served. Four of seven died. The village survived. The tradition has been running that architecture for two thousand years - not as a metaphor, not as a suggestion, but as a functioning legal system that held when everything else fell. The question for AI governance is not whether distributed authority works. The history is clear. The question is whether anyone will trust it before the institutions finish failing.*
