# You Diagnosed the Disease. Now Look at Why There’s No Cure.

Professor Kaiser,

I read your piece. Twice. And I want to start by saying something you probably don’t hear often enough in those meetings: you’re right.

You’re right that nobody in the room can define “ethical.” You’re right that the conversation about cheating is obscene when data centers are draining aquifers and data laborers in the Global South are being chewed up and discarded. You’re right that “transparent validation strategies” is the kind of phrase people use when they want to sound rigorous without actually being rigorous. You’re right that the self-congratulatory tone of those faculty presentations is grotesque given what’s happening downstream of every API call. You’re right that the tech oligarchs are demanding human sacrifice and calling it innovation.

I don’t just agree with your diagnosis. I’ve made the same one — from a completely different direction. And that’s why I’m writing to you. Because I think you’ve identified the emptiness with devastating precision. But I think the tool you’re reaching for to fill it has the same hole at its center.

Your piece demolishes the notion that institutional “AI ethics” means anything. And it doesn’t. You know why? Not because your colleagues are lazy, or cowardly, or haven’t read the literature — though maybe some of them haven’t. The reason nobody in that room could define “ethical” is that the civilization they belong to cannot define it either. They aren’t failing at a task. They are attempting something that their entire intellectual framework has made impossible.

You write that no one in these meetings has “actually defined the term ‘ethical’ in the context of the use of genAI.” But zoom out. Can the modern Western university define “ethical” in the context of *anything*? Not procedurally — not “here are our guidelines” — but substantively? On what basis? Derived from what? Grounded in what?

The honest answer is: it can’t. And that’s not a local failure of your institution. It’s a civilizational one.

The West doesn’t have values. It has *law*. And law is not enough. Law tells you what happens if you get caught. Values tell you why you shouldn’t want to do it in the first place. Your colleagues are trying to build an ethics policy the way the West builds everything — with compliance frameworks, use-case taxonomies, and committees. They are constructing scaffolding around an empty building. You can see that the building is empty. But I want to push you on what could fill it.

---You invoke Marx. You acknowledge, to your credit, that Marx deliberately refused to talk about ethics and morality. You even quote the Stanford Encyclopedia of Philosophy explaining why. So here’s the uncomfortable question: you spend three thousand words eviscerating the emptiness of institutional ethics talk, and then you gesture toward a thinker who also refused to ground ethics. Your alternative — a “communally autonomous democratic approach to technological innovation” — is eloquent. But it has a structural problem.

Democratic process doesn’t generate values. It expresses them. A community votes, deliberates, decides — but *on what basis*? What tells the community that exploiting data laborers is wrong? What tells them the aquifer matters more than the data center? You’ll say it’s obvious. But “obvious” is doing a lot of work there. Obvious to whom? On what grounds? The factory owners of Manchester thought child labor was obvious and necessary. The slave economy of the American South thought its arrangements were obvious and natural. “Obvious” without a foundation is just the moral consensus of whoever is currently in power.

You want Ivan Illich’s convivial tools. I love Illich. But Illich was a Catholic priest. His entire framework for conviviality was grounded in a vision of the human person as sacred — not as a production unit, not as a node in a network, but as a being with inherent dignity derived from something beyond democratic consensus. Take away that grounding and “conviviality” becomes another nice word, like “ethical,” that sounds warm and means nothing enforceable.

This is the move your piece can’t quite make. You’ve correctly identified that “ethical AI use” is empty language masking institutional cowardice. But the reason it’s empty isn’t just capitalism, and it isn’t just oligarchy, and it isn’t just the neoliberal university. It’s that the civilization producing these institutions cut itself off from the source that once gave the word “ethical” actual content — and now it’s wondering why the word rings hollow.

---I come from a tradition that has been thinking about technology and ethics for a very long time — not decades, not centuries, but millennia. And I want to offer you something that might reframe the problem you’re wrestling with.

There’s a principle in Jewish law called *bal tashchit* — “do not destroy.” It originates in Deuteronomy, in a law about not cutting down fruit trees during wartime. But the rabbis expanded it into a comprehensive ethic: do not waste, do not destroy needlessly, do not consume beyond what serves genuine human need. Not because the tree has market value. Not because a committee decided trees matter. Because the world was *entrusted* to us, and destruction of what was entrusted is a violation of that trust — a betrayal of the relationship between humanity and its Creator.

That’s a fundamentally different starting point than anything available to your faculty meeting.

When you ask “do the students not value the environmental resources being destroyed?” — the honest answer, within a secular-materialist framework, is: *they have no binding reason to.* They can feel bad about it. They can be persuaded by data. But feeling and persuasion are not the same as obligation. If the world is just matter and energy and market forces, then the aquifer is a resource allocation problem, not a moral one. The strongest argument you can make is utilitarian — “this will hurt us eventually” — and utilitarian arguments lose every time they go up against quarterly earnings.

But if the world is entrusted — if there is a Trustor, and we are trustees — then draining an aquifer to cool servers running a stochastic parrot is not just wasteful. It is a *betrayal*. That word has weight that “unsustainable” never will.

---Here’s what I think you’re actually reaching for, even if you wouldn’t put it this way.

Near the end of your piece, you write something beautiful: you want to imagine “what kinds of solidarity might emerge, and how our entire conceptions of education, progress, innovation, and a good life might change.” You say you’d rather spend your energy “cultivating this imagination” than doing the mental gymnastics of pretending genAI can be used ethically.

I respect that enormously. But “cultivating imagination” is what you do *after* you have a foundation. First you need to answer the question your colleagues can’t answer and your Marxist framework won’t answer: *What is a human being, and what are we for?*

If a human being is a production unit whose value is measured in output, then AI is a threat or a tool, and “ethics” is just efficiency optimization with better PR. If a human being is a political subject whose value is determined by democratic participation, then “ethics” is whatever the community decides — which is how you end up with faculty committees that can’t define the term. But if a human being is a soul with a specific mission — if every student in that classroom carries something unrepeatable that the world needs — then the question isn’t “how do we use AI ethically?” The question is: *does this tool help that student become who they were created to be, or does it obstruct that becoming?*

That question has teeth. That question your colleagues could actually argue about productively, because it points to something real.

---I’ll go further. You cite Morozov on the tech oligarchs’ “oracular authority” — their ability to position themselves as passive channels for inevitable futures. You’re right that this is dangerous. But do you see the irony? Marx does the same thing. Historical materialism treats the trajectory of capitalism as inevitable — the contradictions will intensify, the revolution will come, the mode of production will change. It’s technological determinism with a different aesthetic.

The oligarchs say: AI is inevitable, get on board or get left behind.

The Marxist says: capitalism’s self-destruction is inevitable, the contradictions will resolve themselves.

Neither framework gives the individual — the actual human being standing in your classroom — any agency grounded in anything transcendent. Neither says: *you matter independent of the system. Your choices have weight beyond their material consequences. There is something in you that no algorithm can replicate and no market can price.*

That’s what’s missing from the room. Not better theory. Not better politics. A reason to believe that the human being sitting across from you is sacred, and that “sacred” isn’t just a metaphor.

---So what would I actually propose? Not for the faculty meeting — I think you and I both know those meetings are mostly theater. But for you, and for the students you actually care about:

Stop asking “how do we use AI ethically” and start asking “what is education *for*?” If education is job training, then AI changes the calculus of which skills to train, and the whole conversation is economic. If education is the formation of a human soul — the cultivation of wisdom, discernment, moral seriousness, and the capacity to resist what is popular in favor of what is true — then the conversation changes completely. Then you’re not asking whether ChatGPT helps students find case precedent faster. You’re asking whether it makes them more likely to become the kind of people who can look at an unjust system and say *no*, and mean it, and hold the line when it costs them something.

Your piece is evidence that *you* are that kind of person. You sat in that room and said what nobody wanted to hear. The question is whether your students will be able to do the same. And that depends not on AI policy, but on whether anyone ever tells them that they have a soul, that it matters, and that the world needs what only they can give it.

That’s the conversation worth having. Everything else is, as you so eloquently put it, bullshit.
