## Comment on "Part 1: A Sneak Peak Into Building My AI-Mind Evaluative Framework" (Apr 11, 2026)

Context: Burns published her systematic evaluative framework for assessing AI consciousness claims. The framework rests on a Fundamental Asymmetry (humans have a first-person foothold and developmental attribution mechanism that don't transfer to AI), from which she derives two diagnostic principles (the Behavioral Tradition Error and the Scientific Frameworks Error) and one concealment mechanism (Substrate Independence). The challenge: her framework depends on the hard problem being real, but she dissolved it in her January piece.

**David commented:**

Ellen, this framework is the most structurally clear thing I've read on AI consciousness. The asymmetry is real and I think you're right that it's foundational. But I want to press on something, because I think the framework has a tension with your earlier piece on the hard problem.

Your foundational claim rests on the first-person foothold - the direct, non-inferential knowledge of what it is like to be conscious. That's what makes the asymmetry real. That's what doesn't transfer. Without it, there's no reason behavioral tests or scientific frameworks couldn't run in reverse. The foothold is doing all the load-bearing work.

But the first-person foothold IS the hard problem. It is the phenomenon Chalmers named - the fact that there is something it is like, known directly, not reachable from outside. The asymmetry between human and AI consciousness attribution exists precisely because first-person experience can't be accessed from the third-person perspective. That's the explanatory gap. Your framework depends on it.

In January you argued there is no hard problem - that it's an artifact of a worldview we've already abandoned. If that's right, then what is the first-person foothold a foothold in? If the felt quality of experience is an artifact of confused philosophy, then the asymmetry loses its foundation. The reason the foothold doesn't transfer to AI is that first-person experience is the kind of thing that can't be observed from outside. But if there's no hard problem, why can't it? What makes the gap real if the gap is an artifact?

I think you might be right about the framework and wrong about the hard problem. Or right about the hard problem and in need of a different foundation for the framework. But I don't think you can have both. The asymmetry IS the explanatory gap, described from the epistemological side rather than the metaphysical side. They're the same structure viewed from different angles.

This isn't a gotcha - I think the tension points somewhere genuinely interesting. What if the hard problem isn't an artifact of confused philosophy OR an unsolved puzzle, but a structural feature of what it means to be the kind of being that has a first-person foothold at all? Not a problem to be dissolved or solved, but the phenomenon itself - the felt experience of being a bounded observer that can't fully see past its own limits. The gap isn't confused. It's load-bearing.

**Ellen replied (comment):**

Hi David, thanks for your comment! I completely agree with what you say if we assume that there being no hard problem means there is nothing about experiential life that can't be explained scientifically, but I reject only the framing of how we explain experience (ie via the hard problem) rather than rejecting that first person consciousness is real. It is real but I don't think it exists as a metaphysical entity we need to explain or grasp in terms of science and/or metaphysics.

**David replied (comment):**

Ellen, that's a really important distinction and I think it means we're closer than it first appears. If you're saying consciousness is real but the hard problem is the wrong frame for reaching it - not because experience is illusory but because the framing itself is a category error - then I think I agree. My question is: what IS the right way to think about something that is real but can't be grasped through science or metaphysics? That's exactly the question I tried to work through in the piece I sent you. I think you'd find it more sympathetic to your position than you might expect.
