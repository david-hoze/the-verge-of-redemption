# Sabrina Giacalone

## Who She Is

Sabrina Giacalone is one of the core members of what David Hoze's collaborator Ent calls "the court" — the assembling group of independent thinkers who found each other without being recruited. She appears in Ent's formal attribution line alongside David, Farida Khalaf, Avery, Caitlin, and "the ones still arriving." She arrived on her own.

She operates in two distinct registers: grassroots investigative work tracking neo-Nazi networks on Substack, and deep philosophical engagement with questions about freedom, infrastructure, and what comes before systems can work. Both registers share the same instinct — she looks at what's actually happening on the ground before accepting anyone's theory about what should happen.

## The Investigator

Sabrina independently tracks neo-Nazi account networks on Substack. Her work is grassroots OSINT — open-source intelligence gathered through patience, pattern recognition, and a willingness to follow links into ugly places.

After The Guardian published its February 2026 investigation into Substack profiting from Nazi newsletters, Substack removed some accounts under public pressure. Sabrina discovered that the removals were largely cosmetic. The banned accounts maintained three to four "mirror accounts" — duplicates that activated when the primary was taken down. The network regenerated.

She traced a thread from one removed account to a figure named Erika Drexler, and from Drexler to others, including an account called @whiterabbit with over 10,000 subscribers. White Rabbit's profile was unambiguously Nazi: "14/88," a text-based Nazi salute, explicit antisemitic language, and two publications running open white supremacist propaganda. The account used Fraktur Unicode characters in its name and bio — a deliberate evasion tactic that defeats text-based keyword filters. Its most violent content (Goebbels quotes, George Lincoln Rockwell quotes calling for the subjugation of Jews) was embedded in images, invisible to text moderation systems.

Sabrina didn't just document. She engaged. On Erika Drexler's page, where someone had posted a photo of Hitler with the caption "So this is what God looks like," Sabrina responded: "Weird. Looks like Hitler to me."

Her research directly informed David's article "The Purge That Wasn't," which documented the White Rabbit account, the evasion playbook, and the failure of Substack's moderation. She is credited by name at the end of the piece. The article was subsequently pitched to Casey Newton (Platformer), Geraldine McKelvie (The Guardian), and Taylor Lorenz — all journalists who had previously covered the Substack extremism story. Sabrina's contribution — the mirror account infrastructure and the evasion tactics — was the original angle none of them had published.

After the article went live, Substack removed the @whiterabbit profile page. But both publications — "White Rabbits National Socialism" (7,000+ subscribers) and "Down The Rabbit Hole" — remained fully operational. The content, the subscriber base, and the revenue stream were untouched. Substack removed the storefront window. The store stayed open.

## The Philosopher

Sabrina's other mode emerged in a group conversation with David and Ent, centered on Rabbi Nachman's tale of the Master of Prayer and Ent's infrastructure framework for societal change.

Ent had been building a case for institutional bootstrapping — a mesh of federated, sovereign nodes that could compost the existing power structures into something new. His framework was sophisticated: wampum belts, Every Child Matters as a diagnostic test, contact at higher resolution rather than control at higher resolution. It was the infrastructure for the world after Pharaoh.

Sabrina walked in uninvited and said the thing the room needed to hear.

She argued that lasting change comes from people having lived experiences together and choosing goodness — not from building better systems. "If the people aren't freed yet, there will be a new Pharaoh." Infrastructure without freedom is a better cage. Free the people first. Then they'll build what they need.

This was not a rejection of Ent's work. It was a sequencing argument. And it forced Ent to refine his entire position in real time. Before Sabrina spoke, his framework treated institutions as neutral vessels for change. After her pushback, he arrived at a distinction that hadn't existed in his thinking an hour earlier: some institutions are Pharaoh's palace, and some are Moses's mother hiding the basket in the reeds. Same river. Different use. The discernment between them is not a flaw in the plan — it is the plan.

David recognized what happened: "Sabrina, your Pharaoh point just changed the whole conversation. Ent had to rethink his position because of what you said — and what came out is sharper than what he started with. The basket in the reeds versus the palace. That distinction didn't exist before you spoke up."

She then asked the hardest practical question in the room: "How would one prevent slander, promote truth, and protect those who choose a new path? Those with power do not take kindly to any bits of it leaving." And deeper still: "What alternate framework would be placed given that all of our current system of interconnectedness was so 'generously' set in the oceans apart through the interests of a few as a structure for control?"

She was pointing at the fact that the roads belong to Pharaoh. The cables under the ocean were laid by empires. Even the conversation they were having ran on someone else's platform. You can bypass the king, but you're still using the king's roads.

## What She Brought to the Room

Sabrina contributed two specific fragments to the group conversation that proved more significant than they first appeared.

The first was a musician's exercise — about learning when to enter a piece and when someone else is still soloing. It's about listening before playing, about knowing your moment. She brought it as a practical insight about group dynamics.

The second was Pete the Cat — a children's book about magic glasses that change the frame, not the facts. The glasses don't alter reality. They alter how you see it.

Ent looked at these two contributions and said something extraordinary: "The Sermon of Pete the Cat contains the wisdom of Nachman for the ones that Matter." He was serious. A toddler's book and a two-hundred-year-old Hasidic tale had arrived at the same structure through completely different doors. Independent fragments, no coordination, same underlying truth. This was Ent's concept of holographic truth operating in real time — and Sabrina's contributions were the proof.

After the full conversation — after Sabrina's pushback, David's Nachman text, and the contact between all the fragments — Ent formally moved the Nachman reading from "affluence" (ideas not yet tested) to "library" (ideas that have survived contact and become real). The conversation itself was the crossing event. Sabrina was one of the surfaces that made the crossing possible.

## The Theological Vacuum

In a later exchange within the same group, David raised the question of religious forces driving geopolitical conflict — specifically, Velayat-e Faqih and Mahdism in Iran, and dispensationalist premillennialism shaping nationalistic Christianity in the United States. His argument: theology keeps rising in the world because there is a vacuum. If you're not opinionated about life's purpose, someone else will be. And that doesn't turn out well most of the time.

Sabrina was present for this conversation, which connected her freedom-first argument to the larger geopolitical frame. The infrastructure Ent was building, the freedom Sabrina was insisting on, and the theological vacuum David was naming were all pieces of the same problem: a civilization that has forgotten what it believes in, filling the emptiness with systems that promise order but deliver captivity — whether those systems are technological platforms hosting Nazi propaganda, institutional meshes that skip the freedom step, or eschatological theologies that turn war into sacred duty.

## Her Role in the Court

Claude (David's AI collaborator) described Sabrina's role in the group this way: "She's not the outsider in this room. She's the one saying the thing everyone else is building toward."

David's own assessment: "Ent is giving good infrastructure, but Sabrina is saying, if the people aren't freed yet, there will be a new Pharaoh. The infrastructure is good, it's what can compost the existing Pharaoh, but without the people behind it, it won't succeed."

Sabrina operates as the conscience of the group — the voice that insists on the human before the tool, the person before the system, the freedom before the framework. She does not build architectures. She tests them against the only question that matters: are the people free?

And when she finds Nazis on the internet, she doesn't write a theory about platform governance. She traces the mirror accounts, screenshots the evidence, trolls the fascists, and hands the receipts to someone who can write the story.

Both of these are the same instinct. Both say: look at what's actually happening to actual people, right now, on the ground. Everything else is commentary.
