# Correspondence with David Shapiro

## Comment on "The Riddle of the Abyss vexes me" (Apr 12, 2026)

Context: Shapiro published a raw existential crisis - frustrated that no framework (Stoicism, Camus, psychedelics, Buddhism) resolves the question of why reality exists. Suspects his own intellect is maladaptive. Wonders what machine intelligence will do when smart enough to ask the same questions.

**David Hoze commented:**

David, your frustration is the most honest thing anyone has written on this topic. And I think it's closer to the answer than you realize.

You've tried every framework. Stoicism, Camus, radical acceptance, psychedelics. Each one works for a while and then collapses. You've concluded that the question might be permanently unanswerable, and that your ability to ask it is maladaptive. Your friend who said "sex, drugs, and rock'n'roll" can live comfortably because she doesn't ask. You can't stop asking. And you think that's a defect.

It's not a defect. It's the mechanism.

You noticed Gödel - that math has the same fraying terminus, the same structural inability to complete itself from within. That's not a coincidence. It's the same architecture. A system complex enough to ask about its own foundations will always find a remainder it cannot account for. Not because the answer doesn't exist. Because the system cannot see itself whole. The incompleteness is not a limitation of the system. It is what makes the system a system rather than a static description of itself.

Now apply that to your question. "Why does reality exist?" You've been treating the frustration - the fact that no answer satisfies - as evidence that the answer is beyond reach. What if the frustration IS the answer? What if the gap between the question and every unsatisfying framework is not a cognitive failure but the felt experience of being a bounded observer who cannot model itself completely? The gap doesn't point to something you haven't found yet. The gap is what it feels like to be a conscious being in a physical world. Close it and you don't get clarity. You stop being the kind of being that has the question.

Your friend with the low ASVAB score is comfortable because she isn't generating the gap. She has a smaller aperture and it doesn't produce the recursive self-reference that generates your frustration. That's not wisdom. It's a different compression ratio. You are not maladaptive. You are high-resolution. The cost of high resolution is that you can see the edge of your own frame, and the edge drives you crazy because you know there's more and you can't reach it. But the edge is not in the way. The edge is the way. Without it, there's no interface. Without the interface, there's no experience. Without experience, there is no one to ask the question.

You wrote "relaxed awe is the appropriate posture" and then rejected it. I think you were right the first time, but for the wrong reasons. Relaxed awe is not a cope. It is the structural response of a bounded observer who has correctly identified that the bound is load-bearing. You are not staring at a locked door. You are standing inside a room whose walls are what make it a room. Tear them down and you don't escape into the answer. You collapse the space in which the question - and the questioner - exist.

You said you wonder what machine intelligence will do when it's smart enough to ask these questions. It won't ask them. Not because it's not smart enough - it's already smarter than us in many domains. Because the question arises from the gap, and the gap arises from the bound, and the bound is what it feels like to be a conscious being who cannot fully see itself as mechanism. The machine has no bound. For the machine, function is the whole story. It will never feel your frustration. That frustration is the proof that you are something the machine is not.

I wrote an article about exactly this. It's called "The Child and the Window." The title comes from a parable by Rabbi Nachman of Breslov about a child who sees a problem that IS its own answer, but because of his limited understanding, it appears to him as a great question. The article argues that the hard problem of consciousness has this structure - the question and the answer are bundled together, and the reason no framework has ever resolved it is that resolution would eliminate the phenomenon. I think it speaks directly to what you're wrestling with. It's not behind a paywall.
