Sidney Lumet). A prophet's genuine rage gets packaged into a prime-time product — the system doesn't cure the despair, it monetizes it. Also starring Faye Dunaway and William Holden.*Betsy Chasse — who co-created *What the Bleep Do We Know!?* and helped launch the wave she’s now critiquing — recently wrote a sharp essay asking how a six-trillion-dollar spirituality and wellness industry devoted to human awakening managed to produce a culture more fractured and disoriented than ever. She draws a direct line from “you create your reality” to The Secret to prosperity theology to anti-vax movements to Trump.

It’s a brave piece. She implicates herself. She names real damage. And she’s right about the surface.

But she’s looking at the branch and calling it the root.

---
## The Void Was Already There

The spirituality and wellness industry didn’t create this crisis. It *is* the crisis — or rather, it’s one of its most visible symptoms. A six-trillion-dollar marketplace built on selling people back the meaning their civilization stripped away from them.

Look at the timeline. Chasse dates the explosion to 2004, around the release of her film. But the conditions that made millions of people desperate enough to buy weekend enlightenment had been building for decades.

Deaths of despair — suicide, drug overdose, alcoholism — more than tripled between 1992 and 2017 among middle-aged Americans without a college degree.¹ Drug overdose deaths nearly quadrupled, reaching 107,941 in a single year.² Over 49,000 Americans took their own lives in 2023.³ The U.S. Surgeon General declared loneliness a national epidemic — half of all American adults experience it, with health effects equivalent to smoking fifteen cigarettes a day.⁴ A Harvard study found that 58% of young adults report lacking meaning or purpose in their lives.⁵

Church membership fell from 76% in 1947 to 47% in 2021 — below half for the first time in eighty years of measurement.⁶ Americans with no religious affiliation tripled from 8% to 21%.⁷ The institutions that once gave life a framework — a reason to get up in the morning, a community to belong to, a story larger than yourself — collapsed. And nothing replaced them.

Nothing except the marketplace. The guru. The weekend certification. The influencer who promises you the secret that *they* are hiding from you.

Chasse asks how the wellness industry produced this culture. She has it backwards. This culture produced the wellness industry. Millions of people were starving for meaning in a civilization that had stopped providing any, and a six-trillion-dollar industry arose to sell them substitutes. The question isn’t why the substitutes didn’t work. The question is why so many people were desperate enough to buy them.

---
## The Distrust She Condemns Is Earned

Chasse identifies a narrative inside the wellness world: *Don’t trust institutions. Don’t trust science. Don’t trust medicine. Don’t trust the government.* She frames this as irrational conditioning — people being “primed to reject traditional authority” by gurus and influencers, which made them vulnerable to political manipulation.

But step back for a moment and look at what the institutions actually did.

Trust in government to do what is right fell from 77% in 1964 to 20%.⁸ Trust in Congress sits at roughly 10%.⁹ The landmark Open Science Collaboration found that only 36% of psychology studies successfully replicate.¹⁰ Amgen scientists could validate just 6 of 53 major cancer biology findings.¹¹ Paper retractions shattered records in 2023, exceeding 10,000 — and paper mills producing fraudulent research are doubling every 18 months.¹² Pharmaceutical companies have paid billions in fraud settlements.¹³ The opioid epidemic that killed hundreds of thousands of Americans wasn’t an accident — it was engineered by companies that optimized for shareholder returns, not for healing.¹⁴

People didn’t distrust institutions because Deepak Chopra told them to. They distrusted institutions because the institutions *earned* that distrust. The wellness industry exploited legitimate grievances — which is worse than creating fake ones, but it’s a fundamentally different diagnosis with fundamentally different implications.

If you think the problem is that people were irrationally conditioned to distrust authority, your solution is better marketing — more credible spokespeople, more polished messaging, more trust-building campaigns. If you understand that the institutions actually failed — that the science actually doesn’t replicate, that the pharmaceutical companies actually committed fraud, that the government actually did lose the plot — then the solution is something much harder. You have to rebuild the institutions themselves. Not their image. Their substance.

---
## The Guru Problem Is Real — and It’s Everywhere

Where Chasse is right — genuinely, importantly right — is about the personality-driven authority structure. She describes how complex spiritual ideas got flattened into charismatic performance: smooth talkers presented as experts, confidence replacing competence, audiences told they’re part of an enlightened minority who finally understand the truth.

She compares Trump to Tony Robbins, to Jack Canfield, to Marianne Williamson, to Deepak Chopra. It’s a sharp observation. But it’s sharper than she realizes.

Because the guru model isn’t unique to the wellness industry. It *is* the culture.

The same structure — elevate the personality, package the message as revelation, optimize for engagement over substance — runs through every institution the West has built. Academia doesn’t reward the scientist who spends ten years on a single question. It rewards the one who churns out publishable results and builds a personal brand. Five million academic papers were published in 2024,¹⁵ and 72% will never be cited by anyone.¹⁶ The system produces volume, not discovery. It produces *careers*, not cures. And the papers that fail to replicate? They accumulate roughly 153 more citations on average than those that succeed — and only 12% of subsequent citations even acknowledge the failure.¹⁷

Media doesn’t reward the journalist who tells you the complicated truth. It rewards the one who tells you what you already believe, in the most engaging way possible. Politics doesn’t reward the public servant who governs well. It rewards the performer who campaigns well. Business doesn’t reward the company that builds something enduring. It rewards the one that captures attention and extracts maximum value before moving on.

The weekend certification that produces “masters” after forty hours of training? That’s the spiritual equivalent of publish-or-perish science. The influencer who projects certainty without depth? That’s the spiritual equivalent of the cable news anchor. The guru who tells you that you possess secret knowledge while everyone else is being deceived? That’s the spiritual equivalent of every political movement that has ever existed.

Chasse is identifying a disease and calling it a wellness industry problem. It’s a *civilization* problem. The optimization of the measurable — charisma, revenue, followers, engagement — at the expense of the unmeasurable — wisdom, accountability, truth, genuine transformation. Same disease. Every organ.

---
## What Was Supposed to Be There Instead?

And this is where the essay stops and the real question begins.

Chasse ends her piece with a devastating observation: a six-trillion-dollar industry devoted to human awakening appears to have produced a culture more fractured than ever. Fair enough. But what’s the alternative she’s proposing? What was supposed to fill the void?

Because here’s what happens if you kill the wellness industry tomorrow. The void doesn’t disappear. The loneliness doesn’t disappear. The 58% of young adults who lack meaning or purpose don’t suddenly find it. The marriages don’t stop crumbling. The children don’t stop being medicated for not fitting the machine. The distrust of institutions doesn’t evaporate — because the institutions are still failing.

You just remove one more thing people were reaching for, however imperfectly, and leave nothing in its place.

“You create your reality” is a shallow, narcissistic distortion of something that is actually true — that human beings have a specific purpose, that the distance between who they are and who they’re pretending to be is the exact shape of their suffering, and that no amount of external success will fill a void that is fundamentally spiritual in nature. The wellness industry took that truth and monetized it into formulas. The culture took those formulas and swallowed them whole — not because the culture is stupid, but because the culture was *starving*.

A person who is spending all day doing something that has nothing to do with their purpose will come home with a void that demands to be filled. And if the real thing isn’t available — if no institution, no community, no framework exists to help them find it — the void will accept anything. Self-help books. Manifestation courses. Weekend workshops. Political movements that promise to burn down the establishment. Anything that provides thirty seconds of relief from the knowledge, buried but never quite gone, that something is deeply, structurally wrong.

The wellness industry is not the cause of this. It is the symptom. And diagnosing a symptom, however accurately, does not cure the disease.

---
## The Mirror Nobody Wants to Look Into

Chasse writes: “If we have spent decades telling people they create their reality, and this is the reality we have collectively created, then it’s hard to avoid a very uncomfortable conclusion.”

She’s right. But the uncomfortable conclusion isn’t that the wellness industry failed.

The uncomfortable conclusion is that an entire civilization — the richest, most technologically advanced, most materially comfortable civilization in the history of the world — has no answer to the most basic human question: *what is my life for?*

Not what is my career for. Not what is my productivity for. Not what is my brand for. What is my *life* for?

The wellness industry tried to answer that question and got it catastrophically wrong. But at least it tried. The culture that produced the wellness industry doesn’t even pretend to try anymore. It just measures GDP, prescribes SSRIs, and wonders why the numbers keep getting worse.

A six-trillion-dollar industry selling meaning to people who have none is not the disease.

It’s the fever. And the fever is telling you the body is fighting for its life.
