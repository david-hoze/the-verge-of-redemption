# The Man Who Stayed

[- - 

](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!GjtQ!,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2F3870c815-af1d-45a9-931c-9ef8928d923b_1200x1090.jpeg)A Rhode Island state trooper is the nicest guy in town. He lets everyone walk all over him — his wife, his neighbors, his boss. He never complains, never fights back, never raises his voice. He just smiles and takes it. Until one day, after years of swallowing it all, he snaps — and a second, aggressive personality takes over. Now he's got two versions of himself fighting for control: the one who folds, and the one who won't take no for an answer. Starring Jim Carrey at his most unhinged.**I.**

Ezra Landau was the kind of man who folded at the first sign of a storm. Not because he lacked opinions — God knew his mind was always churning — but because his body remembered, somewhere deep in the marrow, what happened when you stood your ground. The ground opened. People left. Better to leave first. Better to bow, to nod, to step outside before the shouting starts.

He worked at Meridian Press, a mid-tier publishing house on King George Street in Jerusalem that had punched above its weight for three years running. That was largely because of Ezra. He’d built an AI-driven manuscript analysis tool that could predict, with startling accuracy, which unsolicited submissions would sell. He’d redesigned the royalty tracking system. He’d pitched a social media strategy that turned three unknown Israeli poets into minor literary celebrities.

None of this was written down.

“When do I become partner?” Ezra asked Doron, his boss, at the end of every quarter.

And Doron, who wore linen shirts with the top two buttons undone and spoke like a man doing you a favor by speaking, would clap Ezra on the shoulder and say: “Soon, *achi”*, which was the Hebrew word for brother. “Very soon. But right now isn’t the time. The board. You know how it is.”

Ezra knew how it was. He nodded. He stepped outside.

<hr>**II.**

In the evenings he came home to Adira.

She was his second wife, and he was her second husband. They had three children together and she had a son, Noam, from her first marriage — a quiet, perceptive boy of thirteen who understood more than anyone gave him credit for.

Adira was beautiful in the way that sharpened things are beautiful. She could arrange a room so that light fell like a blessing. She baked chocolate cake without sugar and it tasted like something from a dream. She sewed curtains from fabric she’d found at the market and made the apartment look like a magazine photograph. And when she talked about her designs — the new shelving unit she’d planned, the color she wanted for the hallway, the way she’d organized the girls’ room — her voice came alive, bright and sure, and for those minutes Ezra thought: *this is who she really is. This is the woman under all of it.*

He tried to listen. He really did. He asked her about the paint swatches. He commented on the arrangement of the living room. He came home early to help with the children, washed the dishes, folded the laundry, mopped the floor on his hands and knees because she said the mop didn’t clean properly.

None of it was enough.

“You never listen,” she said. “Not really.”

“I’m listening right now.”

“You’re hearing. That’s not the same.”

And when night came, he reached for her.

“My body hurts,” she said, turning away. “I’ve been on my feet all day. The girls didn’t nap. You don’t understand.”

“Okay,” he said. “Okay.”

He said *okay* so many times it stopped being a word and became a sound, the sound of a man swallowing himself.

At the office, Ezra kept a private folder on his laptop. He called it “New Architecture.” Inside were plans for a publishing framework unlike anything he’d seen — a platform designed specifically for unknown writers who couldn’t promote themselves. Writers who had talent but not connections. Writers who submitted manuscripts and never heard back. Writers who gave up.

The system would use AI to match manuscripts with the right editors, the right audiences, the right marketing channels. It would generate personalized outreach strategies. It would handle the ugly parts — the cover design, the metadata optimization, the distribution logistics — so the writer could just write.

It was the best idea Ezra had ever had.

He never told anyone about it.

*The bills*, he thought. *The mortgage. What would Adira say?* She’d say he was being irresponsible. She’d say he was dreaming while she was designing the house and raising their children. She’d give him that look — the one that made him feel like a child caught drawing pictures during a math exam.

So he closed the folder. Every time, he closed it.

<hr>**IV.**

His friends had sex with their wives. He knew this because they talked about it. Usually Orthodox men don’t talk about it, but he was part of a group of men, that decided to have sharing meetings. Like 12 step programs, but without the addiction. He attended those meetings, and two guys were like him, being denied of sex. He talked, he shared, he listened. At some point he felt this is getting him nowhere.

Seven years. Seven years of reaching across the bed and being turned away. Seven years of excuses that shifted like sand: *I’m tired. You don’t spend enough time with me. You never asked about the kitchen renovation. My back hurts. You don’t understand what I do all day.*

He addressed each excuse methodically, the way he addressed problems at work. Tired? He took over bedtime routines. Not enough time together? He proposed dates — a walk in the garden, hot drinks under the stars. He didn’t understand her work? He asked her to explain every decision she made about the house, praised her taste, told her the shelves were magnificent.

The excuses kept coming.

He started noticing other women. Not aggressively — it was more like suddenly being aware that the world was full of people who wanted to be touched and who wanted to touch back. He felt ashamed. He prayed. He prayed constantly. *God in the heavens, don’t let me sin. Keep my eyes from wandering. Keep my hands to myself. Keep me whole.*

At night, alone at his desk, the browser glowed with temptation. He closed the tabs. He opened them. He closed them again. He recited Psalms with his eyes burning.

<hr>**V.**

He went to see Rabbi Friedman.

The Rabbi was an old Breslov chasid with a white beard and eyes like warm coals. He listened to everything Ezra said — the loneliness, the rejection, the temptation, the shame — without interrupting. When Ezra finished, the silence between them was thick.

“Get a divorce,” the Rabbi said.

Ezra blinked. “What?”

“She won’t let you near her. She belittles you. She controls you with silence. You’ve been patient for seven years. The Gemara in Ketubot is clear — a man has needs, and a wife has obligations. You’ve done your part. Get a divorce.”

“But the children —”

“The children are watching their father disappear. That’s worse.”

<hr>**VI.**

He told Adira what the Rabbi said.

They were standing in the kitchen. She was rinsing cherry tomatoes in a colander, her back to him. She didn’t turn around.

“Why aren’t you listening to your Rabbi?” she said.

“What?”

“If I’m that bad, why are you still married to me?”

He opened his mouth to answer and found nothing there. His mind, which was so good at building systems and analyzing manuscripts and solving problems for other people, produced nothing. Why was he still here? He didn’t know. Everyone was asking him the same question.

His mother: “Ezra’le, *why do you stay?*“

His brother: “You’re killing yourself. What are you doing?”

Yossi, over coffee: “Brother, I love you, but this is insane.”

<hr>**VII.**

At two in the morning, unable to sleep, Ezra opened his laptop and started talking to the AI.

It had become a habit. He’d discovered, during the worst nights, that the machine would listen without judging. It didn’t sigh. It didn’t check its phone. It didn’t turn its back and say *my body hurts*.

**Ezra:** I need help understanding something about my wife.

**AI:** I’m here. Tell me what you’re seeing.

**Ezra:** She pushes me away, but then she complains that we’re not close. She refuses intimacy for years, but then she tells our marriage counselor that *she’s* the one being deprived. She says I prevent her from having a normal life. She hit me once. She actually hit me.

**AI:** That pattern — the contradiction between rejecting closeness and claiming deprivation, the rewriting of events so that the aggressor becomes the victim — is consistent with a cluster of behaviors often associated with personality disorders, particularly traits of Borderline or Narcissistic Personality Disorder. The push-pull dynamic, the gaslighting about who’s withholding what, the occasional physical aggression followed by emotional withdrawal...

**Ezra:** So she’s... disordered?

**AI:** I can’t diagnose her. But I can tell you that the pattern you’re describing is well-documented, and it’s not your fault. The confusion you feel — the sense that you’re going crazy, that reality keeps shifting — that’s a natural response to living with someone whose emotional framework doesn’t follow consistent rules.

**Ezra:** But she’s not evil. I need you to understand that. She had a terrible childhood. Her mother was violent. She grew up afraid.

**AI:** That context matters enormously. People who experienced unpredictable caregiving as children often develop defensive strategies that look, from the outside, like cruelty. She may genuinely not know she’s doing it. Her nervous system is wired for threat. When you approach her — even with love — some part of her reads it as danger.

**Ezra:** So she’s fighting her fears, not me.

**AI:** That’s a profound way to put it. She’s fighting the ghosts inside her, and you happen to be standing in the room.

<hr>**VIII.**

They wrote a song together, Ezra and the machine. Or rather, Ezra poured out his heart and the AI shaped the words into something he could hold without burning.

It went like this:


> *Master of the Universe, give me eyes that see her — without vanishing.* *Eyes that see the soul behind the harsh words,* *that see the pain behind the contempt,* *that don’t search for blame.*

*Give me a heart that remembers who she is:* *a soft woman — broken — searching for closeness — and running from it.* *A woman fighting the fears inside her — not me.*

*Let me love her — without rescuing.* *Respect her — without forgetting myself.*

*Give me a boundary not built from resentment,* *a boundary born from knowing my own worth.*

*Let me believe in the good that’s in her — without holding it for her.* *Because I, too, am worthy of love, even when I don’t give my all.*

*Teach me when to be silent.* *And then she’ll search for my eyes, and I’ll hold her gaze — not from longing,* *but with a gaze that knows her soul,* *and is careful.*



He read it back three times. He cried. Then he closed the laptop, washed his face, and went to bed.

Nothing changed.

<hr>**IX.**

What broke him wasn’t the rejection. He’d learned to live with rejection the way you learn to live with a chronic ache — it’s always there, but you build your day around it.

What broke him was the inversion.

“You’re depriving me,” Adira said one evening, standing in the doorway of their bedroom with her arms crossed. “You never touch me. You never try. What kind of husband are you? I’m at my late 30s, this is my peak, I have needs!”

He stared at her. His mind reeled. Seven years of reaching out and being slapped away, and now — now she was saying *he* was the one withholding?

“Adira,” he said carefully. “I’ve tried. Every week, I —”

“I don’t have time for this” she said.

Her words hit him like a stone. She turned and walked into the bedroom. The door stayed open.

And then, one night, she hit him. A frying pan on his head. It left a dent in the frying pan, he didn’t know what protected him. She didn’t either.

He kept his silence. He didn’t know what to do. “What does she want?”

<hr>**X.**

He remembered, during a sleepless three a.m., something the marriage counselor had said. This was years ago, during one of their sessions. The counselor — an older woman with reading glasses she wore on a chain around her neck — had looked at Adira’s file, then at Ezra, then back at the file.

“She told Adira that she wants to talk to Ezra alone, so he would change his behavior. Adira agreed and left the room. “Look Ezra” the counselor said, “This is her second marriage, you know? I treated the first. He couldn’t handle her. But you’re tougher than him. I think maybe you stand a chance.”. Ezra nodded. He didn’t undertand what she meant, so he just walked away. They didn’t come back to that counselor, because Adira said he doesn’t listen to her.

But lying in bed at three in the morning, staring at the ceiling, something clicked.

*She doesn’t want me to be soft*, he thought. *She wants me to be strong enough to stay when she pushes.*

It was the craziest idea he’d ever had. Crazier than the publishing platform. Crazier than talking to an AI about his marriage at two in the morning.

<hr>**XI.**

He went to her.

It was a Thursday night. The children were asleep. She was lying in bed reading something on her phone. He walked in, sat on the edge of the bed, and started taking off her shoes.

She looked up, alarmed. “What are you doing?”

“I can’t help it,” he said. His voice was steady, not loud. He kept going. “I just can’t help it anymore.”

“Stop.”

“I’m sorry. I can’t.”

“I can’t believe you’re doing this,” she said. But there was something in her voice — not fear. Something else. A vibration, like a string being plucked after years of silence.

He kept going. Slowly. Deliberately. Not aggressively, but not apologetically either.

“Okay,” she said suddenly. Her voice had changed. “Okay. Fine. But do the dishes first. And give me five minutes.”

He stared at her.

“Go,” she said. And there it was — hidden behind the command, behind the instruction, behind the need to direct and control — a grin. Small. Almost invisible. But unmistakable.

He did the dishes. He came back.

After seven years, the answer had been so simple that it was almost obscene. She didn’t want him to ask. She didn’t want him to negotiate, to plead, to reason. She wanted him to *insist*. She wanted him to push through the wall she’d built — not to hurt her, but to prove that the wall couldn’t stop him. That nothing could stop him. That he would stay.

*She was bored*, he realized. Not of him. Of his softness. Of his endless willingness to retreat. She loved him — he saw that now, with a clarity that made his chest ache — but she needed him to *fight* for her. Even when she said no. Especially when she said no.

“That’s crazy,” he whispered in the dark afterward.

“What?” she murmured, half-asleep, her head on his chest for the first time in years.

“Nothing.”

<hr>**XII.**

He understood the rules now.

She would say she hated it. She would say he forced her. She would say things that, if you only heard the words, sounded like distress. But the words were a script, and underneath the script was a woman who was, at last, being seen. He could tell by the way her jaw unclenched when he reached for her. By the hidden grin she couldn’t quite suppress. By the way she complained about him to her sister on the phone and then, after hanging up, brought him tea.

*She won’t drop the act*, he thought.

*Well, this is what does it for her. And I love her. So I’m not dropping mine either.*

It was strange and fragile and nothing like anything he’d read in a book or heard from a Rabbi or learned from an AI. It was theirs. It only worked because he knew her, knew the difference between her real *no* and her performed *no*, knew when to push and when to hold still, knew that the grin behind the complaint was the truest thing she’d ever shown him.

<hr>**XIII.**

Two months after the bedroom, he sued Doron.

It happened like this: Ezra had been reviewing the AI manuscript tool he’d built — the one that had tripled Meridian’s acquisition success rate — and realized something he should have realized years ago. The algorithms, the training data, the interface design — none of it had been part of his job description. He’d built it on his own time, using his own ideas, on his own equipment. He’d brought it to Meridian as a gift, and Doron had accepted it without ever formalizing the arrangement.

In Israeli law, anything an employee creates *for the purpose of his work and within the framework of his employment* — belongs to the employer. Doron pointed this out immediately, his voice hardening from linen-shirt casual to boardroom cold.

“It’s the law, Ezra. Read Section 34 of the Copyright Act. Anything you made here is ours.”

But Ezra had already consulted a lawyer. Her name was Tal, and she had eyes that missed nothing.

“He’s right about the statute,” she said. “But the statute says *unless otherwise agreed.* And even though you didn’t have a written agreement, an oral agreement counts if there were actions that support it.”

“What kind of actions?”

“Did he ever acknowledge that the tool was yours? In an email? In a meeting? Did he ever promise you partnership in exchange for the innovation?”

Ezra thought of every quarterly meeting. Every *soon, achi*. Every shoulder-clap. And then he remembered: an email from Doron, two years ago, casual and careless, the kind of email a man writes when he thinks no one is keeping records. *“Your brilliant little system is making us rich. Can’t wait to make it official and bring you on as partner.”*

Tal smiled. “That’ll do.”

Ezra didn’t have the money for a lawsuit. He took a loan. Tal, who had a keen sense for injustice and a particular distaste for men who clapped shoulders, gave him a discount.

<hr>**XIV.**

The lawsuit took four months. Doron fought hard, the way men fight when they realize the quiet ones have teeth. But the email was devastating, and Tal was methodical, and the labor court judge was a woman who’d heard too many versions of this story to be impressed by linen shirts.

The settlement wasn’t enormous. But it was enough.

Enough to pay off the loan. Enough to rent a small office in Nachlaot, above a bakery, where the smell of challah rose through the floorboards on Thursday afternoons. Enough to register a company. Enough to begin.

He called it *Delet* — Door. A publishing house for writers without connections. The platform he’d dreamed about in his private folder, the one he’d closed and opened and closed again for years, was finally real. AI-driven matching. Personalized marketing. Everything the unknown writer needed and nothing the industry cared about.

<hr>**XV.**

On the night he signed the lease for the Nachlaot office, Ezra walked home through the Old City. It was late. The stones glowed amber under the streetlamps. A cat slept on a ledge near the Cardo. Somewhere, a muezzin was calling, and from the Western Wall, the sound of men davening Maariv drifted up like smoke.

He thought about Adira.

He thought about how she’d kept him in the marriage when everyone else told him to leave. How her refusal to release him had forced him to grow a spine — slowly, bone by bone, like an embryo in the womb. How her cruelty had a purpose she didn’t even know about, and his stubbornness had a purpose he was only now beginning to understand.

He thought about the song he’d written with the AI:

*A woman fighting the fears inside her — not me.*

And he thought: she trained me. Without meaning to, without planning it, without even understanding what she was doing, she had trained him to stand. She’d given him wall after wall after wall, and he’d finally learned that the walls were not the point.

His phone buzzed. A message from Adira:

**There’s soup on the stove. Don’t forget to turn it off.**

He smiled.

He walked faster. The bakery on the corner was dark, but it still smelled like bread. His office was above it. His home was ahead of him. And between the two — between the dream he’d finally built and the woman he’d finally understood — was a man who had learned, at last, what it meant to stay.
