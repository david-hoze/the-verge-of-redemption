## The concept is dead. The problem isn’t.

A recent paper in philosophy of mind argues that the “hard problem of consciousness” — the famous puzzle of why there is *something it is like* to be you — is not a real problem at all. It is an artifact of a broken concept.[1](#footnote-1)

The target is “qualia” — the technical term philosophers use for the subjective quality of experience. The redness of red. The painfulness of pain. The thing that makes your experience of a sunset different from a camera’s recording of the same sunset, even if the camera captures every photon you do.

The argument is sharp. It defines qualia as non-functional, non-relational properties — things that don’t participate in causal processes, don’t stand in relations to other features of the world, and float outside the physical story of what happens in the brain. Then it applies three diagnostic tests: Does the concept play a causal role? Does it make a distinguishable difference? Can we specify conditions under which it applies? Qualia fail all three. The concept is explanatorily inert. Remove it, the paper argues, and the hard problem dissolves.

The paper is largely right about the concept. It was asked to do too much — to be simultaneously part of your experience and outside of physical description, to be private yet somehow communicable, to be real yet causally idle. The diagnostic criteria are well-constructed: they don’t obviously presuppose physicalism, and the conclusion that qualia, *as defined in the paper*, fail those criteria is difficult to resist on its own terms.

But the paper makes a further move that I don’t follow it on. It treats the death of the concept as the death of the problem.

<hr>
## The definitional question

Before getting to the substantive disagreement, it’s worth noticing something about the argumentative structure. The paper defines qualia as “non-functional, non-relational, intrinsically subjective properties posited as explanatory additions to physical or functional descriptions of experience.” It acknowledges this is not the only way the term is used. But this definition is load-bearing. The entire argument works by showing that *this kind of thing* — non-functional, non-relational — cannot satisfy criteria that require functional or relational engagement with explanatory structures.

This risks deflation. If qualia are defined as the kind of thing that cannot do explanatory work, then demonstrating that they cannot do explanatory work is less informative than it first appears. The move has a precedent: Daniel Dennett’s “Quining Qualia” similarly loaded the definition with properties — intrinsic, private, ineffable, directly apprehensible — that made qualia vulnerable to his subsequent attack.[2](#footnote-2) The interesting question is not whether qualia *so defined* survive the test, but whether the people who invoke the concept are best understood as positing non-functional, non-relational properties in the first place — or whether they are doing something else, something the paper’s definition may not capture.

Consider a subject who says: “I can see that you’ve fully described the neural correlates of my pain. But there is something it is like to be in this pain, and your description does not capture that.”

The paper reads this as the introduction of an additional entity — a non-functional, non-relational property smuggled into a story that was already complete. But the subject may not be positing an entity at all. She may be pointing to an *aspect* of the very system already described — an aspect that is available from within but not from without. Her complaint is not “you’ve left something out of your inventory of the world.” It is “your inventory, however complete, does not convey what it is like to *be* this system.”

If this is what she means, then the three diagnostic criteria don’t apply. The criteria test whether an additional *posit* earns its keep within an explanatory framework. She is not offering a posit. She is reporting a feature of her situation that she takes to be epistemically prior to any framework.

One could reply that if the feature is not a posit, it is not part of the explanation, and therefore not a philosopher’s concern. That is a coherent position. But it does not dissolve the hard problem. It declines to address it. The hard problem, for those who feel its force, was never primarily about an explanatory posit. It was about the gap between any third-person description, however complete, and the first-person reality of being the described system — the gap that Levine identified as “explanatory” and that Chalmers argued was irreducible.[3](#footnote-3)

## What counts as evidence?

This brings us to the real locus of disagreement. It is not about qualia. It is about a prior question: *what is the scope of legitimate evidence in a theory of consciousness?*

The paper’s three criteria are presented as “minimal” and “not tied to any particular ontology.” But each criterion requires that the concept’s contribution be evaluable from a perspective other than the subject’s own. Causal role means participation in processes that generate *observable* states. Distinguishable difference means a difference detectable by an observer or inferential framework. Conditions of application means criteria that can be *publicly evaluated*. These are third-person criteria. That is not a criticism — it is a description. Third-person criteria are appropriate for most explanatory contexts. The question is whether they are appropriate for *this* explanatory context, where the phenomenon at issue is defined by its first-person character.

The paper does not argue that only third-person evidence is legitimate. It assumes it. The assumption is widely shared in the sciences and in much of analytic philosophy, and it has powerful pragmatic justification — it is what makes science replicable, what makes medicine possible, what separates inquiry from anecdote. But it is not a neutral starting point. It is a position in epistemology — one that has been contested from Descartes through phenomenology to contemporary acquaintance theorists.[4](#footnote-4)

I want to state this carefully, because it is easy to overstate. I am not claiming the paper smuggles in an assumption in bad faith — the criteria are stated openly. I am not claiming the criteria are wrong — they are productive for many purposes. I am claiming that the dissolution of the hard problem depends on restricting evidence to the third-person kind, and that this restriction is the real locus of disagreement, not the three criteria themselves.

If only third-person evidence counts, the argument wins. The criteria are appropriate, qualia fail them, and the hard problem is dissolved. This is a coherent and defensible position.

If first-person evidence also counts — if the fact that there is something it is like to be me is a datum that any adequate theory must accommodate, not merely acknowledge and set aside — then the hard problem survives the dissolution of the concept. The concept may be poorly formulated. The term “qualia” may be more trouble than it is worth. But the phenomenon remains: the gap between what can be described from outside and what is experienced from inside.

## What direct acquaintance can and cannot do

Saying “first-person evidence counts” isn’t enough. You have to specify what kind of evidence it provides and where its authority stops.

Bertrand Russell introduced the distinction between knowledge by acquaintance and knowledge by description to mark exactly this difference: acquaintance is a direct cognitive relation to its object, not mediated by inference or judgment.[5](#footnote-5) Contemporary acquaintance theorists have developed this line specifically for consciousness. Brie Gertler argues that introspective judgments grounded in acquaintance are “tied directly to their truthmakers” and “depend only on the subject’s conscious states of mind for their justification.”[6](#footnote-6) Richard Fumerton develops the foundationalist case that acquaintance provides non-inferential justification independent of the kind of causal or reportive chains the paper demands.[7](#footnote-7)

When you are in pain, you do not infer that you are in pain from your reports about pain. You know it directly. This knowledge is not mediated by a causal chain that could be interrupted or rerouted. It is, for the subject, the most certain thing there is.

But here the argument must be honest about its limits. A defender of the paper’s position can grant all of this and reply: “I agree that you know you are in pain. I agree that this knowledge is direct and not inferential. But what does this establish about the *explanatory* status of qualia? You know you are in pain — but do you know, by acquaintance, that your pain is a non-functional, non-relational property? Or do you simply know that you are in pain, full stop — which is compatible with pain being a feature of your system’s functional organization?”

That is a serious reply, and acquaintance alone does not defeat it. Acquaintance tells me *that* there is something it is like. It does not tell me *what kind of thing* this something-it-is-like is — whether it is a separate property, an aspect of organization, or something else entirely. The argument against qualia as non-functional, non-relational properties may survive even if acquaintance is granted as epistemically legitimate.

What acquaintance *does* defeat is the conclusion that the hard problem is dissolved. Even after granting everything the paper argues about the concept of qualia, the first-person subject is still left with the fact that no third-person description, however complete, conveys what it is like to be her. This fact does not depend on the concept of qualia. It precedes it. The concept may be a poor attempt to capture it — and the paper may be right about that — but discrediting the concept does not make the fact disappear.

## The gap in the laws of nature

Now here is the part that I haven’t seen anyone else put together in quite this way.

The paper’s strongest argument is its causal dilemma — the structural backbone of the whole case. Either consciousness does something, in which case it is integrated into the physical/functional story and there is no hard problem, or it doesn’t do anything, in which case it cannot epistemically justify belief in itself. You are either causally efficacious or causally inert. Pick one.

There is a third option. I want to describe it carefully, because the consciousness literature is littered with people who overstate their case. What follows is a proof of *possibility*, not a theory. The distinction matters.

Three facts, each uncontroversial on its own:

**One.** Quantum mechanics is indeterministic at the molecular level. This is not a fringe claim about the brain specifically — it is what quantum mechanics says about *all* physical events at the relevant scale. Ion channels are molecular structures. Their state transitions involve individual ions, conformational changes in proteins, and the binding of neurotransmitter molecules to receptors — all processes that bottom out in quantum mechanics. The outcomes of these events are not determined by prior physical states; physics assigns probabilities, not determinate results. This is simply what it means for quantum mechanics to be the correct description of physics at this scale.

**Two.** The brain is a chaotic dynamical system. This is well-established in computational neuroscience.[8](#footnote-8) A small difference at the synaptic level — one ion channel opening instead of staying closed — can be amplified through nonlinear dynamics into a macroscopically different neural trajectory. A different thought. A different decision. A different life.

**Three.** The Heisenberg uncertainty principle prevents an outside observer from determining, even in principle, whether a given quantum outcome at a particular synapse was genuinely random or was selected by some factor operating within the space of permitted outcomes.

Now combine them.

If consciousness acts at quantum decision points — selecting which way an indeterminate molecular event resolves — then chaotic amplification makes those selections consequential. A conscious intention doesn’t need to violate any physical law or move any mountain. It needs to nudge a single molecular event within the range of outcomes that quantum mechanics already permits. The brain’s chaos does the rest, amplifying a micro-level difference into a macro-level consequence.

And no external measurement can ever distinguish this from plain quantum randomness. Not because our instruments aren’t good enough. Because of the *structure of physics itself*. The uncertainty principle guarantees that the boundary between “random” and “chosen within the permitted range” is permanent and impassable from outside.

Note what this argument does *not* require. It does not require quantum coherence across large neural structures of the kind proposed by Hameroff and Penrose, which faces serious decoherence objections.[9](#footnote-9) It does not require that quantum effects stay coherent long enough to perform computation. It requires only that individual molecular events in neurons are quantum-indeterminate — which they are, because all molecular events are — and that the brain’s chaotic dynamics amplify micro-level differences into macro-level consequences. Each component is uncontroversial in its respective field. The philosophical work is in the combination.

The result is a scenario — physically coherent, consistent with everything we know about quantum mechanics and neuroscience — in which consciousness is causally efficacious and no third-person observation can ever detect its contribution. A consciousness that is *distinguishable from the inside and indistinguishable from the outside*. The subject experiences choosing. The outside observer sees stochastic processes producing behavior. Both descriptions are correct from their respective vantage points, and no possible measurement can adjudicate between them.

## What this doesn’t prove

The limitations of this argument must be stated plainly. There are four, and they are significant.

First: this is a proof of possibility, not a theory. I am not claiming that consciousness works this way. I am claiming that the causal dilemma — causal efficacy or causal inertness — is not exhaustive. There exists a third option: causal efficacy that is principally unmeasurable from outside. Whether consciousness actually operates in this manner is an open question I am not attempting to settle.

Second: the proof faces an Occam’s razor problem, and it is serious. From the outside, “quantum randomness plus chaotic amplification” and “consciousness choosing plus chaotic amplification” produce identical observational signatures. The simpler story — plain randomness — explains everything the consciousness story explains, without an additional posit. A defender of the original paper could reply: “You’ve shown me a scenario where your posit produces no explanatory difference from the third-person perspective. That is precisely my point.”

This is a strong reply, and it must be taken seriously. The rebuttal is narrower than one might wish: the randomness-only story is simpler as a *third-person* explanation, but it does not account for the first-person datum. From the inside, my decisions do not feel random. I experience deliberation, choice, the difference between deciding and having something happen to me. Whether this first-person datum is evidence that should figure in our best total theory, or an appearance that the third-person theory is entitled to set aside — that is the question. And that question returns us to the dispute about the scope of evidence that is the real heart of the matter.

Third: there is a further consideration that complicates the parsimony calculus. The wave function of even a modest physical system contains exponentially more information than its classical measurement outcomes reveal. This is not a speculative claim — it is the reason quantum computing is interesting, and why simulating quantum systems on classical computers is intractable. When collapse occurs (or decoherence, depending on interpretation), the vast majority of that information becomes inaccessible to external measurement. Not hidden behind technological limits — inaccessible in principle, by the structure of the theory. Classical observable reality is a thin projection of quantum reality.

This means the space identified in the possibility proof is not a narrow crack in the laws of nature. It is the larger room. The informational content that is in principle inaccessible to third-person observation dwarfs the content that is accessible. The randomness-only interpretation of the possibility proof therefore requires a quiet but substantial commitment: that the overwhelmingly larger portion of physical reality’s information content is doing nothing relevant to consciousness — that the one phenomenon we cannot explain from within the classical, measurable layer has nothing to do with the vast informational space that lies beyond that layer.

That is a possible position. But it is not the *default* position, and it is not obviously the parsimonious one. Parsimony usually favors the story with fewer posits. But it also usually favors the story that does not require declaring most of reality’s information content irrelevant to the question at hand. Here the two senses of parsimony pull in opposite directions, and the Occam’s razor objection loses the clean force it initially appeared to have.

I want to be precise about what this does *not* establish. The inference from “there is a vast informational space that third-person observation cannot reach” to “consciousness operates in that space” is a gap argument — and gap arguments are the weakest form of reasoning in both science and philosophy. The mystery of where all that quantum information “goes” does not by itself support any particular theory of consciousness. What the observation does is neutralise the claim that the randomness-only story is straightforwardly simpler. It isn’t. It purchases its simplicity by ignoring most of the data.

Fourth: even as a proof of possibility, the argument is silent on mechanism. It shows that there is *room* in the laws of nature for consciousness to be causally efficacious and externally undetectable. It does not explain how consciousness would interact with quantum events, what determines which events it influences, or how a unified experience arises from what would presumably be billions of micro-level selections. These are real gaps, not hand-waving. Any future theory that tries to turn this possibility proof into a positive account would have to fill them.

The possibility proof therefore does not defeat the paper’s argument on its own terms. What it does is defeat the *strongest possible* version of that argument — the version that says there is *no physically coherent way* for consciousness to be causally real and yet not be part of the third-person functional story. There is such a way. Whether it is *actual* is a further question. Whether it is *explanatorily superior* depends on whether one admits first-person evidence. But it is *possible*, and that matters, because it means the causal dilemma does not close the door.

## Why this matters now

I started thinking about this because of AI. The consciousness question used to be a philosophical curiosity — interesting at dinner parties, irrelevant to policy. That era is over.

Twenty-five percent of AI researchers in a 2024 survey expected AI consciousness within a decade. David Chalmers told a Princeton panel in March 2025 that there is a significant chance we will have conscious language models within five to ten years. The EU is drafting frameworks. Ethicists are publishing papers on “digital minds.” Eric Schwitzgebel’s forthcoming book warns that we will soon face AI systems claiming consciousness with no way to verify or refute the claim.[10](#footnote-10)

The entire edifice rests on an assumption: that the consciousness question is answerable. That with the right test, the right theory, the right measurement, we can determine whether the machine is conscious and then decide how to treat it. Prove it is conscious — grant rights. Prove it isn’t — treat it as a toaster.

But what if the argument above is correct — not the speculative part about how consciousness might work, but the structural part about what physics permits? What if consciousness is *inherently unmeasurable from outside*? Not because our instruments aren’t good enough, but because the uncertainty principle makes the gap between “random” and “chosen” permanently impassable from the third-person perspective?

Then the consciousness test everyone is waiting for will never arrive. And the policy frameworks built on its expected arrival will need a different foundation — one that doesn’t depend on resolving a question that may be, by the structure of physical law, unresolvable from outside.

I’ve argued elsewhere that such a foundation exists, in a 3,000-year-old legal tradition that already has specific rulings for beings of pure intellect — rulings that hold whether or not the being can feel.[11](#footnote-11) But that is a different argument.

The argument here is narrower and, I think, sufficient on its own terms: the concept of qualia is broken. The ontologization move — the leap from “experience feels a certain way” to “there exists an additional non-physical property” — is unjustified. But discrediting the concept does not dissolve the phenomenon. And the laws of physics contain a gap — not a gap of ignorance, but a structural gap written into quantum mechanics and amplified by the brain’s chaotic dynamics — exactly wide enough for the phenomenon to persist: real from the inside, invisible from the outside. Not a narrow crack, but the larger room — one whose informational content dwarfs everything classical observation can reach.

Whether that gap is occupied is a question that cannot be answered from outside alone.

And that, precisely, is the hard problem.
