# Masechet Gittin - Full Study Report

Nine chapters. Ninety dapim. The tractate that begins with agents carrying documents across the sea and ends with the altar weeping over a first marriage.

---

## Chapter 1: HaMeivi Get (2a-12b)

### The Opening

An agent brings a bill of divorce from overseas to Eretz Yisrael. He must declare: **"Before me it was written and before me it was signed."** Why?

Two explanations drive the entire chapter:

- **Rabba**: People overseas are not expert in the lishmah requirement - they don't know the get must be written specifically for this woman. The declaration certifies proper intent.
- **Rava**: Witnesses aren't available to ratify the document. The declaration substitutes for standard court verification.

The Gemara doesn't choose between them. Both concerns shape the law.

### The Geography

Rabbi Yehuda defines the borders: Rekem to the east, Ashkelon to the south, Akko to the north. Everything beyond is "overseas." Rabbi Meir holds Akko is like Eretz Yisrael for gittin purposes.

### Eilu V'Eilu (6b)

One of the most famous passages in the entire Talmud, embedded here in a discussion about Rabbi Evyatar's reliability. Elijah tells Rabbi Evyatar what God is studying: the concubine of Gibeah. Two sages disagreed about the husband's anger - one said a fly, the other a hair.

What does God say?

**"Both these and these are the words of the living God."** He found a fly and did not take umbrage. He found a hair and took umbrage. The husband's excessive strictness in the home led to catastrophe.

Three practical halakhot about household management on Erev Shabbat follow immediately. The Talmud does not let theology float - it lands in how you run your kitchen on Friday afternoon.

### Single Witness (3a)

A married woman has a presumption of marriage (chezkat eishet ish), normally requiring two witnesses to change. How can a single agent's testimony suffice? Answer: one witness is deemed credible for prohibitions (issur), and the agent delivers the document publicly before two or three people.

### Gentile Courts (9b-10b)

All documents from gentile courts are valid - **except** bills of divorce and manumission. Commercial documents work because the acquisition happens through money, not the document. But personal status changes (married to single, slave to free) are internal to the Jewish legal system.

**Shmuel's principle: Dina d'malkhuta dina** - the law of the kingdom is the law. Jewish law recognizes civil authority for commercial matters. The boundary is functional: property relations (social conventions) vs. personal identity (covenantal).

### Principles Established

1. **Lishmah** - a get must be written with specific intent for the named parties (Torah-level)
2. **Befanai nikhtav u-befanai nechtam** - the agent's declaration as witness substitute
3. **Single witness credibility for prohibitions**
4. **Dina d'malkhuta dina** - civil law recognized for commercial matters
5. **Divorce and manumission as parallel institutions** - shared rules reflect shared structure

## Chapter 2: HaMeivi Get MiMedinat HaYam (13a-20a)

### The Document Itself

The chapter works through what a get is made of - physically and formally.

**Materials for writing**: Ink, paint, red dye, tree resin, iron sulfate - anything permanent. Fruit juice, other liquids - anything impermanent: invalid. The get must outlast the moment of its delivery.

**Writing surfaces**: Anything - olive leaf, cow horn, a slave's hand - provided it's transferred to the woman. Rabbi Yosi HaGalili prohibits writing on living things or food.

**Carving vs. writing** (20a): Carving letters into a surface counts as writing. Carving away surrounding material to leave raised letters does not. Positive vs. negative inscription - a distinction that appears throughout Shabbat, tefillin, and mezuzot.

### Who Can Write a Get

Anyone - women, minors, even deaf-mutes. **"Ratification of a bill of divorce is only through its signatories."** The writer's competence is irrelevant; only the signing witnesses matter. But only competent adults may serve as delivery agents.

### Why Dating Matters (17a)

- **Rabbi Yochanan**: To prevent a man from backdating a get to cover for a relative's infidelity.
- **Reish Lakish**: To protect the husband's rights to his wife's field produce until the actual date of divorce.

Both views: precise dating protects vulnerable parties.

### Edei Chatimah vs. Edei Mesirah (22b)

A foundational machloket:

- **Rabbi Meir**: The witnesses who **sign** the get effect the divorce.
- **Rabbi Elazar**: The witnesses who observe the **transmission** effect the divorce.

Under Rabbi Elazar, the document itself is less critical - what matters is that qualified witnesses saw the get change hands. Under Rabbi Meir, the signatures are everything. This dispute echoes through every chapter that follows.

### Illiterate Witnesses (19a)

Witnesses who cannot write: write names in temporary medium, let witnesses trace over with permanent ink. This leniency applies **only to gittin**, because concern for deserted wives (agunot) overrides normal strictness. Someone who allowed tracing on financial documents was flogged.

### Agency Competence

Must exist at appointment and at delivery. Gaps in between don't invalidate. A minor who was appointed, reached majority, then delivered - invalid (incompetent at appointment). The reverse - competent, temporarily incapacitated, competent again - valid.

## Chapter 3: Kol HaGet (21a-32a)

### Lishmah in Action

**"Any bill of divorce not written for the sake of a specific woman is invalid."** Four cases:

1. A man overhears scribes dictating a template matching his and his wife's names - invalid (coincidental).
2. A man wrote a get, reconsidered; someone with identical names wants it - invalid.
3. Two wives with the same name - a get for one cannot serve the other.
4. Man tells scribe to write, he'll decide which wife later - invalid for both.

**Templates**: Scribes may prepare standardized boilerplate, leaving names and date blank. The specific details must be written lishmah. The boilerplate may be pre-written. The resolution: pure formalism (every letter intentional) meets institutional reality (scribes handle volume). The boundary is found, not abolished.

### Chezkat Chaim - The Presumption of Life (28a)

Jewish law presumes life. A person is presumed alive until proven dead. No upper age limit. Abaye refutes Rava's attempt to limit this presumption to under eighty: a baraita permits delivery of a get "even one hundred years old."

Three categories of danger create presumptions:

| Danger | Presumption |
|--------|-------------|
| Besieged city | Alive |
| Ship in distress | Alive |
| Led out for trial | Alive |

But once actualized (city conquered, ship lost, execution begun) - **the stringencies of both living and dead apply**. A priest's wife in a conquered city: cannot eat terumah (maybe he's dead), cannot remarry (maybe he's alive). The system holds both possibilities simultaneously.

### Sub-Agency (29a)

"Deliver this get" - the agent may delegate. "**You** deliver this get" - no delegation. The husband's intent regarding the agent's identity is legally operative.

### Rabban Gamliel's Enactment (32a)

The bridge to Chapter 4's tikun ha'olam legislation. Originally a husband could void a get by convening a court elsewhere. The wife, unaware, considers herself divorced, remarries, and her children are mamzerim.

Rabban Gamliel the Elder enacted: you cannot void a get before a court not in the wife's presence. **Mipnei tikun ha'olam.**

A sharp distinction: "It is void" or "I do not desire it" - cancels the get (performative speech). "It is invalid" or "it is not a get" - has no effect (mere factual claim). The difference between speech that changes reality and speech that merely describes it.

## Chapter 4: HaShole'ach (32a-45a)

### The Tikun Ha'Olam Chapters Begin

From here through Chapter 5, the phrase *mipnei tikun ha'olam* recurs as justification for enactment after enactment. The Sages are not deriving law from text. They are diagnosing systemic failure and patching the system.

### The Explosive Passage (33a)

Can a rabbinic enactment override Torah law? The husband has a Torah-level right to void his get. How can the Sages take that away?

> **Anyone who betroths a woman betroths her contingent upon the will of the Sages, and when one fails to conform to their will the Sages expropriated his betrothal from him retroactively.**

Every marriage operates *al da'at hakhamim* - subject to rabbinic authorization. The Sages can retroactively annul a marriage. Even betrothal through intercourse - the Sages declared the intercourse licentious retroactively. The entire institution of kiddushin rests on rabbinic authority.

### Hillel's Prozbul (34b, 36a-37a)

The single most discussed tikun ha'olam enactment in Jewish legal history.

**The problem**: The Torah cancels debts in the seventh year (shemittah). People stopped lending as the seventh year approached. A law designed to protect the poor was destroying them.

**The Torah itself anticipated this**: "Beware that there be not a base thought in your heart, saying: The seventh year is at hand; and your eye be evil against your needy brother" (Deut. 15:9). People were doing it anyway.

**The mechanism**: A document transferring debt-collection rights to a court. Court debts aren't cancelled by shemittah. Legal fiction that preserves the loan.

**How can Hillel override Torah?** Abaye: debt cancellation in the current era (no Jubilee) is itself only rabbinic. Hillel overrides rabbinic law with rabbinic law.

**The authority**: Hefker beit din hefker - the court can declare property ownerless. Rav Nahman would even deem a prozbul valid retroactively.

**Who benefits?** Rava: *bulei* (the wealthy) because their loans are protected. *Butei* (the poor) because they can still find lenders.

**Land requirement**: The debtor must own some land. How much? "Even the amount sufficient to grow a stalk of cabbage." A perforated pot on the ground counts.

**A moment of self-doubt (37a)**: Rabbi Yohanan was asked about a halakhic ruling. He was challenged with his own prior statement. His response: "Because we think this should be the halakha, should we perform an action based on this?" Even the greatest authority distinguishes theoretical halakha from practical ruling.

### Rav's Fish (36a)

Rav drew a fish instead of signing his name. Rabbi Hanina drew a palm branch. Rav Hisda drew the letter samekh. Rabba bar Rav Huna drew a sail. Why was this acceptable? "The Sages are different, as everyone is well versed in their pictorial marks."

### Emancipation of Slaves (38b-45a)

**The half-slave problem** (41b): Two partners own a slave; one frees his half. The slave cannot marry a maidservant (half-free) or a free woman (half-slave). Beit Shammai: the master must free him, because "the world was created only for procreation." Beit Hillel initially disagreed but retracted.

**Selling slaves to gentiles** (44a-45a): Forbidden. If done, the slave goes free (penalty on the seller). Abaye asks why penalize the buyer. Rav Yosef: "It is not the mouse that steals, but the hole that steals."

### Captive Redemption (45a-45b)

**Captives are not redeemed for more than their value.** Two reasons:

1. Financial burden on the community
2. Incentive structure - overpaying encourages more kidnapping

Levi bar Darga redeemed his daughter for 13,000 gold dinars. Abaye: "Who says he acted in accordance with the Sages?"

**One may not aid captives to escape** - for the betterment of the captives (Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel). The remaining captives face harsher treatment. If only one captive? Different analysis.

## Chapter 5: HaNizakin (45a-55b)

### The Land Quality Hierarchy (49a-50a)

Three tiers:

| Collection | Land Quality | Why |
|-----------|-------------|-----|
| Damages | Superior (idith) | Deterrence - "Why should I rob? The court will take my finest field" |
| Debts | Intermediate (beinonit) | Prevent predatory lending - "I'll lend to him so I can collect that fine field" |
| Ketubah | Inferior (ziburit) | "More than a man wants to marry, a woman wants to be married" |

Incentive design, not abstract justice. Each tier calibrated to human behavior.

### Guardians for Orphans (52a)

**May do**: Tithe produce, sell property for sustenance, make items for mitzvot, separate tithes for consumption.

**May NOT do**: Give charity from orphans' funds, redeem captives with their property, free their slaves, exchange fields (even advantageously).

**Shmuel**: "Orphans' property is like consecrated property."

**Rabbi Meir's dream** (52a): A guardian was selling orphans' land improperly. Rabbi Meir stopped him. In a dream: "I wish to destroy and you build?" - God wanted the orphans to lose money. Rabbi Meir's response: **"Words appearing in dreams do not bring up and do not take down."** He ignored the dream and followed the law.

**Satan and peace** (52a): Two people fought every Erev Shabbat. Rabbi Meir made peace between them for three weeks. He heard Satan say: "Woe, that Rabbi Meir removed that man from his house." Even the accuser mourns when peace is made.

### Non-Physical Damage (53a)

**Hizkiyya**: Damage that changes halakhic status without physical alteration is categorized as damage. Liable regardless of intent.

**Rabbi Yochanan**: Not actionable by Torah law. Rabbinic fines apply only for intentional acts. Deter malice, protect accidental violators.

### The Ordinance for the Penitent (55a)

A stolen beam built into a large building - the victim receives monetary value, not the beam itself. By Torah law, return the stolen item. But requiring demolition of an entire building to extract one beam discourages repentance. This is *takanat hashavim* - removing barriers to teshuvah.

### Darkhei Shalom Enactments (55b)

A second legislative category alongside tikun ha'olam:

- A priest reads Torah first, then Levite, then Israelite
- Eruv placed in the same house as before (prevent disputes)
- Cistern nearest a water channel fills first
- Interference with another's traps is robbery *mipnei darkhei shalom*

### The Law of Sicarii (55b)

Armed bandits extorted land under threat of death. Three phases of Roman decrees:

1. Kill a Jew or be killed - genuine transfers (owner gave up hope)
2. Kill a Jew, pay 4 dinars - still dangerous
3. Kill a Jew, be killed - owner thinks "I'll get it back in court" - coerced and invalid

Later courts required purchasers from sicarii to compensate original owners one-quarter of property value.

## Chapter 6: HaMeivi Get B'Eretz Yisrael (55b-67a)

### Agency for Delivering Gets

Receipt agent (shaliach l'kabbalah) vs. delivery agent (shaliach l'holakhah). The receipt agent **is** the wife for legal purposes - divorce takes effect the moment the agent receives it, and the husband cannot retract. The delivery agent is a courier. The distinction is enormous.

### The Voice from the Pit (66a)

A man thrown into a pit, believing he will die, calls out to write a get for his wife. Those who hear should write and deliver it - even without seeing him. But what if the voice is from a demon? Rabbi Hanina's test: check for "the shadow of a shadow" - demons don't cast one.

### THE DESTRUCTION NARRATIVE (55b-58a)

The Talmud's most extensive account of Jerusalem's fall. Not history - theology of catastrophe.

**The bridge** (55b): "Happy is the man who fears always, but he who hardens his heart shall fall into mischief" (Proverbs 28:14). Three destructions caused by absurdly small things:

- Jerusalem - Kamtza and Bar Kamtza
- The King's Mountain - a rooster and a hen
- Beitar - a shaft from a chariot

### Kamtza and Bar Kamtza (55b-56a)

A man sent his servant to invite Kamtza, his friend. The servant brought Bar Kamtza, his enemy. Bar Kamtza, at the feast, pleaded not to be humiliated:

- "Let me stay, I'll pay for what I eat and drink." - No.
- "I'll pay for half the feast." - No.
- "I'll pay for the entire feast." - No.

The host threw him out.

**The silence of the Sages**: Bar Kamtza saw the Sages sitting there and not protesting. He concluded they approved. He went to Rome.

**The blemished offering**: Rome sent a choice calf. Bar Kamtza blemished its upper lip - disqualifying under Jewish law but irrelevant under Roman standards.

**The fatal impasse**: Sacrifice it anyway for peace? Rabbi Zekharya ben Avkolas: "People will say blemished animals may be offered." Kill Bar Kamtza? "People will say one who blemishes sacrifices is executed."

They did nothing.

**Rabbi Yohanan**: "The excessive humility (anvetanut) of Rabbi Zekharya ben Avkolas destroyed our Temple, burned our Sanctuary, and exiled us from our land."

Not arrogance. Not cruelty. *Humility*. An excess of caution that could not make a hard decision. He saw every possible negative consequence of action and none of the consequences of inaction.

### The Siege (56a)

Three wealthy men - Nakdimon ben Guryon, ben Kalba Savua, ben Tzitzit HaKesat - provisioned Jerusalem for twenty-one years. The Zealots burned the storehouses to force a military confrontation.

**Marta bat Baitos**: A woman of immense wealth. She sends servants for flour: fine flour gone, ordinary flour gone, coarse flour gone, barley flour gone. She goes out herself, steps on dung, dies. As she dies, she throws her gold and silver into the street: "Why do I need this?"

### Rabban Yohanan ben Zakkai (56a-56b)

Smuggled out of Jerusalem in a coffin with help from his nephew Abba Sikkara (a Zealot leader).

Before Vespasian, he greets him as "king." Vespasian objects: "I'm not a king." A messenger arrives: the emperor has died. Vespasian is Caesar.

**The shoe**: Vespasian's foot swelled from the good news. He couldn't get his shoe on. Rabbi Yohanan quoted: "Good tidings make the bone fat" (Prov. 15:30). Have someone you dislike pass before you. It worked.

**The barrel and the snake**: Vespasian: "If a snake coils around a barrel, you break the barrel to kill the snake." Justifying destroying Jerusalem. Rabbi Yohanan should have answered: "Take tongs and remove the snake without breaking the barrel." He didn't. Rabbi Akiva applied the verse: "He turns the wise backward" (Isaiah 44:25).

**The three requests**:

1. Give me Yavneh and its sages
2. The dynasty of Rabban Gamliel
3. Doctors to heal Rabbi Tzadok (who fasted forty years to avert the destruction)

The Gemara critiques: he should have asked for Jerusalem itself. But he judged that Vespasian would not do that much, and partial salvation was better than none.

### Titus (56b)

Entered the Holy of Holies with a prostitute. Spread a Torah scroll, sinned upon it. Slashed the curtain. Blood flowed. He thought he'd killed God.

> "Who is like You among the gods (elim)?" Read: "Who is like You among the mute (ilmim)?"

God's response was silence. And then a gnat. Which tormented Titus for seven years until it grew to the size of a bird.

### Nero (57a)

Shot arrows in all four directions - all pointed toward Jerusalem. Asked a Jewish child to recite a verse. The child said: "I will lay my vengeance upon Edom by the hand of my people Israel" (Ezekiel 25:14). Nero understood: God would exact vengeance on whoever destroyed the Temple. **Nero fled and converted.** Rabbi Meir was his descendant.

### The Captive Children (57b)

Four hundred boys and girls taken for immoral purposes. They asked: "If we drown ourselves, will we merit the World to Come?" The eldest cited a verse proving they would. They jumped into the sea. The girls reasoned a fortiori and followed.

### The High Priest's Children (58a)

Son and daughter, enslaved separately, brought together by their masters. In the dark, they wept all night. At dawn, they recognized each other. "They wept until their souls departed."

## Chapter 7: Mi SheAchazo (67a-77a)

### Conditional Divorce

A man seized by illness. How does incapacity affect his ability to issue a get?

**Kordeyakos** (67b): Temporary insanity (Shmuel identifies it as intoxication from new wine). His words during the episode are legally meaningless. But if a lucid person says "write a get," then becomes afflicted and retracts - the retraction is invalid. You cannot retract in a state the law doesn't recognize.

**"If I die"** - does not take effect (a dead man cannot divorce). **"From today if I die"** - valid (the get took effect retroactively while he was alive). The distinction between these two formulations generates enormous complexity about timing, retroactivity, and the woman's status in the interim.

### The Bnei Gad/Bnei Reuven Paradigm (75a)

Rules for valid conditions derive from Moses's condition to the tribes (Numbers 32):

1. **Compounded** - stated in both positive and negative form
2. **Condition precedes the action** - "if X" before "this is your get"
3. **Condition must be possible** - impossible conditions void the get
4. **Geographic precision** - Antipatris (Judea-Galilee border), Kefar Otnai, Akko (Israel-overseas)

### Medical Remedies (67b-69b)

One of the longest medical sections in the Talmud. Not systematic pharmacology - folk medicine, sympathetic magic, and practical observation:

- Eye disease: seven-colored scorpion, dried, ground with stibium
- Night blindness: tie patient's leg to a dog's leg with a hair rope
- Day blindness: roast seven animal spleens on a bloodletter's shard
- Nosebleed: write names backwards
- Lung bleeding test: wheat straw - if blood sticks, from the lungs (treatable). If not, from the liver (untreatable).

The legal question framing this section: what counts as competent mental state for issuing a get? The medical material contextualizes what "illness" means.

### King Solomon and Ashmedai (68a-68b)

Solomon needed to build the Temple without iron tools. He needed the shamir - a creature that could cut through anything. The Sages told him: capture demons, they may know.

**Capturing Ashmedai**: Benayahu found the demon king's sealed well, drained the water, refilled it with wine. Ashmedai returned, quoted Proverbs against drinking - then, overcome by thirst, drank and passed out. Chained with the Divine Name.

**On the journey to Jerusalem**, Ashmedai:

- Guided a blind man back to the road (completely righteous - heavenly merit)
- Guided a drunk (completely wicked - consuming his reward in this world)
- **Wept at a wedding** (the groom would die within thirty days)
- **Laughed at seven-year shoes** (the man had seven days to live)
- **Laughed at a sorcerer** (sitting on a buried treasure he couldn't perceive)

The demon sees through surfaces. The gap between what people believe about their situation and what is actually true.

**Solomon's downfall**: "What makes you greater than us?" Ashmedai asked. Solomon removed the chain and gave him his ring. Ashmedai grew until one wing touched heaven and one touched earth, and hurled Solomon four hundred parasangs away. Assumed the throne.

Solomon went door to door: "I, Kohelet, was king over Israel in Jerusalem." No one believed him.

**Detection**: The Sanhedrin grew suspicious. The impostor demanded relations during menstruation and approached Batsheva. They returned Solomon's ring. Ashmedai fled.

The king who wrote Proverbs about wine being a mocker trapped the demon with wine - and was undone by his own curiosity about the demon's power.

## Chapter 8: HaZorek Get (77a-83a)

### Throwing a Get

When a man throws a get to his wife: if it lands in her house or courtyard, she is divorced. Her domain receives on her behalf.

**The great conceptual move** (77b): A married woman's hand technically "belongs" to her husband (he has rights to her earnings). How can she acquire her own get? Rava: **"Her bill of divorce and her hand enter her possession simultaneously."** The document of freedom creates the capacity to receive the document of freedom. The same logic extends to her courtyard.

### Domain Analysis (78a-79a)

Her bed, her lap, her basket - all her personal domains even within his house. Rabbi Yochanan: "the place of her lap belongs to her."

If the husband holds a string attached to the get while it's in her hand - not divorced, unless he can't pull it back. Severance must be complete.

Once the get enters her domain's airspace, she is divorced - **even if the writing is erased or the document burns before reaching her.**

### "Permitted Except..." (82a-83a)

A husband delivers a get: "You are permitted to marry any man except [a specific person]." Rabbi Eliezer validates. The Rabbis prohibit.

After Rabbi Eliezer's death, four Sages demolished his position:

1. **Rabbi Tarfon**: Could prevent levirate marriage, uprooting Torah law
2. **Rabbi Yosei HaGelili**: "What is forbidden is forbidden to everyone; what is permitted is permitted to everyone"
3. **Rabbi Elazar ben Azarya**: **Keritut** (severance) must completely sever the bond. A conditional divorce fails this.
4. **Rabbi Akiva**: The divorce becomes retroactively void if she marries the forbidden person

Rava: Rabbi Elazar ben Azarya's argument was uniquely irrefutable. The concept of *keritut* - complete severance - becomes the controlling principle. A get that leaves any thread of connection is not a get.

## Chapter 9: HaMegaresh (83a-90b)

### The Essential Text (85b-86a)

The core language of the get: **"And this that you shall have from me is a scroll of divorce, and a letter of leave, and a bill of dismissal, to go to marry any man that you wish."**

Does "You are hereby your own" work? Yes - if a slave acquires autonomy through these words, how much more so a wife, whose body was never acquired by her husband. A master owns a slave's person. A husband has rights to a wife's labor but never owned her person.

### Coerced Gets (88a-89a)

A get compelled by a Jewish court is valid. By gentiles, invalid. But gentiles beating the husband at Jewish court direction ("Do what the Jews tell you") - valid.

Shmuel's framework:

| Compulsion | Status |
|-----------|--------|
| Jewish, lawful | Fully valid |
| Jewish, unlawful | Invalid, but disqualifies from priesthood |
| Gentile, at beit din's direction | Torah-valid, but Sages invalidated it |
| Gentile, unlawful | No validity at all |

### THE FINAL MISHNA: GROUNDS FOR DIVORCE (90a-90b)

Three readings of Deuteronomy 24:1 - "Because he has found some unseemly matter (ervat davar) in her":

**Beit Shammai: Only for sexual immorality (dvar ervah).** The word "ervah" controls. Marriage is a sacred covenant. Only its fundamental violation justifies dissolution.

**Beit Hillel: Even if she burned his food (dvar).** "Ervah" and "davar" are two independent terms. Any "matter" suffices. Marriage is a partnership with real expectations. When those expectations are consistently unmet, the partnership can be dissolved.

**Rabbi Akiva: Even if he found someone more beautiful.** He reads "if she finds no favor in his eyes." The husband's subjective experience is the operative criterion. When desire ends, the marriage has already ended in fact. The get merely recognizes what the heart decided.

**Rabbi Meir's analogy**: Three attitudes toward food. Examining carefully, rejecting only what's spoiled (Beit Shammai). Rejecting what's poorly prepared (Beit Hillel). Moving to whatever looks better (Rabbi Akiva).

The halakha follows Beit Hillel.

### The Altar Weeps (90b)

Two statements:

- **Rabbi Yehuda**: "If you hate your wife, divorce her."
- **Rabbi Yochanan**: "One who divorces is hated by God."

They don't disagree. Rabbi Yehuda speaks of a second marriage (better to divorce than live in hatred). Rabbi Yochanan speaks of a first marriage.

Then Rabbi Elazar:

> **"Anyone who divorces his first wife - even the altar sheds tears over him."**

Citing Malachi 2:13-14: "The Lord has been witness between you and the wife of your youth, against whom you have dealt treacherously, though she is your companion and the wife of your covenant."

The altar - the junction point between heaven and earth - weeps when a first marriage is dissolved. Not because divorce is always wrong. The Mishna just spent nine chapters establishing when and how it is permitted. But because the covenant of youth has a weight that even valid legal dissolution cannot erase.

**The altar weeps. The get is still valid.**

## The Architecture of the Masechet

### The Arc

Gittin moves from the technical to the theological through a precise architecture:

| Chapters | Content | Movement |
|----------|---------|----------|
| 1-3 | Agency, documents, formalism | How does divorce travel across distance? |
| 4-5 | Tikun ha'olam enactments | What breaks when correct law meets actual behavior? |
| 6 | The destruction narrative | What breaks when the Sages stop acting? |
| 7 | Conditional gets, Ashmedai | What is certain? What depends on what hasn't happened yet? |
| 8-9 | Domains, grounds, the altar | What is severance? When is it justified? |

### The Pattern of Tikun Ha'Olam

Every enactment in chapters 4-5 follows the same logic:

1. A technically correct legal rule exists
2. Human behavior in response creates systemic damage
3. The Sages legislate a workaround

This is engineering, not idealism. Not "what would be perfect?" but "given how people actually behave, what rule produces the least damage?"

The prozbul is the test case: when the law as written produces the opposite of its intended effect - when the poor can't get loans because of a law designed to protect the poor - the law must be bent. The principle remains; the mechanism adapts.

### The Destruction as Counter-Case

The tikun ha'olam chapters show the Sages at their best: diagnosing systemic failure and legislating to prevent it. The destruction narrative shows what happens when that capacity fails.

- The host's cruelty was private sin; the Sages' silence made it public
- Rabbi Zekharya applied second-order thinking (what will people say?) toward paralysis instead of action
- The same faculty that produced the prozbul, when turned to excessive caution, produced the destruction

*Tikun ha'olam* and *anvetanut* are the same muscle used in opposite directions.

### Three Theories of Marriage (90a)

The three-way machloket at the end is three theories of what marriage is:

- **Beit Shammai**: Covenant. Only its fundamental violation dissolves it.
- **Beit Hillel**: Partnership. When expectations are unmet, it can be dissolved.
- **Rabbi Akiva**: Desire. When desire ends, the marriage has already ended.

The halakha follows Beit Hillel. But the masechet doesn't let Beit Hillel win cleanly. It immediately follows the ruling with the altar's tears. You may divorce for burnt food. The altar still weeps.

The closing verse uses "berit" (covenant) for marriage - the same word for Sinai, for Abraham. And yet the Torah itself provides the mechanism for dissolving it.

### What Ashmedai Saw

The demon saw what humans could not: the groom would die in thirty days, the shoe-buyer had seven days left, the sorcerer sat on treasure he couldn't perceive. The destruction narrative runs on the same principle: everyone at the feast saw a social embarrassment. The Talmud saw the destruction of Jerusalem.

Masechet Gittin is nine chapters of taking dissolution seriously: technically, legally, procedurally, socially, and - in its final lines - theologically.

The first date never ends. The altar weeps. Both are true.
