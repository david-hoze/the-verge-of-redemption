# Masechet Gittin, Chapters 4-5: The Tikun Ha'Olam Chapters

## Overview

Chapters 4-5 of Gittin (32a-55b) are called the "tikun ha'olam" chapters because the phrase "mipnei tikun ha'olam" (for the betterment of the world) appears repeatedly as the justification for rabbinic enactments. These chapters represent something remarkable in Jewish law: the Sages legislating not from textual derivation but from systemic concern - what happens when technically correct law produces broken outcomes.

---

## CHAPTER 4: HaShole'ach (The One Who Sends) - 32a-49b

### Opening Mishna (32a)

> In the case of one who sends a bill of divorce to his wife with an agent, and he reached the agent, or where he sent another agent after him, and he said to the agent delivering the bill of divorce: The bill of divorce that I gave you, it is void, then this bill of divorce is hereby void. Similarly, if the husband reached his wife before the bill of divorce reached her... However, if he stated this once the bill of divorce had entered her possession, he can no longer render it void.

> Initially, a husband who wished to render the bill of divorce void would convene a court elsewhere and render the bill of divorce void in the presence of the court before it reached his wife. **Rabban Gamliel the Elder instituted an ordinance that one should not do this, for the betterment of the world.**

This is the first tikun ha'olam enactment in the chapter, and it sets the pattern: technically valid law (a husband can void a get before delivery) creates a systemic danger (women unknowingly still married who then remarry, producing mamzerim).

### Key Sugyot and Tikun Ha'Olam Enactments

---

### 1. VOIDING A GET IN ABSENTIA (32a-33b)

**The problem:** A husband sends a get via agent, then convenes a court in another city and declares the get void. The wife does not know. She remarries. Her children from the second marriage are mamzerim (products of adultery).

**The enactment:** Rabban Gamliel the Elder: You cannot void a get before a court that is not in the presence of your wife or her agent.

**The Gemara's debate (33a):**
- Rabbi Yohanan says: The reason is **for the benefit of potential mamzerim** - she might remarry not knowing the get was voided
- Reish Lakish says: **For the betterment of deserted wives** - women who received gittin by agent would be afraid to remarry, fearing the husband voided it somewhere

The underlying dispute: R. Yohanan holds (like Rav Nahman) you can void a get before just 2 people (low publicity, she will not hear). Reish Lakish holds (like Rav Sheshet) you need 3 people (generates publicity, she would hear - so no mamzer risk, but she would be afraid to marry).

**Critical passage (33a):** When the Gemara asks "Can a rabbinic enactment override Torah law?":

> Anyone who betroths a woman betroths her contingent upon the will of the Sages, and when one fails to conform to their will the Sages expropriated his betrothal from him retroactively.

This is one of the most radical statements in the Talmud - the entire institution of kiddushin exists "al da'at hakhamim" (contingent on the Sages' will). Ravina challenges: what about betrothal via intercourse? Answer: The Sages declared his intercourse to be licentious - retroactively.

---

### 2. WRITING NAMES IN A GET (34b)

**The problem:** People move between cities and are known by different names. A get written with only the local name might be contested elsewhere.

**The enactment:** Rabban Gamliel the Elder: Write "The man so-and-so, and any other name that he has; the woman so-and-so, and any other name that she has."

**For the betterment of the world** - so no one can contest the validity of the divorce.

---

### 3. WIDOW'S OATH FOR KETUBAH COLLECTION (34b)

**The problem:** A widow collects her ketubah from orphans only via oath. But courts stopped administering oaths (apparently the oath-taking was causing spiritual harm).

**The enactment:** Rabban Gamliel the Elder: She takes a vow instead of an oath (e.g., "All produce be forbidden to me if I already received payment"), and then collects her ketubah.

**For the benefit of the orphans** - who need a mechanism to ensure the widow has not already been paid.

---

### 4. WITNESSES SIGNING GITTIN (36a)

**The problem:** According to Rabbi Elazar, witnesses to the *transmission* of the get effect the divorce, not signatories. But what if those witnesses die or travel overseas?

**The enactment:** The Sages instituted that witnesses sign their names on the get, even though by Torah law (per R. Elazar) signatures are not required.

**For the betterment of the world** - so the get can be authenticated later.

**Sub-enactment (Rabban Gamliel, 36a):** Initially witnesses would just sign "I, so-and-so, signed as witness" without full names. If no matching signature existed elsewhere, the get could not be ratified.

> Rabban Gamliel said: They instituted a great ordinance that the witnesses must specify their full names on bills of divorce, stating that they are so-and-so, son of so-and-so, and other identifying features, for the betterment of the world.

**Delightful detail (36a):** The Gemara asks about pictorial marks as signatures:
> Rav drew a fish instead of a signature, and Rabbi Hanina drew a palm branch; Rav Hisda drew the letter samekh, and Rav Hoshaya drew the letter ayin; and Rabba bar Rav Huna drew a sail.

The answer: "The Sages are different, as everyone is well versed in their pictorial marks." They first used their marks on letters (no legal requirement), and once known, used them on legal documents.

---

### 5. HILLEL'S PROZBUL (34b, 36a-37a) - THE BIG ONE

**The Mishna (34b):**
> Hillel instituted a document that prevents the Sabbatical Year from abrogating an outstanding debt [prosbol] for the betterment of the world.

**The problem (36a):**
> He saw that the people of the nation were refraining from lending to one another around the time of the Sabbatical Year, as they were concerned that the debtor would not repay the loan, and they violated that which is written in the Torah: "Beware that there be not a base thought in your heart, saying: The seventh year, the year of release, is at hand; and your eye be evil against your needy brother, and you give him nothing" (Deuteronomy 15:9).

The Torah itself warns against this exact behavior. People were doing it anyway. Hillel's move: create a legal mechanism that transfers the debt to the court, which the Sabbatical year does not cancel.

**The text of the prozbul (36a):**
> I transfer to you, so-and-so the judges, who are in such and such a place, so that I will collect any debt that I am owed by so-and-so whenever I wish.

**The explosive question (36a):**
> But is there anything like this, where by Torah law the Sabbatical Year cancels the debt but Hillel instituted that it does not cancel the debt?

**The answer - Abaye:** The prozbul applies to the Sabbatical Year *in the present era*, following Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi's position:

> At a time when you release land (Jubilee), you abrogate monetary debts; at a time when you do not release land (no Jubilee in current era), you also do not abrogate monetary debts.

So the cancellation of debts in the current era is itself only rabbinic - and Hillel can override a rabbinic law. The prozbul is rabbinic authority overriding rabbinic law, not Torah law. (Though the Gemara explores this tension carefully.)

**Etymology of "prozbul" (37a):**
> Rava said to a foreigner who spoke Greek: What is the meaning of the word prosbol? He said to him: It means the institution [pursa] of a matter.

> Bulei - these are the wealthy... Butei - these are the poor... Therefore, the prosbol was instituted both for the sake of the wealthy, so that the loans they would give to the poor person would not be canceled, and for the sake of the poor, so that they would continue to find those willing to lend them money.

**Technical requirements:** The debtor must own some land for a prozbul to be written. How much? "Even the amount of land sufficient to grow a stalk of cabbage" (Rav Hiyya bar Ashi). Even a perforated pot on the ground counts (Hillel in Tosefta). Rav Ashi would transfer a stump of a palm tree to the borrower.

**An extraordinary passage - R. Yohanan's ambivalence (37a):**
Rabbi Asi's relative had a promissory note with a property guarantee. He asked R. Yohanan: Does shemittah cancel this? R. Yohanan said yes. Rabbi Asi challenged: But you yourself said it does not! R. Yohanan: "Because we think that this should be the halakha, should we perform an action based on this?" Even R. Yohanan distinguishes between theoretical halakha and practical ruling.

### 6. EMANCIPATION OF SLAVES - MULTIPLE ENACTMENTS (38b-45a)

**Principle (38b, Shmuel):** Anyone who emancipates his Canaanite slave violates the positive mitzva of "Of them may you take your bondmen forever" (Lev. 25:46).

**Exception:** For a mitzva purpose, it is permitted. Example: Rabbi Eliezer freed his slave to complete a minyan of ten.

**Half-slave, half-free (41b) - Mishna:**
A slave owned by two partners, one of whom freed his half:

> It is not possible for him to marry a maidservant, as he is half-free. It is also not possible for him to marry a free woman, as he is still a half-slave. If you say he should be idle and not marry, but isn't it true that the world was created only for procreation, as it is stated: "He did not create it to be a waste; He formed it to be inhabited" (Isaiah 45:18)? Rather, **for the betterment of the world** his master is forced to make him a freeman.

Beit Hillel initially disagreed with Beit Shammai on this but ultimately retracted to agree.

**Selling slaves to gentiles or outside Eretz Yisrael (45a):**
If one sells a slave to a gentile, the slave goes free (penalty on the seller). If sold to a Jew outside Eretz Yisrael, the purchaser must free him (penalty on the buyer).

Abaye asks: why penalize the buyer? Rav Yosef: "It is not the mouse that steals, but the hole that steals" - the buyer enabled it.

**Slave who flees to Eretz Yisrael (45a):**
Based on "You shall not deliver to his master a slave who escaped to you" (Deut. 23:16) - R. Ahai son of R. Yoshiya interprets this as a slave who flees from outside Israel to Israel. He is not returned. His master receives a promissory note for his value and must write a bill of manumission.

---

### 7. CAPTIVE REDEMPTION (45a)

**Mishna:**
> The captives are not redeemed for more than their actual monetary value, for the betterment of the world; and one may not aid the captives in their attempt to escape, for the betterment of the world. Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel says: For the betterment of the captives.

**Two possible reasons for the price cap:**
1. Financial pressure on the community (cannot sustain inflated ransoms)
2. Incentive structure - do not encourage more kidnapping

The Gemara notes Levi bar Darga redeemed his daughter for 13,000 gold dinars. Abaye: "Who says he acted in accordance with the Sages?"

**The escape prohibition:** First tanna says it is about not incentivizing harsher treatment of captives generally. R. Shimon ben Gamliel says it is about protecting the specific remaining captives. Practical difference: if there is only one captive.

---

### 8. PURCHASING TORAH SCROLLS FROM GENTILES (45a)

**Mishna:**
> Torah scrolls, phylacteries, or mezuzot are not purchased from the gentiles when they acquire these objects, if they request more than their actual monetary value.

Same logic as captive redemption - do not incentivize theft of sacred objects.

---

### 9. DAMAGES COLLECTION TIERS (49b) - Beginning of Chapter 5 Material

**Mishna:**
- Damages: collected from superior-quality land (idith)
- Debts: collected from intermediate-quality land (beinonit)
- Ketubah: collected from inferior-quality land (ziburit)

**Rabbi Shimon's reasoning (49b):**

> For what reason did the Sages say that the court appraises land of superior quality for payment to injured parties? So that a person will say: Why should I rob and why should I take by force? Tomorrow the court will come down to my property and take my finest field.

> For what reason did the Sages say a creditor collects from intermediate-quality land? So that a person should not see another's fine field and say: I will jump in and lend him money so that later I will collect the field for my debt.

> Why not collect debts from inferior land? If so, you would be locking the door before potential borrowers.

> Why is ketubah from inferior land? Because more than a man wants to marry a woman, a woman wants to become married to a man. (She will agree even with less favorable terms.)

## CHAPTER 5: HaNizakin (The Damaged Parties) - 49b-55b

### Stewardship of Orphans (52a)

Extensive laws about what a steward (apotropos) may and may not do with orphans' property:

**May do:**
- Tithe produce to feed orphans
- Sell cattle, slaves, houses, fields, vineyards to feed orphans
- Make items for mitzvot: lulav, sukka, tzitzit, shofar, Torah scroll, tefillin, mezuzot, Megillah

**May NOT do:**
- Give charity from orphans' funds
- Redeem captives with orphans' property
- Free orphans' slaves
- Enter litigation that risks orphans' estate

**Rabbi Meir's dream (52a):** A steward was selling orphans' land and buying slaves (against halakha). Rabbi Meir stopped him. In a dream, he was shown: "I wish to destroy and you build?" (God wanted the orphans to lose money.) Rabbi Meir paid no heed: **"Words appearing in dreams do not bring up and do not take down."**

**Satan and peace (52a):** Two people would fight every Friday afternoon. Rabbi Meir came and made peace between them for three consecutive weeks. Then he heard Satan say: "Woe, that Rabbi Meir removed that man from his house."

---

### The Ordinance for the Penitent (55a)

**Mishna - Rabbi Yohanan ben Gudgeda's testimony:**
> A stolen beam that was already built into a large building - the victim receives only the value of the beam but not the beam itself, due to an ordinance instituted for the penitent.

By Torah law, a robber returns the stolen item. But if he built it into a structure, making him destroy the building discourages repentance. Beit Shammai: Destroy the building. Beit Hillel: Return the value. Halakha follows Beit Hillel.

---

### The Law of Sicarii (55b)

**Mishna:**
> The law of Sicarii did not apply in Judea in the time that people were being killed in the war. From the time that people were being killed in the war and onward, the law of Sicarii did apply.

The Sicarii were Roman-era thugs who extorted land from owners under threat of death. Three phases of Roman decrees:
1. First decree: Anyone who does not kill a Jew is killed (total danger - sales to Sicarii considered genuine transfers)
2. Second decree: Anyone who kills a Jew pays 4 dinars (still dangerous)
3. Third decree: Anyone who kills a Jew is killed (now the owner thinks "let him take it, I will get it back in court" - so the sale was coerced and invalid)

### THE DESTRUCTION OF THE TEMPLE: KAMTZA AND BAR KAMTZA (55b-56b)

This is the famous aggadic passage. The Gemara transitions from legal enactments to narrative theology with this bridge:

> Rabbi Yohanan said: What is the meaning of that which is written: "Happy is the man who fears always, but he who hardens his heart shall fall into mischief" (Proverbs 28:14)?

Then three destructions, each caused by something absurdly small:
- **Jerusalem** - destroyed on account of Kamtza and bar Kamtza
- **The King's Mountain** - destroyed on account of a rooster and a hen
- **Beitar** - destroyed on account of a shaft from a chariot

### THE FULL STORY (55b-56a):

> There was a certain man whose friend was named Kamtza and whose enemy was named bar Kamtza. He once made a large feast and said to his servant: Go bring me my friend Kamtza. The servant went and mistakenly brought him his enemy bar Kamtza.

> The man who was hosting the feast came and found bar Kamtza sitting at the feast. The host said to bar Kamtza: That man is the enemy of that man, that is, you are my enemy. What then do you want here? Arise and leave.

> Bar Kamtza said to him: Since I have already come, let me stay and I will give you money for whatever I eat and drink. Just do not embarrass me by sending me out.

> The host said to him: No, you must leave.

> Bar Kamtza said to him: I will give you money for half of the feast; just do not send me away. The host said to him: No, you must leave.

> Bar Kamtza then said to him: I will give you money for the entire feast; just let me stay. The host said to him: No, you must leave.

> Finally, the host took bar Kamtza by his hand, stood him up, and took him out.

Then the turn - the indictment of the Sages:

> After having been cast out from the feast, bar Kamtza said to himself: **Since the Sages were sitting there and did not protest the actions of the host, although they saw how he humiliated me, learn from it that they were content with what he did.** I will therefore go and inform against them to the king.

> He went and said to the emperor: The Jews have rebelled against you. The emperor said to him: Who says that this is the case? Bar Kamtza said to him: Go and test them; send them an offering to be brought in honor of the government, and see whether they will sacrifice it.

> The emperor went and sent with him a choice three-year-old calf. While bar Kamtza was coming with the calf to the Temple, **he made a blemish on the calf's upper lip. And some say on its eyelids - a place where according to us (halakha) it is a blemish, but according to them (gentile rules) it is not a blemish.**

Then the fatal impasse:

> The Sages thought to sacrifice the animal as an offering due to the imperative to maintain peace with the government. **Rabbi Zekharya ben Avkolas said to them: If the priests do that, people will say that blemished animals may be sacrificed as offerings on the altar.**

> The Sages said: If we do not sacrifice it, then we must prevent bar Kamtza from reporting this to the emperor. The Sages thought to kill him so that he would not go and speak against them. **Rabbi Zekharya said to them: If you kill him, people will say that one who makes a blemish on sacrificial animals is to be killed.**

> As a result, they did nothing, bar Kamtza's slander was accepted by the authorities, and consequently the war between the Jews and the Romans began.

**The verdict:**
> **Rabbi Yohanan says: The excessive humility [anvetanuto] of Rabbi Zekharya ben Avkolas destroyed our Temple, burned our Sanctuary, and exiled us from our land.**

### Nero's Test (56a)

> Nero Caesar... shot an arrow to the east and the arrow came and fell in Jerusalem. He then shot another arrow to the west and it also fell in Jerusalem. He shot an arrow in all four directions of the heavens, and each time the arrow fell in Jerusalem.

> He said to a child: Tell me a verse. The child said: "And I will lay My vengeance upon Edom by the hand of My people Israel" (Ezekiel 25:14).

> Nero said: The Holy One, Blessed be He, wishes to destroy His Temple, and He wishes to wipe his hands with that man, i.e., with me... **So he fled and became a convert, and ultimately Rabbi Meir descended from him.**

### The Siege and Famine (56a)

The three wealthy men of Jerusalem - Nakdimon ben Guryon, ben Kalba Savua, and ben Tzitzit HaKesat - had enough provisions for 21 years. But:

> There were certain zealots among the people of Jerusalem. The Sages said to them: Let us go out and make peace with the Romans. But the zealots did not allow them. The zealots said: Let us go out and engage in battle. But the Sages said: You will not be successful. **The zealots arose and burned down these storehouses of wheat and barley, and there was a general famine.**

Marta bat Baitos - the wealthy woman reduced to nothing. Each time she sends her servant for flour, the next grade down is already sold. Fine flour, ordinary flour, coarse flour, barley flour - all gone.

> She had just removed her shoes, but she said: I will go out myself and see if I can find something to eat. **She stepped on some dung, which stuck to her foot, and, overcome by disgust, she died.**

> Rabban Yohanan ben Zakkai read concerning her: "The tender and delicate woman among you who would not adventure to set the sole of her foot upon the ground" (Deuteronomy 28:56).

As she was dying:
> She took out all of her gold and silver and threw it in the marketplace. She said: Why do I need this? And this is as it is written: "Their silver and their gold shall not be able to deliver them in the day of the wrath of the Lord" (Ezekiel 7:19).

### Rabban Yohanan ben Zakkai's Escape (56a-56b)

His nephew Abba Sikkara, leader of the zealots, helped him escape Jerusalem in a coffin:

> Pretend to be sick... bring something putrid and place it near you, so that people will say that you have died... have your students enter to bring you to burial...

> The zealots wanted to pierce him with their swords... Abba Sikkara said to them: The Romans will say that they pierce even their teacher. They wanted to push him... They will say that they push even their teacher. They opened the gate and he was taken out.

Before Vespasian:

> He said: Greetings to you, the king. Vespasian said: You are liable for two death penalties - I am not a king, and if I am, why did you not come until now?

Then a messenger arrives: the emperor has died, Vespasian is the new emperor. His foot swells with joy and his shoe will not fit. Rabban Yohanan ben Zakkai prescribes: "Have someone with whom you are displeased pass before you, as it is written: A broken spirit dries the bones."

> Vespasian said: Ask something of me. Rabban Yohanan ben Zakkai said: **Give me Yavne and its Sages and do not destroy it, and spare the dynasty of Rabban Gamliel, and give me doctors to heal Rabbi Tzadok.**

The Gemara's critique:
> He should have said to him: Leave the Jews alone this time.

> And why did he not? **He maintained that Vespasian might not do that much for him, and there would not be even a small amount of salvation.** Therefore, he made only a modest request.

### Titus in the Temple (56b)

> He took a prostitute with his hand, and entered the Holy of Holies with her. He spread out a Torah scroll underneath him and committed a sin on it. Afterward he took a sword and cut into the curtain... and a miracle was performed and blood spurted forth. He mistakenly thought that he had killed himself [a euphemism for God].

> Abba Hanan says: "Who is strong like You, O Lord?" - Who is strong and indurate like You, as You hear the abuse of that wicked man and remain silent.

> The school of Rabbi Yishmael: "Who is like You among the gods [elim]" should be read as: Who is like You among the mute [ilmim].

The gnat in Titus's brain for seven years. A blacksmith's hammering would quiet it - he paid gentile blacksmiths 4 dinars; to Jewish ones he said: "It is enough for you that you see your enemy in so much pain."

When he died they found the gnat had grown to the size of a sparrow (or a one-year-old pigeon).

> When Titus was dying, he said: Burn that man and scatter his ashes across the seven seas, so that the God of the Jews should not find me and stand me for judgment.

## STRUCTURAL ANALYSIS: What These Chapters Establish

### The Logic of Tikun Ha'Olam

Every enactment follows the same pattern:
1. A technically correct legal rule exists
2. Human behavior in response to that rule creates systemic damage
3. The Sages legislate a workaround, citing "mipnei tikun ha'olam"

This is not utopian thinking. It is engineering. The Sages are not asking "what would be ideal?" but "given how people actually behave, what rule produces the least damage?"

### The Hierarchy of Concerns

The enactments protect, in order of frequency:
1. **Women** - from being chained (agunot), from mamzer children, from losing ketubah rights
2. **The poor** - from losing access to loans (prozbul)
3. **Slaves** - from being stuck in impossible status (half-free)
4. **Captives** - from inflated ransoms that incentivize more kidnapping
5. **Penitents** - from barriers to repentance (stolen beam)
6. **The community** - from systemic collapse

### The Audacity of Hillel's Prozbul

The prozbul is the test case for rabbinic authority itself. The Torah says debts are canceled in the seventh year. Hillel says: here is a legal fiction that prevents cancellation. The Gemara's anxiety about this is palpable - it spends significant time establishing that current-era shemittah is only rabbinic, so Hillel is overriding rabbinic law, not Torah law. But the underlying move is clear: when the law as written produces the opposite of its intended effect (the poor cannot get loans), the law must be bent.

### From Tikun Ha'Olam to Destruction

The placement of the Kamtza/Bar Kamtza story at the end of these chapters is not accidental. The tikun ha'olam enactments show the Sages at their best - reading systemic failure and legislating to prevent it. The destruction narrative shows what happens when that capacity fails:

- The host's cruelty was a private sin, but the Sages' silence made it public
- Rabbi Zekharya ben Avkolas applied "what will people say" reasoning - the same kind of systemic thinking that drives tikun ha'olam - but in the wrong direction, toward paralysis instead of action
- His "humility" (anvetanut) was the mirror image of tikun ha'olam: both are concerned with consequences, but tikun ha'olam acts while anvetanut freezes

The destroyer of the Temple was not wickedness but excessive caution dressed as piety.
