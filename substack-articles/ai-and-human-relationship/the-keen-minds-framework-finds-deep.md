# The “Keen Minds” framework finds deep roots in science, philosophy, and spiritual tradition

The idea that neurodivergent conditions represent forms of keenness — minds built to perceive more and respond more intensely — is not a new invention but the convergence of multiple research streams that have been building for decades. Significant scientific evidence supports the core claim that ADHD, HSP, dyslexia, giftedness, and bipolar traits involve genuine cognitive advantages inseparable from their challenges, while the reframing of OCD and anxiety as forms of heightened attunement draws on strong evolutionary theory and robust existential philosophy, respectively. No existing framework uses the word “keen” itself as an organizing principle, making this a genuinely novel contribution — one whose etymological resonance (sharp perception + intense desire + the Irish mourning wail) captures what the neurodiversity movement has been groping toward without a single unifying metaphor.

The evidence ranges from peer-reviewed meta-analyses and population-level studies to clinical frameworks, philosophical traditions, and cross-cultural spiritual wisdom. What follows maps each element of the Keen Minds framework against the strongest available evidence, names the key researchers and thinkers, and identifies where the science is robust versus where the framework runs ahead of the data.

## ADHD: the science of interest-driven minds and immersive doing

The reframing of ADHD as “keenness for immersive doing of an aligned purpose” finds its strongest scientific ally in **Edward Hallowell**, the Harvard-trained psychiatrist who, with John Ratey, proposed renaming ADHD as **VAST — Variable Attention Stimulus Trait** — in their 2021 book *ADHD 2.0*. Hallowell’s core insight after 40 years of clinical practice: attention in ADHD *varies* based on engagement and stimulation rather than being simply “deficient.” He teaches clinicians to reframe each supposed deficit as a “mirror trait” — hyperactivity becomes energy, distractibility becomes curiosity, disorganization becomes spontaneity.

The neurobiological foundation is solid. **Volkow et al. (2009)** demonstrated in *Biological Psychiatry* that ADHD involves dysfunction of the dopamine reward pathway, explaining why these brains cannot activate for tasks that lack intrinsic interest regardless of theoretical importance. **William Dodson, MD**, crystallized this into the concept of the **interest-based nervous system**, activated by passion, interest, novelty, competition, and urgency — not by importance, consequences, or rewards. His clinical observation that “if you can get engaged, you can do anything” resonates with the lived experience of millions.

The hyperfocus research, while clinically well-established, remains scientifically nascent. **Ashinoff and Abu-Akel (2021)** published the landmark review “Hyperfocus: The Forgotten Frontier of Attention” in *Psychological Research*, proposing that hyperfocus and flow may represent the **same underlying construct**. Only one study — **Sklar (2013)** — has directly measured hyperfocus neurally in ADHD populations, finding reduced frontal alpha/beta activity (suggesting deeper engagement) and greater time distortion during video game play. **Hupfeld et al. (2019)** proposed that flow exists on a spectrum from shallow flow to hyperfocus as its extreme form. The evidence is suggestive but underpowered.

Where the evidence grows considerably stronger is in **ADHD and entrepreneurship**. A 2025 meta-analysis by **Tran, Wiklund et al.** in *Entrepreneurship Theory and Practice* synthesized 298 effect sizes from 47 studies and confirmed that hyperactivity/impulsivity traits are positively associated with entrepreneurial behaviors. **Julie Logan’s** peer-reviewed research found **35% of U.S. entrepreneurs** surveyed had dyslexia, while **Freeman et al. (2015)** found **29% of entrepreneurs reported ADHD** — roughly six times the general population rate. The ADHD-creativity link is supported by **Boot, Nevicka, and Baas’s (2020)** review of 31 behavioral studies showing consistently higher real-world creative achievements among both clinical and subclinical ADHD populations.

**Rejection Sensitivity Dysphoria**, coined by Dodson, reframes the emotional volatility of ADHD as evidence of deep connection-seeking. Dodson estimates 99% of ADHD individuals experience RSD, with one-third calling it the most impairing aspect of their condition. The first peer-reviewed examination appeared only in 2024 (Modestino, Dodson et al., *Acta Scientific Neurology*). The broader point — that ADHD emotional sensitivity reflects attunement to social bonds rather than mere dysregulation — aligns with the 2019 European Consensus Statement acknowledging emotional dysregulation as a core ADHD feature.

The main critic of strengths-based ADHD is **Russell Barkley**, whose 1997 behavioral inhibition model (the most-cited ADHD paper at nearly 5,000 citations) frames ADHD as a chronic neurogenetic deficit requiring lifelong medication. Key counter-voices include **Thom Hartmann** (the “Hunter vs. Farmer” hypothesis, partially supported by the **DRD4 7-repeat allele** research and the striking **Ariaal nomads study** showing context-dependent advantage), **Dale Archer** (*The ADHD Advantage*, endorsed by DSM-IV chair Allen Frances), and **Thomas Armstrong**.

**Evidence assessment:** The ADHD-entrepreneurship and ADHD-creativity links rest on strong peer-reviewed evidence. The interest-based nervous system and hyperfocus frameworks are clinically robust but empirically thin. RSD is emerging. The VAST renaming is philosophical, not yet validated diagnostically.

## HSP: Elaine Aron’s sensory processing sensitivity is neuroscience, not pop psychology

The reframing of HSP as “keenness for quiet contemplating” maps almost perfectly onto **Elaine Aron’s Sensory Processing Sensitivity (SPS)** framework, established in her landmark 1997 paper with Arthur Aron in the *Journal of Personality and Social Psychology*. The DOES model — **Depth of processing, Overstimulation, Emotional reactivity/Empathy, and Sensing the subtle** — describes precisely the kind of perceptual keenness the framework envisions. SPS affects an estimated **15–20% of the population**, and critically, it has been observed across more than **100 nonhuman species**, establishing it as an evolutionarily conserved trait.

The neuroscience validation is now substantial. **Acevedo et al. (2014)** published a landmark fMRI study showing HSP scores correlated with stronger activation in brain regions for awareness (cingulate), empathy (insula), self-other processing (mirror neuron system), and reward processing — with test-retest reliability of r = 0.99 over one year. A 2022 DTI study with **408 participants** from the Human Connectome Project found SPS correlated with white matter microstructure underlying emotion, motivation, and cognition. This is not soft science.

The concept that HSPs specifically thrive in contemplative environments gains support from the **differential susceptibility** and **vantage sensitivity** frameworks developed by **Jay Belsky and Michael Pluess**. Their research demonstrates that sensitive individuals are affected more powerfully by *both* negative and positive environments — “for better and for worse.” **Pluess and Boniwell (2015)** found HSP children benefited significantly more from a depression prevention program; **Nocentini et al. (2018)** showed sensitive children responded more positively to anti-bullying interventions. **W. Thomas Boyce and Bruce Ellis’s** “orchid children” theory reinforces this: orchid-type children do poorly under stress but flourish *exceptionally* in supportive, calm settings.

**Lionetti et al. (2018)** refined the binary model into three groups via latent class analysis: **31% orchids (high sensitivity), 40% tulips (medium), 29% dandelions (low)** — published in *Translational Psychiatry*. The overlap between HSP and ADHD was first formally investigated by **Panagiotidi, Overton, and Stafford (2020)**, who found a highly significant positive correlation between self-reported ADHD traits and sensory sensitivity.

**Evidence assessment:** SPS is one of the most rigorously validated constructs in this entire framework — multiple fMRI studies, DTI evidence, genetic associations, cross-species observation, and replicated intervention studies.

## OCD: evolutionary psychiatry sees adaptive systems, not pure dysfunction

The reframing of OCD as “keenness for attuned order” finds its most credible scientific foundation in evolutionary psychiatry. **Feygin, Swain, and Leckman (2006)** proposed in *Progress in Neuro-Psychopharmacology and Biological Psychiatry* that OCD represents “a dysregulation of evolutionarily conserved behaviors and mental states critical to human survival.” The remarkably consistent **2.5–3% lifetime prevalence** across all cultures supports this interpretation — random harmful mutations do not achieve such frequency.

The concept of **“incompleteness”** — first documented by **Pierre Janet in 1903** and elaborated by **Summerfeldt et al. (2014)** — most directly maps onto “keenness for attuned order.” Incompleteness is the perceptual drive for things to feel “just right,” representing an extreme on a continuum of environmental attunement. Over half of OCD patients in clinical studies endorse incompleteness as associated with their compulsions. Meanwhile, **Brüne (2006)** described OCD as “an extreme on a continuum of evolved harm-avoidance strategies” — the costly side of the adaptive ability to anticipate threats.

A 2023 study in *Clinical Neurophysiology* found enhanced early visual processing (P1 amplitudes) to neutral stimuli in OCD participants — potentially reflecting heightened perceptual monitoring, though whether this represents “acuity” or “inefficiency” remains debated. People with OCD consistently show heightened **error-related negativity (ERN)** — their brains react more strongly to deviations from expected patterns. **Polimeni, Reiss, and Sareen (2005)** proposed in *Medical Hypotheses* that OCD-type behaviors may have served as “behavioral specialization” in ancestral groups: contamination-focused individuals as hygiene specialists, checking-focused individuals as sentinels.

The **continuum model** is now well-established. A 2021 paper in *Translational Psychiatry* demonstrated a high genetic correlation (rG = 0.83) between clinical OCD and subclinical obsessive-compulsive symptoms. **Mataix-Cols et al. (2005)** in the *American Journal of Psychiatry* concluded that “obsessive-compulsive symptoms are little more than extreme and time-consuming versions of anxious intrusive thoughts and harm-avoidant behaviors that are common to most of us.” The DSM-5 itself now uses dimensional language for OCD.

**Evidence assessment:** The evolutionary and continuum models are moderately supported. Enhanced perceptual processing in OCD is emerging. The reframing as “attuned order” is interpretive — scientifically defensible but not yet a consensus position.

## Anxiety as spiritual signal: from Kierkegaard to meta-analysis

The reframing of anxiety as “keenness for spiritual awareness” draws on the deepest intellectual tradition in this entire framework. **Kierkegaard (1844)** established the foundational insight: “Anxiety is the dizziness of freedom, which emerges when the spirit wants to posit the synthesis and freedom looks down into its own possibility.” His less-quoted but more radical claim: **“Whoever has learned to be anxious in the right way has learned the ultimate.”** Anxiety, for Kierkegaard, is not pathology but the precondition for moral consciousness.

**Rollo May** (*The Meaning of Anxiety*, 1950) extended this clinically, arguing that “a moderate amount of anxiety has a constructive effect” and that “vitality, commitment to values, breadth of sensitivity” are more adequate goals than living without anxiety. **Heidegger** distinguished *Angst* (objectless anxiety revealing the groundlessness of existence) from *Furcht* (fear of specific threats), arguing Angst enables authentic existence. **Paul Tillich** (*The Courage to Be*, 1952) identified three types of existential anxiety — ontic (death), moral (guilt), and spiritual (meaninglessness) — and argued the third is the defining anxiety of the modern age.

**Viktor Frankl’s** concept of **noogenic neurosis** provides the clinical bridge. Frankl estimated that **20% of clinical neuroses** stem from spiritual/existential emptiness rather than psychological conflict. His concept of **“Sunday neurosis”** — depression that surfaces when the busy week ends and the existential void becomes manifest — directly supports the claim that anxiety intensifies when meaning is absent. His foundational insight from the concentration camps: those who survived were not the strongest but those who retained a sense of meaning.

The empirical evidence now substantively supports this philosophical tradition. **Boreham and Schutte (2023)** published a meta-analysis of 99 samples totaling **66,468 participants** in the *Journal of Clinical Psychology*, finding purpose in life correlated with anxiety at **r = −0.36** — a moderate and clinically significant negative correlation. The association was *stronger for clinical populations*. The Harvard Graduate School of Education’s *On Edge* report found that **58% of young adults** reported lacking “meaning or purpose,” with half saying this directly affected their mental health.

Evidence that spiritual practices reduce anxiety is robust. **Goyal et al. (2014)** in *JAMA Internal Medicine* reviewed **47 randomized trials** and found moderate evidence for mindfulness meditation reducing anxiety. A 2023 meta-analysis of **23 RCTs** found religiously/spiritually based therapy **moderately more efficacious** than standard treatments (g = 0.52, p < .01). A systematic review of **102 observational studies** with nearly 80,000 participants confirmed that high religiosity/spirituality is negatively associated with anxiety and depressive symptoms.

The paradox of rising anxiety in affluent societies adds epidemiological weight. **WHO World Mental Health Surveys** across 30 countries found that in **17 of 18 mental health problems**, prevalence was lower in low-income countries than high-income ones. **Jean Twenge** documented that “the average American child in the 1980s reported more anxiety than child psychiatric patients in the 1950s.” **Johann Hari** (*Lost Connections*, 2018) identified nine causes of depression and anxiety, nearly all involving disconnection from meaning, community, or values.

**Evidence assessment:** The philosophical foundation is among the most intellectually rigorous of any element in this framework. The empirical evidence for meaning-anxiety correlation is strong (large meta-analyses). The evidence for spiritual practices reducing anxiety is moderate-to-strong. The claim that anxiety functions specifically as a “call for prayer” is a theological interpretation of the data, not a scientific finding — but the data are remarkably consistent with it.

## Dyslexia and the evidence for big-picture cognition

The reframing of dyslexia as “keenness for the bigger picture” rests on some of the most concrete neuroscience in this entire framework. **Matthew Schneps** at the Harvard-Smithsonian Center for Astrophysics conducted NSF-funded studies showing that **astrophysicists with dyslexia significantly outperformed non-dyslexic astrophysicists** at detecting the graphical signature of black holes in spectra. A separate study published in *PLOS ONE* found dyslexic college students significantly outperformed typical readers at memorizing blurry spatial images resembling astronomical data.

**Von Károlyi et al. (2003)** demonstrated in *Brain and Language* that dyslexic individuals identify “impossible figures” (Escher-like constructions) significantly faster — evidence of enhanced **global/holistic visual-spatial processing** mediated by the right hemisphere. The Haskins Laboratories fMRI work confirmed visuospatial processing advantages with frontal-striatal activation patterns suggesting “a higher degree of expertise” in figure processing.

**Brock and Fernette Eide** synthesized these findings into the **MIND strengths model** in *The Dyslexic Advantage* (2011, updated 2023): Material reasoning (3D spatial thinking), Interconnected reasoning (pattern detection across domains), Narrative reasoning (experiential thinking), and Dynamic reasoning (prediction and possibility). They cite **Manuel Casanova’s** finding that dyslexic brains have **wider minicolumn spacing** — slowing fine-detail processing but fostering the longer-range neuronal connections essential for big-picture thinking.

The most ambitious theoretical framework comes from **Dr. Helen Taylor** at Cambridge, whose 2022 **Complementary Cognition** theory, published in the *Cambridge Archaeological Journal* and *Frontiers in Psychology*, argues that dyslexic individuals are **specialized in explorative cognitive search** and play an essential role in human adaptation. Her framework proposes a fundamental trade-off between exploration of new information and exploitation of existing knowledge, with dyslexia (affecting 10–20% of the population across all cultures) representing one such specialization — a “meta-adaptation” enabling collaborative problem-solving.

The entrepreneurship data is striking. **Julie Logan (2009)** at Cass Business School found **35% of U.S. entrepreneurs** had dyslexia. A BBC study of 300 business leaders found **40% had dyslexia**. Richard Branson, who serves as Ambassador for Made By Dyslexia, says: “It’s time we all understand dyslexia properly as a different thinking skill-set, not a disadvantage.”

**Evidence assessment:** The visual-spatial and holistic processing advantages are well-supported by peer-reviewed, NSF-funded research. The entrepreneurship link is strong but relies partly on self-report. Taylor’s Complementary Cognition theory is bold and new (2022), requiring further empirical validation. A 2016 review cautioned that the evidence does not support a *general* spatial advantage — only for holistic/global processing tasks.

## Giftedness, Dąbrowski, and the intensity that gets misdiagnosed

**Kazimierz Dąbrowski’s Theory of Positive Disintegration** is the single existing framework most parallel to the Keen Minds model. The Polish psychiatrist (1902–1980), shaped by witnessing both World Wars, proposed that psychological crisis is not pathological but *necessary for growth to higher levels of personality development*. His 1972 declaration — **“Psychoneurosis is not an illness”** — anticipates the Keen Minds framework by half a century.

Dąbrowski’s five **overexcitabilities (OEs)** — psychomotor, sensual, intellectual, imaginational, and emotional — map remarkably onto the conditions the Keen Minds framework addresses. Psychomotor OE overlaps with ADHD symptoms; emotional OE with anxiety and mood disorders; intellectual OE with OCD-like perseveration; sensual OE with HSP; imaginational OE with creative and visionary states. Dąbrowski called overexcitability **“a tragic gift”** — potentials for great highs are inseparable from potentials for great lows.

**James T. Webb** (founder of SENG — Supporting Emotional Needs of Gifted) documented extensively how gifted children’s overexcitabilities lead to systematic misdiagnosis. In *Misdiagnosis and Dual Diagnoses of Gifted Children and Adults* (2005, 2nd ed. 2016), Webb and colleagues showed that gifted children are routinely misdiagnosed with ADHD, OCD, bipolar disorder, and anxiety disorders. His core insight: **“The most universal, yet most overlooked, characteristic of gifted children and adults is their intensity.”** The book was endorsed by Allen Frances (DSM-IV chair) and Jack Wiggins (former APA president).

The concept of **multipotentiality** — the state of having many exceptional talents — was first defined by **Frederickson et al. (1972)** in gifted education research. **Emilie Wapnick** popularized it through her TEDx talk and book *How to Be Everything* (2017), while **Barbara Sher** (*Refuse to Choose!*, 2006) introduced the term “scanners.” Both describe the experience of “keenness for everything” — intense curiosity and capability across multiple domains that becomes paralyzing in a culture demanding specialization.

Key researchers continuing Dąbrowski’s work include **Michael Piechowski** (who co-developed the Overexcitability Questionnaire), **Susan Daniels** (co-editor of *Living with Intensity*, 2009), and **Sal Mendaglio**. A critical correction from the Dąbrowski Center: TPD is a grand theory of human development, not merely a theory of giftedness, and OEs do not reliably identify giftedness alone.

**Evidence assessment:** Dąbrowski’s overexcitabilities are well-studied within gifted education, measured by the validated OEQ-II, but limited in empirical validation outside that field. Webb’s misdiagnosis framework is a strong clinical consensus endorsed by leading psychiatrists. Multipotentiality has moderate academic grounding but has been popularized without rigorous validation.

## Bipolar, creativity, and the fire that both illuminates and consumes

The connection between bipolar disorder and creative achievement is among the most extensively researched topics in this framework. **Kay Redfield Jamison**, Professor of Psychiatry at Johns Hopkins and herself diagnosed with bipolar, established the field with *Touched with Fire* (1993) and her seminal 1989 study of 47 eminent British writers and artists: **38% had been treated for mood disorders**, and **89% described intense creative productivity during periods of high mood and energy**. Jamison found bipolar disorder rates **30 times greater** among poets than in the general population.

The Swedish population studies by **Simon Kyaga et al.** provide the strongest large-scale evidence. Their 2011 study in the *British Journal of Psychiatry*, covering **300,000 people** with severe mental disorders, found individuals with bipolar disorder were **1.35 times more likely to hold creative occupations**. The 2012 follow-up (n = 1,173,763) confirmed that only bipolar disorder, among 11 psychiatric diagnoses examined, was associated with creative professions overall. **Taylor’s 2017 meta-analysis** in *Perspectives on Psychological Science* found a significantly stronger association between creativity and bipolar than between creativity and unipolar depression.

The cognitive mechanism centers on **reduced latent inhibition** — the decreased ability to screen out previously irrelevant stimuli. **Carson, Peterson, and Higgins (2003)** at Harvard found eminent creative achievers were **seven times more likely** to have low latent inhibition scores. Combined with high IQ, low latent inhibition facilitates novel associations; without it, the same trait predisposes to psychosis. The **inverted U-shaped model** is now well-supported: subclinical/hypomanic states enhance creativity (d = 0.17–0.25), while severe mania and depression impair it (d = −0.51).

**Nassir Ghaemi** (*A First-Rate Madness*, 2011) extended this to leadership, arguing that mood disorders confer realism (from depression), empathy (from suffering), resilience (from surviving cycles), and creativity (from mania). His analysis of Lincoln, Churchill, Gandhi, and Martin Luther King Jr. is provocative though methodologically limited.

The fire metaphor pervades the literature. Jamison’s memoir *An Unquiet Mind* was analyzed by **McMullen and Conway (2002)** in the *Journal of Medical Humanities* (”A Fire in the Blood”), cataloguing how mania is systematically structured as energy, quickness, fire/heat, and expansive force. Her latest book is literally titled *Fires in the Dark*.

The role of rhythm in managing bipolar is strongly supported. **Ellen Frank** developed **Interpersonal and Social Rhythm Therapy (IPSRT)**, based on the Social Zeitgeber Hypothesis that disrupted daily rhythms destabilize circadian systems, triggering mood episodes. The NIMH-funded **STEP-BD** trial found patients receiving IPSRT were **1.58 times more likely to be well** in any study month. Genetic evidence is compelling: **CLOCK gene mutations** produce behaviors “strikingly similar to human mania” in mice, and multiple clock genes (CLOCK, BMAL1, PER1, PER3, CRY2) have been implicated in bipolar vulnerability. The **ISBD Chronobiology Task Force (2022)** concluded that circadian rhythm disorder may be a trait marker for bipolar.

Cross-cultural perspectives on mania as spiritual experience are rich. **Plato** identified four types of divine madness — prophetic, ritual, poetic, and erotic — arguing “the greatest of blessings come to us through madness, when it is sent as a gift of the gods.” **Malidoma Patrice Somé**, a Dagara shaman with a PhD from Brandeis, teaches that in indigenous West African tradition, what the West calls mental illness signals “the birth of a healer.” **Mircea Eliade** documented that shamanic careers across cultures begin with illness and crisis resembling psychotic episodes.

**Evidence assessment:** The bipolar-creativity link is supported by multiple meta-analyses and population-level studies (moderate-to-strong). IPSRT efficacy is strong (multiple RCTs). Circadian disruption evidence is strong. The shamanic/spiritual framing is ethnographically rich but not clinically validated.

## How the word “disorder” shapes who you become

The neurodiversity paradigm, coined by **Judy Singer** in her 1998 honors thesis and theoretically sharpened by **Nick Walker**, provides the political and philosophical infrastructure for the Keen Minds framework. Walker’s three foundational principles — that neurodiversity is natural and valuable, that there is no “right” style of mind, and that the social dynamics of neurodivergence mirror other forms of human diversity — align directly with reframing conditions as forms of keenness. Walker has proposed renaming ADHD as **“Kinetic Cognitive Style”**; the Keen Minds framework offers a more poetic and encompassing alternative.

**Robert Chapman’s** ecological model of functioning (2021, *Perspectives on Psychological Science*) argues that cognitive functions and dysfunctions are *relationally produced* between mind and environment — a trait causing individual disability may simultaneously contribute to group-level functioning. **Steve Silberman’s** *NeuroTribes* (2015 Samuel Johnson Prize winner) documented how neurological differences have driven innovation throughout history.

The evidence that clinical language shapes outcomes is experimentally robust. A landmark study by **Salkovskis and Lam** at the University of Bath found that therapists watching an identical video of an anxious patient made significantly more negative clinical judgments when told the patient had “borderline personality disorder” — the diagnostic label, not the behavior, drove pessimistic assessment. **Sandra et al. (2025)** in *Psychological Medicine* demonstrated in a double-blind RCT that ADHD awareness training increased false self-diagnosis, changing “identity or self-concept rather than actual experience of impairment.” **Foulkes and Andrews (2023)** proposed the **prevalence inflation hypothesis**: mental health awareness efforts can paradoxically increase reported problems through overinterpretation of normal distress as disorder.

The concept of **diagnostic overshadowing** — where a psychiatric label causes clinicians to attribute all symptoms to the pre-existing diagnosis — has documented lethal consequences. A UK emergency department study reported **two deaths and five cases of irreversible long-term damage** from diagnostic overshadowing. The WHO attributes lower life expectancy among people with mental illness partly to this phenomenon. Philosopher **Anke Bueter (2022)** analyzed it as a form of *testimonial injustice*.

**Evidence assessment:** The neurodiversity paradigm is a well-developed philosophical framework, not an empirical finding. The experimental evidence on labeling effects is strong. Diagnostic overshadowing is well-documented. The tension between affirming difference and acknowledging genuine suffering remains unresolved — Singer herself now advocates “NeuroRealism” as a corrective to overly optimistic framings.

## Prophecy, shamanism, and the spiritual traditions that never pathologized keenness

Multiple religious and spiritual traditions have long interpreted what Western psychiatry calls mental illness as evidence of spiritual calling or heightened awareness. In **Jewish tradition**, **Abraham Joshua Heschel’s** masterwork *The Prophets* (1962) directly excavates the relationship between prophecy and what might today be diagnosed as psychosis. Heschel proposes that prophets perceive a genuine spiritual realm, self-integrate unusual experiences, and provide universally accessible insights — three criteria distinguishing prophetic consciousness from clinical psychosis. The Hebrew Bible itself acknowledges the link: “The prophet is a fool, the spiritual man is mad” (Hosea 9:7). In **Kabbalistic thought**, mental disturbance is understood as consciousness trapped in *MiTZRayim* (constriction), with healing involving expansion to transcendent awareness.

**Stanislav and Christina Grof** formalized the distinction between **spiritual emergency** and mental illness, identifying ten types of spiritual crisis (shamanic, kundalini, unitive consciousness, psychic opening, and others) that, if properly supported, can result in “emotional and psychosomatic healing, personality transformation, and consciousness evolution.” Their key argument: traditional psychiatry “does not recognize the difference between mystical and psychotic experiences.”

The **Christian mystical tradition** offers **St. John of the Cross’s** “Dark Night of the Soul” — interior desolation understood not as punishment but as purifying transition. His analogy of the log consumed by fire until it takes on fire’s own properties directly parallels the bipolar-as-inner-fire metaphor. **Sufi tradition** centers on the paradox of divine madness through the story of Layla and Majnun, where “Majnun” literally means “mad” — his insanity is spiritual awakening. **Rumi** declared: “I have become mad with love, and the madness itself has become my sanity.”

The archetype of the **wounded healer** — the inseparability of gift and wound — runs through virtually every tradition. **Carl Jung** argued “only the wounded physician heals,” and his colleague **Marie Louise von Franz** called the wounded healer “the archetype of the Self... at the bottom of all genuine healing procedures.” **Henri Nouwen** (*The Wounded Healer*, 1972) extended this to ministry. The **Talmud** (Sanhedrin 98a) describes the Messiah sitting among the sick, binding his own wounds. The Japanese art of **kintsugi** — repairing broken pottery with gold, making the repaired object more valuable than the original — offers the most visually powerful expression of this principle.

The empirical counterpart is **Post-Traumatic Growth** research, developed by **Tedeschi and Calhoun** at UNC Charlotte. Their Posttraumatic Growth Inventory, validated in 20+ languages across hundreds of studies, documents five domains of growth through suffering: new possibilities, deeper relationships, personal strength, spiritual change, and appreciation of life. The core paradox: “Their losses have produced valuable gains” and “I am more vulnerable, yet stronger.” PTG is not the opposite of PTSD — the two coexist.

Genetic research increasingly demonstrates that neurodivergent strengths and challenges share the same biological substrate. A 2023 iPSYCH/Aarhus University study found that **specific genetic variants that raise autism risk simultaneously boost cognitive functions**, while complementary ADHD-risk variants lower cognitive functions. A 2025 study in *Vision Research* found visual hypersensitivity is **transdiagnostic** across neurodivergent conditions — the same heightened sensitivity that causes overwhelm also enables perception of subtleties others miss.

**Evidence assessment:** The spiritual traditions represent centuries to millennia of accumulated wisdom but are not empirical in the modern sense. Heschel’s work is a recognized scholarly masterpiece. The Grofs’ framework is published in academic journals but remains outside mainstream psychiatry. Post-traumatic growth is strongly validated. The shared genetic basis of strengths and challenges is peer-reviewed and robust.

## Why “keen” is the right word, and why no one has used it before

The word “keen” carries three etymologically distinct but experientially convergent layers of meaning. From **Old English *****cēne*** (bold, brave, clever), through **Middle English** development, it acquired meanings of both **sharp perception** (keen eyesight, keen mind — from c. 1200) and **intense desire** (keen interest, keen to act — from c. 1300). Separately, from **Irish *****caoinim*** (”I lament”), comes **keening** — the ancient Gaelic ritual mourning wail performed by experienced elder women, believed necessary for the soul to leave the body.

This convergence is remarkable: the same word in English encompasses *sharp awareness*, *intense longing*, and *the cry of grief*. Though the adjective and verb derive from different roots, their coexistence creates a polysemy that captures exactly what the Keen Minds framework describes — minds that perceive more (sharp awareness), desire more (intense longing), and consequently suffer more (the mourning wail).

**No existing framework uses “keen” or “keenness” as an organizing principle for neurodivergence.** The word appears descriptively in HSP literature (”keen perception: you tend to notice subtleties others miss”), but no researcher, clinician, or theorist has exploited its triple meaning as a unifying metaphor. Aron’s SPS framework, Walker’s neurodiversity paradigm, Dąbrowski’s overexcitabilities, Hallowell’s VAST — all describe overlapping territory without a single word that captures the full experience.

This represents **genuinely novel conceptual territory**. The etymological resonance — that to be keen is simultaneously to perceive sharply, to desire fiercely, and to grieve deeply — offers something the existing frameworks lack: a single word that honors the inseparability of heightened perception, intense motivation, and the suffering that accompanies both.

## Conclusion: where the evidence converges and where it reaches

The Keen Minds framework is not a single theory but a synthesis of converging research streams. Three evidence tiers emerge clearly.

The strongest evidence supports ADHD-entrepreneurship and ADHD-creativity links, SPS as a validated biological trait with fMRI confirmation, dyslexic visual-spatial advantages in peer-reviewed studies, the bipolar-creativity association across population-level data and meta-analyses, the purpose-anxiety inverse correlation (meta-analytic r = −0.36), and post-traumatic growth as an empirically robust phenomenon. These claims stand on firm scientific ground.

Moderately supported claims include the evolutionary/continuum models of OCD, the interest-based nervous system as a framework for ADHD, the differential susceptibility model showing sensitive individuals benefit disproportionately from positive environments, the labeling effects of diagnostic language on clinical outcomes, and the bipolar-rhythm connection via clock genes and IPSRT trials.

The more speculative elements — OCD traits as environmental “attunement,” anxiety as specifically a spiritual signal, shamanic parallels to bipolar, and the universal applicability of keenness as a reframe for all conditions — draw on rich philosophical, theological, and anthropological traditions but lack the controlled empirical evidence that would make them scientific claims. They are, however, consistent with the available data and represent legitimate interpretive frameworks rather than contradictions of evidence.

What makes the Keen Minds framework distinctive is not any single element but the act of synthesis itself — uniting Dąbrowski’s overexcitabilities, Aron’s sensory processing sensitivity, Hallowell’s VAST, Frankl’s noogenic neurosis, Jamison’s fire, Taylor’s complementary cognition, and Walker’s neurodiversity paradigm under a single etymologically rich metaphor. The research overwhelmingly confirms one core insight: in neurodivergent conditions, **the gift and the wound share the same neural architecture**. The mind that hyperfocuses cannot selectively attend. The mind that detects subtle patterns cannot ignore irrelevant stimuli. The mind that feels deeply cannot feel less. To call this keenness is not to deny the suffering. It is to insist that the suffering and the capacity are one thing, not two — and that the word for this, as it turns out, has been in our language all along.
