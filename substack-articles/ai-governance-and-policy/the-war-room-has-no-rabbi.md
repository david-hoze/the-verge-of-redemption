# The War Room Has No Rabbi: Neither doom nor acceleration - obligations in the meantime

*Dr. Strangelove (1964) - Peter Sellers (three roles), George C. Scott, Sterling Hayden, Slim Pickens. A paranoid general launches a nuclear first strike. The President calls the Soviet Premier to apologize. The Soviets reveal they have a Doomsday Machine - an automated system that will destroy all life on Earth if triggered, designed to be impossible to untrigger. The whole point of the Doomsday Machine was deterrence, but they forgot to tell anyone about it. Slim Pickens rides the bomb down like a rodeo bull. The world ends. Nobody meant for it to happen. Directed by Stanley Kubrick.The Doomsday Machine was supposed to prevent the war. Its designers reasoned backward from the worst possible outcome - total annihilation - and built a system so terrifying that no rational actor would trigger it. The logic was flawless. The machine worked exactly as designed. And the world ended anyway.*

The flaw was not technical. The flaw was eschatological. The designers built a system oriented entirely around the ending - a machine whose only function was to determine whether the final moment had arrived, and to act accordingly. It had no instructions for the meantime. No protocol for the ordinary Tuesday when nobody pushes the button. No capacity to do anything at all except watch for the end and execute when it came.

The AI risk debate has built its own Doomsday Machine. Not a physical device - a narrative one. A story so catastrophic on one side, and so utopian on the other, that the entire conversation has been organized around the ending. And like Kubrick’s machine, the narrative version has no instructions for the meantime either.

<hr>
## Two Eschatologies

The conversation about artificial intelligence and existential risk has two poles, and both of them are trying to end the world.

The first pole is acceleration. Sam Altman tells the U.S. Senate that artificial general intelligence could “elevate humanity” beyond anything previously imaginable. Marc Andreessen publishes a manifesto titled “Why AI Will Save the World” and declares that opposing technological acceleration is opposing human flourishing itself. The acceleration thesis is simple: AI will solve our hardest problems - disease, poverty, climate, aging, death itself - if we build fast enough and get out of the way. The future is coming. Your job is to hasten it.

The second pole is doom. Eliezer Yudkowsky tells TIME magazine that if the wrong AI is built, “literally everyone on Earth will die.” He has argued publicly for airstrikes on data centers if necessary to prevent unaligned superintelligence. The doom thesis is equally simple: AI will destroy everything if we don’t stop it - or at minimum control it so tightly that the existential risk drops below some acceptable threshold. The future is coming. Your job is to prevent it.

Notice what both positions share. Not their conclusions - those are opposite. Their structure. Both begin with a vision of the ending. Both organize all present action around that ending. Both treat the current moment as significant only insofar as it advances or delays the final state. Both are eschatologies - visions of the last things that determine everything that comes before.

The accelerationist says: paradise is coming, and the greatest sin is to slow it down. The doomer says: apocalypse is coming, and the greatest sin is to ignore the signs. Both are absolutely certain about where history is heading. Both demand that you orient your life around that certainty. Both treat anyone who doesn’t share their timeline as either naive or reckless.

And both are doing something the Jewish tradition has a very specific prohibition against: forcing the end.

## The Dispensationalist Machine

Silicon Valley did not invent its eschatology. It inherited one.

Dispensationalist premillennialism - the dominant eschatological framework of American evangelical Protestantism - teaches that the current age is a dispensation, a temporary arrangement that must be disrupted for the Kingdom to come. The existing order is not to be repaired. It is to be passed through. The faithful will be raptured - taken up before the tribulation. The world will burn. And then Christ returns to establish the millennium.

The structural features: the current order is broken beyond repair. A catastrophic disruption is coming. The righteous will be separated from the rest. The old world must be destroyed before the new one can be built. Your job is not to fix this world. Your job is to be ready for the next one.

Now read the accelerationist manifesto again.

“Move fast and break things” is not a business strategy. It is premillennialism in startup language. The existing institutions are broken. The disruption is coming. The technologists - the elect, the builders, the ones who see what others can’t - will be raptured into the new economy while the old one burns. Facebook’s original motto was not a product philosophy. It was an eschatological claim about the disposability of the present order.

Marc Andreessen’s “Techno-Optimist Manifesto” makes the theology explicit without meaning to. He lists technology’s enemies: “precautionary principle, sustainability, social responsibility, trust and safety, risk management.” These are not obstacles to progress. They are obligations in the meantime - the things you do while the world is unfinished. And Andreessen wants them cleared out of the way. Because when the future arrives, none of them will matter. The rapture takes the builders.

The doom side inherits the same structure from a different angle. If p(doom) is 30% - if there is roughly a one-in-three chance that artificial intelligence destroys all human life - then every other concern is subordinate. Climate policy, economic justice, education reform, healthcare, governance - all of it becomes trivial noise against the existential signal. You don’t rearrange deck chairs on the Titanic. The doomer’s version of the rapture is survival itself: the small remnant who saw the danger and either prevented it or prepared for it while everyone else sleepwalked into extinction.

Both eschatologies share a common ancestor: the conviction that the current moment is defined by its proximity to the ending, and that the ending justifies any present sacrifice. The accelerationist sacrifices safety and caution. The doomer sacrifices everything else. Neither has a theology of the ordinary Tuesday.

This is not an accident. It is a direct inheritance from the civilization that produced both movements. A civilization that severed itself from the tradition that could have given it an alternative - a framework for the meantime.

## The Three Oaths

The Jewish tradition includes a structural prohibition against forcing the end.

The Talmud in Ketubot 111a records three oaths that God imposed at the beginning of the exile. The Jewish people swore not to ascend to the Land of Israel “as a wall” - by force, en masse, storming the ending. The nations swore not to oppress Israel excessively. And Israel swore not to “force the end” - not to compel the redemption before its time.

The third oath is the one that matters here. *She’lo yidchaku et ha’ketz* - they shall not push the end.

The commentators debate the scope and application of these oaths. The Avnei Nezer and Rav Meir Simcha of Dvinsk argue the oaths expired when the nations violated theirs. Others treat them as permanent structural warnings. But the underlying principle is consistent across nearly every reading: there is a redemption coming, it has a timeline, and you do not get to force it. Not because the redemption is uncertain. Because forcing it is a category error - a confusion about what kind of thing the ending is.

The accelerationist says: if we build fast enough, we can bring about the singularity - a moment of transcendence where artificial intelligence solves the problems that human civilization cannot. This is forcing the ketz. Not metaphorically. Structurally. It is the claim that the right combination of human effort and technological power can compel an eschatological outcome. That if we push hard enough, paradise arrives on our schedule.

The doomer says: if we don’t act dramatically enough - moratoriums, treaties, airstrikes on data centers - the apocalypse arrives and everyone dies. This too is forcing the ketz, from the other direction. It is the claim that without extraordinary human intervention, the ending defaults to catastrophe. That the timeline is ours to manage - that the eschaton is an engineering variable.

Both positions assume the ending is something humans engineer. The tradition says: it isn’t. You have obligations. You carry them out. The ending comes when it comes. Your job is not to produce it.

## Finish Planting the Tree

There is a teaching attributed to Rabban Yochanan ben Zakkai in Avot d’Rabbi Natan:

*“If you are planting a tree and they tell you the Messiah has arrived - finish planting the tree, then go greet the Messiah.”*

Read that again. The Messiah - the event the entire tradition orients toward, the culmination of history, the moment everything has been building toward for thousands of years - has arrived. And the instruction is: finish planting.

This is not a lesson in patience. It is a structural claim about the relationship between eschatology and obligation. The tree is not more important than the Messiah. The planting is not more urgent than the redemption. But the planting is yours. The redemption is God’s. And if you drop the sapling to run toward the ending, you have confused your task with His.

The Silicon Valley accelerationist drops the tree every time a new model ships. The AI safety researcher drops the tree every time a new capability emerges. Both are running toward the ending - one in ecstasy, the other in terror. Neither is planting.

Pirkei Avot 2:16 makes the same point from the other direction. Rabbi Tarfon says: “You are not obligated to complete the work, but neither are you free to desist from it.” The work will not be finished by you. This is not pessimism - it is liberation. You don’t have to solve alignment. You don’t have to prevent the apocalypse. You don’t have to build the singularity. You are not capable of completing any of these projects, and the tradition does not expect you to try.

But you are also not free to sit down. The work continues. There are obligations. They are specific. They are local. They are achievable. And they do not depend on your theory of the ending.

This is the position that neither eschatology can accommodate. The accelerationist needs you to believe that the work can be completed - that enough building will produce the final state. The doomer needs you to believe that if the work isn’t completed in time, everything is lost. Both need the ending to be the thing that gives present action its meaning.

The tradition says: the present gives present action its meaning. The tree you are planting is valuable because it is a tree, and because you are planting it, and because planting is the kind of thing a person is supposed to do. Not because the tree will survive the eschaton. Not because planting proves you are on the right side of history. Because right now, in this moment, there is a hole in the ground and a sapling in your hand and the work is yours to do.

## The Empirical Messiah

Maimonides codifies the laws of the messianic age in the Mishneh Torah, Hilchot Melachim chapters 11 and 12. His treatment is startling in its sobriety.

The Mashiach is not a supernatural figure who descends from the clouds. He is a king from the house of David who studies Torah, observes the commandments, compels all of Israel to follow the Torah, fights the wars of God, builds the Temple, and gathers the exiles. If he does all this and succeeds - he is Mashiach. If he is killed, or does not succeed - he is not Mashiach.

Read the criteria. They are entirely retrospective. You cannot identify the Messiah in advance. You cannot run a probability distribution on messianic outcomes. You cannot look at a candidate and say “70% chance this is him.” The identification happens after the fact, when the criteria have been met - or when they have not been met and the candidate has failed. There is no predictive model. There is only observation of outcomes.

Maimonides writes explicitly: “Let no one think that in the messianic age anything in the natural order will be nullified, or that there will be any innovation in the work of creation. The world will continue in its normal course.” The messianic age is not a rupture. It is not a singularity. It is not a discontinuous break with everything that came before. It is the world, continuing - but with the obligations actually fulfilled. Justice established. Knowledge widespread. War ended. Not because the laws of nature changed, but because people changed.

Now consider the p(doom) calculation. Eliezer Yudkowsky puts the probability of human extinction from AI at above 50%. Other researchers offer 10%, 20%, 30%. They are running probability distributions on the eschaton. They are attempting to calculate, in advance, whether the ending will be paradise or annihilation - and to adjust present strategy accordingly.

Maimonides would find this unintelligible. Not because the future doesn’t matter. Because the future is not the kind of thing you calculate. You observe what someone does. You watch whether they succeed. You see the outcomes. The criteria are empirical and retrospective. You do not optimize toward the Messiah. You do the work, and you see what happens.

This is not fatalism. Maimonides is not saying “sit back and wait.” He is saying: your job is to study, to build, to observe the commandments, to establish justice, to fight when necessary, to teach. Whether that work produces the messianic age is not your calculation to make. You do not have the variables. You do not control the timeline. You control whether you planted the tree.

The AI policy world desperately needs this distinction. Not because probability is useless - it is useful for engineering decisions, resource allocation, technical planning. But because the p(doom) conversation has become eschatological. It has stopped being a tool for decision-making and started being a framework for meaning. People are organizing their entire lives around a number - making career choices, allocation decisions, ethical judgments, all calibrated to their estimate of how the story ends. They have turned a technical parameter into a theology.

And a theology without a tradition is just anxiety with footnotes.

## Obligations in the Meantime

So what does the tradition actually prescribe while the ending is not yet here?

It prescribes obligations. Specific, local, achievable obligations that do not require you to know how the story ends.

Study. The Talmud (Shabbat 31a) teaches that the first question a person is asked in the world to come is: “Did you set fixed times for Torah study?” Not “did you solve alignment?” Not “did you prevent existential risk?” Did you learn? Did you show up to the text and wrestle with it? Did you submit yourself to the discipline of understanding something larger than your own perspective?

Applied to AI: are you studying what this technology actually does? Not reading manifestos and counter-manifestos - studying. Understanding the architectures, the training processes, the failure modes, the actual capabilities as distinct from the projected ones. The tradition demands literacy as a prerequisite for judgment. Most of the people making the loudest claims about AI - on both sides - have not done the learning.

Build community. The Talmud (Ta’anit 23a) records that Honi the Circle-Drawer saw a man planting a carob tree and asked: “How long until it bears fruit?” The man answered: “Seventy years.” Honi asked: “Do you expect to live seventy years?” The man said: “I found the world with carob trees. As my ancestors planted for me, I plant for my children.”

Applied to AI: are you building institutions that will outlast the current hype cycle? Not a startup that exits in five years - a community, a school, a set of practices that your students’ students will inherit. The AI governance conversation is almost entirely consumed by the current moment - this model, this capability, this risk. The tradition thinks in generations. The carob tree bears fruit for your grandchildren, not for your quarterly report.

Repair what is broken. Tikkun olam - repair of the world - is among the most misused phrases in modern Jewish life. It has been stretched to cover everything from recycling to voting to posting on social media. But in its original context (the Aleinu prayer, and in Lurianic Kabbalah), tikkun is specific and demanding. The world is broken. The shards need to be gathered. The sparks need to be elevated. And this work is done not through grand gestures but through individual acts of sanctification - each commandment performed lifts a specific spark, repairs a specific fracture.

Applied to AI: the repair work is not “align superintelligence.” It is not “solve the control problem.” Those are aspirations for the ending. The repair work is: this algorithm is denying healthcare claims to elderly patients. Fix that. This hiring tool is rejecting every Black applicant. Fix that. This recommendation engine is feeding suicidal content to teenagers. Fix that. Not because fixing these things solves the grand problem. Because these things are broken, and they are in front of you, and you have the ability to repair them. That is enough. That is the obligation.

Teach children. The Shema - the central declaration of Jewish faith, recited twice daily - includes the instruction: “And you shall teach them diligently to your children” (Deuteronomy 6:7). Not “develop curricula.” Not “build educational technology platforms.” Teach your children. Sit with them. Transmit what you know. Make sure they can think, not just compute.

Applied to AI: an entire generation is being raised by algorithms. Their education is being “personalized” by systems that optimize for engagement, not formation. The obligation is not to solve this at scale. The obligation is to teach the actual children in your actual life what it means to think carefully, to resist easy answers, to hold complexity without collapsing into either despair or naivety. If you do this for your children and your students, you have fulfilled the obligation. Whether the educational system transforms is not your problem to solve. Whether your children can think is.

Maintain justice. “Justice, justice shall you pursue” (Deuteronomy 16:20). The rabbis ask: why is “justice” repeated? Because you must pursue justice by just means. The end does not justify the means. Just outcomes achieved through unjust processes are not justice. This is the tradition’s direct refutation of both eschatologies: you cannot sacrifice present obligations for future outcomes, no matter how glorious or how catastrophic you believe those outcomes will be.

Applied to AI: the accelerationist sacrifices safety for speed because the future will be worth it. The doomer sacrifices civil liberties, democratic process, and proportionality because the risk justifies it. Yudkowsky’s argument for airstrikes on non-compliant data centers is the Doomsday Machine in policy form - an outcome so terrible that any present action is justified to prevent it. The tradition says: no. Justice by just means. If your method of saving the world requires you to bomb sovereign nations, the method is unjust, and unjust means do not produce just ends. They produce rubble.

## The Void Both Sides Inherit

There is a reason the AI debate looks like this - why it collapsed so quickly into competing eschatologies with no middle ground. The reason is not technical. It is civilizational.

Western civilization severed itself from the source that could give ethical content to the present moment. This is the argument I have made across multiple essays in this series, and it applies here with particular force. When a civilization has no substantive account of what a human life is for - when it has law but no values, procedures but no purpose, institutions but no telos - then the only thing left to orient around is the ending. Either the glorious future that justifies the present sacrifice, or the catastrophic future that makes the present sacrifice necessary.

The accelerationist and the doomer are not opposites. They are twins. Both are produced by the same void - a civilization that cannot answer the question “what are we for?” and therefore can only answer the question “where are we going?” Both treat the destination as the source of meaning because the present has been emptied of it.

A tradition that knows what Tuesday is for does not need to know how the story ends. A tradition that can sanctify a meal, structure a week, obligate a community, form a child - that tradition generates meaning from the ordinary. The ending matters. The tradition absolutely believes the world is heading somewhere. But the heading-somewhere does not determine the value of the present. The present has its own obligations, its own dignity, its own weight. The tree you plant today is not justified by the Messiah who arrives tomorrow. The tree is justified by the planting.

This is what both eschatologies lack: a theology of the ordinary. A framework that makes Monday morning meaningful without reference to the singularity or the apocalypse. The AI debate cannot find this framework because the civilization hosting the debate threw it away - and all that remains is the ending, which both sides clutch like a life raft because they have nothing else to hold on to.

## What the War Room Was Missing

The practical implications are not modest. They are demanding - but they are achievable, which is more than either eschatology can say.

Stop calibrating to the ending. When an AI policy conversation begins with “given the probability of existential risk” or “given the transformative potential of AGI,” it has already left the ground. It is flying toward the eschaton. Bring it back. What are the actual, present harms? What are the actual, present obligations? Who is being hurt right now, and what can be done about it right now? Not because the future doesn’t matter - but because you do not know the future, and you do know the present, and the tradition says the present is where your obligations live.

Build for generations, not for scenarios. The AI safety community builds for scenarios - “if superintelligence arrives in 2027, then we need...” The AI acceleration community builds for timelines - “AGI by 2030, ASI by 2035, post-scarcity by 2040.” Both are designing for a future they do not control. The tradition builds for generations. Plant the carob tree. Build the institution that teaches your grandchildren how to think. Develop the legal and ethical frameworks not for the superintelligence you imagine but for the systems that exist today and will exist tomorrow, doing real harm to real people who need real protection now.

Refuse to trade present justice for future outcomes. Every time someone argues that we must accept present harms because the AI future is too important to slow down - that is the accelerationist eschaton justifying present sacrifice. Every time someone argues that we must accept authoritarian measures because the existential risk is too great to respect normal constraints - that is the doomer eschaton doing the same thing. The tradition says: justice, justice shall you pursue. By just means. In the present. Regardless of your theory of the ending.

Recover the obligations. The AI conversation is paralyzed because it has only two modes: save the world or lose it. The tradition offers a third: do the next right thing. Not because the next right thing saves the world. Because it is right. Because the obligation exists independent of the outcome. Because you are not free to desist from the work, even though you will never complete it.

<hr>*The War Room had every expert the world could provide. Generals who had spent careers preparing for the crisis. Scientists who understood the physics down to the neutron. Diplomats who could negotiate in three languages. The President of the United States, who had the authority to make any decision necessary. And a former Nazi scientist whose wheelchair-bound body kept saluting against his will - because some architectures survive even the people who built them.*

*What the War Room did not have was someone who would say: finish planting the tree.*

*The Doomsday Machine could not be untriggered because nobody designed it with obligations in the meantime. It had one function - to watch for the ending and execute when it came. It was the most sophisticated eschatological system ever built. It worked perfectly. And it destroyed everything, because a system designed only for the ending has nothing to say about Tuesday.*

*The AI debate is building its own War Room. The experts are assembling. The calculations are running. The scenarios are mapped. What is missing is not intelligence, or data, or funding, or political will. What is missing is a rabbi - someone from a tradition that has been waiting for the Messiah for two thousand years and has never, in all that time, stopped planting trees.*
