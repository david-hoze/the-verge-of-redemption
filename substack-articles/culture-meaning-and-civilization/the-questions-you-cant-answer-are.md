# The Questions You Can’t Answer Are Not the Questions You Need to Answer

*Crimes and Misdemeanors (1989), directed by Woody Allen. Starring Martin Landau as Judah Rosenthal and Anjelica Huston as Dolores Paley. A wealthy ophthalmologist arranges the murder of his mistress — and discovers that without the moral tradition he abandoned, nothing prevents him from living comfortably with what he's done.You cannot prove, in any rigorous philosophical sense, that torturing a child for fun is wrong.*

This is not a provocation. It is a straightforward observation about the state of moral philosophy after twenty-five centuries of sustained effort. You can appeal to consequences, but then someone asks why suffering matters. You can appeal to duty, but then someone asks where duties come from. You can appeal to human dignity, but then someone asks what grounds dignity in a material universe. At every level, the conversation bottoms out in an intuition that cannot be argued for without circularity.

And yet you know — with a certainty that dwarfs your confidence in most empirical claims — that torturing a child for fun is wrong. You don’t need to solve the grounding problem to know this. You don’t need to adjudicate between Kant and Mill. You don’t need a peer-reviewed study. You know it the way you know that you exist: not because you’ve constructed an airtight argument, but because the denial is unintelligible.

This gap — between the unshakeable confidence of basic moral knowledge and the persistent inability of formal disciplines to ground it — is the starting point of this essay. The thesis is simple, and old, and increasingly urgent: philosophy and science are not the right tools for answering moral questions, not because they are bad tools, but because they are structurally limited in ways that make the task impossible. The real repositories of moral knowledge are the ancient moral traditions that have carried human civilization for millennia. And the modern impulse to discard those traditions because they cannot survive philosophical cross-examination is not enlightenment. It is epistemological suicide.

---
## I. The Philosophical Deadlock

Moral philosophy has been running for roughly 2,500 years in the Western tradition, and considerably longer if we count its antecedents in Mesopotamian, Egyptian, and Indic thought. In all that time, it has not produced consensus on a single foundational question.

**Is morality about outcomes or principles?** Utilitarians say the right action is the one that produces the most well-being. Deontologists say certain actions are right or wrong regardless of outcomes. The debate between these positions has not been resolved. It has merely been elaborated.

**Where do moral obligations come from?** Social contract theorists say from agreement. Natural law theorists say from the structure of reality. Sentimentalists say from human emotions. Constructivists say we build them. Error theorists say they don’t exist at all. Each position has sophisticated defenders. None has won.

**What makes a life good?** Hedonists say pleasure. Aristotelians say flourishing. Stoics say virtue. Existentialists say authenticity. Buddhists say liberation from craving. Confucians say right relationship. These are not converging.

**Do moral facts exist?** Moral realists say yes — there are objective truths about right and wrong. Anti-realists say no — morality is a human projection onto a value-neutral universe. This question, arguably the most fundamental in the entire discipline, remains wide open. A 2020 PhilPapers survey of professional philosophers found them split: roughly 62% lean toward moral realism, but the minority position is held by serious, credentialed thinkers who have spent careers defending it. No experiment can settle this. No argument has.

This is not a failure of intelligence or effort. The people who have worked on these problems — Plato, Aristotle, Aquinas, Hume, Kant, Mill, Nietzsche, Rawls, Parfit — are among the most brilliant minds in human history. They have not been lazy. They have not been confused. They have been working on a problem that their tools cannot solve, and the twenty-five centuries of inconclusive debate are the evidence.

The standard response from within philosophy is that the deadlock is productive — that the conversation itself refines our understanding, even if it never terminates. There is some truth in this. But there is also an unexamined assumption: that moral knowledge must take the form of philosophical propositions to count as knowledge at all. What if the deadlock is not a sign that the answers are elusive, but a sign that the questions are being asked in the wrong language?

## II. The Scientific Boundary

If philosophy cannot settle moral questions, perhaps science can. This hope has animated various intellectual movements — from Auguste Comte’s positivism in the nineteenth century to Sam Harris’s *The Moral Landscape* in the twenty-first. The intuition is natural: science is humanity’s most successful knowledge-generating enterprise. It has decoded the genome, split the atom, and mapped the cosmic microwave background. Surely it can tell us how to live.

It cannot. And the reason is not contingent but structural.

David Hume identified the core problem in 1739: you cannot derive an “ought” from an “is.” No accumulation of factual premises — however vast, however precise — logically entails a moral conclusion. You can establish that a certain policy reduces poverty. You cannot establish, by the same methods, that reducing poverty is good. That judgment requires a value premise that science, by its own methodology, cannot supply.

This is not a gap that more data can fill. It is a categorical boundary. Science investigates the empirical world — what exists, how it behaves, what causes what. Morality concerns a different domain — what should exist, how we ought to behave, what we are obligated to cause or prevent. The two domains intersect (moral decisions require factual information), but one does not reduce to the other. Knowing that cigarettes cause cancer does not tell you whether people should be free to smoke. Knowing that capital punishment deters crime (if it does) does not tell you whether the state has the right to kill. The factual claim and the moral claim live in different logical spaces.

Sam Harris attempted to bridge this gap by defining well-being as the foundation of morality and arguing that science can measure well-being. But this merely relocates the problem. Why should well-being be the foundation? Harris’s answer — that the alternative is “the worst possible misery for everyone” — is an appeal to moral intuition, not a scientific finding. He is smuggling in the value premise that his system was supposed to derive. As philosopher Patricia Churchland noted, Harris “has not in fact shown that science can determine moral values. He has shown that, once you accept a certain value (well-being), science can inform how to achieve it.” The “once you accept” is doing all the heavy lifting.

The deeper problem is that science, to function, must adopt a posture of value-neutrality toward its subject matter. A physicist does not ask whether gravity is just. A biologist does not ask whether natural selection is fair. This methodological neutrality is precisely what makes science powerful — it allows investigation unclouded by preference. But it also means that science, in its own terms, has nothing to say about what matters. It can model consequences with extraordinary precision. It cannot tell you which consequences to care about.

Some have tried to get around this through evolutionary psychology — arguing that our moral intuitions are products of natural selection and therefore have a kind of biological grounding. But this commits a well-known fallacy. The fact that evolution shaped our moral instincts does not validate them. Evolution also shaped our capacity for tribalism, aggression, and xenophobia. You cannot selectively naturalize the moral intuitions you approve of and dismiss the ones you don’t. And even if you could, “evolution selected for it” is a causal explanation, not a moral justification. Rape exists in nature. The “naturalness” of a behavior is entirely orthogonal to its rightness.

The scientific boundary is not a limitation to be overcome. It is a feature of what science is. Asking science to ground morality is like asking a telescope to taste wine. The instrument is magnificent. It is simply pointed at the wrong domain.

## III. The Philosophical Boundary — Less Obvious, More Important

The limitation of science is well-known, even if frequently ignored. The limitation of philosophy is subtler, more controversial, and ultimately more consequential.

Philosophy, unlike science, operates in the right domain. It asks the right questions — about justice, virtue, obligation, the good. It uses the right tools — reason, argument, conceptual analysis. And yet it has not arrived at answers. Why?

The standard explanation is that the questions are hard. This is true but insufficient. Physics questions are also hard, and physics makes progress. The deeper explanation is that moral knowledge involves a kind of content that is resistant to the medium of philosophical argument — not because the content is irrational, but because it is richer than what propositions can capture.

Consider an analogy. You know what it is like to taste coffee. You can describe it — bitter, aromatic, complex — but your description does not transmit the knowledge. Someone who has never tasted coffee cannot acquire the knowledge of what coffee tastes like by reading your description, no matter how precise it is. The knowledge is real. It is not mysterious. But it is experiential, and it does not reduce to propositional form.

Moral knowledge has a similar structure — not identical, but similar. When you apprehend that cruelty is wrong, you are not simply registering a logical relationship between premises. You are grasping something about the nature of cruelty and the nature of persons that resists full articulation. You can gesture at it. You can construct arguments that point toward it. But the arguments are not the knowledge itself — they are scaffolding around the knowledge, and reasonable people can reject the scaffolding while still possessing the knowledge, or accept the scaffolding while missing the point entirely.

This is why philosophical debates about morality have a peculiar structure that debates in logic or mathematics do not. In mathematics, when someone proves a theorem, the proof compels assent — not because of social pressure, but because the logical structure is transparent and complete. In moral philosophy, even the most rigorous arguments leave room for the interlocutor to say, “I see your reasoning, but I don’t accept your premise.” And the premise in question is always, at bottom, a moral intuition.

The philosopher’s dilemma is this: moral intuitions are the data of ethics. Every moral theory must account for them, and every moral theory appeals to them (even those that claim not to — the utilitarian’s conviction that suffering matters is itself an intuition). But intuitions cannot be argued for without circularity. You can show that an intuition is consistent with other intuitions, or that denying it leads to conclusions most people find abhorrent. But “most people find it abhorrent” is itself an appeal to intuition. The chain never grounds out in something harder.

This does not mean moral intuitions are arbitrary. A person who genuinely lacks the intuition that gratuitous cruelty is wrong is not exercising philosophical independence — they are exhibiting a pathology. The intuition is not culturally contingent in the way that, say, table manners are. It appears, with remarkable consistency, across every human civilization that has left records. The prohibition against murder, the obligation to care for the vulnerable, the condemnation of betrayal, the praise of courage — these are not Western constructs projected onto other cultures. They are human universals, attested in Sumerian law codes, Vedic hymns, Confucian analects, and Aboriginal dreamtime stories. C.S. Lewis documented this convergence extensively in *The Abolition of Man*, assembling parallel moral teachings from Egyptian, Norse, Babylonian, Hindu, Chinese, Jewish, Greek, Roman, Christian, and Australian Aboriginal traditions. The convergence is not perfect — but it is far more striking than the divergence.

The intuitions are real. But they are not the kind of thing that philosophy can prove. Philosophy can clarify them, test their consistency, explore their implications. It cannot establish them from scratch. And this means that philosophy, as a method, is permanently dependent on a source of moral knowledge that it did not generate and cannot fully articulate.

This is the limitation that matters. Science cannot enter the moral domain at all. Philosophy can enter it but cannot reach the bottom. The foundations of moral knowledge are pre-philosophical — not in the sense of being primitive, but in the sense of being prior. They are the ground on which philosophy stands, not the building it constructs.

## IV. What Traditions Actually Do

If the foundations of moral knowledge are pre-philosophical — if they are intuitions that resist full articulation and cannot be derived from either empirical data or logical argument — then the question becomes: where are these intuitions preserved, transmitted, and refined?

The answer is: in moral traditions.

A tradition, in the relevant sense, is not a collection of arbitrary customs. It is a multigenerational system for encoding, transmitting, testing, and refining moral knowledge. The great moral traditions — the ones that have persisted across centuries and shaped civilizations — are not philosophical theories. They are something more like operating systems for human moral life. They include rules, but they are not reducible to rules. They include reasoning, but they are not reducible to reasoning. They include stories, rituals, institutions, exemplary figures, and accumulated case law that together constitute a form of knowledge no single generation could produce and no philosophical treatise can fully capture.

Consider what a mature moral tradition contains:

**A hierarchy of values.** Not just a list of good things, but an ordering — a sense of what matters more when goods conflict. Every real moral decision involves tradeoffs, and traditions provide architectures for navigating them. “Life takes precedence over property.” “Justice tempers mercy.” “The community’s survival outweighs individual convenience.” These priority rules were not derived from first principles. They were distilled from millennia of hard cases.

**A body of case law.** Traditions do not operate primarily through abstract principles but through accumulated decisions about specific situations. “In this case, the obligation to tell the truth yielded to the obligation to protect the innocent.” The specificity matters. Abstract principles — “be honest,” “do no harm” — conflict in practice, and no amount of philosophical theorizing can resolve the conflicts in advance. What resolves them is the weight of accumulated judgment: generation after generation of people confronting the specific tensions and arriving, through lived experience, at workable resolutions.

**Mechanisms for handling uncertainty.** What do you do when you genuinely don’t know what’s right? Traditions have developed sophisticated answers. Precautionary principles for high-stakes decisions. Default rules for ambiguous cases. Hierarchies of authority for when individuals disagree. These mechanisms acknowledge that moral certainty is often unavailable — and they provide structured ways of acting well despite the uncertainty. Philosophy, by contrast, tends to treat uncertainty as a problem to be solved rather than a condition to be navigated.

**Institutions for moral formation.** Traditions do not merely state moral truths — they shape people capable of recognizing and acting on them. Apprenticeship, mentorship, ritual, communal practice, confession, study — these are technologies for moral development. They work not by transmitting propositions but by cultivating perception. A person formed within a moral tradition does not simply know a list of rules. They perceive situations differently. They notice features of a situation — the vulnerability of a person, the asymmetry of power, the weight of a promise — that an unformed person might miss entirely. This perceptual capacity is itself a form of moral knowledge, and it cannot be transmitted by argument alone.

**Feedback mechanisms.** Traditions are not static. They evolve — but they evolve slowly, through structured processes that privilege continuity while allowing adaptation. New cases arise, and the tradition’s authorities reason from existing principles to new applications. This process is conservative in the deepest sense: it conserves the accumulated wisdom of the past while extending it to novel circumstances. The result is a system that adapts without losing coherence — unlike philosophical fashions, which swing between extremes with each generation.

What traditions are doing, at a structural level, is carrying forward a body of moral knowledge that is too complex, too context-sensitive, and too dependent on cultivated perception to be reduced to a set of explicit propositions. They are the institutional memory of moral civilization. And the fact that they cannot be philosophically “justified” from the ground up is not a deficiency — it is a reflection of the kind of knowledge they carry.

## V. The Convergence Argument

A common objection to relying on moral traditions is that they disagree with each other. If traditions are the repository of moral knowledge, which tradition is right? Doesn’t the existence of conflicting traditions prove that they are culturally relative rather than genuinely tracking moral truth?

This objection overstates the disagreement and understates the convergence.

The major moral traditions of humanity agree on far more than they disagree. The prohibitions against murder, theft, lying, and betrayal are universal. The obligations to care for the weak, honor commitments, show hospitality, and restrain the powerful appear in every tradition that has been studied. The virtues of courage, justice, temperance, and compassion recur across cultures that had no contact with each other.

Where traditions diverge is not usually at the level of fundamental moral intuitions but at the level of application, emphasis, and institutional structure. They differ on questions like: How should property be distributed? What are the limits of justified violence? What obligations do we owe strangers versus kin? These are genuine disagreements, but they are disagreements within a shared moral framework, not disagreements about whether morality exists or what its broad contours are.

The analogy to perception is useful here. Different people, looking at the same landscape, notice different features. A geologist sees rock formations. A painter sees light and color. A farmer sees soil quality. They are not contradicting each other — they are attending to different aspects of the same reality. The differences in their reports are informative, not invalidating. Similarly, the fact that Confucian ethics emphasizes relational harmony while Stoic ethics emphasizes inner virtue does not prove that morality is subjective. It suggests that morality is complex enough to be approached from multiple angles, each of which captures something real.

The deepest convergence is not at the level of specific rules but at the level of what might be called moral seriousness — the shared conviction that how we treat each other matters, that some actions are genuinely wrong regardless of what we feel about them, that human beings have obligations they did not choose and cannot discard. This conviction is so widespread, so persistent, and so independent of local cultural conditions that dismissing it as a contingent evolutionary artifact requires ignoring the testimony of virtually every civilization that has existed.

## VI. The Modern Danger

We are living through an experiment: what happens when a civilization decides that moral traditions are obsolete and that morality can be rebuilt from scratch using the tools of philosophy and science?

The results are not encouraging.

The twentieth century — the century most committed to the Enlightenment project of grounding morality in reason alone — produced the most systematic atrocities in human history. The Holocaust was perpetrated by the most philosophically sophisticated nation in Europe. The Soviet gulags were justified by a theory of historical progress. The Chinese Cultural Revolution was explicitly aimed at destroying traditional moral frameworks in favor of a “scientific” social order. Mao’s Great Leap Forward, driven by ideological certainty and contempt for traditional agricultural wisdom, killed an estimated 30 to 45 million people.

This is not to say that Enlightenment philosophy caused these atrocities — the causal story is far more complex. But it is to observe that the removal of traditional moral constraints did not produce the rational utopia the Enlightenment promised. It produced a vacuum that ideological systems rushed to fill, systems unchecked by the accumulated wisdom and institutional restraints that traditions provide.

The contemporary version of this experiment is subtler but no less consequential. We are not replacing moral traditions with totalitarian ideologies. We are replacing them with nothing — with a therapeutic culture of individual preference, where “what feels right to me” has become the operative moral standard. The philosopher Alasdair MacIntyre diagnosed this condition in *After Virtue* (1981): modern moral discourse has become “interminable” because we have retained the vocabulary of morality — rights, justice, duty — while severing it from the traditions that gave those terms determinate meaning. We are speaking a language whose grammar we have forgotten.

The consequences are visible in every domain. In bioethics, we cannot agree on the moral status of embryos, the limits of genetic engineering, or the ethics of assisted dying — not because these are new questions (traditions have addressed all of them), but because we have decided that traditional answers are inadmissible and have found nothing to replace them. In technology, we build systems of enormous power — artificial intelligence, surveillance infrastructure, social media platforms — with no coherent moral framework to govern them. Corporate ethics boards publish principles that have, by empirical measurement, no effect on developer behavior. International guidelines proliferate and are universally ignored.

The pattern is consistent: when you remove the moral traditions and try to rebuild from “reason alone,” you do not get a more rational morality. You get moral paralysis on questions that matter and moral confidence only on questions that don’t.

## VII. The Objections

Several objections to this argument are serious enough to deserve direct responses.

**“Traditions are oppressive.”** Some traditional moral rules are indeed oppressive — they have been used to justify slavery, the subjugation of women, and the persecution of minorities. This is true and important. But it does not invalidate the entire category of traditional moral knowledge any more than the existence of bad science invalidates the scientific enterprise. Traditions contain errors. They also contain self-correcting mechanisms — internal resources for critique and reform. The abolition of slavery was driven, in significant part, by people working within moral traditions who argued that the tradition’s own deepest commitments were incompatible with the institution. The civil rights movement in America was led by a Baptist minister citing Scripture, not by a philosopher citing Kant.

The right response to oppressive elements within traditions is not to discard tradition as a category but to do what traditions have always done: subject specific rules to scrutiny in light of deeper principles. This is reform, not rejection. And it requires the tradition to remain intact as a living system, because the deeper principles that ground the reform are themselves carried by the tradition.

**“We can’t go back.”** This objection assumes that relying on moral traditions means returning to a pre-modern world. It does not. It means recognizing that the moral knowledge encoded in traditions is real, irreplaceable, and not superseded by anything modernity has produced. You can fly in airplanes and use antibiotics while still recognizing that your grandmother was right about honesty. Modernity is a technological and institutional achievement. It is not a moral achievement. The assumption that newer moral ideas are better moral ideas has no more warrant than the assumption that newer music is better music.

**“Which tradition?”** As discussed in Section V, this objection overstates the divergence between traditions. But it also misunderstands the claim. The argument is not that one particular tradition has all the answers. It is that the category of tradition — multigenerational moral systems refined through centuries of lived experience — is epistemically superior to individual philosophical reasoning or scientific investigation when it comes to moral questions. You can learn from multiple traditions. You can compare their answers. What you cannot do — what has been tried and has failed — is replace them all with a system built from scratch.

**“Intuitions are unreliable.”** Moral intuitions can be wrong. People have had strong intuitions supporting terrible practices. But the unreliability of individual intuitions is precisely why traditions matter — they function as aggregators and refiners of intuition across generations. A single person’s moral intuition is fallible. The converging intuitions of billions of people across thousands of years, tested against real consequences and refined through structured debate, constitute something far more robust. Not infallible — but far more reliable than any individual philosopher’s theory or any algorithm’s optimization function.

**“This is just an appeal to authority.”** It is an appeal to a specific kind of authority — the authority of accumulated experience. This is not the same as blind deference to whoever happens to be in charge. It is the recognition that some kinds of knowledge are inherently cumulative and communal — that no individual, however brilliant, can replicate in a lifetime what a tradition produces over millennia. We accept this in every other domain. No one thinks they can reinvent mathematics from scratch. No one thinks their personal experience of disease is a substitute for the accumulated knowledge of medicine. The insistence that every individual must independently derive their moral framework from first principles is an anomaly — a demand we make in no other area of knowledge.

## VIII. The Humility We Need

The deepest point of this essay is epistemological, not moral. It is about what kinds of knowledge exist and how they are transmitted.

The Enlightenment bet — the bet that human reason, operating independently of tradition, could ground morality — has been running for roughly three centuries. The returns are in. Reason operating independently of tradition does not ground morality. It generates an endless sequence of competing theories, none of which can establish its superiority over the others, all of which depend, at their foundations, on the very intuitions they were supposed to replace.

This is not a failure of reason. It is a discovery about the limits of reason — specifically, about the limits of explicit, propositional, individually derivable reason. There are things that human beings know — really know, not just feel — that cannot be captured in the form of philosophical arguments or scientific findings. These things are not irrational. They are not primitive. They are not arbitrary. But they are carried by traditions — by stories and rituals and institutions and case law and communal practices — because that is the kind of vehicle this kind of knowledge requires.

The appropriate response is not to abandon reason. It is to abandon the pretense that reason is self-sufficient. Reason is an extraordinary tool for clarifying, testing, and extending moral knowledge. It is not a tool for generating moral knowledge from nothing. The raw material comes from somewhere else — from the pre-philosophical moral perception that traditions cultivate and carry.

We do not need to solve the trolley problem to know that we should not murder. We do not need to resolve the debate between moral realism and anti-realism to know that we should keep our promises. We do not need a unified theory of well-being to know that we should feed the hungry. These are not conclusions waiting for premises. They are starting points — and the traditions that have carried them, tested them, refined them, and transmitted them for millennia deserve not our condescension but our gratitude, and our attention.

The questions that philosophy cannot answer are not the questions we need to answer. The questions we need to answer — how should I live? what do I owe others? what matters? — have been answered, imperfectly but substantively, by every serious moral tradition that has shaped human civilization. The answers are not written in philosophical journals. They are written in the lives of people who lived by them, in the communities those people built, and in the traditions those communities bequeathed to us.

We would do well to listen.
