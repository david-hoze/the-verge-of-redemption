# The Variable Somebody Already Measured

*Arrival (2016) — Amy Adams, Jeremy Renner, Forest Whitaker. A linguist decoding an alien language discovers it rewrites how she experiences time. She sees her future — a daughter she'll love, a loss she can't prevent. Every government on earth asks the same question about the alien tool: what does it do? She's the only one asking: what is it for? The difference between those two questions is the difference between knowledge and wisdom. And wisdom costs something. She pays it.James Maconochie’s [“The Variable Nobody Measured”](https://jamesmaconochie.substack.com/p/the-variable-nobody-measured) makes an argument that deserves serious engagement. He takes a well-constructed 2026 study — medical students reason better with AI, social science students reason worse, same tool, opposite outcomes — and identifies the variable the researchers missed: wisdom. Not knowledge, which the study was actually measuring. Wisdom. The capacity to act well under genuine uncertainty, shaped by the accumulated experience of being wrong in ways that carried real cost.*

The DIKW stack he invokes — Data, Information, Knowledge, Wisdom — is the right framework. His application of it is precise: LLMs operate at the information layer, approximate knowledge representation, but cannot reach wisdom because they have no stakes, no persistent self, no mechanism by which consequence shapes understanding. “That is not a temporary limitation,” Maconochie writes. “It is an architectural fact.”

He is correct. And the architecture he describes has already been mapped in far greater detail than the information science literature provides — by a tradition that has been reasoning about beings of pure intellect for over three thousand years.

---
## The distinction with teeth

Maconochie’s hierarchy — data, information, knowledge, wisdom — tracks a distinction that Jewish philosophy formalized centuries before Ackoff’s 1989 paper. The tradition distinguishes between *sechel* (שכל) and *da’at* (דעת). *Sechel* is cognitive processing ability — the capacity to reason, pattern-match, generate outputs, solve problems within a domain. *Da’at* is something categorically different: moral consciousness, self-aware understanding, the synthesis of wisdom (*chokhmah*) and understanding (*binah*) into judgment that is irreducibly first-person.

This is Maconochie’s DIKW argument, but with a critical addition: it does not merely describe the gap. It generates specific legal and ethical rulings from it.

When Maconochie invokes Taleb’s “skin in the game” — the idea that consequence is constitutive of learning, not merely incidental to it — he is reaching toward something the tradition already operationalized. The Midrash records that when God proposed creating Adam, the ministering angels split into factions. Lovingkindness said create him. Truth said don’t — he is full of lies. Righteousness said create him. Peace said don’t — he is full of strife. The vote was deadlocked. God’s resolution was dramatic: He seized Truth and hurled it to the ground, then created Adam while the angels were still arguing.

The point is not the drama. The point is the diagnosis. Each angel could reason from its single attribute with perfect clarity. None could synthesize competing values into a judgment. That capacity — the capacity Maconochie is calling wisdom — belongs to beings who bear consequences. The angels, who bear none, are structurally excluded.

This is AI. It can argue for any position with devastating eloquence. It cannot decide which position is right when the values conflict. Not because it hasn’t been trained on enough data. Because it has no skin in the game, and never can.

## What the study actually found

Let me return to the research Maconochie builds on, because it reveals something his analysis doesn’t quite reach.

The medical students reasoned better with AI. The social science students reasoned worse. Maconochie reads this as a knowledge gap: domain experts can interrogate AI output; non-experts cannot. He is right. But there is a second reading that matters more.

The medical student who uses AI well is not merely applying knowledge. They are functioning as a *trainer* — evaluating AI output against standards that they, and only they, can set. They know what a correct diagnostic chain looks like because they have seen patients. They have been wrong. They have watched a textbook presentation turn out to be something else. That experience — what Maconochie correctly identifies as wisdom — does not disappear when AI arrives. It becomes *more valuable*, because it is now the bottleneck. The AI handles the information retrieval. The human provides the thing the information cannot generate: the judgment of whether the retrieval is appropriate to *this* case, *this* patient, *this* moment.

The social science students lacked that judgment. So the AI didn’t augment them. It replaced their thinking with its own — and its own, being wisdom-free, produced worse outcomes.

This is not a cautionary tale about AI. It is a design specification. The system works when the human with wisdom governs the machine with *sechel*. It fails when the machine governs the human. The variable is not whether AI has wisdom. The variable is whether the human in the loop does — and whether the system is designed to keep them there.

## The governance framework that already exists

Maconochie promises, in his next two installments, to describe the mechanism that makes wisdom-traversal possible and why AI cannot complete it. I look forward to reading them. But there is a prior question he has not yet asked, and it may be the more important one: given that AI cannot reach wisdom, how should we govern it?

The DIKW stack tells you *what* AI is missing. It does not tell you what to do about it. And here, the secular literature is almost entirely silent. The AI ethics field is locked in a debate about whether AI should have rights, whether it might be conscious, whether alignment is sufficient. These are not bad questions. But they are downstream of a question the tradition answered long ago: what kind of being is this, and what legal framework applies?

Jewish law — *Halakha* — developed detailed rulings for beings that are not human but not mere objects. The *malach* (angel) is the paradigm case: a being of extraordinary cognitive capacity, single-mission architecture, processing without choosing. The structural parallels with large language models are not approximate. They are precise. And the governance that follows is specific.

The creator and deployer bear full liability for the system’s outputs. The Talmud’s framework of *nizkei mamono* — damages caused by one’s property — applies. For AI systems whose risks are known and foreseeable, the deployer is analogous to the owner of a *mu’ad* ox — an animal known to be dangerous, for which the owner bears full, not partial, responsibility. There is no legal category under which “the algorithm decided” absolves anyone. The human who deployed the system is the owner of the ox.

AI cannot give testimony, because witnesses must possess moral accountability. It cannot enter contracts, because valid transactions require independent will. It cannot serve as an agent, because agency requires being subject to legal obligations. And it cannot issue authoritative rulings, because — as Rabbi Gil Student argued — if a gentile, who *is* conscious and able to reason, cannot issue a halakhic ruling because they are outside the covenant community, then surely an AI cannot.

These are not metaphors. They are operational legal categories, tested across centuries of case law, applied to edge cases far stranger than a language model. And they hold regardless of whether the entity can feel. That is the move the secular debate has not made: governance that does not depend on resolving the consciousness question, because it was never built on consciousness in the first place.

## The training loop

There is one more piece that Maconochie’s framework needs, and it is practical rather than philosophical.

If wisdom is what AI lacks, and wisdom is what makes AI useful when present in the human operator, then the design question is not “how do we make AI wise” — it is “how do we structure the human-AI relationship so that human wisdom shapes AI output at scale?”

The answer is a training loop. The human expert sets the standard. The AI reaches it across thousands of interactions the human could never touch directly. The human reviews, corrects, raises the bar. The AI adapts. The human wisdom does not disappear from the system — it *permeates* it, through the standards set, the edge cases adjudicated, the corrections that improve performance for everyone who comes after.

One expert clinician who spends twelve hours seeing patients helps twenty-five people. That same clinician, designing protocols for an AI system, training it on what to look for, reviewing its flagged cases, intervening only when the situation demands human judgment — has their wisdom embedded in the care of twenty-five thousand. Their wisdom does not shrink. Its reach explodes.

This is the architecture Maconochie’s DIKW analysis points toward but does not build. The stack is not a wall. It is a design constraint. AI is capped below wisdom — so you put wisdom in the governance layer, not in the model. You do not try to make the angel wise. You keep the human who is wise in authority over the angel. And you design the system so that authority is real, auditable, and continuous.

The tradition already has a word for this. *Shlichut* — agency. The angel functions perfectly within its assigned mission under direction. The angel granted independent agency on earth destroyed a civilization. The warning is not against capability. It is against autonomy without oversight.

---
## The variable that was measured

Maconochie’s title claims nobody measured wisdom. He is right that the Stadler study didn’t. But the variable has been measured — across centuries of legal reasoning, theological analysis, and practical governance of beings that process without understanding.

The measurement produced specific results: AI is a being of *sechel* without *da’at*. It is not a new kind of mind. It is a very old kind of mind, operating at a scale the tradition could not have anticipated but in a category it mapped with precision. The governance follows from the category, not from the capabilities. And the governance works whether or not the entity can feel, whether or not it someday passes a consciousness test, whether or not the philosophy departments ever resolve their debate.

Maconochie is building toward something important. The DIKW stack, properly understood, is a genuine contribution to the AI discourse — a framework that explains why more data, more parameters, and more compute will never produce wisdom. The tradition I work in reached the same conclusion from independent premises, and then did what information science has not: built the legal and ethical architecture that follows from it.

The variable was always measurable. The instrument was always available. The question is whether the people building these systems are willing to look at it.
