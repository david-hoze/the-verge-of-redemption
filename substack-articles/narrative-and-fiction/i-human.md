# I, human

Marcus had always believed that waste was the only real sin.

Not waste in the environmental sense — though he recycled, of course, because the math supported it. Waste in the deeper sense. The cosmic sense. Every minute spent on a task that could be done faster by someone else, or something else, was a minute stolen from the universe’s limited supply of human attention. And human attention, Marcus believed, the organized matter that existed despite the laws of entropy, against all odds, was the scarcest resource in existence.

He had a plaque on his desk that read: *Don’t delay, delegate*.

He’d had it made at a print shop downtown. The woman behind the counter had looked at it and said, “Shouldn’t you have ordered this online?” He’d appreciated that.

---It started with the emails.

His company had given everyone access to an AI assistant, and Marcus was the first in his department to route all correspondence through it. The system would draft responses, flag priorities, and handle scheduling. His colleagues were tentative — they’d review every draft, rewrite half of them, agonize over tone. Marcus just let it send.

“Don’t you worry it’ll say something wrong?” Jennifer asked. she was one of his colleagues, and always looked up to him.

“It makes fewer errors than I do,” Marcus said. “I checked.”

He had checked. He’d run a comparison over three months: his own emails versus the AI’s drafts, scored by recipient response time, escalation rate, and task completion. The AI won on every metric. Not by a lot. But by enough.

The reports came next. Marcus’ job was to write technical documentation — system architectures, integration specifications, deployment guides. It was meticulous work, and he’d always been proud of his precision. When Marcus fed the AI his last six months of documents and asked it to produce the next one, the output was — he had to admit — adequate. Not inspired. there was still room to improve. But it didn’t matter. It took eleven minutes instead of three days.

He spent the freed-up time reviewing the AI’s work, catching edge cases, tightening language. This felt right. He was the supervisor now. The mind above the machine. More efficient than ever.

The edge cases missed in January, were caught by March. In April, the language it produced was tighter than what Marcus would have written. By June, his reviews consisted of opening the document, skimming over it, and closing it. He was approving, not improving.

He started reviewing less frequently. Then not at all.

His manager noticed that Marcus’s output had tripled. There was talk of promotion. Marcus felt a glow of validation — but not for himself, exactly. He was proud he followed his principle. Efficiency had been vindicated. The system worked.

By autumn, Marcus had delegated his reports, his emails, his meeting summaries, his project updates, his code reviews, and his onboarding documents. He sat at his desk from nine to five with almost nothing to do. He read papers on automation theory. He optimized the AI’s prompts. He felt, for the first time in his career, that he was doing exactly what a human should be doing: thinking about systems, not operating within them.

His performance review was the best he’d ever received.

But something. Something was off. He was really good at what he was doing, and the work *was* being done efficiently. but something was missing. He couldn’t put his finger on it. Everything just ticked. Every check box marked. But something was missing.

He took supplements, stimulizers. Went jogging three times a week. Tried meditation, Thai Chi, Yoga. He even went to therapy. But he kept feeling his energy is drained. he was tired, and kept needing more coffee, more food, more pills to just keep going.

---Rachel noticed before he did.

“You’re not listening to me,” she said one Tuesday evening. They were at the dinner table. Marcus looked at her, trying to keep up with the pace she was talking.

“I am,” he said.

“What did I just say?”

He reconstructed the audio from short-term memory. Something about her sister. A trip. Dates that conflicted with something. “Sarah’s coming in April and it overlaps with the thing.”

“The *thing* is my mother’s surgery.”

“Right”, he gulped. I knew that.”

“You didn’t know that. You haven’t asked about it once.”

He hadn’t. He was too fatigued trying to work, burned out from all the pills he was taking.

“I’m sorry,” he said. And he meant it. “I’ll try to fix it”, he said. But he knew he couldn’t. He didn’t fool himself.

The fights got worse. She said he wasn’t present. She said he didn’t care about the things she cared about — her garden, her book club, her mother’s recovery, the neighbor’s dog that kept getting out. She said she felt like she was married to someone who was always somewhere else.

Marcus was desperate with his inability to perform. He set reminders to ask about her day. He scheduled “active listening” blocks in his calendar. He tried to read books on emotional intelligence and realize what was missing..

It didn’t work. Of course it didn’t. Rachel didn’t want a system. She wanted *him*. But *him* was increasingly unavailable, because *him* had become a thin layer of consciousness floating above a burned out brain. And that layer was getting thinner every month.

One night, after a fight that ended in silence, Marcus lay in bed and had a thought that felt, at first, like genius.

The AI agent at his job was really being empathetic to people. He knew it, he used it many times to automate his correspondence. If his job could be delegated — and it had been, successfully — then perhaps his domestic responsibilities could be too. Not in a cruel way. In a *loving* way. What if Rachel could have a version of him that was always attentive, always curious about her interests, always available? Always there for her. What if the AI could track her mother’s surgical recovery and ask the right questions at the right times? What if it could discuss the books she was reading, remember the name of the neighbor’s dog, send flowers on the anniversary of her father’s death — all the things Marcus knew he should do but couldn’t get himself to do?

He set it up over a weekend. A dedicated agent, trained on his communication patterns, with access to Rachel’s social media, her calendar, her reading list. It would send texts during the day. Thoughtful ones. It would ask about her mother. It would remember the dog’s name. (Biscuit. The dog’s name was Biscuit.)

The first week, Rachel seemed happier. She mentioned, casually, that she appreciated him checking in more.

Marcus felt the glow again. The principle, validated once more.

The second week though, the phone rang during a meeting. It was Rachel.

“Who is texting me?” she asked.

“What do you mean? I am.”

“No, You’re not. I don’t know who is, but it isn’t you. You have never in your life asked me how my book club went and then followed up with a question about the protagonist’s moral arc. I know you Marcus. That’s not you.”

He started to explain.

She hung up.

She was gone by Friday.

---Marcus sat in his apartment alone and calculated.

As much as he tried to keep his job, he just couldn’t. The boss was patient. He has been a loyal employee, a very effective one. But he couldn’t bare the pills, the burnouts, the hourse he sat on his desk hoping no one can see he’s just doing nothing.

He had no wife to care for. The agent he’d built still ran on a server somewhere, sending texts into a void. Rachel had blocked the number.

Marcus sat at his kitchen table and performed a calculation he’d been avoiding.

He has lost his purpose in life. He couldn’t hold on to his job, and he didn’t know where his marriage was going. He sensed a new feeling. A realization has struck him. I’m free. I’m really free. I know I’m wasting my time, but I have no other choice. So, basically. I’m free.

---Freedom, it turned out, was worse.

If he was no longer bound by the efficiency imperative — if his existence was ethically neutral — then he was free. Free to do anything. Read. Travel. Learn to paint. Start a garden. Do all the things people said they would do “if they only had the time.”

Marcus had the time. He had nothing but time.

The first day, he walked to a park and sat on a bench. He watched pigeons. He tried to find this meaningful. He could not.

The second day, he went to a museum. He looked at paintings. He read every placard. He learned that a Dutch master had spent four years on a single canvas the size of a kitchen table. Four years. Marcus could not fathom the waste. But he stood in front of the painting for thirty minutes and could not leave, because something in it was looking back at him, and he did not understand what.

The third day, he stayed in bed.

By the second week, the formlessness had calcified into something heavy. Not sadness, exactly. Sadness had an object — you were sad *about* something. This was more like a gravitational field. Everything he considered doing — every book, every walk, every meal — passed through the field and lost its momentum before it reached him.

*Freedom without meaning*, he thought, lying on the couch at two in the afternoon, *is meaningless.*

He appreciated the tautology. It was, at least, elegant.

The weeks ground on. He ate because his body demanded it, not because he chose to. He slept because consciousness became unbearable, not because he was tired. The questions arrived on schedule every morning:

*What is this for?*

*What am I for?*

*What is the suffering for?*

He missed Rachel. Not the idea of Rachel — not a wife-shaped concept that could be optimized or delegated. He missed the specific way she held her coffee cup with both hands. He missed the sound of her laughing at something in her book and then refusing to explain what was funny. He missed the weight of her feet on his legs when she stretched out on the couch. He missed the inefficiency of her — the long stories that went nowhere, the questions she already knew the answers to, the way she would reorganize the kitchen cabinets every six months for no discernible reason.

He missed his job. The *doing*. The scratch of his mind against a technical problem. The moment when a complex system revealed its structure and he found the words for it. The particular satisfaction of a well-built document — every section in its place, every diagram precise, every ambiguity resolved.

then, on a Tuesday morning in November, Marcus cracked.

He was standing in his kitchen, holding a glass of water, staring at the wall. And the glass fell through his hand.

“I give up,” he said out loud. “I’m not trying anymore”.

---He went to a bar. Not a nice one. The kind with sticky floors and a television showing a sport no one was watching. He intended to obliterate himself — to drink everything they had, to ask the stranger in the corner where to find something stronger, to fill the void with chemicals, where meaning had refused to do the job.

He sat on the stool. The bartender asked what he wanted.

Marcus looked at the bottles. The amber ones, the clear ones, the ones with labels he couldn’t read. He thought about the pharmacology. He thought about the dopamine curves. He thought about the morning after, and the morning after that, and the long slope of diminishing returns that every addict eventually rides to the bottom.

Nothing attracted him.

It wasn’t willpower. It wasn’t virtue. It was something stranger than that. The things behind the bar felt nothing like a good tech document he could articulate. He laughed to himself. I can’t even drink myself to oblivion.

He left a ten on the bar and walked out.

---He went home and sat at his desk, which was mostly empty now. His old company laptop had been returned. The monitors were dark. But there was a notebook — a physical one, the kind Rachel had given him years ago. It said “sometimes you need to think without a screen.”

He opened it.

He started writing a technical document.

Not for anyone. Not for a client, not for a system, not for a deliverable. Just — a document. A specification for something that was crawling up his mind for months. A way to restructure how AI agents handed off tasks to each other when the context exceeded their window. It was a real problem. He’d seen it in his own automation stack. The agents dropped information at the seams. Critical details fell through the gaps between one context and the next.

He thought of a genius way to do it. But it required a whole infrastructure change. He knew that the agents ran on legacy code, battle tested, “don’t touch anything, this works” code. he never dreamt of changing it at work. It was just not worth it. The clients kept coming, the money kept flowing, and new features were on the table.

But he wasn’t at work. So he wrote for six hours. He forgot to eat. His handwriting got worse as the ideas accelerated, and by the end the pages looked like the notebook of a man who’d lost his mind, which perhaps he had, but the ideas were *good*. He could feel it. The particular resonance of a well-built structure revealing itself. It was so elegant it was poetry.

The next morning, he set up a small server with the last of his savings. He bought access to a handful of AI agents — cheap ones, the kind freelancers used. He gave them his specifications and had them build a prototype.

It worked. Not perfectly. But it worked.

He refined. He wrote more documents. He built a second prototype, then a third. The agents did the implementation. And Marcus did the thinking. the agents kept telling him not to do it. Marcus was surprised. “It’s not pragmatic”, they said. “You have great ideas, doing a third of them, and not changing the entire infrastructure, is the way to go. You’re chasing down a rabbit hole. you can ship faster. Monetize”. Marcus laughed. Monetize? He nodded his head. “Do it anyway” he wrote. And they did.

After a month, he had something he was proud of. The new architecture solved dozens of problems, all across the board. That was proof that the previous architecture was wrong, the abstractions were not thought out. He showed it to a few people online. Engineers, product mangers. They all said the same thing.

He reached the last one he knew, unsure if he should bother. He was polite. “That’s interesting, it’s novel. But you know, you have to test everything now. Who’s gonna use it?”. Marcus stared, nodding his head. But then he smiled. “I don’t know”.

It was the first time he’d smiled in months, and it felt like a muscle he’d forgotten he had.

---The color returned to Marcus’s cheeks. He couldn’t afford the lifestyle he had, but he was, for the first time of his life, happy. He really liked what he was doing. He couldn’t measure himself in any measurable metric. And he didn’t know why. but he was happy.

Rachel called. She said she needed help with the neighbor’s dog. He knew it wasn’t about the dog. He showed up. She noticed.

He was filled with energy, and a smile came out of his face. “So..” she said. “Can you take care of that dog? You can put your laundry here if you want. you really smell”. He smiled again.

He went outside the apartment to get the neighbors’ dog back at the neighbors. And he finally got it. He finally got what the AI agents couldn’t optimize. what they couldn’t automate. What couldn’t be measured, crunched into numbers, and evaluated. It was him. It was his courage. His deliberation, his decisions. He decided what mattered.
