# The Hand That Opens Empty

*Why your wanting system was never designed to deliver on its promises - and what a rabbi's joke about the evil inclination reveals about the neuroscience of desire*

*The Wizard of Oz (1939). Directed by Victor Fleming. Judy Garland, Frank Morgan, Ray Bolger, Bert Lahr, Jack Haley, Margaret Hamilton. A tornado lifts a girl from gray Kansas into a world of saturated color. She follows a yellow brick road to an Emerald City where a great and powerful wizard will grant whatever she needs. She gathers companions - a Scarecrow who wants a brain, a Tin Man who wants a heart, a Lion who wants courage. They arrive. Toto pulls back the curtain. A small, ordinary man is working levers. He has nothing. He opens his hand and there is nothing in it.*

---

## The brighter world

Victor Fleming knew something about the brain that neuroscience wouldn't articulate for sixty years. Kansas is gray. Oz is color. The imagined world - the promised world, the world at the end of the yellow brick road - is literally more vivid than the real one. Dorothy doesn't walk toward a better place. She walks toward a brighter picture. And when the wizard is finally revealed as a small man working levers behind a curtain, he gives his petitioners exactly what they asked for - a diploma, a testimonial, a ticking clock-heart. Paper. Metal. Hot air. The gifts are placebos. They change nothing. But somehow, inexplicably, they work.

Here is what nobody tells you about wanting: the thing you want is more vivid than the thing you get.

Not sometimes. Always. Not as a poetic observation about the human condition. As a finding replicated across three decades of neuroscience, psychology, and behavioral economics so consistently that the researchers who study it have stopped being surprised.

In 1998, Kent Berridge and Terry Robinson at the University of Michigan published a paper that should have rewritten every self-help book on the shelf. They demonstrated that the brain system responsible for wanting and the brain system responsible for liking are anatomically and functionally separate. Wanting runs on dopamine. Liking runs on opioids. They can be independently activated and independently destroyed. You can want something intensely without liking it when you get it. You can like something without wanting it again. The two systems are not early and late stages of the same process. They are different processes serving different purposes.

The wanting system generates what Berridge calls "incentive salience" - a vivid internal representation of the reward that is designed to make you pursue it. The representation is brighter, more saturated, more compelling than the actual experience of obtaining the reward. This is not a malfunction. This is the design specification. The wanting system evolved to make you chase, not to make you satisfied. Satisfaction would stop the chase. A satisfied animal does not look for food. A satisfied animal gets eaten.

Daniel Gilbert and Timothy Wilson, in a landmark 2005 paper, showed that human beings systematically mispredict how future events will make them feel. We overestimate the intensity. We overestimate the duration. We imagine the future in higher resolution than reality delivers. Gilbert called this "affective forecasting error." Kahneman called the underlying mechanism the "focusing illusion": nothing in life is as important as you think it is while you are thinking about it. The very act of attending to a future event inflates its significance beyond what the event can bear when it arrives.

Philip Brickman demonstrated this in 1978 with a study so clean it became a foundational text. He compared lottery winners with paralysis victims. Within a year, lottery winners were no happier than controls. Within a year, paralysis victims were not as unhappy as anyone predicted. The hedonic treadmill resets everything. You get what you wanted. The wanting was in Technicolor. The having is in sepia. The dopamine system produces Oz. Reality produces Kansas. You spend your whole life trying to get back to the color.

## The rabbi's joke

In the early 1800s, Rebbe Nachman of Breslov taught a Torah (Likutey Moharan 25) that his student Reb Noson considered one of the most important he ever recorded. It is an extended analysis of a faculty Nachman calls the *koach hamedameh* - the faculty of imagination. Not imagination as in creativity. Imagination as in the thing that makes you see what isn't there.

The teaching opens with a declaration: "Every person must extract themselves from the medameh and ascend to the sechel" - from the imaginative faculty to the intellect. When a person is drawn after the medameh, Nachman says, this is *sherirut lev* - stubbornness of the heart. Not stubbornness as in willfulness. Stubbornness as in the heart following where imagination leads, regardless of what the intellect knows. The person who follows the medameh of the heart does not follow imagined desires because they've reasoned their way there. They follow them because the picture is brighter than reality. The medameh produces Technicolor. The sechel produces Kansas. The heart, untrained, follows the color.

After delivering this teaching - after naming the medameh as the source of all desire, all sin, all distortion - Nachman paused. And then, according to Reb Noson, he said something extraordinary.

"We need to give the yetzer hara a new name." The yetzer hara - the evil inclination, the adversary, the force that Jewish tradition holds responsible for every wrong turn a person takes. "Don't call it 'evil inclination' anymore. Call it the *koach hamedameh*."

Reb Noson records: "He said this as a joke (b'derech s'chok), but I understood that he had a complete intention behind it, and I did not merit to understand his intention."

The intention is this: desire and imagination are not two things. They are one thing. The yetzer hara does not use imagination as a tool. It IS imagination. What you want is what you imagine. The wanting is the imagining. The dopamine system does not merely assist the pursuit of rewards - it generates an internal picture of the reward that is systematically more vivid than reality, and you chase the picture. This is not a Western insight waiting for an Eastern framework to validate it. It is a clinical observation made two centuries before the neuroscience caught up, by a man who renamed the oldest adversary in Jewish tradition because he realized it was wearing the wrong name.

## The spirit that never got a body

The Torah goes further. Not only does Nachman identify what the medameh is - he tells you where it came from.

"The koach hamedameh was created on Friday at twilight," he says, drawing on the Zohar, "and the day became sanctified before it was given a body. It is spirit without body." The medameh was the last thing created before Shabbat. Time ran out. The body was never built. So the medameh - this faculty that generates vivid pictures of things that don't exist - wanders the world as pure spirit, looking for a vessel to inhabit.

"Therefore this koach hamedameh, which is spirit without body, seeks for itself a body to inhabit, and goes to the Torah scholars, because they create through their words of Torah, so it goes to rest upon them so that a body will be created for it through them."

Read that again. The medameh seeks out the people who create through language. It goes where words build worlds. Because words can give it what God didn't finish giving it: a body.

If you are reading this on a screen, you are looking at the body. Social media is the vessel the medameh spent six thousand years waiting for. Curated profiles. Filtered images. Algorithmic feeds calibrated to the dopamine system. Imagined lives. Imagined versions of other people's happiness. Pure imagination with no physical reality - a spirit that finally, in the twenty-first century, found a body to inhabit.

Verduyn et al. (2015) demonstrated that passive social media use - scrolling, watching, consuming without engaging - significantly predicts declines in well-being. Not because the content is harmful. Because the content is imagined. You are watching a Technicolor Oz that someone else curated, and your Kansas looks grayer by comparison. The medameh never had a body this powerful. It never had a distribution system this efficient. It can now enter every home, every pocket, every moment of downtime, and do what it was created to do: show you a vivid picture of something that doesn't exist, and make you chase it.

## The hand that opens

There is a passage in the Sichot Haran - Reb Noson's record of Nachman's informal conversations - that says everything the neuroscience says, in three sentences and an image.

"To each person it seems (*nidmeh* - same root as medameh) as if [the yetzer hara] holds what he desires. Everyone runs after him, because each one thinks there is in his hand what he wants. And afterward he opens his hand - and there is nothing in it."

*Nidmeh*. It seems. It appears. It is imagined. The very word for "it seems to you" is built from the same root as the faculty of imagination. The grammar of the language is telling you: what seems true and what is imagined are the same operation. When you say "it seems to me that this will make me happy," you are literally saying "I am imagining that this will make me happy." Hebrew doesn't distinguish between the two. Because there is no distinction.

"Exactly so is the yetzer hara: it deceives the entire world, and everyone runs after it, and it deceives each and every person until each one imagines that in his hand is what he desires, each according to his foolishness and craving. And at the end it opens its hand and there is nothing in it, because no one can fill his desire through it."

Each according to his foolishness. Each according to his craving. The picture is different for every person. The Scarecrow wants a brain. The Tin Man wants a heart. The Lion wants courage. The medameh customizes. It shows each person exactly what they're missing. Not the same thing - their thing. The specific absence that aches. And everyone runs, because the hand appears to hold exactly that.

Then Nachman adds the image that should stop every scroll:

"All the desires of the world are like pillars of sunlight that enter a house. They seem like pillars from the sunlight's radiance. When you try to grasp them - you grab and snatch - and there is nothing inside your hand. Such are all the desires of this world."

Pillars of sunlight. They look structural. They look solid. Light makes them visible. Dust makes them vivid. A child reaches for one. Their hand closes on nothing. An adult reaches for the promotion, the body, the house, the recognition. Their hand closes on nothing. Not because the light isn't real - the light is real. But a pillar of light is not a pillar. It has no weight. It supports nothing. You cannot build on it.

Brickman's lottery winners, one year later, no happier than controls. The hand opened. There was nothing in it.

## Every level has its own Oz

Here is where the teaching becomes devastating.

"In every world and at every level," Nachman says, "there are these dimyonot - these imaginations - and they are the klipot (husks) that precede the fruit. When a person moves from level to level, the klipot of the new level awaken and surround them. They must subdue them anew."

The hedonic treadmill is not a bug. It is a feature of the architecture. Each level has its own Oz. The delusions of the entry-level employee are not the delusions of the executive. The executive's are better tailored. Higher resolution. More plausible. The person who has conquered wanting-a-bigger-house now faces wanting-respect. The person who has conquered wanting-respect now faces wanting-legacy. The medameh upgrades. The yellow brick road never ends. There is always another Emerald City, and the wizard behind the curtain always has the same amount of nothing in his hand.

"And although the klipah of the higher level was already broken by the person who stood there before," Nachman continues, "when that person ascends further and the newcomer arrives, the klipah awakens again, because it was subdued only in the presence of the higher light, not the lower one. The newcomer must break it again from scratch."

Every therapist knows this phenomenon. Every honest person in recovery knows it. You make genuine progress. You reach a new level. And the new level has new delusions, calibrated precisely to the person who conquered the old ones. The recovering addict who is now sober faces the medameh running on identity. The trauma survivor who processed the event faces the medameh running on "who am I without my wound?" The rich man who no longer chases money faces the medameh running on meaning. Each Oz is more convincing than the last. The curtain is better hidden. The levers are more sophisticated. The wizard's voice is deeper.

And the person who just arrived at the new level - the one who broke through the delusions of the previous level - is the most vulnerable. Because they think they know what delusion looks like. They think they've learned to see through it. But the new delusion doesn't look like the old one. It looks like progress. It looks like depth. It looks like the real thing, finally.

## The gift that changes nothing

The Wizard gives the Scarecrow a diploma. The Scarecrow immediately begins quoting the Pythagorean theorem - incorrectly. He gets it wrong. But it doesn't matter, because the diploma was never about competence. It was about recognition. The Scarecrow always had a brain. He solved problems, made plans, outwitted the witch. What he didn't have was the feeling of having a brain. The medameh told him he was missing something. The placebo let him stop believing it.

This is the part the Breslov framework addresses that the neuroscience doesn't: the medameh is not only the problem. It is also, when refined, the solution.

Nachman in Likutey Moharan 25 doesn't say: destroy the medameh. He says: extract yourself from it and ascend to the sechel. He says: break it, subdue it, refine it through the sacrifices - through structured, embodied practice that takes the animal faculty and channels it upward. The Likutey Halachot (Hilchot Tzitzit 3:4) is explicit: "Through ruach nevuah (the spirit of prophecy), one rectifies and clarifies the medameh, and through this one merits complete emunah (faith)."

The medameh, refined, becomes the vehicle for faith. Not destroyed - refined. Because emunah itself cannot operate through pure sechel. You cannot reason your way to faith. Faith requires the capacity to hold something that is not yet proven, something that "seems" true before you can verify it. That capacity IS the medameh. The same faculty that produces false desire, when it runs unsupervised, produces genuine faith when it runs under the guidance of intellect.

Wine, the Likutey Halachot says, has two powers: "when deserving, it gladdens; when not deserving, it confuses." The confusion is the medameh running wild - "the drunkenness of wine is the confusion of the medameh, through which one speaks words of foolishness and madness." The gladness is the medameh running true - the capacity for joy that exceeds what strict logic can justify.

The Wizard's gifts are medameh running true. The diploma doesn't give the Scarecrow a brain. It gives him access to the brain he already had by changing what he imagines about himself. The testimonial doesn't give the Lion courage. It changes the picture. The ticking heart-clock doesn't give the Tin Man feelings. It gives him permission to feel the feelings he was already having. The medameh can imprison you in Oz, or it can hand you the ruby slippers.

## The shoes you were already wearing

Dorothy always had the power to go home. The ruby slippers were on her feet from the first scene in Oz. Glinda tells her this at the end, and Dorothy, reasonably, asks why she didn't mention it sooner. "Because you wouldn't have believed me," Glinda says. "You had to learn it for yourself."

This is not a cop-out. This is the mechanism. The medameh cannot be bypassed by information. Telling someone that their desire is imaginary does not weaken the desire. Kahneman admitted, late in life, that knowing about cognitive biases did not protect him from cognitive biases. Gilbert's own research shows that people who read his book on affective forecasting errors go right on making affective forecasting errors. The knowledge is sechel. The error is medameh. The sechel is always one step behind.

Dorothy had to walk the yellow brick road. She had to meet the Wizard. She had to see the curtain pulled back and the empty hand. She had to experience the Technicolor Oz and discover that it contained nothing she didn't already have. Not because the journey taught her a fact she was missing. Because the journey refined the faculty that was deceiving her. She walked in with a medameh that produced "anywhere but Kansas." She walked out with a medameh that produced "there's no place like home." The faculty didn't shut down. It changed direction.

The instruction from Likutey Moharan 25:5 is practical: "In order to subdue the klipah that surrounds each level, one must awaken simchah shel mitzvah - joy of the commandment - upon oneself, by remembering that one merited to draw close to Hashem and to the tzaddikim. Through this joy, one breaks the klipah and enters the next level." The antidote to the false picture is not a truer picture. It is joy rooted in what actually exists. Not imagined joy about an imagined future. Real joy about a real present.

Click your heels. Say it three times. Not because the formula has power. Because the act of choosing what you already have - the act of wanting what you already hold - reverses the direction of the medameh. The faculty that showed you a brighter world somewhere else now shows you the brightness that was under your feet the whole time. The light is real. The pillar was imagined. But the light, when you stop trying to grab it and simply stand in it, warms your face the same way.

Kansas was always in color. You just couldn't see it until you stopped chasing Oz.
