# What Divorce Is For

## You Need to Divorce First if You Want to Stay Married

*Casablanca* (1942). Directed by Michael Curtiz. Humphrey Bogart, Ingrid Bergman, Paul Henreid, Claude Rains. A nightclub owner in wartime Morocco discovers that the woman who broke his heart has walked back into his bar - with her husband, a resistance leader who needs to escape. Rick has the letters of transit. He has the plane. He has the woman. He could keep her. She wants to be kept. She stands on the tarmac in the fog and says: stay with me. He puts her on the plane. "Here's looking at you, kid." The most precise act of leaving in cinema. He doesn't stumble into it. He doesn't agonize on screen. He executes a plan he built in secret, with documents he acquired for exactly this moment, and he sends her away knowing that the sending is the only thing that keeps what they had alive. If he'd kept her, the cause dies, the husband dies, Paris curdles into guilt, and the woman who loved him in the City of Light learns to resent him in a North African port. The leaving is what preserves the love. Rick knows this. That's why he does it at an airport, with paperwork, at exactly the right time. The leaving had to be precise.

---

## Nine Chapters

Masechet Gittin is ninety pages of Talmud about divorce. The word *get* is the document - a bill of severance a husband writes to end a marriage. The tractate opens with an agent carrying one across the sea, and it closes with the altar weeping. In between: two hundred and sixty-three arguments about how exactly to leave.

That is not what Gittin is about.

Every argument in the tractate - lishmah, keritut, the conditional get, the prozbul, the destruction of Jerusalem - is doing the same thing: making leaving precise enough that staying becomes a choice.

---

## Lishmah

The first three chapters are obsessed with specificity. A get must be written *lishmah* - for the sake of this specific woman. Not a template. Not a coincidence. If a scribe happens to be dictating a practice document and the names match, the document is worthless. If a man has two wives with the same name, a get for one cannot serve the other. Even when the letters are identical, the person is different.

You must know exactly who you are leaving.

The dating requirement: you must know exactly *when* you are leaving. The witnesses: you must prove you saw it happen. The agent's declaration - "before me it was written and before me it was signed" - is a chain of custody for a severed bond. Every requirement forces the same thing: do not leave accidentally. Do not leave generically. Do not leave in the dark hoping no one noticed.

Rick could have just not shown up at the airport. He could have let the plane leave without her and shrugged later. That's not what he does. He drives there. He hands over the documents. He delivers the exact words she will carry for the rest of her life. Lishmah. For her specifically.

---

## Keritut

In Chapter 8, a husband delivers a get with a condition: "You are permitted to marry any man except so-and-so." Rabbi Eliezer validates it. The Sages don't. After Rabbi Eliezer dies, four of them dismantle his position from four different angles, but the argument that cannot be answered comes from Rabbi Elazar ben Azarya: *keritut*. Severance. A get must completely sever the bond. A get that leaves any thread of connection is not a get.

Partial leaving is no leaving.

A man who says "you can see other people but I'll still text you at 2 AM" has not left. A man who moves out but keeps a key has not left. A man who says he's done but monitors her social media has not left. The Talmud knew this in the fifth century and codified it: if the severance is not complete, the document is void. You are still married. You have changed nothing. You have confused two people and called it freedom.

Rick doesn't keep a thread. He doesn't say "write to me" or "if things don't work out with Victor." He puts her on the plane. He walks into the fog with Louis Renault. The severance is complete. That's why it holds.

---

## The Conditional Get

Chapter 7 introduces a legal technology for uncertainty: the conditional get. A man who is ill, who might die, who is leaving for a dangerous journey, can write a get that activates only if a condition is met. "From today, if I die" - the get takes effect retroactively, so that if he's gone, she's free. He builds the mechanism of departure while he's still present, in order for her to be able to stay in the world.

The conditional get is not despair. It is the most loving document in the tractate. A man says: I don't know what will happen. But I refuse to let my uncertainty trap you. Here is your freedom, loaded and ready, in case you need it. I hope you never do.

Every honest relationship has a conditional get somewhere in its foundation. Not written. Not spoken. Felt. The knowledge that this person could leave - that nothing is chaining them here except the choice to stay - is what makes the staying real. A relationship without the possibility of leaving is not a relationship. It is a prison with good lighting.

There is a dating coach who runs a Substack called Day. He writes about attraction with the precision of an engineer and the instincts of someone who has actually been in a room with a woman. His foundational line: **"I want you but I don't need you."** Build a life so full that a woman is dessert, not the meal. The man who could leave - who has a craft, a body, friends, a purpose - is the only man whose staying means anything. The man who can't leave, whose staying is need dressed up as devotion, repels the very person he's trying to hold.

Day figured this out by watching people. The Talmud figured it out by watching marriages dissolve. Same principle. The capacity to leave is what gives staying its weight.

Day's other line: "Her presence is not her permanence. She is choosing you every single day and she can choose differently tomorrow." That's the conditional get. The document is always potentially active. The leaving is always possible. The possibility is what makes the staying real. "The first date never ends" - because the conditions have to be met every day, not just once.

## The Prozbul

Chapters 4 and 5 are called the tikun ha'olam chapters - "for the betterment of the world." Here the Sages do something that should be impossible: they override the Torah's own rules when those rules produce the opposite of their intended effect.

The Torah cancels debts every seventh year. This was supposed to protect the poor. Instead, it destroyed them - nobody would lend money as the seventh year approached. The poor couldn't get loans because of a law designed to help the poor.

Hillel created the prozbul: a legal document that routes around the cancellation. A mechanism that departs from the letter of the law in order to stay inside its purpose.

The prozbul is a get for bad legislation. The old rule was producing damage. Hillel didn't abolish it - he left it precisely, with paperwork, at exactly the right time. The principle survived. The mechanism adapted. He had to leave the law in order to stay in the law.

Rabban Gamliel's enactment works the same way. A husband could secretly void a get by convening a court his wife didn't know about. She'd think she was free, remarry, and her children would be illegitimate. The Sages took away the husband's right to do this. They curtailed one freedom to protect a larger one. They left something in order to stay in something bigger.

## The Destruction

Then the masechet does something extraordinary. In the middle of all this legal precision, it tells the story of how Jerusalem fell.

A man threw a party. His servant invited the wrong person. The host humiliated his enemy in front of the entire room. The Sages were sitting there. They said nothing.

This is the origin story of the destruction. Not a military defeat. A dinner party where a man was thrown out imprecisely. The host thought he was removing an enemy from a feast. He was actually tearing the fabric that held a civilization together. He didn't know who he was leaving. He didn't know what he was severing.

The enemy went to Rome. Rome sent a test offering. It arrived blemished. Rabbi Zekharya ben Avkolas, a man of excessive caution, could not decide what to do. Sacrifice the blemished animal? People will talk. Kill the informer? People will talk. He saw every consequence of action and none of the consequences of inaction.

Rabbi Yohanan's verdict: "The excessive humility of Rabbi Zekharya ben Avkolas destroyed our Temple, burned our Sanctuary, and exiled us from our land."

The same faculty that created the prozbul - the ability to think about second-order effects, to see what a rule will produce in practice - became fatal when it produced paralysis instead of decision. The Sages who could leave a bad law precisely, who could sever a bond cleanly, who could write a conditional get that freed a woman without trapping her - those same Sages could not act when the moment demanded action. Tikun ha'olam and anvetanut are the same muscle used in opposite directions.

And then Rabban Yohanan ben Zakkai did the only thing left. He left Jerusalem in a coffin. He left the city so that Torah could stay. He asked Vespasian for Yavneh and its sages. Not Jerusalem - he knew that was gone. A small academy in a coastal town. The most precise act of leaving in Jewish history: he knew exactly what he was giving up, exactly what he was preserving, and exactly what the leaving would cost.

"Give me Yavneh and its sages" is the get that saved Judaism.

---

## The Altar

The final Mishna. Three opinions on when divorce is justified.

Beit Shammai: only for sexual betrayal. Marriage is a covenant. Only its fundamental violation dissolves it.

Beit Hillel: even if she burned his food. Marriage is a partnership. When the partnership fails, it can be dissolved.

Rabbi Akiva: even if he found someone more beautiful. When desire ends, the marriage has already ended. The get merely recognizes what the heart decided.

Three theories of marriage. Three theories of what justifies leaving. The halakha follows Beit Hillel. You may leave for a burnt dinner.

And then - in the same breath - Rabbi Elazar:

**"Anyone who divorces his first wife - even the altar sheds tears over him."**

The altar. The junction point between heaven and earth. The place where offerings meet God. It weeps. Not because the leaving was wrong - the Mishna just spent nine chapters making it legal, precise, and valid. The altar weeps because even correct leaving is loss. Even when the get is lishmah, even when the keritut is complete, even when every condition is met and every witness has signed and the document is delivered at the right time to the right person in the right domain - something that existed between two people is gone, and the place where heaven touches earth can feel it.

The masechet permits the leaving and mourns it simultaneously. Both are true. The system works and the loss is real.

---

## The Fog

Day, the dating coach, has the mechanics right. Be full. Lead. Create uncertainty and investment and novelty. Make the first date never end. "Her presence is not her permanence." All true. All structurally identical to what the Talmud encoded in its laws of dissolution: the capacity to leave is what makes staying a choice.

But Day operates entirely inside Rabbi Akiva's position. Desire is the engine. When it ends, the relationship has ended. Keep the desire alive and the relationship lives. The whole framework is attraction mechanics - brilliantly observed, practically effective, and missing the altar.

The Talmud holds all three positions. The covenant of Beit Shammai. The partnership of Beit Hillel. The desire of Rabbi Akiva. And then it adds something none of them can account for: the altar weeps. Even when you leave correctly - even when you leave precisely, with lishmah and keritut and every document in order - something that existed is gone. Not a failure of mechanics. Not a partnership that ran its course. A covenant that was torn.

Day knows how to keep her. The Talmud knows what it means when you can't.

Rick Blaine stands in the fog at an airfield in Casablanca. He has done everything right. The leaving was precise, necessary, and loving. The letters of transit were in order. The plane took off. Ilsa is safe. Victor is safe. The cause is safe. Paris will always be Paris.

The altar weeps anyway.
