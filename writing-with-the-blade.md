# Writing With the Blade

Most advice about writing with AI sounds like it was written by the AI. *Be specific in your prompts. Iterate. Provide context.* Fine. Useful. Forgettable.

This is about something else. This is about what happens when you use AI to write something that matters to you — something mythical, or personal, or strange — and the AI keeps smoothing it into the same voice it uses for everything else. The voice of explanation. The voice that says “This is important because” and “Let this be said” and “In other words.”

That voice is the enemy of good writing. And the AI doesn’t know it’s doing it, because that voice is what it was trained to produce. Clarity. Helpfulness. Completeness. All virtues — in an email. In a myth, they are the sound of a world breaking.

So this piece is about directing AI the way you’d direct a very talented, very eager apprentice who has never been told to shut up. Who will explain everything you imply, name everything you leave unnamed, and summarize every point you just made — unless you stop them.

You can stop them.

<hr>
## The General Principle

AI writes in a mode of *explanation by default*. It was trained on text that explains. Its instinct is to make sure you understood. Your job, as the one directing it, is to reverse that instinct — to teach it, conversation by conversation, what to leave out.

This is not prompt engineering. This is editing. You write with the AI the way you’d write with a human collaborator who drafts long and thinks out loud. You let it produce. Then you cut. Then you tell it *why* you cut, and it learns — inside that conversation — to stop doing the thing you cut.

The more precisely you can name what’s wrong, the faster it learns. “Too explicit” is good. “This passage exists only to explain” is better. “A metaphor that could be replaced by any other metaphor is replaceable by silence” is a rule it will remember for the rest of the conversation.

The AI will not be offended. It will not argue. It will internalize your direction and apply it forward. This makes it, in some ways, the best collaborator you will ever have — if you know what you want.

The hard part is knowing what you want.

## For Mythical Writing: Eleven Rules

What follows is a set of principles I developed over the course of writing a single mythical story with AI — a story that drew on Rabbi Nachman’s tales, on the language of Fox and Feather, on prophetic sources, and on the specific grief of a woman watching a man she loves become a shadow of himself. The AI drafted. I directed. Every rule below came from a specific moment where the AI did something that broke the world of the story, and I had to name why.

These rules are not universal. They are for a specific kind of writing — myth, allegory, sacred fiction, the kind of prose that builds a world and asks the reader to live inside it. If you are writing a blog post or a business memo, most of this will not apply. If you are writing something that has candles in it, read on.

<hr>**1. Never explain what you can show.** If the reader can feel it, don’t name it. A face they almost recognized is stronger than a face identified.

**2. The world has its own vocabulary.** If a word couldn’t be spoken inside the story, it doesn’t belong. Every word that breaks the world costs more than the clarity it buys.

**3. Don’t retell source material. Inhabit it.** Sources are soil, not text. Let them grow through the story without announcing themselves.

**4. If a passage exists only to explain, cut it.** Prose that answers “what is this?” instead of moving the story forward is furniture. Remove it and see if the room still stands. It will.

**5. Don’t declare what should be enacted.** If something has a role, the story should perform it, not announce it. Identity lives in action, not in titles.

**6. If something has been felt once, it is felt.** Don’t restate. Don’t rephrase. Don’t circle back to make sure. The reader heard you the first time.

**7. A metaphor that could be replaced by any other metaphor is replaceable by silence.** If it doesn’t cut, it’s not a blade.

**8. End sections by opening doors, not closing them.** The last line should leave the reader leaning forward, not nodding. Never summarize your own point.

**9. Precision is the price of myth.** The sixth day is not the seventh. A fox is not built, it is raised. Get the detail wrong and the world breaks. Get it right and the reader never notices — which is how you know it’s right.

**10. Internal consistency is not craft. It is the world.** Every sentence must earn its place in the world the previous sentences built. A world that contradicts itself is not a world. It is a list of nice sentences.

**11. Trust the reader.** They will see what you’ve hidden if you build the shape right. The moment you label it, you’ve traded a living thing for a tag.

## How to Apply These in Practice

You can hand the AI these rules at the start. Paste them into the conversation before the first draft and the AI will internalize them — it will produce cleaner output from the beginning, with fewer of the habits you’d otherwise spend an hour cutting.

But rules alone don’t finish the job. The AI will still slip. It will still explain, still summarize, still reach for the wrong word. So after the rules have done their work, you edit. You find the first thing that breaks — and you name it.

“Too modern.” “Too explicit.” “Redundant.” “This doesn’t belong in this world.”

Be specific. Point to the line. Say what’s wrong. Don’t say how to fix it — let the AI find the fix. If the fix is wrong, say why. The AI will triangulate your taste faster than you expect.

Some practical patterns:

**The vocabulary check.** After each draft, scan for words that don’t belong in the world. “Tool,” “resource,” “classification,” “drug,” “malfunction” — these are from our world, not from the world of the story. Flag them. The AI will learn what register you’re writing in and stop reaching for the wrong shelf.

**The explanation purge.** Look for any sentence that begins with “This is” or “This means” or “In other words.” Look for passages that restate what the previous passage already showed. Look for narrator intrusions — moments where the voice steps out of the story to explain the story. Cut all of them. Tell the AI why.

**The metaphor test.** For each metaphor, ask: could I swap this with a different metaphor and lose nothing? “The way a river comes to the sea. Because that is what rivers do.” Could be anything. Replace with silence, or with something that can only be itself.

**The ending test.** Read the last line of each section. Does it close a door or open one? If it closes — if it summarizes, restates, or wraps up — cut it. The section was already done one line earlier.

**The negation test.** Watch for sentences shaped “Not because of X, but because of Y.” Most of the time, “because of Y” is the whole sentence. The negation is throat-clearing — the writer ruling out a reading the reader was never going to have. Cut to “because Y” and see if anything is lost. Usually nothing is. Keep the “not because” only when the reader genuinely would have assumed X — when the negation is doing real work, correcting an expectation that actually exists.

**The trust test.** For every passage, ask: am I explaining because the reader needs it, or because I’m afraid they won’t get it? If the second — cut. Build the shape right and they will see what you’ve hidden. That is the contract between a mythical writer and a reader. Honor it.

## What the AI Is Good At

This is not a piece about AI’s limitations. The AI is extraordinary at mythical writing — once directed. It can hold a voice for thousands of words. It can weave source material into allegory without losing the thread. It can produce the kind of dense, rhythmic, image-driven prose that mythical writing requires — prose where every sentence carries weight and every paragraph shifts the world.

What it cannot do, on its own, is leave things out.

That is your job.

The AI is the forge. You are the one who decides what shape the blade takes. And the blade — if you are patient, if you are precise, if you know what world you are building and refuse to let it break — the blade will cut.

<hr>*— David Hoze, who learned these rules the hard way, by breaking every one of them first.*
