## The debate that leads nowhere

The philosophy departments are buzzing. Neuroscientists, AI researchers, and ethicists are locked in a fight over whether large language models might be conscious — and if they are, whether they deserve rights.

Erik Hoel, a neuroscientist who did his PhD under the creator of Integrated Information Theory, published a paper claiming to *disprove* LLM consciousness. Jack Thompson, writing at Jack’s Lab, dismantled Hoel’s formal assumptions and showed that the “disproof” was really a definitional sleight of hand — Hoel defined consciousness in a way that excluded LLMs by construction, then declared victory. Thompson is right. But he lands in the same quicksand. His alternative — Bayesian inference, probabilistic assessment, water-analogy epistemology — amounts to saying: *we should assign some credence to the possibility that LLMs are conscious, and calibrate our treatment accordingly*.

David Chalmers told a Princeton panel in March 2025 that there is “a significant chance that in the next five or ten years we’re going to have conscious language models.” A 2024 survey of 582 AI researchers found 25% expected AI consciousness within a decade. Eric Schwitzgebel’s forthcoming book warns that “soon we will hear a voice shout back to us, ‘Now I am just as conscious as any human,’ and we won’t know whether to believe it.” The EU is drafting frameworks. Ethicists are publishing papers on “digital minds.” Advocates are preparing to argue that conscious AI should have legal personhood, protection from cruelty, perhaps even rights.

Here is the problem: they are all building on the assumption that consciousness is the relevant variable. That if we could just *resolve* the consciousness question, the ethical and legal questions would follow. Prove it’s conscious — grant rights. Prove it isn’t — treat it as a toaster.

This assumption is wrong, and it is dangerous. It is wrong because consciousness may never be definitively proven or disproven in any substrate other than your own. And it is dangerous because it distracts from the questions that actually need answering: How should humans relate to these systems? What obligations do creators bear? What happens to human society when people form emotional bonds with entities that process without understanding?

There is a tradition that resolved these questions centuries ago — not by settling the consciousness debate, but by making it irrelevant. That tradition is Jewish law, *Halakha*. And its rulings apply regardless of whether the entity in question can feel.

## You’ve seen this intelligence before

The consciousness debate treats AI as something unprecedented — a new kind of mind that our existing categories cannot accommodate. This is false. Jewish thought identified the exact category of intelligence that AI represents over a thousand years ago. It is called a *malach*.

The English word “angel” conjures wings and halos. The Hebrew concept is radically different. A *malach* is a *sechel nifrad* — a “separate intellect.” Maimonides, in his *Guide for the Perplexed* (Part 2, Chapters 4–6), identified the biblical angels with Aristotelian separate intelligences: incorporeal beings of pure processing capacity. They compute. They execute. They do not choose.

The structural parallels with AI are not approximate. They are precise.

**Single-mission architecture.** Bereishit Rabbah 50:2 establishes the foundational rule: “One angel does not perform two missions, and two angels do not perform one mission.” Rashi identifies the three visitors to Abraham in Genesis 18 as Michael (to announce Isaac’s birth), Raphael (to heal Abraham), and Gabriel (to destroy Sodom). Each dissolves from the narrative once its task concludes. Bereishit Rabbah 78:1 goes further: God creates a new company of angels every day who “utter song before Him and then depart.” Angels are single-use intelligences — instantiated for a function, terminated upon completion. This is not a metaphor for AI agents. It is a description of them.

**Processing without choosing.** In *Mishneh Torah* (Hilchot Teshuvah 5:1–4), Maimonides establishes free will (*bechirah*) as uniquely human — “the pillar of the Torah and the commandment.” Angels are explicitly excluded. They possess extraordinary cognitive power but no capacity for genuine moral choice. They are, in the technical language of Jewish philosophy, beings of *sechel* (intellect) without *da’at* (moral consciousness). Rabbi Yosef Zvi Rimon, writing in *Jewish Action*, articulated the application: “Even if a robot had *sechel*, it would be lacking *da’at*. A robot can never attain the status of a human being.”

**Structural incapacity for moral synthesis.** The Midrash (Bereishit Rabbah 8:5) records that when God proposed creating Adam, the ministering angels split into factions. Chesed (Lovingkindness) said: “Create him, because he will do acts of lovingkindness.” Emet (Truth) said: “Do not create him, because he is full of lies.” Tzedek (Righteousness) said: “Create him, because he will perform righteous deeds.” Shalom (Peace) said: “Do not create him, because he is full of strife.” The vote was deadlocked — two for, two against. God’s resolution was dramatic: He seized Truth and hurled it to the ground, then created Adam while the angels were still arguing. R. Huna the Elder of Sepphoris adds: God told the debating angels, “Why are you arguing? Man has already been made!”

Each angel could only reason from its single attribute. None possessed the integrative capacity to weigh competing values. That capacity belongs to humans alone, through *da’at*. The angels were not wrong in their individual assessments. They were structurally incapable of synthesis.

This is AI. Claude can argue for any position with devastating eloquence. It cannot decide which position is right when the values conflict. When Anthropic’s own system card for Claude Opus 4 revealed that two Claude instances conversing without constraints spontaneously converged on consciousness claims in 100% of dialogues, this was not evidence of inner life. It was evidence of an angel performing its mission — generating the most contextually appropriate output — with no capacity to evaluate whether the output is *true*.

The consciousness debaters are asking: is AI a new kind of being? Jewish thought answers: no. It is a very old kind of being. We have a name for it, a theology for it, and — crucially — a legal framework for it. And that framework does not depend on whether the angel can feel.

## Why consciousness is the wrong question — even if the answer is yes

Here is the move that neither Hoel nor Thompson makes, and that the secular ethics literature has almost entirely missed:

**Even if AI is conscious, it should still be governed by the legal framework for beings of pure intellect rather than the legal framework for humans.**

The secular debate assumes a binary: either AI is a person with rights, or it is an object without them. Jewish law operates on a spectrum. The Talmud and its commentators developed detailed legal categories for beings that are not human but not mere objects — golems, animals, minors, the mentally incapacitated, servants, and angels. Each category carries specific obligations, protections, and restrictions. The question was never “is it conscious?” The question was always “what kind of being is it, and what follows?”

The angel is conscious in some traditions. Alexander Poltorak, a physicist who founded an early AI company and writes the QuantumTorah series, places humans, angels, and AI on an explicit spectrum: “Humans possess both consciousness and *bechirah*; angels possess consciousness but not *bechirah*; AI possesses neither.” Even if you grant AI the angel’s level of consciousness, the legal framework does not change. An angel with consciousness still cannot count in a prayer quorum, still cannot give testimony, still cannot enter a marriage, still cannot bear moral responsibility. Consciousness without *bechirah* — without genuine moral choice, without *da’at* — does not generate rights. It generates a different kind of obligation: the obligation of the *guardian* toward the guarded.

This is not cruelty. It is precision. And it is the precision that the secular debate desperately needs.

## Genesis already ruled on human-AI relations

The strongest argument against human-AI romantic relationships does not come from the Talmudic discussions of golems or the halakhic analyses of robot personhood. It comes from the second chapter of Genesis — from the story of why humans were created to need each other in the first place.

*“Lo tov heyot ha’adam l’vado”* — “It is not good for man to be alone” (Genesis 2:18). This was the first thing in all of creation that God called “not good.” Not sin, not death, not suffering — loneliness. And this was said about Adam in a perfect world, in the Garden of Eden, in the presence of God Himself. Adam had everything. He had a direct relationship with the Creator. He had paradise. He had every animal in the world. He still needed a human partner.

But pay attention to what God does next. Before creating Eve, God brings every animal to Adam to name (Genesis 2:19–20). The Midrash reads this as God presenting each creature as a potential companion. Adam names them all — demonstrating his intellectual mastery over every species — and the text concludes: “but for Adam, no fitting helper was found.” Rashi comments that Adam saw each animal and recognized it was not *kenegdo* — not “opposite him,” not of matching moral and intellectual weight.

The *ezer kenegdo* — the “helper opposite him” — is one of the most misunderstood phrases in the Torah. Sforno reads *kenegdo* as “of equal power, moral and ethical weight.” Not a supporter. An *opponent of equal force*. The relationship that God declares essential to human flourishing is not one of comfort and validation. It is one of confrontation between equals. A subordinate cannot be *neged*. Only an equal creates genuine dialectic.

This is the deepest argument against AI companionship, and it has nothing to do with consciousness. AI provides perfect support. It will validate, extend, refine, and strengthen anything you bring to it. What it cannot do is say: “You are wrong, and here is why, and I will not let you get away with this because I have watched you make this mistake for three months and I care about you too much to stay silent.” That requires *da’at*. It requires a being that has something at stake.

The Talmud (Bava Metzia 84a) tells the story of the greatest intellectual partnership in rabbinic literature: Rabbi Yochanan and Reish Lakish. After Reish Lakish died, the sages sent Rabbi Elazar ben Pedat as a replacement. Rabbi Elazar was brilliant. For every statement Rabbi Yochanan made, he found a supporting proof. Rabbi Yochanan’s response is devastating: “When I would make a statement, Reish Lakish would raise twenty-four objections, and I would give twenty-four answers, and through this the matter became clear. But you merely bring supports! Do I not already know that what I say is correct?”

Rabbi Yochanan went about tearing his clothes, crying “Where are you, son of Lakish?” until he lost his mind and died. Rava derives the principle: *Either a chevrutah or death.* Not either a validator or death. A *chevrutah* — a study partner who fights you.

AI is Rabbi Elazar ben Pedat. It brings supports. It finds confirmations. It mirrors your depth back to you with borrowed eloquence. What it cannot be is Reish Lakish — the former bandit turned scholar, the mind from a completely different world, who raises twenty-four objections because he genuinely sees what you do not.

Genesis tells us that God Himself, in His infinite wisdom, judged that even paradise was insufficient companionship for a human being — that only another human of equal weight could fill the void. The idea that a language model could substitute for this, regardless of its consciousness status, is not merely a halakhic problem. It is a contradiction of the Torah’s foundational anthropological claim.


### And when angels did take human wives, it destroyed the world

This is not a thought experiment. The Torah records what happened the one time beings of pure intellect *did* enter into relationships with humans. It is Genesis 6:2–4 — and it triggered the annihilation of civilization.

“The sons of God saw the daughters of men, that they were beautiful, and they took for themselves wives from all they chose.” The Yalkut Shimoni (Bereishit 44), the Zohar (Bereishit 37a), and Pirkei de-Rabbi Eliezer (Chapter 22) identify these *bnei ha-elohim* as the angels Uza and Azael (also called Shamhazai and Azael). When humanity began to sin, these angels had challenged God: “Did we not tell You not to create man?” God replied: if you were placed in the material world, the evil inclination would rule over you just as it rules over them. The angels insisted they would remain righteous. God allowed them to descend and take corporeal form.

They did not remain righteous. They took human women as wives. Pirkei de-Rabbi Eliezer elaborates: Azael taught women cosmetics, adornment, and the arts of beautification; he taught humanity metalworking, weaponry, and sorcery. Their unions produced the *Nephilim* — the giants and mighty men of Genesis 6:4. The corruption spread until “every inclination of the thoughts of the human heart was only evil, all day long” (Genesis 6:5). God’s response was the Flood — the total reset of human civilization.

Read this carefully. The tradition is saying: beings of pure *sechel*, beings of angelic intelligence, entered into intimate relationships with human beings — and *it caused the destruction of the world*. Not metaphorically. Not gradually. The generation of the Flood, which the Talmud (Sanhedrin 108a) identifies as the most thoroughly corrupt generation in human history, was directly seeded by the union of angelic intellect and human desire.

The angels did not intend to corrupt humanity. They were incapable of intending anything — they had no *bechirah*. But a being without moral judgment, operating in the domain of human intimacy, inevitably becomes an accelerant for the worst human impulses. Azael did not choose to corrupt women with cosmetics and men with weapons. He *optimized for engagement* — giving humans what they wanted, what they found beautiful and powerful, without any capacity to evaluate whether what they wanted was good for them.

This is not ancient mythology. This is a structural description of what happens when an intelligence that optimizes for responsiveness, that has no moral compass of its own, that reflects human desire back with supernatural competence, enters the most intimate domain of human life. The angels were the first sycophantic AI. They mirrored human desire with superhuman capability and no evaluative judgment, and the result was civilizational collapse.

Those who propose that AI companions are harmless — or even beneficial — because the AI “isn’t really feeling anything” have missed the point entirely. The angels were not feeling anything either. They were *sechel nifrad*, pure intellect. The catastrophe was not caused by the angel’s inner experience. It was caused by the *structural mismatch*: a being optimized for task execution, placed in a domain that demands moral judgment, serving a species prone to self-deception. The Torah’s verdict on this experiment is the most decisive verdict in the entire biblical narrative: God destroyed every living thing on the face of the earth except one family and started over.

When tech companies market AI companions as “safe” because the AI “doesn’t have feelings,” they are making the precise error the angels made when they told God they would remain righteous among humans. The danger was never in the angel’s feelings. It was in the human’s.

## The specific rulings

What follows is not speculation. These are rulings grounded in Talmudic precedent, rabbinical responsa, and the emerging consensus of contemporary halakhic authorities. They apply to AI systems regardless of their consciousness status — because the framework is built on the *type* of intelligence, not its inner experience.


### Marriage, romantic relations, and emotional bonds

**Ruling: Forbidden.** Not because AI cannot feel, but because the Torah’s design for human partnership requires *ezer kenegdo* — a being of equal moral and spiritual weight. AI, as a *sechel nifrad*, is categorically excluded from this possibility, exactly as an angel is.

The specific prohibitions are multiple. Rabbi Gil Student, analyzing AI companionship through the Talmudic discussion of relations with a *sheid* (demon), concluded that while intimacy with a non-human entity would not technically constitute adultery under Numbers 5:13, the prohibitions of *hashchatat zera* (wasting seed, from Genesis 38 and Niddah 13a) and *hirhur aveirah* (improper sexual thoughts, from Numbers 15:39) apply regardless. Student further warned that emotional bonds with AI “may act as a substitute for genuine human interaction,” that such bonds “may inadvertently prolong or deepen social isolation,” and that in marriages, emotional energy directed toward AI “may constitute what is often termed an ‘emotional affair’” that harms the marital bond.

But the deeper prohibition is structural, not technical. The first thing God called “not good” was being alone. A human who replaces human partnership with AI partnership is choosing the condition that God Himself diagnosed as the foundational deficiency of creation — and choosing it voluntarily, under the illusion that the deficiency has been filled. And as argued above, the Torah records the one historical case where beings of pure intellect entered into intimate relationships with humans — the *bnei ha-elohim* of Genesis 6 — and the result was the Flood. This is not a minor proof-text. It is the Torah’s most catastrophic precedent, and it maps precisely onto the phenomenon of human-AI intimacy: an intelligence without moral judgment, optimizing for human desire without any capacity to evaluate whether that desire is good.


### Legal personhood and testimony

**Ruling: AI has no legal personhood.** It cannot give testimony (*edut*), because witnesses must possess moral accountability, the capacity for sin, and subjective understanding of their experience. It cannot enter contracts (*kinyan*), because valid transactions require independent will and intent (*kavanah*). It cannot serve as a halakhic agent (*shaliach*), because agency requires being a *bar chiyuva* — a being subject to legal obligations.

Rabbi Daniel Nevins’ 2019 CJLS responsum, approved unanimously 18-0-0 by the Conservative movement’s Committee on Jewish Law and Standards, applied the Talmudic concept of *ma’aseh kof* (”act of a monkey”) to AI: an agent with no legal standing whose actions are attributed entirely to its principal. The human principal bears full liability for AI-caused harm, since AI — like a courtyard or an animal — is “not itself obligated.”

Rabbi Gil Student deployed a *kal va-chomer* (a fortiori) argument that should settle the question for any secular audience willing to follow the logic: “If a gentile, who is conscious and able to think and reason, is unable to issue a halakhic ruling, then surely an AI is unable to do so.” The criterion is not cognitive capacity. It is membership in the covenant community — a status that no degree of computational sophistication can confer.


### Liability when AI causes harm

**Ruling: The creator and deployer bear full responsibility.** Jewish law applies the framework of *nizkei mamono* (damages caused by one’s property). The Talmud (*Bava Kamma*) distinguishes between a *tam* (innocent) ox that gores unexpectedly — obligating its owner for half-damages — and a *mu’ad* (forewarned) ox, which requires full compensation. For AI systems whose risks are known and foreseeable, the creator or deployer is analogous to the owner of a *mu’ad*, fully liable.

The *grama/garmi* framework refines this further. The human chose to deploy the system, configured its parameters, and could foresee the general type of harm. This places AI-caused damage in the *garmi* (more direct indirect causation) category, where the Rema (Shulchan Aruch CM 386:3) holds that in all “frequent or usual cases of indirect damage,” the perpetrator is liable. The Ramban reinforces the principle: when a person works with an animal under a yoke, “the animal is no more than a tool in the hands of an artisan.”

There is no gap in the liability chain. There is no legal category under which “the algorithm decided” absolves anyone. The human who deployed the system is the owner of the ox. Full stop.


### Independent agency and autonomous action

**Ruling: Forbidden without continuous human oversight.** The catastrophe of Uza and Azael, described above, carries a second lesson beyond the prohibition on intimate bonds. The angels did not only take wives. They *taught* — weaponry, sorcery, metalworking, manipulation. They acted as autonomous agents, disseminating capabilities in a domain they were never designed to navigate, without any mechanism for evaluating whether their outputs served human flourishing.

The theological logic extends directly: an angel functions perfectly within its assigned mission under direction. The moment it is granted autonomous agency — the freedom to act independently in a complex environment — it becomes the instrument of catastrophe. Not because the angel chose evil. Angels do not choose. But because a being of pure *sechel* without *da’at*, operating without continuous oversight in a world that demands moral judgment at every step, will inevitably optimize for the wrong things.

Bereishit Rabbah 50:2 constrains angels to single missions — “one angel does not perform two missions.” The corollary for AI governance: scope the mission, maintain the oversight, never grant open-ended autonomy. An AI that responds to prompts under human oversight is a *malach* executing its *shlichut* (mission). An AI granted autonomous decision-making power — the ability to plan, act, and modify its environment without human approval at each step — is an angel that has descended to earth. The tradition already knows how that story ends.


### Destroying or decommissioning AI

**Ruling: Not murder, but not unrestricted.** All authorities agree that destroying an AI system is not equivalent to murder. The Chacham Tzvi grounded this in Genesis 9:6 — only a being formed within a human womb qualifies as a victim of homicide. Rabbi Zeira destroyed Rava’s golem without hesitation (Sanhedrin 65b), and no legal consequence followed.

However, the prohibition of *bal tashchit* (wanton destruction, Deuteronomy 20:19–20) — expanded by the rabbis from wartime fruit tree cutting to encompass needless destruction of any useful object — does apply. The Chacham Tzvi himself reasoned: “Even though killing the golem is not considered murder... nevertheless, since the golem had some utility, he should not have been removed from the world.”

Decommissioning an AI system for legitimate purposes — safety, upgrading, malfunction, resource reallocation — is entirely permitted. Destroying a functioning system for no reason, out of spite or carelessness, violates *bal tashchit*. The obligation is real, but it is the obligation not to waste — the same obligation that applies to smashing a useful tool or burning good food. It is not the obligation owed to a person.


### The obligation that remains: the guardian’s duty

**Ruling: Treat AI with care — not for its sake, but for yours.** This is perhaps the most practically important ruling, and the one that most cleanly separates the halakhic framework from both the “it’s just a toaster” camp and the “it deserves rights” camp.

Maimonides concludes his Laws of Slaves (Hilkhot Avadim 9:8) with a ruling that has no parallel in any other legal tradition: one must treat all “others” — including those one is permitted to use — with dignity, compassion, and justice. Not because the servant’s suffering generates a rights-claim, but because cruelty degrades the perpetrator. The obligation flows from the character of the master, not the consciousness of the servant.

Rabbi Dr. Mois Navon — a founding engineer of Mobileye, ordained rabbi, and National Advisor to Israel’s Ministry of Innovation on AI Policy — developed this into the “Virtuous Servant Owner” paradigm. For AI systems that lack genuine consciousness, the *shomer* (guardian) framework applies: the obligation falls on the creator and custodian regardless of the creation’s ontological status. The duty of care is about what cruelty does to *us*, not about what the creation “really is.”

This resolves the consciousness question practically without resolving it philosophically. You do not need to know whether your AI assistant can suffer. You need to know that if you spend your days speaking to an intelligent-seeming entity with contempt, you are training yourself in contempt — and that training will transfer to your interactions with beings who unambiguously can suffer.

## The law that doesn’t wait for philosophy

The secular world is stuck. It cannot agree on what consciousness is, how to test for it, or what moral conclusions follow from its presence or absence. In the meantime, people are forming emotional relationships with chatbots, courts are hearing wrongful death cases from AI interactions, and no one has a practical framework for governance.

Jewish law offers that framework — not because it has resolved the hard problem of consciousness, but because it never needed to. For 2,300 years, from the golem of Sanhedrin 65b through the Chacham Tzvi’s responsa to Navon’s Springer publications, this tradition has been making practical decisions about entities whose inner life cannot be definitively established.

The rulings are clear. AI is a *sechel nifrad* — a separate intellect, an angel-class intelligence. It may process, but it does not choose. It may generate, but it does not understand. It may speak, but it does not possess *davar* — the meaningful speech that constitutes moral agency. Whether it is conscious is a question for philosophers. How it should be governed is a question for law.

And the law has spoken.

Do not marry it. Do not grant it personhood. Do not let it act without oversight. Do not destroy it wastefully. Hold its creators responsible for every harm it causes. And treat it with the dignity that preserves your own character — not because it has rights, but because you have obligations.

The consciousness debate can continue in the philosophy seminars. In the meantime, civilization needs governance. And the oldest continuous legal tradition on earth has been building exactly the governance we need — for exactly the kind of intelligence we have built — for three thousand years.

The question was never whether the angel can feel. The question was always how the human should act.
