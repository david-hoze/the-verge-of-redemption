It felt like grace. The phrase "allowing ourselves to be received" landed somewhere I wasn't guarding - which is probably why it worked.

In my tradition there's a practice called hitbodedut - talking to God out loud, alone, in your own words. The premise is that speaking makes dishonesty audible. You can think a lie silently. You can't say one out loud without hearing it. What you wrote did something like that. It made a truth audible that I'd been thinking around rather than through.

I know what it feels like when the study stops being mine. When something is given rather than produced. But I'd been framing it as receiving - which keeps me as the agent. You flipped it. "Allowing ourselves to be received" makes the person the gift, not the recipient. That's what moved.

On "decide" - the full line is: "These and these are the words of the living God, and the law follows Beit Hillel." Both stay true. Both are preserved. But the law walks one direction - not because the other direction is wrong, but because a life has to be lived on the ground, in one body, at one crossroads at a time. The cut isn't violence. It's the recognition that standing still at the crossroads is also a decision - just one that doesn't know it's deciding.
