Farida, the Malthus inversion is a genuine insight. Scarcity used to be the problem - now abundance concentrating in fewer hands is the problem. And your Mexico analysis is devastating. Nearshoring was supposed to be the model: FDI flows in, formal employment grows, the middle class solidifies. Instead, informality hit 54.9% while investment hit records. The abundance arrived and the distribution mechanism failed simultaneously. That's not a policy lag. That's a structural feature.

Your three solutions - sovereign wealth fund, universal basic dividend, AI licensing fees - are the best of the policy class. I mean that seriously. They take the Norway model and adapt it for an AI economy where the resource being extracted is cognitive labor rather than oil.

Here's what I want to push on: all three answer the question of how to divide the abundance. None answer the question of what people are for once it arrives.

UBI keeps people alive. A universal basic dividend keeps them comfortable. Neither tells a displaced worker in Monterrey what Monday morning is for when the factory that structured his life, his identity, his role in his family, and his sense of worth is running without him. The economic problem is solved. The existential problem is sitting right where it was.

I wrote about this specifically - what happens when the factory has no exit, when abundance arrives and the test turns out to be purpose, not distribution. The tradition already modeled it three thousand years ago.
