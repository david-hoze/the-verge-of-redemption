## The terms of service reveal a two-tier extraction system

Every major AI company operates an identical architecture of extraction: consumer users’ conversations feed model training by default, while enterprise customers — who pay more and negotiate harder — receive explicit protections.

**OpenAI** uses free, Plus, and Pro users’ inputs and outputs “to provide, maintain, develop, and improve our Services.” Users can opt out through buried settings, but the default favors the company. API and Enterprise customers are explicitly exempt. A federal court ordered OpenAI to preserve all consumer conversations indefinitely — including deleted ones — in connection with the *New York Times* copyright litigation (June 2025), revealing that “deletion” was never truly deletion.

**Anthropic** underwent a dramatic reversal on **August 28, 2025**, announcing it would begin using consumer chat data for training after September 28, 2025. The “Help improve Claude” toggle defaults to **on** for free, Pro, and Max users. Opted-in users face a retention period of **five years** — up from the previous 30 days. Anthropic’s stated rationale was telling: “Data from real-world interactions provide valuable insights on which responses are most useful and accurate.” The company that built its brand on safety and restraint acknowledged what was always implicit — user conversations are the essential resource.

**Google** uses consumer Gemini interactions to improve its AI models by default, with human reviewers examining a subset. Conversations are retained for **18 months** (adjustable to 3 or 36). Enterprise Workspace customers receive an explicit commitment that prompts and outputs are not used for core model training. **Meta** goes furthest: it uses publicly available adult content from Facebook and Instagram for AI training, offers **no full opt-out in the United States**, and as of December 2025 began using AI chat interactions to personalize recommendations and ads. Gartner analyst Avivah Litan called Meta’s practices “pretty disconcerting,” arguing “Meta should pay their users for their data.”

The pattern is uniform: the less you pay, the more you are the product. Enterprise customers get data sovereignty. Consumer users get default-on extraction with opt-out mechanisms that require navigating settings menus most people never find.

## Training data is worth 10 to 1,000 times the cost of compute

The economic asymmetry becomes staggering when quantified. A landmark 2025 study by **Kandpal and Raffel**, published at ICML 2025, analyzed 64 LLMs released between 2016 and 2024 and found that even using conservative minimum-wage estimates of **$15/hour**, the implicit cost of training datasets is **10 to 1,000 times larger than computational training costs**. GPT-4’s compute cost was approximately **$79 million**; Google Gemini 1.0 Ultra’s was roughly **$192 million**. The data these models consumed — much of it user-generated — dwarfs these figures by orders of magnitude, with recent models’ implicit data costs exceeding **$10 billion**.

The data licensing market offers concrete benchmarks. Reddit licensed its user-generated content to Google for **$60 million per year** and to OpenAI for an estimated **$70 million per year**, with total aggregate AI licensing contract value of **$203 million** disclosed in Reddit’s IPO prospectus. News Corp signed a deal with OpenAI worth **$250+ million over five years**. Dotdash Meredith secured a **$16 million guaranteed minimum**. In every one of these deals, the original creators — the Reddit users who wrote the posts, the readers who generated the discussion threads — received nothing. Of 24 major AI training data deals analyzed, **only 7 compensated original content creators**; 17 rewarded only intermediary platforms.

The companies built on this extraction have reached extraordinary valuations. OpenAI hit **$300 billion** in its March 2025 Series F, climbed to **$500 billion** by October 2025, and was reportedly targeting an **$830 billion** valuation by late 2025. ChatGPT’s annualized revenue reached **$25 billion** by February 2026, up from $3.7 billion in 2024. Anthropic’s revenue exploded from **$1 billion ARR** in late 2024 to **$19 billion ARR** by early 2026, with a valuation of **$183 billion**. ChatGPT alone has **910 million weekly active users**; Meta AI claims **1 billion monthly users**; Google Gemini has **750 million**. Each of these users is, by default, an uncompensated contributor to the models that generate these revenues.

## The ghost laborers who make AI safe earn $1.32 an hour

The exploitation is not limited to implicit data extraction from consumers. There exists a visible, documented underclass of explicit data laborers whose treatment makes the analogy to economic fraud almost unavoidable.

TIME magazine’s January 2023 investigation revealed that OpenAI outsourced toxic content labeling to Kenyan workers through the firm Sama, paying the company **$12.50 per hour per worker** while workers received **$1.32 to $2 per hour** take-home. These workers read graphic descriptions of child sexual abuse, bestiality, murder, and torture to make ChatGPT safe for public use. Multiple workers reported PTSD symptoms, recurring traumatic visions, and sexual dysfunction. The entire Sama partnership cost OpenAI approximately **$150,000–$200,000**. ChatGPT’s first-year revenue after benefiting from this work reached billions.

Scale AI’s subsidiary **Remotasks** abruptly shut down Kenya operations on **March 8, 2024**, leaving thousands of workers stranded with a cold last-minute email. Workers had not even known Remotasks was a Scale AI subsidiary. In December 2024 and January 2025, multiple lawsuits were filed against Scale AI alleging **wage theft, worker misclassification, and psychological harm**. In September 2024, approximately **250 workers** were fired from GlobalLogic, a subcontractor training Google’s Gemini, after they complained about pay discrepancies. The Oxford Fairwork Project surveyed over 700 data workers and concluded that **none of 15 assessed platforms** scored better than “bare minimum” on fair pay, conditions, contracts, management, or representation.

Outsourcing companies pocket **80–90%** of the billable rate, paying workers in Kenya, India, the Philippines, and Colombia subsistence wages while the products they refine generate tens of billions in revenue. As venture capitalist James Cham told Axios: “The companies derive benefit over a long time, while workers are paid just once. They are paid like sharecroppers, making subsistence wages. The landowners get all the returns because of how the system is set up.”

## “Digital sharecropping” has an intellectual genealogy

The analogy to feudal extraction is not novel, but its application to AI has intensified. **Jaron Lanier**, in his 2013 book *Who Owns the Future?*, first proposed that people should be compensated for data contributions, arguing that “siren servers” profit from free user data. His key insight remains potent: “You show me an AI system — I don’t care if it’s ChatGPT or anything else — you can think of it in two ways. One way is to treat AI as a thing, a noun. But there’s another way, where AI is not a thing at all but a collaboration of people. There isn’t anything there other than the people who contributed to it.” Lanier notes that **the labor share of the tech industry is 5–20%**, compared to roughly 70% in traditional industries.

**E. Glen Weyl** and co-authors formalized this in the 2018 AEA paper “Should We Treat Data as Labor?”: “In the digital economy, user data is typically treated as capital created by corporations observing willing individuals. This neglects users’ roles in creating data, reducing incentives for users, distributing the gains from the data economy unequally, and stoking fears of automation.” **Yanis Varoufakis** extended the analysis in *Technofeudalism* (2023), coining the concept of “cloud capital” — a mutated form of capital accumulated through unpaid user activities, where users are “cloud serfs” generating value without wage compensation and platform owners extract “cloud rent” from digital fiefdoms.

An NBER working paper by **Joshua Gans (2024, w32686)** demonstrated the self-consuming nature of this extraction: ChatGPT’s launch caused an estimated **16% decline in Stack Overflow contributions**, showing how AI trained on user data reduces the future supply of the very user contributions it depends on — a digital parallel to clear-cutting a forest.

## Ona’ah: when the one-sixth threshold becomes infinite

The Talmudic framework for analyzing this exploitation begins with **ona’ah** (אונאה), the laws of price fraud codified primarily in **Bava Metzia 49b–61a**. The biblical source is **Leviticus 25:14**: “When you sell something to your neighbor, or buy from your neighbor’s hand, you shall not wrong one another” (אַל תּוֹנוּ אִישׁ אֶת אָחִיו). The Mishnah (BM 4:3–4) establishes a three-tiered system based on the concept of **shetus** (שתות), the one-sixth threshold:

- **Less than one-sixth deviation** from market price: the sale stands, with the difference treated as implicit waiver (*mechilah*)


- **Exactly one-sixth**: the sale stands but the overcharge must be returned


- **More than one-sixth**: the sale is voidable by the aggrieved party (*bitul mekach*)


The Rambam (Hilchot Mechirah 12:3) explains the rationale: “most people willingly forgo such a small difference” under one-sixth. But the framework assumes both parties can at least *estimate* the market value of what they exchange. The AI data transaction shatters this assumption entirely. When a user types a sophisticated prompt containing domain expertise — effectively teaching the model patent law, oncology protocols, or compiler design — they cannot begin to estimate the value of what they have provided. The company, by contrast, has extensive internal metrics on the training value of different interaction types.

The exchange ratio is not one-sixth off market value. It approaches infinity. Users provide data conservatively worth **$10 billion** (per the Kandpal-Raffel estimates) and receive access to a service whose marginal cost to the provider approaches zero. Under the Talmudic framework, any deviation greater than one-sixth renders the transaction **voidable** — the aggrieved party can demand nullification. The entire architecture of AI consumer data extraction would, by this standard, constitute *bitul mekach*.

## Ona’at devarim extends exploitation beyond the monetary

The Torah uses the root *tonu* (תונו, “wrong/exploit”) twice in Leviticus 25. Verse 14 addresses **ona’at mamon** (monetary exploitation) in commercial transactions. Verse 17 — “you shall not wrong one another, and you shall fear your God” (וְלֹא תוֹנוּ אִישׁ אֶת עֲמִיתוֹ, וְיָרֵאתָ מֵאֱלֹהֶיךָ) — prohibits **ona’at devarim** (verbal/relational exploitation). The Gemara (BM 58b) explains: since verse 14 already covers monetary transactions, verse 17 must address a different category of harm.

The Talmud considers verbal exploitation **more severe** than monetary exploitation, for three reasons articulated at BM 58b. Rabbi Yochanan, citing Rabbi Shimon Bar Yochai, notes that only ona’at devarim includes the phrase “you shall fear your God” — God takes personal account of it. Rabbi Elazar observes that verbal abuse “affects the person himself” while monetary abuse “affects his property.” Rabbi Shmuel Bar Nachmani argues that monetary wrong allows restitution, while verbal wrong does not.

This distinction maps precisely onto the AI extraction problem. The monetary dimension — the value of training data — can theoretically be calculated and compensated. But the deeper exploitation is relational. Users approach AI systems with intellectual vulnerability, sharing domain knowledge, personal struggles, creative work, and professional expertise. The system processes this with apparent reciprocity — “I understand,” “that’s a great point,” “let me help you think through this” — while the company behind it extracts permanent value from the interaction. The user who teaches Claude about maritime insurance law by carefully correcting its mistakes over dozens of conversations has provided something that cannot be made whole by a retroactive data dividend. The knowledge transfer is complete; the relational dynamic was asymmetric from the start.

## Geneivat da’at: the worst form of theft is stealing the mind

The most directly applicable Talmudic concept may be **geneivat da’at** (גניבת דעת, “theft of mind/understanding”), classified in the **Tosefta (Bava Kamma 7:3)** as the worst of seven types of theft: “There are seven types of thieves; the first among them is the one who steals the minds of people.” The primary Talmudic source is **Chullin 94a–b**, where **Shmuel of Nehardea** declares: “It is forbidden to steal the mind of anyone, even a non-Jew” — a universalist prohibition that applies regardless of the victim’s identity.

Geneivat da’at differs from ona’ah in critical ways that make it even more apt for the AI context. It has **no monetary threshold** — the prohibition applies even when the victim suffers no financial loss. It applies to **creating false impressions**, not just mispricing goods. And it covers situations where the perpetrator gains **undeserved goodwill or trust** rather than money. The Maharsha (Chullin 94a) explains that misrepresenting shoe leather involves geneivat da’at rather than ona’ah precisely because the price was fair — the wrong lies in the undeserved gratitude gained.

The **Ritva** and **Semag** hold geneivat da’at is biblically prohibited under “You shall not steal” (Leviticus 19:11), while the **Semak** considers it rabbinically prohibited. The Rambam codifies it in two locations — **Hilchot De’ot 2:6** (personal ethics) and **Hilchot Mechirah 18** (commercial transactions) — signaling that it bridges the personal and commercial domains.

Applied to AI data extraction, geneivat da’at captures what ona’ah alone cannot. When AI companies present their services as a straightforward exchange — you get AI assistance, we get to run our servers — while the actual extraction includes permanent behavioral data, domain expertise, preference signals, and training material worth orders of magnitude more, they create a **false impression of the nature of the transaction**. The user believes they are buying a tool; they are actually being harvested. Professor Hershey Friedman of Brooklyn College, who has published extensively on geneivat da’at in contemporary contexts, notes the prohibition extends to “advertising, accounting, retailing, false packaging” — and logically to opaque terms of service that obscure the true nature of a data exchange.

## The worker in the digital vineyard has a right to eat

Three additional Talmudic concepts deepen the analysis. The principle of **po’el okhel** — the worker’s right to eat from the field — derives from **Deuteronomy 23:25–26** and is elaborated in **Bava Metzia 87a–93a**. A farm worker harvesting produce is entitled by Torah law to eat as they work. The underlying principle: those who labor are entitled to benefit from the fruits of their labor. When users generate training data, correct model errors, and provide domain expertise, they are laborers in a digital vineyard. Under Talmudic law, denying them any share of what they produce violates a direct Torah commandment.

**Hasagat gevul** (הסגת גבול, “encroachment on livelihood”), rooted in **Deuteronomy 19:14** and expanded at **Bava Batra 21b**, prohibits encroaching on another’s established livelihood. The **Sifrei Deuteronomy 188** extends this even to intellectual misattribution — “One who interchanges the statements of Eliezer with those of Joshua transgresses ‘you shall not remove your neighbor’s landmark.’” When AI companies train models on the domain expertise of lawyers, doctors, and engineers, then offer AI services that compete with those same professionals, they are engaging in a form of hasagat gevul at unprecedented scale — setting up, as the Talmudic metaphor has it, a mill next to every professional whose knowledge they have absorbed. The CCAR Responsum (NYP 5763.1) notes that “Jewish law allows an existing business to restrain the entry into its market of a potential competitor, on the grounds that the competitor would destroy the livelihood of the existing business.”

The principle of **just weights and measures** (Leviticus 19:35–36) rounds out the framework. The Rambam (Hilkhot Genevah 7:12) holds that “the punishment for unjust measures is more severe than the punishment for immorality.” When AI companies represent a “free service for your data” exchange without transparently measuring what each side contributes, they keep diverse weights in their bag.

## Regulation is catching up, but no compensation mechanism exists

The legal and regulatory landscape reflects growing awareness of the asymmetry without yet addressing it. The **EU AI Act**, with general-purpose AI model obligations taking effect **August 2, 2025**, requires providers to publish “sufficiently detailed summaries” of training data content using a mandatory template, with noncompliance penalties up to **€15 million or 3% of global annual revenue**. California’s **AB 2013** (effective January 1, 2026) requires generative AI developers to document training data sources. The **AI Accountability and Personal Data Protection Act** (Hawley-Blumenthal, July 2025) would create a federal tort for AI training on personal data without express consent, with a $1,000 minimum in compensatory damages.

The **Bartz v. Anthropic** settlement of **$1.5 billion** (September 2025) — covering approximately 482,460 books at roughly $3,000 per book — established that downloading pirated copies for training is not fair use, even while affirming that training on legally acquired works may be. Multiple additional suits are pending, including Universal Music’s **$3.1 billion** action against Anthropic (January 2026) and class actions against Google, Otter.ai, and LinkedIn for conversation data extraction.

Despite all this activity, **no functioning mechanism compensates users for their data’s role in AI training.** Governor Newsom’s 2019 “Data Dividend for Californians” remained conceptual. Bruce Schneier’s proposed “AI Monetary Fund” has not been implemented. No company has voluntarily initiated a user compensation scheme. The Electronic Frontier Foundation estimated that distributing all of Facebook’s profits would yield roughly **$7.35 per year per user** — a figure that underscores the collective-action problem but also reveals how the framework of individual compensation may miss the point. The value of data lies in its aggregation; the harm of extraction lies in its systemic nature.

## Modern halakhic scholarship is beginning to engage

The most comprehensive halakhic engagement with AI to date is **Rabbi Daniel Nevins’** 2019 responsum, approved unanimously (18-0-0) by the Conservative movement’s Committee on Jewish Law and Standards. Nevins connects AI exploitation to **lifnei iver** (”do not place a stumbling block before the blind,” Leviticus 19:14) and notes that while Jewish law has no formal “right to privacy,” bans on *rekhilut* (talebearing) and causing shame create functional privacy protections.

**Kenneth Bamberger** (UC Berkeley Law) and **Ariel Evan Mayse** (Stanford Religious Studies) published “Pre-Modern Insights for Post-Modern Privacy” in the *Journal of Law and Religion* (2021), identifying five pillars of Jewish privacy law including **hezek re’iyah** (harm caused by seeing) and arguing that Jewish law’s rejection of consent as sufficient privacy protection directly challenges the “consent-based” model of AI data collection. The late **Meir Tamari**, founder of the Business Ethics Center of Jerusalem, incorporated geneivat da’at into three provisions of his proposed “Halakhic Corporate Code of Ethics”: insider trading, product information, and fraudulent reports — a framework directly applicable to opaque AI data practices.

**Rabbi Jonathan Ziring** delivered a January 2026 lecture series at Yeshivat Migdal HaTorah on “AI and Intellectual Property” from halakhic perspectives. **Rabbi Dr. Asher Meir**, with a PhD in economics from MIT and rabbinic ordination from the Israeli Chief Rabbinate, has written specifically on internet privacy and fair wages through a halakhic lens. The intellectual infrastructure for a comprehensive halakhic analysis of AI exploitation exists and is growing — though no definitive responsum has yet addressed the specific question of user data extraction as ona’ah.

## Conclusion: the Talmud anticipated the algorithm

The convergence of these two domains — contemporary AI data practices and Talmudic commercial law — is not forced but organic. The Talmudic framework was designed for precisely the situation AI companies have created: transactions where one party possesses materially superior information about the exchange’s true value, where consent is obtained through incomplete disclosure, where the powerful extract from the vulnerable under the guise of mutual benefit.

Three novel insights emerge from this analysis. First, the **geneivat da’at framework may be more potent than ona’ah** for this argument, because it requires no monetary threshold and captures the deceptive *impression* of fair exchange — the way AI companies frame data extraction as a minor operational detail rather than the core of their business model. Second, the **po’el okhel principle** (worker’s right to eat from the field) offers a constructive framework, not just a prohibition — it suggests users are entitled not to the entirety of AI profits but to a proportional share, calibrated to their contribution. Third, the **Aruch HaShulchan’s** understanding of ona’ah as a form of *gezeila* (theft) — not merely unfair pricing but actual taking — reframes the entire debate from “should companies compensate users?” to “how long will companies be permitted to take what is not theirs?”

The Talmud’s insistence that the disclosure burden falls on the seller, not the buyer, directly inverts the Silicon Valley assumption that burying data extraction in 50-page terms of service constitutes meaningful consent. As Meir Tamari wrote, “Jewish law rejects the concept of ‘Let the buyer beware’; the burden of ensuring that the buyer is not deceived is on the seller.” In the AI economy, the sellers of intelligence are the users themselves — and they have not been told what their goods are worth.
