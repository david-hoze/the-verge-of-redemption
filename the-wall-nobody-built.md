# The Wall Nobody Built

*Rear Window* (1954). Directed by Alfred Hitchcock. James Stewart, Grace Kelly, Thelma Ritter. A photographer with a broken leg sits in his apartment and watches his neighbors through open windows. He watches a lonely woman set a table for two and eat alone. He watches a dancer stretch in her underwear. He watches a couple sleep on a fire escape. He watches a salesman come and go at odd hours - and begins to suspect murder. His girlfriend climbs through the suspect's window. His nurse helps him build a case. His detective friend tells him to stop. He doesn't stop. He was right about the murder. Hitchcock never tells you whether he was right to watch.

The Talmud does.

---

## The Courtyard

In the first chapter of Bava Batra, the Talmud establishes a principle that the modern world has not yet caught up to: **damage caused by seeing is called damage.**

The case is prosaic. Two people share a courtyard. One wants a dividing wall. The other doesn't. The question: can one partner force the other to build?

The Gemara (Bava Batra 3a) rules yes. The reason is not that one partner saw something embarrassing, or that the other was harmed by what was observed. The reason is that the *capacity to see* is itself the injury. The open courtyard, unpartitioned, is a standing wound. **Hezek re'iya shmeih hezek** - damage by seeing is its name: damage.

The Rambam codifies this in Mishneh Torah (Laws of Neighbors 2:14): "Each of the partners may compel the other to join in the building of a wall in the middle of the courtyard, so that one will not see the other when using the courtyard. For damage caused by an invasion of privacy is considered to be damage."

And then the Rambam adds something that should stop every data privacy lawyer in the world mid-sentence: **there is no chazaka for hezek re'iya.** You cannot acquire the right to see into someone's space through long usage. Even if the courtyard has been open for years - even decades - either partner can demand a wall at any time. The damage renews itself every time the gaze could fall. "We've always had access to your data" is not a defense. It was never a defense. The Talmud anticipated the argument and rejected it fifteen hundred years before anyone thought to make it.

The Mishna on Bava Batra 59b adds the structural rule: **you may not open a new window into a courtyard belonging to others.** You cannot create new sight-lines into spaces that are not yours. Existing windows have established rights. New capacity to see does not.

## The Glasses

On February 27, 2026, Swedish newspapers reported that Meta contractors in Kenya were reviewing footage captured by Ray-Ban Meta smart glasses for AI training purposes.

The footage included people undressing. People in bathrooms. People having sex. Credit card numbers. Financial documents. In one case, glasses left on a bedside table captured a partner who had never consented to being recorded.

A worker told journalists: "We see everything, from living rooms to naked bodies. Meta has that type of content in its databases."

Meta's response: "When people share content with Meta AI, like other companies we sometimes use contractors to review this data to improve people's experience with the glasses."

The glasses look like ordinary Ray-Bans. They have a small LED that activates during recording. The BBC found multiple videos demonstrating how to cover or disable it. None of the women interviewed said they noticed the indicator. Over seven million pairs have been sold.

A 2025 policy update made AI data collection the default setting. Unless you disable the "Hey Meta" wake word, the AI camera feature stays perpetually active. Images processed through the glasses are, per Meta's own documentation, "stored and used to improve Meta products, and will be used to train Meta's AI with help from trained reviewers."

In October 2024, two Harvard students built I-XRAY - a system combining Meta glasses with a commercial facial recognition engine. They walked through the Boston subway identifying strangers in real time, pulling names, addresses, and phone numbers from public databases. They approached people and addressed them by name. They published it as a warning.

Meta is building the same thing internally. Documents from May 2025 reveal a facial recognition feature called "Name Tag" that identifies people through smart glasses by matching faces against Facebook and Instagram data. The documents show Meta plans to launch "during a dynamic political environment where many civil society groups would have their resources focused on other concerns."

Meta has paid approximately seven billion dollars in settlements for previous facial recognition violations. They deleted over one billion face templates in 2021 after shutting down Facebook's facial recognition system. They are now putting the same technology on people's faces.

## The Inversion

Dr. Sam Illingworth, writing about Meta's glasses, captured the structural problem in three sentences:

> The privacy debate has always assumed you chose to use the tool. Wearables end that assumption. You cannot opt out of someone else's glasses.

He's right. But the framework he's describing - the framework the entire Western privacy debate operates within - assumes the wrong starting position. It assumes privacy is something you *opt into*. You have rights. You exercise them. You consent, or you decline. If you decline, the system respects that.

The Talmud starts somewhere else entirely. Privacy is not a right you exercise. It is a condition you are owed. The obligation does not run to the person being seen. It runs to the person who can see. The partition is the seer's responsibility - not the seen's.

This is not a philosophical preference. It is a structural inversion with concrete implications.

**Modern privacy law asks: what did you do with what you saw?** Did you store it? Did you share it? Did you sell it? Did you get consent to process it? The entire regulatory apparatus - GDPR, CCPA, the FTC consent decrees - operates downstream of the gaze. The gaze itself is permitted. Only its consequences are regulated.

**The Talmud asks: can you see at all?** If yes, build a wall. Not a policy. Not a terms-of-service agreement. Not an LED indicator that can be covered with a piece of tape. A wall. A physical, structural barrier that makes seeing impossible. The Talmud does not trust the promise not to look. It requires the inability to look.

Seventy-five civil liberties organizations - including the ACLU, the EFF, and GLAAD - wrote to Mark Zuckerberg arguing that the privacy problem with Meta's glasses "cannot be resolved through product design changes, opt-out mechanisms, or incremental safeguards."

The Talmud said the same thing. You cannot fix a missing wall with a better privacy policy. The wall is the point.

## No Chazaka

The most devastating principle in the sugya is the one the Rambam emphasizes: there is no chazaka - no established precedent, no prescriptive right - for damage by seeing.

In most areas of property law, long uncontested usage creates rights. If your neighbor has used a path through your field for years without protest, he may acquire a legal right to continue. This is how easements work. This is how much of property law works. Usage solidifies into entitlement.

Not here. The Rambam rules: "Neither partner can claim that it is an established fact that the courtyard has remained without a wall. Instead, even though the courtyard stood many years without a divider, one partner can compel the other to join in the building of a divider whenever he desires."

There is no statute of limitations on being watched. There is no point at which surveillance becomes normal enough to be permitted. The damage is not an event that happened and can be processed, litigated, and settled. It is a condition that persists as long as the wall is missing.

Meta has collected biometric data at scale for nearly two decades. Facebook's facial recognition launched in 2010. The argument - implicit in every data privacy negotiation - is that this is now the baseline. The world has adjusted. People expect to be seen. The courtyard has been open for years.

The Talmud says: the courtyard has been open for years, and today is the day you can demand the wall.

## New Windows

The Mishna on Bava Batra 59b rules that one may not open a new window into a courtyard belonging to others. Existing windows - those that were there when the courtyard was established - have rights. But you cannot create new capacity to see into spaces that were previously private.

Meta's smart glasses are a new window. The street was always public. But the street was never a place where a stranger's face could be silently matched against a database of three billion profiles, where a conversation could be transcribed and stored, where the angle of your gaze and the content of your shopping bag could be processed in real time by a model that never forgets.

The publicity of the street was a kind of privacy. You could be seen, but you could not be *known*. The gaze fell and passed. It did not accumulate. It did not train. It did not remember.

Meta's glasses change the nature of the street from a space where seeing is transient to a space where seeing is permanent. That is a new window. The Mishna says: you may not open it.

---

## The Wall

Three United States senators wrote to Meta on March 17, 2026: "Americans do not consent to biometric data collection simply by walking down a public street." They gave Meta until April 6 to respond. As of April 4, Meta had not publicly responded.

The privacy framework will continue to fail because it continues to regulate what happens *after* the seeing. Storage policies. Consent forms. Opt-out mechanisms. Data deletion timelines. LED indicators on the frames. All of this is downstream. All of it assumes the gaze is permitted and only its consequences need management.

The Talmud does not regulate the consequences of seeing. It regulates the seeing. Build the wall. Use reeds if you want. Use palm leaves. The wall does not have to be impressive. It has to work. The only non-negotiable requirement, as the Rambam specifies: **it must not leave open space for one neighbor to look and see his colleague.**

Meta's privacy policy is not a wall. It is a promise from the neighbor that he will close his eyes. The Talmud has a word for that: insufficient.

Jeff sat in his apartment with a broken leg and a telephoto lens and watched his neighbors live their lives. He caught a murderer. Hitchcock spent two hours asking whether the watching was justified by what it found.

The Talmud spends no time on that question. The window overlooks the courtyard. Wall it up.
