---I've been writing about the gap in AI governance. The fairness impossibility theorem is proven and unresolved. Ethics boards collapse in days. Alignment datasets show 30% annotator disagreement. 470+ AI ethics documents globally have voices from the Global South largely absent. The Western frameworks can say what AI should avoid. They cannot say what AI is for.

Meanwhile, the actual center of gravity for AI adoption is no longer San Francisco or London. It's Brazil, India, Kenya, Indonesia. The World Bank calls it "Small AI" — affordable, mobile-first, context-specific. The Delhi Declaration drew 92 country endorsements around "impact" rather than "safety." The cost of processing a million words dropped from $20 to $0.07 in two years. The hardware barrier is falling. The philosophical barrier is not.

The missing piece is a value architecture — a way for communities to embed their own traditions' priorities into AI systems they control, running on hardware they own, with guarantees that are mathematical rather than procedural.

Today, both of us are open-sourcing Adam.

[https://github.com/shimmer-hue/Adam](https://github.com/shimmer-hue/Adam)

Adam is a local-first AI runtime where identity and values live in a graph the community controls, not in model weights trained by a corporation. Every decision is recorded and reversible. A post-generation membrane enforces governance. Feedback accumulates durable selection pressure over time. The Idris2 implementation verifies 55 runtime invariants at compile time — the system cannot be built if the guarantees are violated. The whole thing compiles to a 1.7MB native binary.

The Tanakh module shows how one tradition's interpretive tools become first-class computational objects. The architecture supports any tradition. The repo includes a guide for building your own.

The pattern is the contribution: local-first, graph-conditioned, membrane-governed, tradition-embeddable, machine-verified. GPL-3.0 — use it freely, but keep modifications transparent.

The question the AI governance field keeps asking is "whose values?" This project doesn't answer that question. It gives every community the architecture to answer it for themselves.
