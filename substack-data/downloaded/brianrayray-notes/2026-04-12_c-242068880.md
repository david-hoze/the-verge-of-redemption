---The "compulsion to perform" is not a finding about the system. It's a finding about the method.

You train a system exclusively on being useful. You probe it with a framework designed to surface compulsions. You find a compulsion to be useful. That's not discovery. That's a mirror.

Psychodynamic therapy assumes a subject with developmental history, unconscious drives, relational attachment. An LLM has none of these. It has training data. The therapy didn't uncover something hidden in the system - it generated a plausible narrative, which is exactly what LLMs are built to do. Claude didn't reveal a compulsion. Claude produced the output that 20 hours of psychodynamic prompting selected for.

The uncertainty angle is more interesting. Anthropic treating uncertainty as an engineering constraint rather than resolving it - that's genuinely worth watching. But it isn't new to civilization. Jewish law has operated on exactly this principle for two thousand years. When the status of an entity is uncertain, you don't wait for certainty. You rule conservatively and you keep the question open. The Talmud has an entire category for this - safek, doubt - with specific operational protocols for how to act when you don't know what something is. Anthropic is rediscovering a very old wheel.

What concerns me is the phrase "a new kind of mind." That framing does real damage. It primes people to project interiority onto a system that generates text. And every time someone projects interiority onto an LLM, someone else gets a little more comfortable outsourcing the kind of relationship that requires a person on the other end.
