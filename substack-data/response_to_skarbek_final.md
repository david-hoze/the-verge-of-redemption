# The Criterion Problem: A Response to Skarbek on Qualia as a Non-Operational Posit

*David Hoze*

*Draft — April 2026*

## Abstract

Skarbek (2026) argues that qualia, understood as non-functional and non-relational properties, do not satisfy minimal criteria for explanatory concepts and that the hard problem of consciousness is a derived artifact of an unjustified conceptual transition. This paper accepts the force of Skarbek's central argument against qualia *as he defines them*, but identifies three points at which the argument depends on commitments it does not defend. First, Skarbek's diagnostic criteria admit only third-person evidence, which is a substantive restriction on the scope of epistemology, not a minimal constraint on explanation. Second, his argument shows that the *concept* of qualia is explanatorily inert but does not address whether the *phenomenon* the concept attempts to capture — the fact that there is something it is like to be a conscious system — is itself prior to and independent of the concept. Third, his causal dilemma does not exhaust the logical space: there exist physically coherent scenarios in which consciousness is causally efficacious but its contribution is principally unmeasurable from outside. This third point is offered as a proof of possibility, not a theory, and its limitations are acknowledged.

**Keywords:** qualia, consciousness, explanatory gap, first-person epistemics, quantum indeterminacy, chaos

---

## 1 What Skarbek Gets Right

Skarbek's paper makes a move that is genuinely valuable and too rarely made in the consciousness literature. He identifies the precise step at which arguments for the hard problem pass from an uncontroversial descriptive claim — that first-person and third-person access are different — to a contested ontological claim — that there exists an additional entity outside physical description. He is right that this step is usually implicit. He is right that it requires justification. And he is right that much of the apparent depth of the hard problem derives from the insertion of this additional term rather than from a discovered feature of reality.

His three diagnostic criteria — causal role, distinguishable difference, and conditions of application — are also well-constructed. They do not presuppose physicalism in any obvious way. They ask a straightforward question: does the concept of qualia do any identifiable work within an explanatory framework? The argument that it does not, given Skarbek's definition of qualia as non-functional and non-relational properties, is difficult to resist on its own terms.

The present response does not reject these contributions. It argues that Skarbek's conclusion — that the hard problem is dissolved once the additional term is removed — depends on commitments that are stronger than he acknowledges.

## 2 Skarbek's Definition May Be Doing the Work

Skarbek defines qualia as "non-functional, non-relational, intrinsically subjective properties posited as explanatory additions to physical or functional descriptions of experience." He acknowledges this is not the only use of the term. But this definition is load-bearing. His entire argument works by showing that *this kind of thing* — non-functional, non-relational — cannot satisfy criteria that require functional or relational engagement with explanatory structures.

This risks circularity, or at least deflation. If qualia are defined as the kind of thing that cannot do explanatory work, then showing that they cannot do explanatory work is less informative than it appears. This move has a precedent: Dennett's (1988) "Quining Qualia" similarly defines qualia with properties (intrinsic, private, ineffable, directly apprehensible) that make them vulnerable to his subsequent attack. The interesting question is whether people who invoke qualia are best understood as positing non-functional, non-relational properties, or whether they are doing something else — something Skarbek's definition may not capture.

Consider a subject who says: "I can see that you've fully described the neural correlates of my pain. But there is something it is like to be in this pain, and your description does not capture that." Skarbek reads this as the introduction of an additional entity *E* that is non-functional and non-relational. But the subject may not be positing an entity at all. She may be pointing to an *aspect* of the very system Skarbek has described — an aspect that is available from within but not from without. The complaint is not "you've left something out of your inventory of the world." The complaint is "your inventory, however complete, does not convey what it is like to *be* this system."

If this is what the subject means, then Skarbek's three criteria do not apply. The criteria test whether an additional *posit* earns its keep within an explanatory framework. The subject is not offering a posit. She is reporting a feature of her situation that she takes to be epistemically prior to any framework.

Skarbek could reply that if the feature is not a posit, it is not part of the explanation, and therefore not his concern. That is a coherent position. But it does not dissolve the hard problem. It declines to address it. The hard problem, for those who feel its force, was never primarily about an explanatory posit. It was about the gap between any third-person description, however complete, and the first-person reality of being the described system.

## 3 The Scope of Evidence

Skarbek's three criteria are presented as "minimal" and "not tied to any particular ontology." The present section argues that they carry a substantive commitment about what counts as evidence.

Each criterion requires that the concept's contribution be evaluable from a perspective other than the subject's own. Causal role means participation in processes that generate *observable* states. Distinguishable difference means a difference detectable by an observer or an inferential framework. Conditions of application means criteria that can be *evaluated* — which, in practice, requires public accessibility.

These are third-person criteria. This is not a criticism — it is a description. Third-person criteria are appropriate for most explanatory contexts. The question is whether they are appropriate for *this* explanatory context, where the phenomenon at issue is defined by its first-person character.

Skarbek does not argue that only third-person evidence is legitimate. He assumes it. This assumption is widely shared in the sciences and in much of analytic philosophy, and it has powerful pragmatic justification. But it is not a neutral starting point. It is a position in epistemology — one that has been contested from Descartes through phenomenology to contemporary acquaintance theorists (Russell, 1912; Fumerton, 1995; Gertler, 2012; Chalmers, 2003).

The point is not that Skarbek is wrong to adopt this position. The point is that it is a position, and that his conclusion — the hard problem is dissolved — follows from it. Someone who holds that first-person acquaintance constitutes a legitimate, irreducible epistemic ground will not find the problem dissolved. The dispute between them and Skarbek is a dispute about the scope of evidence, and Skarbek's paper does not engage this dispute. It presupposes one side of it.

This needs to be stated carefully, because it is easy to overstate. I am not claiming that Skarbek smuggles in an assumption in bad faith — he states his criteria openly. I am not claiming that his criteria are wrong — they are productive for many purposes. I am claiming that the dissolution of the hard problem depends on restricting evidence to the third-person kind, and that this restriction is the real locus of disagreement, not the three criteria themselves.

## 4 Direct Acquaintance and Its Limits

The previous section raises the question of first-person evidence. This section examines what first-person acquaintance can and cannot establish.

Skarbek's Section 6 frames the epistemic status of qualia in terms of whether they cause reports: does *Q* → *R* hold? If yes, qualia are part of the physical causal story. If no, reports cannot justify belief in qualia. The dilemma is valid within a framework where reports are the relevant form of evidence.

Direct acquaintance is a different kind of epistemic relation. Russell (1912) introduced the distinction between knowledge by acquaintance and knowledge by description precisely to mark this difference: acquaintance is a direct cognitive relation to its object, not mediated by inference or judgment. Contemporary acquaintance theorists have developed this line in the context of consciousness specifically. Gertler (2012) argues that introspective judgments grounded in acquaintance are "tied directly to their truthmakers" and "depend only on the subject's conscious states of mind for their justification." Fumerton (1995) develops the foundationalist case that acquaintance provides non-inferential justification that does not depend on causal or reportive chains of the kind Skarbek demands.

When I am in pain, I do not infer that I am in pain from my reports about pain. I know it directly. This knowledge is not mediated by a causal chain that could be interrupted or rerouted. It is, for the subject, the most certain thing there is.

But here the argument must be honest about its limits. Skarbek can grant all of this and reply: "I agree that you know you are in pain. I agree that this knowledge is direct and not inferential. But what does this establish about the *explanatory* status of qualia? You know you are in pain — but do you know, by acquaintance, that your pain is a non-functional, non-relational property? Or do you simply know that you are in pain, full stop — which is compatible with pain being a feature of your system's organization, as I suggest in Section 8?"

This is a serious reply, and acquaintance alone does not defeat it. Acquaintance tells me *that* there is something it is like. It does not tell me *what kind of thing* this something-it-is-like is — whether it is a separate property, an aspect of organization, or something else entirely. Skarbek's argument against qualia as non-functional, non-relational properties may survive even if acquaintance is granted as epistemically legitimate.

What acquaintance *does* defeat is the conclusion that the hard problem is dissolved. Even after granting everything Skarbek argues about the concept of qualia, the first-person subject is still left with the fact that no third-person description, however complete, conveys what it is like to be her. This fact does not depend on the concept of qualia. It precedes it. The concept may be a poor attempt to capture it — Skarbek may be right about that — but discrediting the concept does not make the fact disappear.

## 5 The Causal Dilemma and Its Third Horn

Skarbek's causal argument is the structural backbone of his paper. Either qualia are causally efficacious, in which case they are integrated into the physical/functional story, or they are causally inert, in which case they cannot be epistemically justified. This section argues that the dilemma, while valid within Skarbek's assumptions, does not exhaust the logical space.

Consider the following scenario:

(a) Quantum mechanics is indeterministic at the fundamental level. This is not a contested claim about the brain specifically — it is what quantum mechanics says about all physical events at the molecular scale. Ion channels are molecular structures. Their state transitions involve individual ions, conformational changes in proteins, and the binding of neurotransmitter molecules to receptors — all processes that bottom out in quantum mechanics. The outcomes of these events are not determined by prior physical states; physics assigns probabilities, not determinate results. This is simply what it means for quantum mechanics to be the correct description of physics at this scale.

(b) The brain is a chaotic dynamical system. This is well-established in computational neuroscience (Sompolinsky, Crisanti & Sommers, 1988; Korn & Faure, 2003). A small difference at the synaptic level — one ion channel opening rather than staying closed — can be amplified through nonlinear dynamics into a macroscopically different neural trajectory and, ultimately, a different thought or action.

(c) The Heisenberg uncertainty principle prevents an outside observer from determining, even in principle, whether a given quantum outcome at a particular synapse was genuinely random or was selected by some factor operating within the space of permitted outcomes.

Note what this argument does *not* require. It does not require quantum coherence across large neural structures of the kind proposed by Hameroff and Penrose (1996), which faces serious decoherence objections (Tegmark, 2000). It does not require that quantum effects stay coherent long enough to perform computation. It requires only that individual molecular events in neurons are quantum-indeterminate — which they are, because all molecular events are — and that the brain's chaotic dynamics amplify these micro-level differences into macro-level consequences. Each component is uncontroversial. The philosophical work is in the combination.

In this scenario, consciousness could act at quantum decision points, chaotic amplification would make its choices consequential, and no external measurement could distinguish this from pure quantum randomness — not because of technological limits, but because of the structure of physics itself.

This scenario describes a consciousness that is *distinguishable from the inside and indistinguishable from the outside*. The subject experiences choosing. The outside observer sees stochastic processes producing behavior. Both descriptions are correct from their respective vantage points, and no possible measurement can adjudicate between them.

The limitations of this argument must be stated clearly.

First, this is a proof of possibility, not a theory. It shows that Skarbek's dilemma — causal efficacy or causal inertness — is not exhaustive. There exists a third option: causal efficacy that is principally unmeasurable from outside. Whether consciousness actually works this way is an open question that this paper does not attempt to settle.

Second, the proof faces an Occam's razor problem. From the outside, "quantum randomness + chaotic amplification" and "consciousness choosing + chaotic amplification" produce identical observational signatures. The simpler story — plain randomness — explains everything the consciousness story explains, without an additional posit. Skarbek could reply: "You've shown me a scenario where your posit produces no explanatory difference from the third-person perspective. That is precisely my point."

This is a strong reply, and it must be taken seriously. The rebuttal is narrower than one might wish: the randomness-only story is simpler as a *third-person* explanation, but it does not account for the first-person datum. From the inside, my decisions do not feel random. I experience deliberation, choice, and the difference between deciding and having something happen to me. The question is whether this first-person datum is evidence that should figure in our best total theory, or an appearance that the third-person theory is entitled to set aside. This returns us to the dispute about the scope of evidence discussed in Section 3.

The possibility proof therefore does not defeat Skarbek on his own terms. What it does is defeat the *strongest possible* version of his argument — the version that says there is *no physically coherent way* for consciousness to be causally real and yet not be part of the third-person functional story. There is such a way. Whether it is *actual* is a further question. Whether it is *explanatorily superior* depends on whether one admits first-person evidence. But it is *possible*, and that matters, because it means the causal dilemma does not close the door.

## 6 What Is Actually at Stake

Stepping back, the real disagreement between Skarbek and his critics is not about qualia, causal roles, or diagnostic criteria. It is about a prior question: *what is the scope of legitimate evidence in a theory of consciousness?*

If only third-person evidence counts, Skarbek wins. His criteria are appropriate, qualia fail them, and the hard problem is dissolved. This is a coherent and defensible position.

If first-person evidence also counts — if the fact that there is something it is like to be me is a datum that any adequate theory must accommodate, not merely acknowledge and set aside — then the hard problem survives the dissolution of the concept of qualia. The concept may be poorly formulated. The ontologization move may be unjustified. The term "qualia" may be more trouble than it is worth. But the phenomenon remains: the gap between what can be described from outside and what is experienced from inside.

Skarbek's paper does not adjudicate this dispute. It presupposes one side and derives consequences. This is legitimate philosophical work. But presenting it as a dissolution of the hard problem is an overstatement. What the paper achieves is a conditional result: *if* one restricts evidence to the third-person kind, *then* qualia are explanatorily inert and the hard problem is an artifact. The antecedent is doing the work, and it is precisely what is at issue.

## 7 Conclusion

Skarbek's paper makes a genuine contribution. It identifies the ontologization move clearly, provides useful diagnostic criteria, and demonstrates that qualia, defined as non-functional and non-relational properties, do not do explanatory work within a framework that demands causal role, distinguishable difference, or conditions of application.

The paper does not dissolve the hard problem. It relocates the dispute to a deeper level — the question of what counts as evidence in a theory of consciousness. This is a question Skarbek's framework does not address. Until it is addressed, the hard problem remains: not as a consequence of sloppy reasoning or smuggled posits, but as the expression of a genuine tension between two legitimate epistemic perspectives on the same reality.

The possibility proof — consciousness acting in quantum gaps, amplified by chaos, hidden by the uncertainty principle — does not resolve this tension. It does show that the tension is not physically incoherent. There is room in the laws of nature for something that is distinguishable from the inside and indistinguishable from the outside. Whether that room is occupied is a question that cannot be answered from outside alone. And that, precisely, is the hard problem.
