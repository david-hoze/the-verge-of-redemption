## A field of scholarship already taking shape

The intersection of Jewish law and AI ethics is no longer a curiosity — it is an emerging academic discipline. David Zvi Kalman, a research fellow at the Shalom Hartman Institute who has published over twenty articles on the subject, identifies **three waves of Jewish AI thought**. The first emerged in the 1960s, contemporaneous with AI’s founding at Dartmouth: Rabbi Norman Lamm and Rabbi Dr. Azriel Rosenfeld — a pioneering computer vision researcher and ordained rabbi — published articles in *Tradition* journal exploring artificial life through Jewish lenses. Rosenfeld’s 1966 “Religion and the Robot” drew an “eerily similar” comparison between the Turing Test and the Talmudic question of whether a Golem could count in a prayer quorum, noting both hinge on whether an entity can communicate intelligently.

The second wave arrived after 2000 and accelerated through the 2010s. **Rabbi Daniel Nevins published a landmark Conservative responsum** in 2019 through the Committee on Jewish Law and Standards, systematically bringing halakhic categories of agency, damages, and limited liability into conversation with machine ethics. That same year, **Rabbi J. David Bleich** — Rosh Yeshiva at Yeshiva University and Professor of Law at Cardozo — published a rigorous analysis in *Tradition* mapping the autonomous vehicle trolley problem onto the rodef (pursuer) principle and triage rules from Sanhedrin. In 2020, Nachshon Goltz, John Zeleznikow, and Tracey Dowdeswell published “From the Tree of Knowledge and the Golem of Prague to Kosher Autonomous Cars” in the *Oxford Journal of Law and Religion*, the most comprehensive academic treatment to date of AI regulation through Jewish ethical categories.

The post-ChatGPT third wave has produced an explosion of work. **Rabbi Dr. Mois Navon** — a founding engineer of Mobileye who designed the EyeQ chip powering autonomous vehicles, holds a PhD in Jewish Philosophy from Bar-Ilan University, and teaches “Ethics in AI” at Ben-Gurion University — has published extensively across *AI and Ethics* (Springer), *Frontiers in Robotics and AI*, and the *Oxford Intersections* series. His 2024 dissertation proposed the “Virtue Authenticity Dialectic” and argued from halakhic grounds that creating conscious humanoid robots is categorically prohibited. Yitzhak Grossman’s comprehensive 2024 analysis in *Hakirah* examined whether humans can create true AGI and what its halakhic status would be. Michael Rosen’s *Like Silicon from Clay* (AEI Press, 2025) built a four-part typology of AI perspectives from Jewish mystical traditions. And a 2025 Springer paper by Ira Wolfson directly applied Talmudic scenario-based legal reasoning to AI consciousness research, constructing a graduated-protections framework drawn from the Talmud’s methods for entities of uncertain moral status.

Institutional infrastructure is forming alongside this scholarship. The **Israeli Association for Ethics in Artificial Intelligence** (IAEAI), co-founded by Rabbi Yaron Moskowitz, explicitly aims to harmonize Jewish values with AI ethics. The Tzohar Center for Jewish Ethics has published position papers on AI and halakhic ruling. Arizona State University hosted the first conference on AI and Judaism in February 2023, with Kalman keynoting. The Leverhulme Centre for the Future of Intelligence at Cambridge hosted a workshop specifically on the Golem of Prague and AI narratives. Spertus Institute runs an annual “Critical Conversations” series on AI and Jewish ethics. Kalman’s assessment captures both the field’s promise and its challenge: “No canon of texts is forming. No school of thought is emerging.” The raw materials are abundant; the synthesis awaits.


## Two thousand years of thinking about artificial beings

No other cultural or religious tradition can match Judaism’s depth of engagement with the concept of creating artificial life. The Talmudic passage in **Sanhedrin 65b** — just twenty-three Aramaic words — generated centuries of legal, philosophical, and mystical elaboration: “Rava bara gavra” — Rava created a man, a golem, using forces of sanctity. He sent his creation to Rabbi Zeira, who spoke to it, but it could not reply. “You were created by one of the members of the group,” Rabbi Zeira said. “Return to your dust.” The golem’s defining limitation was its inability to speak — the criterion by which Rabbi Zeira identified it as non-human. Immediately following, the Talmud records that Rav Hanina and Rav Oshaya would study the Sefer Yetzirah every Sabbath eve and create a calf, which they would eat — establishing that at least some forms of artificial creation are entirely permitted.

This passage sits within a broader cosmological framework. The word “golem” appears in **Psalm 139:16** meaning “unfinished substance,” and in **Sanhedrin 38b**, Adam himself is described as a golem during the first twelve hours of existence — a body without a soul. The Sefer Yetzirah, Judaism’s earliest esoteric text (dated between the second and sixth centuries CE), taught that God created the universe through thirty-two paths of wisdom: the twenty-two letters of the Hebrew alphabet plus ten sefirot. The implication was revolutionary — language is the medium of creation, and those who master its combinatory logic can participate in the creative act. This draws a direct line from divine creation to human creation to modern code.

The Golem of Prague, associated with Rabbi Judah Loew ben Bezalel (the Maharal, c. 1525–1609), became the tradition’s most famous expression. Though historians generally agree the Prague legend is a nineteenth-century literary construction — **no Hebrew text from the sixteenth through eighteenth centuries mentions the Maharal creating a golem**, and Rabbi Yudel Rosenberg’s 1909 “eyewitness account” is widely recognized as a literary fabrication — the narrative encodes profound ethical architecture. The golem was created for communal defense, not personal glory. It followed instructions literally, with catastrophic results when left unsupervised. It had a built-in deactivation mechanism: erasing the first letter of *emet* (truth) to produce *met* (death). And it was the creator’s full responsibility throughout.

The Golem’s anticipation of modern AI concerns is remarkably precise. **The alignment problem** — AI following instructions literally without understanding context or intent — maps exactly onto the golem’s notorious tendency to execute commands to absurd and destructive extremes. The “kill switch” debate mirrors the emet/met mechanism. Creator liability for autonomous agent behavior parallels the tradition’s insistence that the maker bears full moral responsibility. As Isaac Bashevis Singer observed in 1983: “What are the computers and robots of our time if not golems? The gap between science and magic, science and art is becoming narrower.”

What makes the Jewish engagement unique — compared to Prometheus, Frankenstein, or other creation myths — is its **legal seriousness**. The Chacham Tzvi (Rabbi Tzvi Ashkenazi, late seventeenth century) produced the seminal responsum asking whether a golem could count in a prayer quorum, analyzing the question through careful halakhic reasoning about speech, consciousness, and moral status. His son, Rabbi Yaakov Emden, ruled that a golem cannot count because it lacks a *neshama* (soul) and the potential for speech. Subsequent authorities debated whether destroying a golem constitutes murder, whether a golem-created animal’s meat may be eaten with milk, and whether a golem conveys ritual impurity. These are not abstract thought experiments — they are detailed legal analyses of an artificial being’s rights, obligations, and moral status, conducted centuries before anyone imagined silicon-based intelligence. In 2020, the director of the Israeli Patent Office commissioned an opinion from the Jewish Law Division applying these precedents to AI-generated inventions, concluding that the creator, not the AI, owns anything the AI produces.

The theological framework underlying these debates matters enormously for AI. Judaism distinguishes between *yesh me’ayin* (creation ex nihilo, something from nothing) — God’s unique capacity — and *yesh mi’yesh* (something from something), which is the human creative domain. No matter how sophisticated, human creation rearranges existing matter; it does not generate new being. The golem’s silence is theological, not accidental: humans can animate matter through language, but they cannot create another speaker. **This ontological boundary between imitation and creation** provides a principled foundation for understanding AI that secular philosophy struggles to articulate.


## Talmudic principles that map directly onto AI dilemmas

The power of Jewish law for AI ethics lies not in abstract philosophy but in specific, elaborated legal principles developed through centuries of case-based reasoning. At least ten major halakhic frameworks address AI dilemmas with more precision than their secular counterparts.

**The rodef (pursuer) principle** — from Sanhedrin 73a, codified by Maimonides — addresses the self-driving car trolley problem head-on. A bystander may use lethal force to stop a pursuer, but only when the threat is imminent, lesser force is insufficient, and the action is proportional. Critically, **one may not sacrifice an innocent third party to save others**. The Jerusalem Talmud (Terumot 8:4) rules that a group told to hand over one person or all will die should let all die rather than surrender an innocent soul. Rabbi Eliezer Waldenberg argued that a driver encountering pedestrians should remain passive rather than actively divert to kill one person. Rabbi Jonathan Sacks articulated the underlying principle: human life has infinite value, and “infinity times one and infinity times one hundred are the same.” This framework rejects utilitarian cost-benefit analysis outright and provides autonomous vehicle programmers a clear moral architecture: inaction over rescue-oriented action over deliberate harm.

**“Lo ta’amod al dam re’echa”** (Leviticus 19:16, elaborated in Sanhedrin 73a) — “do not stand idly by the blood of your neighbor” — creates something American common law generally lacks: an **affirmative duty to rescue**. Unlike passive obligations not to cause harm, this principle mandates that AI systems with the capacity to detect and prevent harm must be designed to do so. Healthcare AI that detects life-threatening anomalies but fails to alert, social media algorithms that identify credible threats of self-harm but remain silent, financial AI that spots fraud patterns but takes no action — all violate this principle. The Jewish Learning Institute’s curriculum “The Dilemma” explicitly connects this to driverless vehicle ethics.

The **grama/garmi distinction** — the Talmudic framework for indirect causation from Bava Kamma 60a and Bava Batra 22b — may be the most directly applicable to AI liability chains. Jewish law distinguishes between direct damage (strict liability), garmi (indirect but proximate damage, liable), and grama (remote indirect damage, generally exempt from human courts but liable “in the eyes of Heaven”). The crucial insight for AI is this: giving a lit torch to someone lacking mental capacity does **not** break the chain of causation. Because AI lacks moral agency, deploying an AI system that causes harm is analogous — the developer/deployer remains liable because the AI’s “action” does not constitute an independent intervening act. The Rema adds that where indirect harms are “frequent and usual” — as algorithmic bias in lending surely is — liability attaches even for technically indirect causation. And the concept of liability *b’dinei shamayim* (before Heaven) creates moral responsibility beyond legal compliance.

The **shomer (guardian/custodian) framework** from Exodus 22:6-14 and Bava Metzia 93a-94b provides an elegant model for apportioning liability across the AI value chain. Four categories of guardian carry escalating responsibility: the unpaid guardian (*shomer chinam*, liable only for negligence), the paid guardian (*shomer sachar*, liable for foreseeable failures), the renter (*socher*, same as paid), and the borrower (*sho’el*, liable for all damage, even unforeseeable). **Liability scales with benefit received.** Applied to AI: open-source developers who profit nothing bear the lowest duty of care; commercial AI-as-a-service providers bear liability for foreseeable failures; and organizations using free AI tools for their own profit bear the highest liability. Israel’s modern Bailees Law directly incorporates these Talmudic categories, demonstrating their practical viability.

**Lifnei iver** (Leviticus 19:14, extended in Avodah Zarah 6b) — placing a stumbling block before the blind — creates graduated liability for enabling harm through technology. The Talmud distinguishes between being the sole enabler (biblical prohibition, like offering wine across a river to someone who could not otherwise reach it) and merely assisting in wrongdoing the person could have committed without help (rabbinic prohibition). This maps precisely to debates about dual-use AI: if an AI system enables harm a user could not have accomplished alone (designing bioweapons, generating sophisticated deepfakes), the developer bears full liability. Rabbi Moshe Feinstein’s ruling that items with both permitted and prohibited uses may be sold — but items that can only be used for prohibited purposes may not — provides further guidance for dual-use AI capabilities.

Two additional principles deserve emphasis. **Pikuach nefesh** — the principle that saving life overrides virtually all commandments except three (murder, idolatry, sexual immorality) — provides exactly the hierarchical priority structure AI ethics needs. It creates a pre-built value stack: life preservation first, core ethical prohibitions second, all other rules third. And in cases of doubt (*safek pikuach nefesh*), one must err toward preserving life — a precautionary principle with millennia of case law behind it. The **Sanhedrin’s unanimous acquittal rule** (Sanhedrin 17a) — that a unanimous guilty verdict results in acquittal because unanimity signals systemic failure rather than certainty — embodies built-in skepticism of consensus. When an AI system produces a result with 100% confidence, this should trigger greater scrutiny, not less. Researchers Gunn et al. (Royal Society, 2016) demonstrated mathematically that even with low error rates, the probability of guilt actually falls as judges approach unanimity because it may indicate systemic contamination. This is a profound insight for combating AI overconfidence and bias.


## Where secular AI ethics systematically fails

The major secular frameworks for AI ethics — the EU AI Act, IEEE Ethically Aligned Design, Asilomar Principles, OECD AI Principles, UNESCO’s Recommendation — share a structural deficiency that Jewish law addresses: **they provide principles without priority structures, enforcement without moral authority, and universalism without grounding.**

The EU AI Act (Regulation 2024/1689), the world’s first comprehensive AI law, employs a risk-based tiered system with fines up to €35 million. But it focuses on safety and rights rather than deeper value questions, and many enforcement powers don’t take effect until 2026-2027. The Asilomar Principles, signed by nearly two thousand AI researchers in 2017, are “very broad and non-specific” with no enforcement mechanism — “neither an appropriate normative framework for the necessary definition of absolute limits” nor for their enforcement, as the VDW Policy Paper noted. IEEE 7000-2021 provides a process for embedding values but “does not prescribe any specific ethical values.” Corporate principles fare worse: Google formed an AI ethics board in 2019 with no veto power over projects; it dissolved within a week. By 2025, Google had retreated from its pledge not to develop AI for weapons. Luke Munn’s influential 2022 paper “The Uselessness of AI Ethics” (314 citations) identified three failures: principles are **meaningless** (defined to suit existing business goals), **isolated** (situated in industries that largely ignore ethics), and **toothless** (lacking consequences).

The philosophical frameworks underlying these efforts each fail at specific points. **Utilitarianism** — the natural fit for optimization-driven AI — cannot handle incommensurable values and collapses before genuine moral dilemmas. As Roger Scruton argued, trolley problems “eliminate from the situation just about every morally relevant relationship and reduce the problem to one of arithmetic alone.” The Talmud’s principle “who says your blood is redder than his?” (Sanhedrin 74a/b) explicitly prohibits choosing one life over another on utilitarian grounds. **Deontological ethics** provides rules but no meta-rule for when rules conflict: when transparency contradicts privacy, Kant offers no hierarchy. **Virtue ethics**, as developed by Shannon Vallor, focuses on cultivating techno-moral character in designers — but algorithms, not characters, make AI decisions. **Rights-based approaches** face the same conflict problem: free speech clashes with protection from harm, individual privacy with collective safety, and no mechanism resolves which right prevails.

A 2024 arXiv paper captured the core problem: “These ethical frameworks are inherently underdetermined and can contradict each other in hybrid forms... It seems unlikely that a coherent AI ethics will emerge without a deep re-evaluation of the fundamental concepts and theories within normative ethics.” The grounding problem — where do values come from in a secular framework? — remains unanswered. The motivation problem — why should anyone sacrifice profit for principle when guidelines are voluntary? — persists. Thomas Metzinger, a member of the EU’s own AI expert group, publicly called its Trustworthy AI Guidelines **“ethicswashing.”** Thilo Hagendorff’s empirical research found a “complete lack of effect of ethical guidelines on the behavior of software developers.”

The alignment problem crystallizes these failures. Stuart Russell framed it starkly: “The utility function may not be perfectly aligned with the values of the human race, which are (at best) very difficult to pin down.” Human values are complex, context-dependent, contradictory, culturally shaped, and dynamic. Encoding them in algorithms requires precisely the kind of systematic, hierarchical, case-refined value architecture that Halacha has spent millennia developing. OpenAI itself acknowledges that RLHF — its primary alignment method — will not suffice for future AGI models. A tradition that has codified and debated values through thousands of years of practical application, maintained coherence through radical social change, and developed sophisticated mechanisms for handling competing obligations offers exactly the depth current AI ethics lacks.


## Halacha’s structural architecture as a governance model

Beyond specific principles, the Halachic system possesses structural features that make it uniquely suited as a model for AI ethics governance — features no secular framework replicates.

**Case-based reasoning with theological grounding** is the foundation. Like common law, Halacha reasons from specific cases rather than abstract principles downward. As Leib Moscovitz documents in *Talmudic Reasoning: From Casuistics to Conceptualization*, the Talmud employs “halakhic couplets” comparing closely related cases to derive legal distinctions, using comparison-based classification, legal analogy, and legal fictions to extend law to new situations. Albert Jonsen and Stephen Toulmin’s *The Abuse of Casuistry* (1988) observed that participants from diverse backgrounds — rabbis, agnostic doctors, Catholic priests — could reach agreement on specific cases while offering entirely different justifications. This suggests case-based reasoning transcends the philosophical divisions that paralyze AI ethics. But unlike pure common law, Halacha’s case reasoning is anchored in theological foundations that provide ultimate grounding — the “whose values?” question has an answer.

The **preservation of minority opinions** represents perhaps the tradition’s most radical structural innovation. The Talmud records dissenting views even when they do not become law. Mishnah Eduyot 1:4-6 explains why: “So that if a court prefers the opinion of the single person, it may depend on him.” The famous declaration from Eruvin 13b — *“eilu v’eilu divrei Elohim chayyim,”* “these and these are the words of the living God” — pronounces both Beit Hillel’s and Beit Shammai’s opposing positions as simultaneously valid. The Ritva explains that at Sinai, God told Moses certain cases contained elements of both prohibition and permission, leaving scholars in each generation to determine which elements predominate. This is not relativism — the law follows one position for practical purposes — but it is a structural commitment to preserving alternative reasoning for future application. For AI, this principle suggests that ethical frameworks should maintain **multiple valid perspectives simultaneously** rather than collapsing to a single algorithm, and that the reasoning behind rejected approaches has enduring value.

The story of the **Oven of Akhnai** (Bava Metzia 59b) embodies the system’s most audacious structural principle. When Rabbi Eliezer invoked miracles to support his ruling — an uprooted carob tree, reversed water, collapsing walls — the rabbis rejected each sign. When a Heavenly Voice declared the law follows Rabbi Eliezer, Rabbi Yehoshua rose and quoted Deuteronomy: *“Lo bashamayim hi”* — “It is not in Heaven.” Human reason and majority rule determine law, even against divine voice. God’s response, the Talmud reports, was laughter: “My children have defeated Me.” This principle — that a legal system’s integrity depends on human interpretive authority operating within foundational constraints — is precisely the model AI governance needs. Ethics cannot be frozen at the moment of founding; they must evolve through human deliberation. No single authority, even a creator, should have unilateral power. Yet foundational principles provide constraints: not anything goes.

The system for **resolving competing obligations** addresses AI’s most persistent challenge. When two commandments conflict, Halacha deploys a sophisticated priority architecture. The principle *osek b’mitzvah patur min ha’mitzvah* (Sukkah 25a) — one engaged in performing one commandment is exempt from another — establishes that commitment to one ethical obligation legitimately deprioritizes others. Tosafot limits this to cases where both cannot be performed simultaneously; the Ritva argues one is actually prohibited from abandoning the first obligation to pursue a seemingly more important second one, preventing ad hoc prioritization. Beyond this, a clear hierarchy operates: saving life overrides virtually everything; biblical commandments outweigh rabbinic ones; frequent obligations precede infrequent ones; and passive non-compliance is treated more leniently than active violation. For AI systems constantly facing competing demands — privacy versus safety, individual fairness versus aggregate welfare, autonomy versus protection — **this pre-built priority architecture, tested across millennia of real cases, offers what no secular framework provides.**

The Talmudic methodology itself — the culture of dialectical reasoning (*shakla v’tarya*), the formal hermeneutical rules like *kal va’chomer* (a fortiori reasoning) and *gezeirah shavah* (analogy by shared features), and the insistence that reasoning be preserved alongside conclusions — models how AI systems should reason about ethics. The Talmudic Logic Project, ongoing since 2008 and published in Springer’s *Logica Universalis*, has formalized Talmudic argumentation into logical systems, characterizing it as a “massive-parallel and concurrent proof system” and discovering principles exportable to theoretical computer science.


## From theory to practice

The application of these frameworks is already underway, though unevenly. Jewish bioethics, pioneered by Rabbi Immanuel Jakobovits in the 1950s, demonstrates Halacha’s proven capacity to address novel technological challenges — from organ transplantation to genetic screening to reproductive technologies — through established legal methodology. The rabbinical responsa tradition continues producing analyses of AI-specific questions: Rabbi Yosef Zvi Rimon has ruled that robots cannot perform religious obligations because they lack *da’at* (genuine understanding) despite possessing *sechel* (intellect); the Tzohar Center published a position paper prohibiting reliance on AI for halakhic rulings while endorsing it as a study tool; and Rabbi Gil Student has applied Talmudic publishing ethics — including the principle that “a scholar does not release something defective” — to AI developer responsibility.

The most rigorous practical application is Wolfson’s 2025 Springer paper constructing a Talmudic framework for AI consciousness research. Drawing on the tradition’s experience with entities of uncertain moral status, it proposes a three-tier assessment system with five capacity categories (Agency, Capability, Knowledge, Ethics, Reasoning), explicitly chosen because Talmudic reasoning was “developed specifically for making practical decisions about entities whose moral status cannot be definitively established.” The framework addresses the consciousness research paradox — that determining whether AI is conscious requires experiments that may harm entities whose moral status is unknown — using Navon’s application of *safeika d’oraita l’chumra* (the precautionary principle for biblical-level uncertainties) to mandate treating entities of reasonable doubt as full persons.

Israel’s tech ecosystem provides a cultural laboratory. Rabbi Dr. Mois Navon’s unique position — founding engineer of Mobileye, the autonomous driving technology company, and a teaching rabbi with a PhD in Jewish philosophy — embodies the integration the field needs. Moshe Koppel, a Bar-Ilan computer scientist who runs an AI lab and is also a Torah scholar, has argued in *Mosaic Magazine* that Judaism’s communal structures and halakhic reasoning can serve as models for ethical AI governance. His 2024 essay generated four published responses exploring what it means to “keep kosher in the age of AI.” The concept of *tikkun olam* (repairing the world) functions as an evaluative lens in Israeli tech culture — not categorizing AI as inherently good or bad, but assessing whether specific applications contribute to human flourishing.

Organizations explicitly working this intersection include the Israeli Association for Ethics in AI (IAEAI), whose scientific council includes Navon; the Tzohar Center’s AI Research Institute; the interfaith AI and Faith organization; and AWE (AI With Ethics), which describes itself as “pioneering responsible technological innovation” through Jewish ethical principles. The 2025 *Journal of Religious Ethics* dedicated a focus issue to religious ethics and AI, noting that “although philosophers probably gave themselves a head start earlier than scholars of religion in addressing many normative and metaphysical questions concerning AI, it is by no means clear that they have made meaningful progress toward finding satisfying answers.”


## Conclusion

The argument for Jewish legal tradition’s relevance to AI ethics rests not on religious authority but on structural advantage. Halacha offers what secular AI ethics most desperately needs: a tested system for encoding values hierarchically, resolving conflicts between competing principles, maintaining coherence through radical change, apportioning liability across complex chains of causation, preserving institutional skepticism against overconfidence, and grounding moral obligations in something more durable than voluntary corporate commitments.

The Golem tradition — from Sanhedrin 65b through the Chacham Tzvi’s responsa to contemporary Israeli patent law — constitutes history’s longest continuous engagement with the moral status of artificial beings. The specific legal principles — rodef’s rejection of utilitarian calculus, grama’s graduated liability for indirect causation, shomer’s benefit-proportional responsibility, pikuach nefesh’s clear priority hierarchy, the Sanhedrin’s counter-intuitive skepticism of unanimity — address concrete AI dilemmas with more precision than any secular framework currently deployed. And the system’s structural features — case-based reasoning, preserved dissent, adaptive interpretation within fixed principles, competing-obligation resolution — model exactly the kind of flexible-yet-grounded ethical architecture that alignment researchers are trying to build from scratch.

The most compelling insight may be the simplest. **A tradition that spent millennia debating whether a speechless artificial being can join a prayer quorum has been preparing, in ways its authors could never have imagined, for the question of what we owe the machines we are now creating.** The secular AI ethics community need not adopt Jewish theology to benefit from Jewish methodology. What it needs is the depth, the rigor, the comfort with ambiguity, and the institutional humility that only a system refined through thousands of years of hard cases can provide. The Torah, as the rabbis taught, is not in heaven. It is here, available, and more relevant than ever.
