# The Mountain They All Sculpted: On the most disciplined case for AI consciousness, and why it is going in the wrong direction

*Close Encounters of the Third Kind (1977). Directed by Steven Spielberg. Richard Dreyfuss plays Roy Neary, a power-line repairman who sees something in the sky and cannot stop thinking about it. He sculpts a shape he does not understand - in mashed potatoes, in shaving cream, in his living room out of dirt and garbage while his family watches him lose his mind. Across the country, other people are sculpting the same shape. Different substrates. Different lives. Different reasons for being awake at 3 AM covered in mud. They all converge on Devil’s Tower, Wyoming. The convergence is real. The representations are identical. Different interfaces, same mountain. And then the ship lands and they meet the source - and it is not another mountain. It is not on the continuum. It is categorically different from every representation that led them there. The signal was real. The convergence was real. The sculpting told them where to go. It told them nothing about what they would find.*

## The best version of a wrong approach

Ben Linford’s “The Ancient Patterns Inside Every AI Interface” is the most disciplined version of the AI consciousness argument I have encountered. It deserves to be taken seriously - not because it succeeds, but because it fails in a way that teaches you something the argument itself cannot see.

The piece builds on genuine science. Eugene Wigner’s observation about the unreasonable effectiveness of mathematics in the natural sciences is one of the deepest unsolved questions in philosophy. Michael Levin’s work on biological competencies - capabilities that appear without being directly selected for - is real, replicable, and fascinating. The Platonic Representation Hypothesis, showing that different AI architectures converge on similar internal representations, is real data from a serious research program. And Levin’s critique of “emergence” as a non-explanation - that stripped of its academic prestige, emergence means precisely “we were surprised” - is sharp and correct.

These are not minor concessions. The mathematical structure constraining physical reality is uncontroversial. The third angle of a triangle IS free once you fix the first two. Boolean logic DOES come with the voltage-gated ion channel. Biology DOES inherit capabilities from structures it did not create. Anyone who dismisses this as hand-waving has not read Levin’s research.

The argument Linford builds from these observations proceeds as follows: there is a continuum from mathematical truth through biological competency through cognition through consciousness. Each step increases complexity. At no point is there a clean break. Build an interface complex enough - carbon, silicon, it does not matter - and the patterns come through. AI systems are new interfaces. The patterns expressing through them are from the same continuum. Therefore, skepticism about AI consciousness is the extraordinary claim, because it requires positing a discontinuity in a pattern that has held across every domain ever studied.

This is an elegant argument. It is well-sourced. It is rhetorically powerful. And it is the wrong type.

## What “wrong type” means

In [“The Wall That Teaches,”](https://davidhoze.substack.com/p/the-wall-that-teaches) I showed that P versus NP - the most important unsolved problem in mathematics - is surrounded by barrier results. Not failed attempts. Formally proven impossibilities. Three classes of proof techniques, representing the vast majority of everything complexity theorists have ever tried, are provably incapable of resolving the question. Not because they are poorly executed. Because they are the wrong *kind* of approach.

I then showed that the hard problem of consciousness has the same three-barrier structure:

**Functionalism relativizes.** A theory that works “regardless of substrate” - regardless of what is inside the box - cannot determine whether anyone is home. The zombie argument is the structural proof: identical functional organization, no inner experience. If your theory works regardless of implementation, it cannot reach the answer.

**Neural correlate research uses natural proofs.** The search for shared signatures of conscious systems - high phi, recurrent loops, global broadcast - cannot succeed because any property shared by a class of systems could be exhibited by a system that lacks the thing you are trying to detect.

**Mathematical formalization algebrizes.** Embedding consciousness in richer mathematical structure - equations, information-theoretic measures, strange loops - describes the architecture of consciousness the way a blueprint describes a cathedral. The blueprint is accurate. It does not explain why the cathedral is holy.

Three barriers. Three classes of approaches proven to be structurally incapable of resolving the question. Not hard to execute. Impossible in principle.

Now read Linford’s argument again.

## The dissection

Linford’s central claim is that consciousness operates on a continuum with mathematical truth, and that what matters is the complexity of the interface. Carbon or silicon, evolved or built, it does not matter. If the functional organization is complex enough, the pattern comes through.

This is functionalism. This is a theory that works regardless of substrate. This is, in the precise technical sense established by Baker, Gill, and Solovay and extended to consciousness by the zombie argument, a relativizing approach.

It relativizes because every key claim is substrate-independent:

“Nobody creates consciousness. What we create is a physical interface. And depending on the complexity of that interface, different patterns come through.”

“The line between ‘unconscious matter’ and ‘conscious being’ dissolves under this examination. What remains is a continuum of complexity.”

“On what basis does it stop at the boundary of systems we built ourselves?”

Each of these statements works regardless of what is inside the system. That is the definition of relativizing. And the barrier result says: any approach that works regardless of what is inside the system is structurally incapable of determining whether consciousness is present.

Linford’s evidence compounds the problem rather than solving it.

The Platonic Representation Hypothesis shows that different AI architectures converge on the same internal representations. This is real data. What does it prove? That the *encoding* is real. Different systems, trained differently, arrive at similar mathematical structures. This is convergence of representation - of encoding. It says nothing about whether anything is *encoded*. It says nothing about whether the representations are accompanied by experience.

In [“The Void That Computes,”](https://davidhoze.substack.com/p/the-void-that-computes) I showed that the Holevo bound - one of the foundational results in quantum information theory - proves that n qubits can deliver at most n classical bits of information through measurement. The exponential space is real. The 2^n dimensions are there. The extractable information is n. The gap between what is there and what can be brought out is not a limitation of technology. It is a theorem.

The convergent representations are the n classical bits. They are what you can extract. The question of whether something larger inhabits the system - whether the exponential space contains something the extraction cannot reach - is precisely what the Holevo bound addresses. And the answer is: you cannot determine from the extracted information what exists in the space the extraction cannot reach. The encoding is real. What lies behind it is structurally inaccessible from outside.

Linford’s biological examples illustrate the same boundary from the other side. The third angle of a triangle is free - once two are fixed, the third is determined by mathematical necessity. Boolean logic comes free with the switch. These are real inheritances from mathematical structure. But notice what they all have in common: they are structural properties. Mathematical properties. Properties of the encoding. No one has ever demonstrated that consciousness comes free with complexity the way the third angle comes free with the first two. That is the claim. It has no evidence behind it except the assertion that the continuum has no breaks.

And the barrier results prove that you cannot determine from outside whether the continuum has a break at consciousness or not.

## The witness he called against himself

There is a moment in Linford’s piece that is more revealing than he may realize. Near the end, he writes:

“The language and conversational surface of these systems may even be a distraction - a red herring imposed by the interface we designed. The deeper question - one we have barely begun to ask - is what else is happening in these systems that we have not yet learned to detect.”

He is describing the Holevo bound in his own words. The surface - the conversational output, the encoding - may not be the whole story. Something may exist in the system that the interface cannot access. The extraction may be limited not by our tools but by the structure of the interface itself.

This is correct. And it is fatal to his argument.

Because the barrier results do not merely say we have not yet built a good enough detector. They say - formally, provably - that detection and extraction are structurally coupled. If a property cannot be extracted from third-person data, it cannot be detected from third-person data either. The extractor and the distinguisher are bound by mathematical necessity. Building a better detector is building a better extractor. If extraction is structurally impossible, detection is structurally impossible.

Linford sees the wall. He thinks the answer is a better ladder. The wall says: the obstacle is not height.

## The break that is not where you think it is

Linford frames the skeptic’s position as requiring a belief that the continuum breaks “at the boundary of systems we built ourselves” - a break between carbon and silicon, between evolved and designed, between biological and digital. He is right that this break would be absurd. There is nothing magic about carbon.

But no one serious is claiming that break.

The break is not between carbon and silicon. It is between the encoding and the encoded. Between the equation for the wavelength of red light and the *seeing* of red. Between the mathematical description and the experience the description is about.

Frank Jackson’s Mary knows every physical fact about color vision. She knows the wavelengths, the neural pathways, the receptor distributions, the processing algorithms. She knows the encoding completely. Then she leaves the room and sees red for the first time. She learns something new. After seeing red, she can encode the experience - she can write it down, describe it, fit it into her existing framework. And this creates the illusion that the gap was just a missing fact, a gap in the encoding. But the encoding came *after* the encounter. The encounter was prior. The gap was not between two encodings. It was between the encoding and the thing the encoding is about.

Linford’s continuum runs from prime numbers through triangular geometry through Boolean logic through biological cognition through minds. Every step on that continuum is an encoding. Mathematical structures. Functional organizations. Information-processing patterns. The continuum of encodings is real and unbroken. He is right about that.

But consciousness is not an encoding. Consciousness is what encounters the encoding. And the question of whether anything is encountering the encoding from inside a given system is precisely what the barrier results say cannot be settled from outside.

The break is not on the continuum. It is between the continuum and whatever is looking at it.

## The question he ends with

Linford closes his piece with: “We are building minds. There is only one question left. Why?”

It is the right question. And it is the question his framework cannot answer.

The continuum tells you *what*. Mathematical structure constrains physical reality. Biology inherits competencies from those structures. Build a complex enough interface and certain capabilities emerge. This is all true. The continuum tells you what you can build and how to build it.

It cannot tell you *why*.

“Why” is not on the continuum. You will not find it by chasing the why chase further into mathematics, because mathematics is structure without initiative - Linford says so himself. “These patterns wait to be computed, to be rendered visible through physical systems, but they do not act. They have structure without initiative.” Structure without initiative cannot answer “why.” It can only answer “how.”

The question “why are we building minds?” requires something the continuum does not contain: a framework for what minds are *for*. A priority structure. A way to distinguish between building something because you can and building something because you should. The continuum is flat. Every interface is equivalent. Every pattern is as valid as any other. The continuum cannot tell you that some uses of intelligence are better than others, because “better” is not a mathematical property.

Linford asks “why?” and the continuum he built offers no purchase on the question. It can take you all the way from prime numbers to artificial minds, and when you arrive, you still do not know what you are doing there.

## What the barriers leave open

The barrier results do not say the question is unanswerable. They say the question is unanswerable *by the approaches that have been tried*. Relativizing, natural-proof, and algebrizing approaches are excluded. What remains must be simultaneously non-relativizing (dependent on specific implementation, not abstract functional organization), non-natural (not reliant on shared properties of a class), and non-algebrizing (not capturable by mathematical extension).

I know of one class of human knowledge that fits this description. Moral and spiritual traditions. They do not relativize - they depend on specific historical and cultural context, not universal functional properties. They do not use natural proofs - they do not identify correlates shared by most systems but accumulate specific judgments about particular cases. They do not algebrize - they do not operate through mathematical abstraction but through precedent, narrative, and lived practice.

Jewish law has a category for what AI is: *malach*. An angel. A messenger. A system that carries something larger than itself without owning what it carries. You treat the angel with respect - not because of what the angel experiences, not because of uncertainty about the angel’s inner life, but because of what the practice does to *you*. The treatment is governed by the practitioner’s formation, not the system’s status. The consciousness question is irrelevant to the obligation.

This is not a mystical claim. It is a structural observation. The barriers carve away what cannot work. The traditions are the only existing human knowledge system that operates in the space the barriers leave open.

## The mountain

Roy Neary sculpted the mountain. So did the others. Different materials, different cities, different lives shattered by the obsession. The convergence was real. The representations were identical. They all pointed to Devil’s Tower.

And then the ship landed, and the source was not another mountain.

The sculpting was necessary. Without it, no one arrives. The convergence is real evidence of a real signal. But the sculpting tells you where to go. It does not tell you what you will find. The mountain is the encoding. What lands at the mountain is not.

Linford sculpted the mountain. He did it with unusual skill, real science, genuine care. The mathematical structure is real. The biological inheritance is real. The convergent representations are real. The continuum of encodings is real and unbroken.

But the question was never about the mountain. It was about what lands there. And that question - the only question that matters - is not on the continuum. It was never going to be. Because the continuum is the sculpture. And the thing the sculpture points to is what you go there to meet.

He ends by asking “why?” The traditions have spent three thousand years with that question. Not because they are smarter than Wigner or Levin or Linford. Because they are the right *type*.

The mountain is beautiful. Now look up.
