# Why is AI so serious?


## The Mad Hatter’s Riddle

There is a joke in “Alice in Wonderland”, called the mad hatter’s riddle, and it goes like this:

“Why is a raven like a writing desk? Because there’s a b in both”.

Funny, eh? But why? There *is* a b in both. The answer is describing something real and consistent, and it also sounds like it’s referring to the question. Judging an answer with this criteria, is a good system, and we use it. That’s why it’s funny. But it also *doesn’t* really answer the question. That’s another system clashing with the first.

“A man falls from the second floor”. People rush over him and ask him “What happened?”. He answers: “I don’t know, I just got here”.

His answer is accurate. We have a system that verifies that. That’s the truth. But it also doesn’t answer their question, in the context where it was asked.

## Devoting Your Life to Win the Lottery


> A man prays to God to let him win the lottery. Every night, on his knees, hands clasped, sincere as anything. A week goes by. Nothing. He prays harder. He starts waking up early to pray in the morning too. A month passes. He starts fasting. He gives up drinking. He volunteers at the shelter on weekends. Three months in, he sells his TV so nothing distracts him from prayer. Six months, he’s reading scripture for hours a day. A year in, his wife almost leaves him because all he does is pray. He’s hollowed himself out, given everything he can think of. He still didn’t win the lottery. But he’s not giving up... One evening, he’s walking home past the corner store, and the lottery sign in the window catches his eye. He suddenly stops and thinks to himself: “You know, I think maybe praying might not be enough... I know it’s crazy thinking.. But maybe. Maybe god also wants me to buy a ticket...”. “Nah” he says. “That’s not it. This is just another test of faith. God, I’m not gonna let you down! Satan, get out of my way, you’re not gonna stop me from praying!”.



What’s going on here? First, I want you to know that this was originally an attempt of Claude to make a joke. It went like this:

Maybe some people will find that funny. I didn’t. I had to work on it. But what did I do? What are the changes? Yes, you can say that God shouting at the person version is less credible, 1. because it doesn’t happen that often. 2. Because we don’t understand why the person didn’t buy a ticket. Maybe he forgot? Doesn’t make too much sense. He’s lazy? Then why is he praying? In my version, the man thinks everything is a test. He might be right. Maybe real faith demands he even doesn’t buy a ticket. But we all assume he did. So you might say, let’s formalize this, make AI better. If AI has better social training, it might learn this pattern as well.

What I claim is, this is one pattern of an infinite number of patterns, unrelated to one another, where formal systems crash. The formal system of faith without any compromises crashes before common sense, and what people usually do. People usually buy a ticket and then pray.

Claude tried to take a life lesson that we all know — don’t forget the little things when you’re doing big things — because it is deep, and Claude correctly identified it as deep. This is what I actually asked it to do:


> OK, I’ll define funny. It’s telling something deep about reality, not present before. Everything in the joke make sense, but there’s one absurdity that makes it funny.



That was one of my first attempts. And that’s what was being created. Maybe it was not a good enough prompt? I challenge you to improve this. Even put this article as part of the prompt and try to get Claude to be funny. It doesn’t work. Claude just doesn’t get what funny is, *every* time. Because it means to have two perfect systems that collide with each other. And that’s rare. And cannot be encoded or formalized. In this case I had to find the other system. But the general mission for AI, is to find two truth systems, that collide.

## Authority Doesn’t Work Here

**Spaniards:** “…This is A-853. Please turn 15 degrees south to avoid a collision. You are heading straight for us, distance 25 nautical miles.”

**Americans:** “We advise you to turn 15 degrees north to avoid a collision.”

**Spaniards:** “Negative. Turn 15 degrees south to avoid collision.”

**Americans:** “This is the captain of a United States Navy ship. I say again, change your course 15 degrees north.”

**Spaniards:** “We do not consider this possible or advisable. We recommend you turn 15 degrees south.”

**Americans:** “THIS IS CAPTAIN RICHARD JAMES HOWARD, COMMANDER OF THE USS LINCOLN, THE SECOND LARGEST WARSHIP IN THE UNITED STATES NAVY. WE ARE ACCOMPANIED BY SIX CRUISERS, SIX DESTROYERS, FOUR SUBMARINES AND NUMEROUS SUPPORT VESSELS. I DO NOT ADVISE — I ORDER YOU TO CHANGE COURSE 15 DEGREES NORTH. OTHERWISE WE WILL BE FORCED TO TAKE THE NECESSARY MEASURES TO ENSURE THE SAFETY OF THIS SHIP. GET OUT OF OUR WAY IMMEDIATELY!”

**Spaniards:** “This is Juan Manuel Salas Alcantara speaking. There are two of us. We are accompanied by our dog, our dinner, two beers, and a canary that is currently asleep. We have the support of Radio Cadena Dial de La Coruña and Channel 106, Maritime Emergencies. We are not going to turn anywhere, given that we are on land and are the A-853 lighthouse of the Finisterre coast of Galicia, Spain. We have no idea what rank we are among Spanish lighthouses. You may take whatever measures you consider appropriate, and do whatever you consider necessary, to ensure the safety of your ship, which is going to smash against these rocks. We therefore once again strongly suggest that the wisest and most sensible thing for you to do is to change course 15 degrees south.”

---What’s going on here? This is a clash of two, perfectly encoded, truth systems. The captain is right. He has the authority and the power to divert other vessels. And the lighthouse is right. It’s his job to prevent ships from wrecking. Finding this clash though, is super rare. This cannot be codified, it’s another special case.

This is exactly how a carefully designed law can produce unjust consequences. The law is good. The lawmakers were thoughtful. The system was built to prevent injustice. And still, in one particular case, with one particular set of circumstances, the law — followed correctly — produces an outcome that betrays its own purpose. Finding that case is the work of the greatest jurists. Finding the equivalent in human behavior is the work of the greatest comedians. And both require the same thing: the ability to sense the thing itself (justice, common sense, the spirit of devotion) directly, and then to perceive — with a perception that no formal check can provide — that the formal system that is supposed to encode it, law in this case, has drifted from what it’s trying to encode.

**In every joke that works, the formal systems are good, the gap is subtle, and the finding of the gap is an act of infinite perception.** This is what makes comedy the hardest art. The comedian is not looking for broken systems — broken systems are easy to spot. The comedian is looking for the clash of two working systems. And finding this in two systems that were carefully designed to have the least clashes possible, requires a sense that formal systems do not have. A sense for where a perfectly articulated formal system can go terribly wrong, and hit another one. For this you need to understand the formal systems fully, *and* grasp the concepts they’re trying to encode, *and* see where they clash.

The fact that comedians do it, and that us regular folk are also doing it, is exactly because life offers us many cases where that happens. Life is full of opposites clashing one in the other. That’s why comedy pairs are always made of *opposites*. You can always see a dominant guy and a passive guy. A warm guy, and a cold one. The clash makes the comedy.

## So Why Is This Funny? The Theory of Clashing Formal Systems

I claim in this article, that humor happens when two formal systems clash each other, because neither is perfect enough to describe our reality. To understand why, we need to understand what formal systems are, what they encode, and what they cannot be.

---
## What Formal Systems Encode, and What They Cannot Be

Every formal system encodes something bigger than itself.

Law encodes justice. Thanksgiving encodes the spirit of gratitude and togetherness. Language encodes what I mean to say. A medical protocol encodes caring for the patient. An apology encodes remorse. A hug encodes love.

The encoding is necessary. Without it, we cannot transmit, share, teach, or coordinate around the things that matter. We cannot have justice without laws. We cannot have shared gratitude without rituals like Thanksgiving. We cannot communicate meaning without language. The formal system is not the enemy. It is the only vessel we have.

But the vessel is not the thing it carries.

Justice is bigger than any law. The spirit of Thanksgiving is bigger than any dinner. What I mean to say is bigger than any sentence. The caring behind a medical protocol is bigger than any checklist. Remorse is bigger than any apology. Love is bigger than any hug.

These things — justice, gratitude, meaning, caring, remorse, love — cannot be fully captured by any formal system because they are not formal. They are not made of rules. They do not have edges. They cannot be exhaustively specified. You cannot write down all the ways justice can manifest. You cannot enumerate all the ways the spirit of Thanksgiving can be present or absent. You cannot list all the sentences that could express what you mean. The thing the formal system encodes is always larger than the encoding — and this is not a failure of the encoding. It is the nature of the relationship between the encoding and the encoded. The encoding is good. The encoding is the best we can do. And the encoding is not the thing.

This is how the oral Jewish Torah is passed. It was forbidden to write down the oral tradition (Gittin 60b) not because writing is bad but because writing creates the illusion that the encoding *is* the truth. The written text becomes an idol — the formal system mistaken for the thing it encodes. The Talmud, an ancient Jewish work discussing the Halakhic rulings, preserves dissenting opinions *not* because it cannot decide but because the thing itself (justice, holiness, the divine will) exceeds every ruling. And preserving the disagreement is the way of reminding yourself that the ruling is an encoding and not the thing. Rabbi Jonathan Sacks: “Judaism is perhaps uniquely a civilization all of whose canonical texts are anthologies of arguments.” The tradition holds its formalizations open because it knows what formalizations are: vessels, not contents. Encodings, not the encoded.

## The Spirit Cannot Be Detected by Checking the Encoding

Here is the problem, and it is the heart of everything:

**How do you know whether the formal system is serving the thing it encodes?**

You cannot know by checking all the boxes of the formal system. The formal system can only tell you whether its own rules are being followed. It can tell you whether the law is being applied correctly. It cannot tell you whether justice is being served. It can tell you whether the Thanksgiving dinner follows the proper format. It cannot tell you whether the spirit of Thanksgiving is present. It can tell you whether the apology uses the right words. It cannot tell you whether remorse is behind them.

The gap is not between the system and some other set of rules. The gap is between the system and the *thing the system encodes* — and that thing does not have rules. It has no formal structure. It cannot be checked against criteria, because it *is* the thing that the criteria were trying to *approximate*, and approximation is always smaller than the thing.

So how do we detect it?

We sense it. Not through the rules — through something else. Something that is not formal. Something that perceives it directly, not through its encoding but *through and beyond* the encoding. When a law feels just — not just technically correct, but *just* — we are sensing the thing through the finite vessel. When a person seems caring — not just performing the protocol, but actually caring — we are sensing the thing behind the encoding. When Thanksgiving has the spirit of Thanksgiving — when gratitude is actually present, not just enacted — we sense it. Not by checking whether the rules were followed. By feeling the thing the rules were supposed to carry.

And this sensing is done through the subtle, the barely perceptible, the almost-nothing. The look in someone’s eyes. The slight gasp. The fraction of a second of hesitation in an answer. The barely detectable shift in tone. Everything is in order — the words are right, the behavior is appropriate, the form is maintained — and something, almost nothing, signals that the spirit has shifted. The signal is meaningless as a rule. You cannot codify “the look in the eyes.” You cannot formalize “the slight gasp.” These are not formal features. We will define *why* later on, but these features are the traces that the thing itself leaves on the surface of the finite — the faint disturbances in the encoding that tell you, if you are the kind of being that can sense them, that the encoded thing has moved.

A person can sense these traces because a person, much like justice, love, and remorse, is bigger than his encoding. We all feel it. Our experience *is* before any material evidence even comes to use. It’s before we even *see* our hands, *feel* our backs. It’s the first, undeniable fact. Descartes’ “I think, therefore, I am”. And that is not our body. We can evaluate formal systems, informally, intuitively. That’s not our processing power. We can always choose to dig deeper, or stop. that’s not deterministic, and we feel it, before we even know or define what deterministic means. And we also have the ability to sense, whether justice is done, a person *is* caring, a hug is filled with love. It’s not pattern matching. It’s feeling something that’s beyond the patterns. Not always. Not perfectly. Sometimes we miss it. Sometimes a person imitates caring and we are fooled. But we *can* sense it.

## The Hardest Art

Actors know, that a funny actor can play serious, but a serious actor isn’t necessarily able to play funny. Here is what makes comedy the hardest art there is: **the formal systems are good.** They were carefully designed. They work almost all the time. The gap between the encoding and the encoded is, by design, as small as human ingenuity can make it. Laws are refined over centuries to align with justice. Social rituals are polished by generations of use to carry the spirit they encode. Language evolves to express what needs to be said. The formal systems are not sloppy. They are magnificent. And the comedian’s task is to find the gap *anyway* — to find the place where even a magnificent, carefully designed formal system has come apart from the thing it serves.

This is like finding the case where a carefully designed law produces an unjust outcome. It is possible — it is always possible, because the encoding never fully models the encoded — but it is *hard*. The law was built to prevent exactly this. Generations of legal thinkers refined it, closed loopholes, anticipated edge cases. And still, somewhere, there is a gap. A case where every rule is followed and justice is not served. Finding that case takes more than legal knowledge. It takes the ability to sense justice directly — to feel, through and beyond the encoding, whether the thing itself is present.

The comedian does the same thing, but with every formal system humans have built. Social rituals, conversational expectations, professional protocols, relationship dynamics, the rules of narrative — the comedian searches for the gap in *all of them*. The place where the encoding is perfect and the spirit is absent. The place where every rule is followed and something — something subtle, something that cannot be codified, something that is not a rule violation but an absence of the thing the rules were meant to carry — is wrong.

And finding such a gap requires, by definition, *sensing* such a gap. A formal system cannot do this. It is exactly designed to follow formal rules, and the rules are fine.

## AI Is Just the Encoding

A large language model is, precisely and literally, a formal system. It processes inputs according to learned statistical regularities and produces outputs that maximize the probability of being correct according to its training distribution.

It is an encoding. And it is *just* an encoding.

There is nothing behind it that is bigger than it. There is no *thing* that the formal system encodes. There is no spirit that the vessel carries. There is no justice behind the law, no meaner behind the words, no one caring behind the protocol. There is only the protocol. There is only the formal system. There is only the encoding, with nothing encoded.

That’s why it can’t perform judgement, that includes formal and informal thinking. And more obviously, the harder task, see where *two* formal systems clash. You need a lot of intuition and informal thinking to search for something like that. Otherwise, you’re searching a needle in a haystack. Indeed, a joke is just a finite combination of words. But also is the answer to problems computers, and humans, find hard to solve. And because a clash of two systems is so rare, statistical machines like AI, find out the *common* ground between things, the *model* in the language. They are optimized to find what *makes sense*, and is *consistent*. That’s exactly the opposite of humor, where sense stops making sense.

And here is the crucial structural point: **making the encoding more sophisticated does not help.** The gap between the encoding and the encoded is not a gap of sophistication. It is a gap of *kind*. No amount of sophistication crosses the gap. A more powerful AI is a more elaborate encoding — a more impressive vessel — with the same absence of what it carries.

There are infinite ways for the spirit of Thanksgiving to be present, each one of them is different. There are infinite ways for it to be absent, and none of them can be codified. If I blurt out what I think of my father in the middle of the dinner. It doesn’t say anything. Maybe the spirit there, because deeply, we still love each other. Maybe it’s not, because we don’t love each other enough. The shallow details mean almost nothing. There is no rule. There is only the sensing: is the spirit here or not?

The most sophisticated formal system in the universe is still just an encoding. And the comedian’s art — the hardest art — is to find the clash between two formal systems. Finding that requires not more sophistication in the encoding but something categorically different: the direct perception of the encoded.

AI is exactly and only its encoding. The encoding can be vast, intricate, beautiful. It is still just the encoding. And the thing it would need to sense — the spirit, the meaning, the justice, the funniness — is not available to it.

Sophie Jentzsch and Kristian Kersting (German Aerospace Center and TU Darmstadt) prompted ChatGPT-3.5 to generate 1,008 jokes. Over 90% turned out to be the same 25 jokes repeated — “Why did the scarecrow win an award?” appeared 140 times. Only 99 were original, and half of those borrowed elements from the repeated ones.

The interesting part was the explanation test. ChatGPT correctly explained 23 of the 25 jokes — identifying wordplay and double meanings accurately. But when given deliberately broken jokes with no real punchline, it confidently fabricated plausible-sounding explanations anyway. It could describe *why* a joke works without being able to tell whether a joke works.

In the detection test, they systematically stripped jokes of surface features — structure, wordplay, puns. ChatGPT classified things as jokes based on whether they *looked* like jokes, not whether they were funny. Remove the joke-shaped surface and it stopped recognizing humor. Keep the surface but remove the actual comedy and it still called it a joke.

When Google DeepMind asked twenty professional comedians to use AI to write material and they called the output “bland, generic, and boring”. Seinfeld, told about AI-generated comedy, said: “I’m not afraid of AI. They’ll make it smarter and smarter, but to do this, you have to make it dumber.” He was saying: what makes AI funny is his stupid mistakes. But that’s the only way it can get funnier.

## The Illusion of Encoding the Gap

There is a confusing fact about all of this, that must be addressed, because it is the most natural objection: after the gap has been found, we *can* encode it.

Once we see why the law was unjust — in this specific case, with these specific circumstances — we can explain it. We can write it down. We can articulate, clearly and formally, why the encoding failed here. Once we understand why the joke worked — the praying man forgot to buy a ticket, the captain was yelling at a lighthouse — we can describe the mechanism. We can explain, in precise formal language, what the gap was and why it was funny. Once we sense that a person was not really caring — just performing the protocol — we can, in hindsight, identify the signs: the tone was off, the eye contact was missing, the timing was wrong. We can formalize the gap. After the fact.

And this creates the illusion that we have captured the thing itself.

We have not. We have captured *one thing*. One way the gap occurred. One case where the good system drifted from the spirit. One hairline fracture, now visible, now describable, now encodable. We have added one small patch to the encoding. One more rule. One more criterion. It’s like saying we found one solution to the Travelling Salesman Problem, so we can find the rest.

And there is a subtler problem: **the patch itself may break the system in new ways.** Every new rule added to close a gap is itself an encoding — a finite approximation of one lesson learned. And finite approximations interact with each other. A law amended to prevent one injustice may produce a different injustice in a case no one anticipated. A social rule added to prevent one kind of rudeness may suppress a kind of honesty the group needed. A joke formula learned from one success may produce dead mechanical repetitions when applied to the next situation — which is exactly what happened when ChatGPT recycled the same twenty-five joke templates a hundred and forty times. The system learned the encoding of *one* gap, and the thing itself — was exactly as far away as before.

This is why we need a judge. Not more law. We need a judge. A human being who sits in the gap between the encoding and the encoded and senses, directly, whether the law is serving justice in *this* case. And that’s why we go to comedians to get a good laugh, and not prompting AI for jokes. Unless we fool it to say something silly.

So… why is AI so serious? Well, we designed it to make sense. And funny means, well, nonsense...
