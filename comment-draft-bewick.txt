Tom, you've identified the three gaps that make Korinek and Stiglitz's framework beautiful on paper and inert in practice - the benevolent social planner assumption, the Turing Trap bias toward replacement, and the undertheorized transition costs. Those are real gaps. The institutional power dynamics point is especially right: who gets a seat at the design table determines everything.

But I want to name something your framework shares with the paper it critiques. Both assume the goal is to optimize outcomes for workers within the existing structure. Augmentation over replacement. Better transitions. Collective bargaining for the terms. All of this is necessary. None of it answers the question underneath.

What is the person for?

Even if you steer AI perfectly toward augmentation, even if you get the policy right and the transitions smooth and the bargaining power balanced - you still have a system that defines human value through productivity. The factory gets better. Nobody asks whether the factory should have an exit.

I wrote something that picks up where the policy register stops - what happens when the economic question is answered and the existential one is still sitting there. The tradition already modeled this: manna economics, Shmitta, Jubilee. Abundance arrived. The test was never distribution. The test was purpose.

https://davidhoze.substack.com/p/the-factory-with-no-exit

Your question about who gets a seat at the design table is the right question. I'm asking what the table is for.
