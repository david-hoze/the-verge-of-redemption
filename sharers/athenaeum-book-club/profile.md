## Focus

Western canon / Great Books through an explicitly Christian lens. Anti-modernism, cultural decline, loss of inherited moral foundations. Iliad, Dante, Dostoevsky, Tolkien, Lewis, Augustine, Chesterton. "Once your culture is gone, it isn't coming back."

---

## Tone

Earnest, declarative, sermonic. Zero irony, zero humor. Punchy sentences, second person. Populist-intellectual: the brave outsider against a vague "they" who don't want you to read. Formulaic structure: provocative title, "we live in a time of..." opener, walk through classic text, rebuke modernity, call to action. Competent but predictable.

---

## Assessment

The diagnosis overlaps with David's framework significantly. Lewis's "men without chests" is sechel without da'at. "Rejecting inherited moral law makes you raw material" is freedom-for-what. "Modern man prides himself on compassion while surrendering every standard that gave compassion meaning" is naval birshut haTorah.

But the prescription is "read the Great Books" - pointing at the Western canon as the source. David's framework says: the canon is a commentary on the source, not the source itself. Philosophy is a commentary that forgot it was a commentary. Lewis himself is more specific - his "Tao" is closer to a claim about objective moral law. This account popularizes Lewis without pushing past him.

31K subscribers in an adjacent audience. Sharing opportunity, not a conversation partner. The depth isn't there for genuine intellectual exchange, but the audience cares about the same diagnosis.

---

## Strategy

Drop comment linking to "Philosophy Is a Commentary That Forgot It Was a Commentary" - the most direct convergence point. Keep it brief. The value is audience exposure, not relationship building. If engagement develops, reassess.
