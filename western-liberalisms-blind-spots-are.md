# Western liberalism’s blind spots are becoming AI’s design flaws

Real ethics boards have collapsed in days, real algorithms have issued 794,000 unlawful debt notices, real AI targeting systems have generated 37,000 kill-list entries approved in 20 seconds per name, and real hiring tools have rejected every Black applicant in controlled tests. The common thread: Western liberal democracies built AI governance on philosophical foundations that contain no shared theory of human flourishing, no workable consent mechanism at computational scale, and no way to adjudicate between their own competing ethical traditions when lives hang in the balance.

## “Align AI with human values” — but whose?

The most foundational promise in AI development — alignment with human values — collapses on contact with reality because no such unified value set exists. This is not a philosophical hypothetical. It has destroyed organizations, dissolved ethics boards, and driven mass resignations.

**OpenAI’s November 2023 board crisis** is the clearest case. The board fired Sam Altman because members linked to effective altruism believed they were “philosophical guardians” with a duty to slow AGI development for humanity’s safety. Within 72 hours, **95% of OpenAI’s 770+ employees** threatened to resign and follow Altman to Microsoft. Neither side was wrong by liberal democratic standards — they held irreconcilable visions of what “benefiting humanity” means. The safety-oriented board was replaced by Bret Taylor (former Salesforce CEO) and Larry Summers (former Treasury Secretary). Harvard Law Review described this as “amoral drift in AI corporate governance.” By May 2024, Jan Leike, co-leader of OpenAI’s Superalignment team, resigned publicly, writing: “Over the past years, safety culture and processes have taken a backseat to shiny products.” His team was disbanded. The market, not ethics, adjudicated the values dispute.

Google’s Advanced Technology External Advisory Council (ATEAC) lasted **exactly nine days** in April 2019 before dissolution. Including Heritage Foundation president Kay Coles James triggered ~2,500 employee signatures demanding her removal over anti-LGBTQ positions. Board member Alessandro Acquisti resigned. Google’s statement: “It’s become clear that in the current environment, ATEAC can’t function as we wanted.” The episode revealed a structural impossibility: liberal pluralism cannot assemble a “representative” ethics board because including one value system makes the board unacceptable to holders of another. Excluding perspectives makes it unrepresentative. There is no neutral ground.

The technical process of alignment itself — RLHF (reinforcement learning from human feedback) — empirically confirms the value vacuum. A 2025 analysis of Anthropic’s HH-RLHF dataset and PKU’s Safe-RLHF dataset found that “principles are often in conflict or indifferent, frequently requiring discretionary judgment from annotators” and that “even when principles reach consensus, human annotators often disagree with this consensus.” Annotator-expert agreement sits at roughly **70%**, meaning nearly a third of all alignment decisions are contested. The paper concluded this “raises questions about whether RLHF can effectively capture and reproduce ethical and legal value systems.” A separate study in *PNAS Nexus* testing five GPT models against World Values Survey data from 112 countries found all models exhibit cultural values “resembling English-speaking and Protestant European countries.” Cultural prompting failed to correct alignment for **19–29% of countries**.

The rift between effective altruism (EA) and social justice camps in AI safety exemplifies the irreconcilability. Timnit Gebru has called EA “a dangerous cult rooted in eugenics” and, with philosopher Émile Torres, coined the term TESCREAL to describe its ideological roots. Torres observed: “Talking about human extinction is just so much more sensational and captivating than Kenyan workers getting paid $1.32 an hour.” Meanwhile, EA-aligned researchers view present-day bias concerns as distractions from existential risk. Both camps invoke Western values. Neither can compel the other. Iason Gabriel of DeepMind wrote in *Minds and Machines*: “The central challenge for theorists is not to identify ‘true’ moral principles for AI; rather, it is to identify fair principles for alignment that receive reflective endorsement despite widespread variation in people’s moral beliefs.” A 2025 arXiv paper put it bluntly: “Political Neutrality in AI is Impossible.”

## When productivity defines personhood, displacement becomes existential

Western liberal democracies have tied human dignity to economic contribution so deeply that the phrase “dignity of work” appears across the entire political spectrum — from Trump executive orders to Biden speeches to Marco Rubio op-eds. **AI is now dismantling that link, and no Western framework has an answer for what comes after.**

The numbers are stark. The IMF estimates **40% of global jobs** face meaningful AI exposure; in advanced economies, **60%**. Goldman Sachs projects AI could automate tasks equivalent to **300 million full-time jobs worldwide**. McKinsey estimates **375 million workers** will need career shifts by 2030. These are not distant projections: U.S. customer service employment declined by ~80,000 positions between 2022 and 2024. Workers in their 20s in AI-exposed occupations saw unemployment rise by nearly **3 percentage points** in early 2025. AI-cited U.S. job cuts reached ~55,000 in 2025 alone — a 12x increase over two years earlier. Major companies have explicitly invoked AI: Salesforce cut 4,000 customer support jobs; CrowdStrike eliminated 500 positions for “AI efficiency”; Chegg slashed 45% of its workforce citing “new realities of AI.”

What happens when work-based identity collapses is not theoretical. Anne Case and Angus Deaton’s research at Princeton documented **158,000 American “deaths of despair”** in 2018 — suicides, drug overdoses, alcoholic liver disease — up from 65,000 in 1995, concentrated among white Americans without college degrees in deindustrialized regions. U.S. life expectancy fell for three consecutive years, a reversal unseen since 1918. Their finding: “Destroy work and, in the end, working-class life cannot survive.” This population saw purchasing power decline 13% since 1979 while per capita GDP rose 85%. The institutions that once provided meaning — unions, churches, community organizations — disintegrated alongside employment.

UBI experiments have addressed material needs but conspicuously failed to address meaning. Sam Altman’s own OpenResearch study — 3,000 participants receiving $1,000/month for three years — found **no evidence of increased creative output, entrepreneurship, community engagement, self-improvement, or time with children**. Stress reduction effects faded by Year 2. The Foundation for Economic Education concluded: “Insofar as the case for UBI is built on unleashing the stifled engine of human creativity, UBI isn’t up for the job.” Finland’s 2017–2018 experiment showed improved wellbeing but minimal employment effects — and Finland’s government declined to adopt it. The EU AI Act, the world’s first comprehensive AI regulation, contains **zero provisions** for managing economic displacement. Carnegie Endowment noted in February 2026: “Regulating how systems are developed and deployed is distinct from managing how societies absorb the structural economic changes.”

Michael Sandel articulated the philosophical core of this failure: “The fundamental human need is to be needed by those with whom we share a common life.” In June 2025, he argued on the Center for Humane Technology podcast that “this isn’t just about economics — it’s about what it means to be human when our work role in society vanishes.” Sam Altman himself acknowledged the void at a March 2026 BlackRock summit: **“If there was an easy consensus answer, we’d have done it by now, so I don’t think anyone knows what to do.”**

## Democracies deploying the surveillance they condemn

Western democracies routinely criticize China’s social credit system and Xinjiang surveillance. They are simultaneously building remarkably similar infrastructure.

**Clearview AI** has amassed over **60 billion facial images** scraped from the internet without consent, selling biometric searches to hundreds of U.S. police departments, the FBI, and U.S. Army. In September 2025, ICE signed a **$9.2 million contract** with Clearview — its largest federal deal — coinciding with immigration enforcement operations. The Trump administration quietly erased DHS’s facial recognition oversight policy from its website while deepening these contracts. As of 2026, **at least eight people have been wrongfully arrested** due to Clearview-powered false positives. Every publicly known victim is Black. Robert Williams was held for 30 hours in Detroit. Porcha Woodruff, eight months pregnant, was arrested for carjacking based on an eight-year-old mugshot. Christopher Gatlin spent **over 16 months in jail** before charges were dropped.

London’s Metropolitan Police used live facial recognition **117 times** in the first eight months of 2024 alone — up from 32 total deployments across 2020–2023. By 2025, LFR scanned **3.5 million faces** and produced 1,010 arrests. But **80% of false alerts** flagged people with a Black background, and a Big Brother Watch study found **over 89% of all UK police LFR alerts** since introduction wrongly identified members of the public. In August 2025, the UK Equality and Human Rights Commission declared the Met’s use **unlawful**. No dedicated UK legislation governs the technology — Parliament held its first debate on police facial recognition only in November 2024, eight years after it was first deployed.

The pattern extends beyond facial recognition. The Dutch SyRI system cross-referenced 17 categories of personal data to flag “unlikely citizen profiles” for welfare fraud investigation — deployed exclusively in poor, immigrant-heavy neighborhoods. UN Special Rapporteur Philip Alston called it “the digital equivalent of fraud inspectors knocking on every door in a certain area... while no such scrutiny is applied to those living in better off areas.” A court struck it down in 2020 for violating Article 8 of the European Convention on Human Rights. **SyRI never detected a single case of fraud.** Australia’s Robodebt scheme issued **794,000 false and unlawful automated debt notices** to welfare recipients, wrongfully recovering A$746 million. At least 2,030 people died after receiving notices. The Royal Commission found it was “a crude and cruel mechanism, neither fair nor legal.”

**Palantir’s** federal contracts surged to **$970.5 million in 2025**, nearly doubling year-over-year. Its ICE contract for “ImmigrationOS” provides “near real-time visibility” tracking immigrants by integrating passports, Social Security numbers, IRS data, license plates, and facial recognition. Its $10 billion Army contract grants access to every Army database. The Atlantic Council captured the contradiction: “The United States itself is confronted with accusations of hypocrisy: in the aftermath of the September 11 terror attacks, US intelligence services massively expanded their surveillance practices.” The EU AI Act nominally prohibits real-time biometric identification in public spaces but carved exceptions for terrorism, missing persons, and serious crime — and retrospective facial recognition was permitted without court approval after a last-minute change. Ella Jakubowska of the Reclaim Your Face coalition warned the exceptions “will have a legitimising effect,” giving mass biometric surveillance an “EU seal of approval.”

## Life-or-death decisions where Western ethics contradicts itself

In autonomous weapons, medical triage, criminal sentencing, and insurance, Western ethical traditions produce mutually exclusive answers — and the impossibility is in some cases mathematically proven.

**Israel’s Lavender AI system** assigned every resident of Gaza — 2.3 million people — a score estimating their likelihood of armed group membership, generating **37,000 people as potential targets**. Six Israeli intelligence officers told +972 Magazine that operatives served as “rubber stamp” for machine decisions, sometimes approving kills in **20 seconds** — just confirming the target was male. The system had an acknowledged ~10% error rate. A companion system, “Where’s Daddy?”, tracked individuals via phone signals specifically to target them at home at night with their families. The utilitarian case (more efficient targeting reduces total conflict duration) and the deontological case (machines cannot make moral judgments about life and death) are both grounded in Western ethical traditions. UN Secretary-General Guterres called autonomous weapons “politically unacceptable and morally repugnant.” The U.S., UK, and Israel argue existing international humanitarian law suffices — effectively blocking binding regulations. Neither framework yields.

In healthcare, **UnitedHealth Group’s nH Predict algorithm** determines how long elderly patients receive post-acute care, overriding physician recommendations. When federal judges hear appeals of denied claims, **roughly 90% are reversed** — but only **0.2% of policyholders ever appeal**. UnitedHealth’s denial rate for post-acute care rose from 10.9% in 2020 to 22.7% in 2022 after implementation. Cigna’s PxDx algorithm enabled a single medical director to reject **60,000 claims in one month** — 1.2 seconds per claim. During COVID-19, the SOFA score used by 95% of hospitals for ventilator triage was shown to assign Black patients higher risk scores despite not being more likely to die — systematically disadvantaging them in resource allocation. Disability rights advocates filed lawsuits in ten states alleging triage protocols treated “an abled life as more worthy than a life with a disability.” The utilitarian imperative to save the most lives clashed irreconcilably with rights-based commitments to equality.

**The impossibility of algorithmic fairness is now mathematically established.** Alexandra Chouldechova (Carnegie Mellon) and Jon Kleinberg, Sendhil Mullainathan, and Manish Raghavan (Cornell) independently proved that when base rates of an outcome differ across groups — as recidivism rates differ by race due to centuries of differential policing — **no imperfect algorithm can simultaneously achieve equal predictive accuracy and equal error rates across groups**. This is not a bug to be fixed. It is a theorem. The COMPAS sentencing algorithm demonstrated this exactly: ProPublica found it correctly predicted recidivism at similar rates for Black and white defendants, but Black defendants were **almost twice as likely** to be falsely labeled high-risk (44.9% vs. 23.5%). Both ProPublica and Northpointe were correct — they were simply measuring different, mathematically irreconcilable definitions of fairness. As Alice Xiang noted: “It’s not really something anyone can answer. It’s asking what a fair criminal justice system would look like. Even if you are a lawyer, even if you are an ethicist, you cannot provide one firm answer.”

MIT’s Moral Machine experiment — **40 million decisions** from 2.3 million people in 233 countries — identified three distinct moral clusters (Western, Eastern, Southern) with systematically different preferences on whom autonomous vehicles should protect. German law prohibits any distinction based on personal features like age; global respondents overwhelmingly preferred saving children over the elderly. No universal answer exists.

## Consent was built for handshakes, not for algorithms

Western liberalism’s commitment to informed consent — the bedrock of its legitimacy — becomes incoherent when AI makes decisions about millions of people who never knew an algorithm was involved.

In **Mobley v. Workday** (2024–2025), Derek Mobley, a Black man over 40 with a disability, applied to 80+ positions through companies using Workday’s hiring platform and was rejected every time, often within minutes. In May 2025, the case was certified as the first nationwide collective action for AI hiring discrimination, representing millions of applicants. A University of Washington audit of AI resume screening found white-associated names preferred in **85.1% of tests**; Black male candidates were disadvantaged in **100% of direct comparisons** with white males. Amazon’s internal hiring tool, trained on a decade of predominantly male résumés, penalized any résumé containing the word “women’s” before being scrapped in 2017. In a 2025 case, HireVue’s AI video interviewing tool scored a deaf employee low and recommended she “practice active listening.” Applicants in most jurisdictions have no right to know AI screened them, no ability to inspect the model, and no practical alternative to submitting to algorithmic evaluation.

Shoshana Zuboff’s framework identifies the structural collapse: “It was understood that users were unlikely to agree to this unilateral claiming of their experience.” By accepting terms of use, users technically authorize data collection, but “it is no genuine consent because users typically do not, and often cannot, understand what they are consenting to.” She compares the strategy to Spanish conquerors’ use of formal consent documents to claim indigenous territories. The GDPR’s celebrated “right to explanation” may not actually exist — Sandra Wachter, Bodo Mittelstadt, and Luciano Floridi argued in a landmark 2017 paper that the regulation contains no such right, and the European Parliament’s own 2020 study concluded its “prescriptions are often vague and open-ended” regarding AI. Article 23 permits member states to restrict transparency rights on national security grounds. When Rotterdam used AI to flag welfare fraud, it disproportionately targeted single mothers and non-Western immigrants using criteria including “a well-groomed appearance.” The “notice and consent” architecture fails because accepting one platform’s terms cascades through third-party data-sharing chains no individual can trace.

Insurance makes the consent problem existential: people cannot opt out of AI-driven decisions when coverage is legally mandated (auto) or practically essential (health, home). Algorithms are proprietary trade secrets. In auto insurance, drivers in predominantly non-white communities pay an average of **$1,728 more annually** in New York State, and premiums in African American communities average **71% higher** than comparable white-population areas.

## The engagement vacuum where purpose should be

Western liberalism’s principled neutrality on what constitutes “the good life” created the vacuum that AI filled with engagement metrics — and the results are documented by the companies themselves.

Facebook’s internal research, leaked by Frances Haugen in 2021, found Instagram “makes body image issues worse for one in three teen girls.” **13% of UK teens** and 6% of U.S. teens reported that desire to kill themselves “started on Instagram.” Meta’s own pilot showed users who paused Instagram for one week reported lower depression, anxiety, loneliness, and social comparison — then Meta reportedly stopped the research project. Meta’s defense was classically liberal: the product helps more teens than it hurts on 11 of 12 metrics, and users choose to engage. As Tristan Harris of the Center for Humane Technology observed: “Technology claims to be showing us a mirror of what was already present in society — but in reality, technology is a funhouse mirror with a feedback loop that’s engineered to show us the most egregious parts.” The reason platforms optimize for engagement is precisely that there is no agreed-upon alternative metric. Harris’s entire project — “align technology with humanity’s best interests” — encounters the same “whose interests?” question that alignment researchers face.

Shannon Vallor, former AI ethicist at Google and now at Edinburgh’s Futures Institute, has argued that both utilitarianism and Kantian deontology are structurally inadequate for AI governance. Kant’s categorical imperative, she writes, cannot be readily applied “against a backdrop of the unpredictable course followed by technological development.” She advocates Aristotelian virtue ethics and phronēsis (practical wisdom), noting these have parallels in Confucian and Buddhist traditions. Kate Crawford’s *Atlas of AI* argues that AI is “neither artificial nor intelligent” but rather “a registry of power” and “politics by other means” — a technology of extraction from minerals to labor to data. Non-Western thinkers are proposing genuinely different foundations: Sabelo Mhlambi at Harvard’s Carr Center has proposed Ubuntu philosophy (”I am because we are”) as an AI ethics framework that centers relational rather than individual identity. Lewis, Arista, Pechawis, and Kite proposed Indigenous epistemologies that “conceive of our computational creations as kin,” critiquing the “rationalist, neoliberal, and Christianity-infused assumptions” embedded in Western AI development. A 2025 paper in *AI and Ethics* argued that “current AI ethics tends to be based on Western liberal individualism while ignoring other, more relational worldviews that do not restrict moral status to individual humans.”

A study tracking over 470 AI ethics documents globally found voices from the Global South and alternative ethical approaches “largely absent from the conversation.” Yet these absent perspectives may contain what Western frameworks lack: a substantive account of what humans are for.

## Conclusion: the architecture is the problem

The pattern across all six domains is consistent. Western liberal democracy excels at procedural protections — rights, consent, transparency, due process — but has deliberately evacuated substantive commitments about human purpose, communal obligation, and the good life. This was a feature, not a bug: Rawlsian neutrality between “comprehensive doctrines” was designed for a pluralistic society. But AI operates in the vacuum that neutrality creates. When there is no shared vision of flourishing, algorithms optimize for engagement. When consent is procedural rather than substantive, terms-of-service checkboxes authorize surveillance capitalism. When fairness has no agreed definition, mathematical impossibility theorems prove that competing formalizations cannot coexist. When human dignity is indexed to productivity, automation becomes an identity crisis, not merely an economic one.

The expert consensus is not that Western values are wrong but that they are **radically incomplete** for governing AI. Sam Altman’s confession — “I don’t think anyone knows what to do” — is not humility. It is an accurate description of a civilization that built world-transforming technology on philosophical foundations that explicitly refuse to answer what technology should be for. The most urgent insight from this research is that the alternative frameworks are not exotic supplements but structural necessities: virtue ethics’ focus on character and practical wisdom, Ubuntu’s relational ontology, Confucian role-based obligations, Indigenous kinship models. These are not replacements for rights and consent. They are the missing load-bearing walls in an architecture that is already buckling under the weight of decisions it was never designed to support.
