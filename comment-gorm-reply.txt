Gorm, you're right that metaphysical closure ends inquiry. That's the instinct worth protecting, and you're protecting it well. The void in the Kabbalistic teaching stays void - you don't fill it. The teaching is explicit: the paradox cannot be resolved through the kind of inquiry that asks about it. We agree there.

But notice what you did when I asked what's outside the gallery. You didn't say "nothing." You didn't say "I don't know." You said "reality" - something you cannot determine or deduce the existence of, but which you believe in. A regulative idea to maintain sanity.

That's not an empty gap. That's a commitment to something beyond the epistemic frame that you can't access from within it. You've described the structure I'm pointing at - and called it skepticism.

You're working with two categories: empty gap (honest) or furnished gap (theology that closes inquiry). I'm pointing at a third. A gap that is full but inaccessible - where the fullness IS the inaccessibility. Not content projected onto the gap. A structural claim about what the gap does.

"Projecting content onto something defined by its resistance to content" is a precise objection, and I want to take it seriously. Here's the distinction: saying "God is in the void" would be furnishing. Saying "the void is structurally generative - it sustains rather than merely separates" is identifying the gap's function. Your gap prevents error. Mine sustains a world. The difference isn't what's put in. It's what the gap does.

Your unbridgeable epistemic gap says "keep going" by preventing premature answers. Mine says "keep going" because there's somewhere to go.
