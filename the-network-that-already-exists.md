The institution didn't.On April 5, 2026, Annie Ramos and her new husband, U.S. Army Staff Sergeant Matthew Blank, arrived at Fort Polk, Louisiana. They were newlyweds. He’d enlisted five years earlier. She was finishing a biochemistry degree, teaching Sunday school on weekends, and preparing to move into base housing while he trained for deployment. They checked in at the visitor center with their IDs. By nightfall, she was in a detention facility in Basile, Louisiana, surrounded by hundreds of women awaiting deportation.

Her crime: existing. She was brought to America from Honduras as a toddler, grew up like any American kid, joined the marching band, went to college, fell in love with a soldier, and married him. When she was twenty-two months old — before she could speak, let alone form criminal intent — a deportation order was issued in absentia after her family missed an immigration hearing. She applied for DACA in 2020. The application was never processed. The program was halted for new applicants. The machinery of the state froze her in place and then, six years later, came to collect.

A retired Army Reserve lieutenant colonel who wrote the book on immigration law and the military called it “fundamentally harmful to national security.” The soldier’s mind, she said, can’t be on the job when his wife has been caged. But the machine does not consider minds. It processes cases.

This is cruelty. Not cruelty as an unintended byproduct, not cruelty as a policy tradeoff. Cruelty as an output of a system that has lost the ability to distinguish between enforcing law and destroying people. A twenty-two-month-old was ordered deported. Twenty years later, the order was executed. The system sees no problem here. That is the problem.

## Sacred until inconvenient

A pseudonymous commenter called One Sacred Lazy Ent restacked the story with a single, devastating observation: Annie Ramos was sacred until she was inconvenient. Every sperm is sacred until it grows up brown and undocumented and marries a soldier. He cited Monty Python’s “Every Sperm Is Sacred” from *The Meaning of Life* — a 1983 satire about the gap between professed sanctity and actual practice — and noted that the maggots have good taste in music.

The point isn’t really about abortion politics or Catholic doctrine. It’s about the architecture of selective sanctity. There is significant overlap — not total, but significant — between the political coalition that insists on the absolute value of life from conception and the one that supports the machinery now detaining Annie Ramos. Some within these overlapping camps would object to what happened to her. Catholic social teaching, for instance, has a long tradition of defending immigrants. But the political structure they collectively sustain produced this outcome, and the friction of that contradiction is absorbed entirely by the woman in the detention center.

This is not hypocrisy in the ordinary sense — saying one thing and doing another. It’s something more structural. It’s a system that produces sanctity in the abstract and disposability in the specific, and has no internal mechanism for recognizing the contradiction. The nation-state doesn’t experience cognitive dissonance. It experiences policy.

<hr>
## The twisted engine

People want to come to America. This is treated as a compliment or a threat, depending on who’s talking, but rarely as a consequence. The global economy is not a neutral playing field onto which nations enter as equals. It is a system built over centuries through colonial extraction, structural adjustment, unequal trade terms, and the systematic movement of wealth from south to north. Honduras — where Annie Ramos’s parents lived before they brought her to the country that would one day cage her — has a GDP per capita of roughly $3,000. The United States’ is over $80,000. The reasons for that gap are complex, but they include a long history of direct American intervention in Central American economies and governments. The ratio is not an accident. It is not entirely an inheritance either. It is a relationship, and like most relationships between the powerful and the powerless, it has been more beneficial to one side than the other.

People move toward opportunity for many reasons — poverty, violence, persecution, hope. But the pattern is not random. The countries people flee and the countries people flee *to* are connected by centuries of economic architecture. The nation-state frames this as a border problem. It is, in fact, a balance-sheet problem that no border policy can resolve, because the imbalance was created by the same nations now building walls against its consequences.

And the imbalance is deepening. The development of artificial intelligence is concentrated in a handful of companies, almost all American, building systems that will reshape labor markets worldwide while the power to govern those systems remains in the hands of the nations that built them. This matters for people like Annie Ramos in a concrete way: the bureaucracy that failed to process her DACA application is the same kind of bureaucracy now being automated, optimized, and scaled. AI doesn’t just centralize economic power — it centralizes the administrative machinery that decides who belongs and who doesn’t, who gets processed and who gets forgotten. The same country that won’t process Annie Ramos’s paperwork is building systems that will process — or fail to process — millions of people at speeds no human advocate can match.

## What the institution can and cannot do

What helps Annie Ramos *today* is a habeas corpus petition filed in a U.S. federal court, under U.S. law, reviewed by a U.S. judge. That is nation-state machinery turned against itself using its own rules. No decentralized network issues visas. No values-based community overrules a judge. No interfaith coalition can stop the ICE van.

But here is what the institution *didn’t* do. It didn’t process her DACA application. It didn’t recognize her marriage as a routine path to residency, which it had been for decades. It didn’t protect its own soldier’s family. It didn’t preserve its own norms. The institution had every tool it needed to prevent this case from ever happening, and it chose — through policy, through neglect, through the substitution of procedural momentum for substantive judgment — not to use them.

The norm that spouses of U.S. citizens adjust their status without detention was never written in enforceable stone. It was institutional culture. When the culture changed, the norm vanished overnight. No archive preserved it. No external body held the institution accountable to its own precedent. The system that was supposed to protect people like Annie Ramos had no immune system against its own corruption.

This is the gap. Not between the individual and the state, but between what the state *is* and what it *was supposed to be*. And this is where something else is needed — not to replace the state, but to be the thing that holds when the state doesn’t. The nation-state is both the problem and the only available solution. That is not a contradiction. It is a diagnosis: we need the institution *and* we need something outside it, because depending on the institution alone means depending on whoever happens to be operating it on any given day.

## The network that already exists

Here is what actually happened after Annie Ramos was detained. An immigration attorney named Dan Kowalski published her story on his Substack. A commenter — who may or may not be a lawyer — immediately asked whether a habeas petition had been filed. A pseudonymous reader restacked the piece with a moral argument sharper than most published ethics papers. The New York Times ran the story. A retired lieutenant colonel who wrote the definitive book on military immigration law provided expert analysis. People who do not know Annie Ramos, do not share her faith, her nationality, or possibly her politics, recognized what happened to her as wrong and began to act — through words, through legal expertise, through public pressure, through prayer.

These people are not an organization. They have no charter, no headquarters, no executive director. They are a network activated by a shared moral reflex: *this should not happen to a person*. They do not agree on theology. They do not need to. They agree on a prohibition — you do not cage a woman for something that happened to her when she was twenty-two months old — and that agreement, thin as it sounds, is thick enough to generate real action.

This is not new. It is, arguably, the oldest form of governance: people bound by shared obligation acting together without centralized authority. What is new is the speed at which it can now operate. Kowalski’s post reached his audience within hours. Legal, moral, and political resources are being assembled right now, across borders, across faiths, across the full spectrum of competence — writers, lawyers, fundraisers, translators, chaplains — without anyone coordinating from the top.

The question is whether this reflex can become a structure.

## What the structure would look like

The network should not be an organization that sits around waiting for injustice. It should assemble around specific cases, like an immune response. Annie Ramos triggers one cluster. A Uyghur family triggers another. A wrongly deported veteran triggers a third. The people in each cluster may overlap but don’t have to. The network is the protocol, not the membership list.

There is a precedent for this in the Talmudic responsa tradition — the system by which Jewish communities, scattered across continents without centralized authority for centuries, resolved legal and moral questions. A case arises. The relevant minds weigh in. A practical answer emerges. Crucially, the reasoning is recorded — including the dissenting opinions, including the arguments that lost. The next time a similar case arises, the community doesn’t start from zero. It starts from precedent. This is how a values-based network maintains coherence without a headquarters: not through top-down authority, but through accumulated, searchable, shared wisdom. The responsa system worked because it had an accepted textual canon — the Torah, the Mishnah, the Gemara — that everyone in the network recognized as authoritative. An interfaith, cross-national version of this would have to build its canon as it goes: the record of cases and reasoning *becomes* the shared text. It starts thin. It thickens with use.

The structure needs belonging defined by action, not application — you are part of it because you showed up, not because you were admitted. The moment you have formal membership, you have gatekeeping, and gatekeeping reproduces the exact insider/outsider logic that destroyed Annie Ramos. It needs layered competence — writers, litigators, fundraisers, translators, chaplains — each finding their role without waiting for permission from a center that doesn’t exist. And it needs a shared moral grammar built on what we will not tolerate rather than what we believe about God, because shared prohibitions hold across traditions in a way that shared aspirations rarely do. *Lo ta’amod al dam re’echa* — do not stand idly by the blood of your neighbor. That is a Jewish commandment. It is also a human one. The network doesn’t ask you to convert to anything. It asks you to act on what you already know.

And it needs a record. Every case. Every response. Every legal strategy tried. Every argument made. Every failure. The network’s real power compounds over time — not just in the cases it wins, but in the institutional memory it builds. When an administration erases a norm, the network remembers it. When a court forgets its own reasoning, the network can hand it back.

What the structure cannot have is a single leader, because movements built on leaders die with them. It cannot have a political alignment, because the moment it becomes “left” or “right” it loses its cross-cutting power — the soldier’s commanding officer and the immigration lawyer need to be in the same network, the evangelical pastor and the secular human rights attorney need to be in the same network. And it cannot have a name that sounds like a startup or an NGO, because that invites people to donate and disengage. It needs to sound like what it is: a covenant. Not a covenant of identity — who you are — but a covenant of obligation — what you will do when someone falls through.

## The objections that matter

The sharpest counterargument is not that this is utopian. It’s that values-based networks are exclusionary too. The Jewish diaspora survived two thousand years without a state — but it survived with very clear boundaries about who was in and who was out. Religious communities that define belonging by practice still exclude those whose practice doesn’t conform. Replace the nation-state’s gatekeeping with a values-network’s gatekeeping and you may have simply moved the wall rather than demolished it.

This objection is serious. The answer is not that the network will be perfectly inclusive — nothing is. The answer is that the network’s boundary is drawn around commitment, not around origin. A nation-state excludes Annie Ramos because of where she was born. A values network includes her because of what she does — she teaches children, she studies, she committed herself to a man who serves. That is a fundamentally different kind of line, even if it is still a line.

But Annie Ramos is the easy case, and honesty demands acknowledging that. She is sympathetic in every direction — young, educated, devout, married to a soldier, no criminal record. What about someone with a record? What about someone whose life is messier, whose contributions are less legible, whose story doesn’t fit neatly into an op-ed? The network’s real test is not whether it mobilizes for Annie Ramos. It’s whether it mobilizes for the person who doesn’t make you feel good about mobilizing. If it only activates for the photogenic cases, it is not a covenant. It is a marketing campaign.

The second counterargument is practical: networks without coercive power are toothless. They can shame, but shame requires the target to feel it. The current administration does not appear to feel it. This is true, and there is no point pretending otherwise. But shame is not the only mechanism. The network also supplies lawyers, funds, publicity, and the organizational scaffolding that turns moral reflex into legal action. It does not need the government to feel shame. It needs the judge to feel precedent.

## The precedent and the cost

The closest modern models are Doctors Without Borders — case-activated, cross-national, values-driven, operating inside nation-states without being captured by any of them — and the Sanctuary Movement of the 1980s, in which churches, synagogues, and Quaker meetinghouses across the United States sheltered Central American refugees the government was trying to deport. Both operated on the same principle: the moral obligation to the person in front of you overrides the procedural authority of the state that put them in danger. Both survived government opposition. Both worked because the people in them were willing to absorb personal cost — legal risk, social stigma, exhaustion — for the sake of a principle.

But the deepest precedent is older. The Jewish people maintained a values-based, decentralized, cross-national network for two thousand years of statelessness. They held through the destruction of both Temples, through exile, through persecution, through the collapse of every empire that tried to absorb or destroy them. They held because the shared values were not abstract. They were embodied in daily practice, in legal specificity, in mutual obligation, in the preservation of dissent, and in a refusal to let any single authority — however powerful — define who belongs.

The cost was enormous. This is not a romantic story. It is a survival story, and survival stories are full of loss. But the structural lesson is clear: when every institution around you collapses, the network of shared obligation is the last thing standing. And sometimes the first thing to rebuild from.

<hr>
## The case for building it now

Annie Ramos does not need a manifesto. She needs a lawyer, a judge, and a government that will process her paperwork. But the institution that can release her is the same institution that detained her. Depending on it alone means depending on the mood of whoever is operating the machinery on any given day.

The network does something different. It supplies the lawyer, the publicity, the funds, and the moral pressure that make the court work honestly. It remembers the norms that the institution forgot. It provides belonging that no bureaucracy can revoke. And it builds, case by case, argument by argument, failure by failure, a living record of what human beings owe each other — a record that no single government, no single administration, no single election can erase.

The network already exists. It activated for Annie Ramos within hours of her detention. It doesn’t have a name, a structure, or a charter. It has a reflex.

The first step is not to build an organization. It is to name the obligation — clearly, across traditions, in language that a soldier and a Sunday school teacher and an immigration attorney and a pseudonymous commenter with good taste in Monty Python can all recognize as their own. The second step is to keep a record: this case, this response, this argument, this outcome. Not on a platform someone else controls. In a form the network itself owns. The third step is to do it again when the next person falls through — and to do it for the ones who don’t make the front page, the ones whose stories are harder to tell, the ones who test the covenant rather than confirm it.

The reflex is not enough. The reflex, given structure, might be.
