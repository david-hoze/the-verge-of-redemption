# America’s Suez moment? The Hormuz crisis and the question of US decline

Stephen Gaghan's 2005 thriller — with George Clooney, Matt Damon, and Jeffrey Wright each navigating a different layer of the petrodollar machine — feels less like a movie and more like a briefing document for 2026. Cold, intricate, and uncomfortably prescient.Five weeks into a war the Trump administration said would last four to six weeks, the Strait of Hormuz remains functionally closed, oil has surged past $110 a barrel, and Iran is running a toll booth in Chinese yuan on what was once an American-guaranteed shipping lane. The White House has confirmed that reopening the strait is not a “core objective” of the military campaign. A 40-nation coalition led by Britain is scrambling to fill the vacuum — without US participation. China, meanwhile, is sailing its tankers through under Iranian escort while positioning itself as the responsible adult in global affairs. These facts do not by themselves prove that a structural power transition is underway. But they create the conditions under which one could accelerate dramatically.


## The mechanism that killed the pound is now testing the dollar

The comparison between Britain’s 1956 Suez debacle and America’s 2026 Hormuz crisis is not new — analysts from Princeton’s Harold James to the London School of Economics’ Fawaz Gerges have drawn it explicitly. But the sharpest articulation came from Italian journalist Federico Fubini in a March 31 interview with Le Grand Continent. His core argument is structural, not analogical: **what destroyed sterling’s status was not a battlefield defeat but the revelation that Britain could no longer guarantee freedom of navigation on the routes that underwrote its commercial empire.** “The pound was tied to the UK’s capacity to maintain these routes,” Fubini wrote. “When that capacity was called into question, the currency followed.”

The parallel is uncomfortably precise. In 1956, Nasser nationalized the Suez Canal and Britain — despite tactical military success alongside France and Israel — was forced into a humiliating withdrawal when the United States refused support and threatened to dump sterling bonds. The Bank of England hemorrhaged $200 million in reserves in days. Britain accepted a ceasefire within a week. The canal reopened under Egyptian sovereignty the following April. Britain’s status as a superpower was effectively over.

In 2026, Iran has closed the Strait of Hormuz — through which **20% of global oil supply** and roughly a third of seaborne LNG transits — in retaliation for Operation Epic Fury, the US-Israeli strikes that killed Supreme Leader Ali Khamenei. The US military has “decimated” Iranian conventional forces, as Trump repeatedly claims. Yet the strait remains closed. Traffic is down approximately **95%** from pre-war levels. The IRGC operates a toll corridor near Larak Island, charging roughly $2 million per vessel in yuan or cryptocurrency, escorting approved ships through Iranian territorial waters. Iran has launched 21 confirmed attacks on merchant vessels, killing crew members. Nearly 3,000 ships sit stranded.

Trump’s public statements have lurched from promising to escort tankers personally to telling Britain on Truth Social to “start learning how to fight for yourself” and posting “Go get your own oil!” Defense Secretary Pete Hegseth called Hormuz “not just a United States of America problem set.” Secretary of State Rubio described reopening as “a post-conflict necessity” and said the US was “prepared to be a part of that plan. We don’t have to lead that plan.” CNN and the Wall Street Journal both reported in late March that the administration was preparing to declare victory and exit without securing the strait’s reopening.

This is the dynamic that makes the Suez analogy bite. A superpower that cannot — or will not — keep the world’s most critical trade route open is a superpower whose guarantees ring hollow. Fubini captured it precisely: “If the United States cannot reopen the Strait of Hormuz — or are perceived as incapable of doing so — the question simply arises: what does dollar dominance guarantee, exactly, if it no longer guarantees the routes of energy and commerce?”


## China sails through while Washington retreats

Beijing’s response to the Hormuz crisis has been a masterclass in strategic positioning — or, as former US diplomat Danny Russel put it, “messaging, not mediation.” Both characterizations contain truth, and understanding which matters more is central to evaluating the power-transition thesis.

The facts first. On March 31, China and Pakistan jointly issued a five-point peace initiative calling for an immediate ceasefire, protection of shipping lanes, and a UN-based peace framework. Foreign Minister Wang Yi conducted more than **20 phone calls** with regional counterparts in the first five weeks of the crisis. China opposed Bahrain’s UN Security Council resolution authorizing force to protect shipping — Ambassador Fu Cong argued it would “legitimize the unlawful and indiscriminate use of force.” Most consequentially, Iranian forces began selectively allowing Chinese-flagged and Chinese-linked vessels through the strait in late March. Dozens of ships updated their AIS signals to broadcast Chinese ties, hoping to gain safe passage. Retired Chinese colonel Zhou Bo captured the symbolism: “Security is linked to China, and chaos is linked to the United States.”

The critique that this is “performative” has real weight. Russel noted the five-point plan resembled China’s 12-point Ukraine proposal from 2023 — “filled with platitudes but never acted on.” The Diplomat assessed it as “less about conflict resolution and more about managing Beijing’s exposure to the war’s economic externalities.” Ali Wyne of the International Crisis Group observed that China “welcomes the opportunity to suggest that it is helping mitigate a crisis of America’s making” — without committing to anything that would require follow-through.

Yet dismissing China’s positioning as mere theater misses a deeper point. **The performative and the structural are not opposites here — they are sequential.** Britain’s replacement by America as the guarantor of Middle Eastern sea lanes did not happen in a single dramatic act. It happened through an accumulation of demonstrations — of American capacity, American willingness, American reliability — against a backdrop of British inability. China does not need to reopen the Strait of Hormuz tomorrow. It needs to be seen as the power willing to engage, to mediate, and to provide alternatives, while the United States is seen as the power that broke the system and walked away. Fubini’s sharpest line resonates: “We will speak of Hormuz in the 21st century as we spoke of Suez in the 20th — but with a Chinese accent.”

That said, China’s capacity to actually replace American functions remains severely limited. Beijing has no military bases in the Gulf, no CENTCOM-equivalent command structure, no alliance architecture comparable to what the US built over decades. Its Navy, while growing rapidly, cannot project power in the Persian Gulf the way the US Fifth Fleet does. The crisis reveals American weakness without yet demonstrating Chinese strength — a critical distinction.


## The economic architecture of a power shift

Three economic dimensions of the Hormuz crisis deserve serious analysis: the dollar’s reserve status, the energy transition, and the defense industrial base.

**On the dollar**, the headlines are dramatic but the data is more ambiguous. The dollar’s share of global foreign exchange reserves has declined from roughly 72% in the early 2000s to **57% as of late 2025** — a meaningful erosion but not a collapse. The yuan, despite years of internationalization rhetoric, accounts for just **2.1% of global reserves**, actually slipping from 2.3%. Structural barriers remain formidable: China’s capital account is among the world’s least open, its legal system provides weak contract enforcement by international standards, and its bond markets lack the depth and liquidity of US Treasuries. Cleveland Fed President Beth Hammack noted in March 2026 that “while the dollar’s share of global reserves may have peaked, it’s difficult to envision imminent major changes.”

But the Hormuz crisis is introducing a novel wedge. Iran is demanding yuan or cryptocurrency for toll payments. Bloomberg confirmed that vessels are paying in Chinese currency for escorted passage. Saudi Arabia effectively ended its exclusive dollar oil-pricing commitment in June 2024 and participates in China’s mBridge payment platform. Deutsche Bank called the crisis potentially “the most significant challenge to the petrodollar system in its fifty-two-year history.” Fubini noted that “every oil contract signed in yuan, every central bank reserve partially reconstituted in gold, every bilateral agreement denominated in renminbi imperceptibly reduces” the cost of exiting the dollar system. The risk is not sudden collapse but gradual erosion — death by a thousand cuts over decades. Rabobank’s assessment of a **20-to-30-year timeframe** for meaningful de-dollarization seems right, but the Hormuz crisis is accelerating the clock.

**On energy**, the crisis sharpens an asymmetry that already favored China. Beijing dominates the technologies that replace fossil fuels: **over 80% of global solar manufacturing**, **70% of EV battery production** (CATL alone holds 39% of the global market), and **over 70% of global EV production**. Every week of elevated oil prices strengthens the economic case for electrification — and China controls the supply chain. Since the war began, BYD’s stock rose 18% and CATL gained 22%, while Stellantis fell 15% and Volkswagen dropped 14%. EVs already account for 52% of new car sales in China versus roughly 10% in the United States. Rising fuel costs hit American consumers directly; Chinese consumers are increasingly insulated.

**On defense**, the picture is genuinely alarming. China controls approximately **90% of global rare earth processing**, **83% of tungsten production**, and **98% of gallium output** — minerals essential for precision munitions, jet engines, and electronics. The West Point Modern War Institute published an analysis in mid-March warning that the Hormuz closure was causing “a paralyzing, real-time problem” for the US defense industrial base, particularly through disrupted sulfur supply chains (half of global seaborne sulfur transits Hormuz) needed to extract copper and cobalt for military hardware. The weapons stockpile situation is stark: the US has fired over **850 Tomahawk cruise missiles in 30 days** — representing roughly 15 years of procurement at the pre-war production rate of about 57 per year. THAAD interceptor inventories were already depleted from Israel’s 2025 conflict. The Heritage Foundation estimated before the war that US initial munitions stocks would last just 25 days in a high-intensity conflict. Pentagon sources have warned bluntly: “If China cuts off magnesium exports, the US would have six months to decide to go to war. After that, we wouldn’t be able to wage war at all.”


## Why the Suez analogy may still break down

The strongest counterargument to the “Suez moment” thesis rests on three pillars: China’s own profound vulnerabilities, the enduring power of American alliances and innovation, and the absence of a viable replacement for American hegemony.

China’s internal challenges are not cosmetic. Its **total fertility rate has collapsed to approximately 1.0** — half the replacement level — with just 7.92 million births in 2025, a 17% plunge from the previous year. The property sector, once 25-30% of GDP, remains in crisis: real estate investment fell nearly 15% in the first ten months of 2025, Evergrande has been liquidated, and even state-backed developer Vanke posted a record $6.8 billion loss. Foreign direct investment collapsed from $344 billion in 2021 to $42.7 billion in 2023. Capital flight averaged $217 billion annually from 2020 to 2024. Youth unemployment hovers above 16%. Retail sales growth slumped to under 1% in December 2025. These are not the characteristics of an economy poised to assume global hegemony.

The alliance argument also carries weight, though less than it did a year ago. The combined GDP of the United States and its treaty allies exceeds $50 trillion — dwarfing China and its loose partners. NATO’s 32 members collectively spend over $1.2 trillion on defense. The US maintains a roughly **tenfold advantage over China in AI compute capacity** and its tech firms invested over $100 billion in AI infrastructure in 2024-2025 alone, compared to China’s estimated $9.3 billion. The semiconductor chokepoint — ASML’s monopoly on extreme ultraviolet lithography — gives Washington and its allies a structural veto over China’s most advanced chip production. These advantages are real and durable.

Perhaps most importantly, there is no ready successor. In 1956, the United States had spent a decade building the institutional architecture — NATO, Bretton Woods, the Marshall Plan, CENTCOM’s predecessors — to replace Britain. China has nothing comparable. It has no formal military alliances. Its Belt and Road Initiative, far from expanding smoothly, is being disrupted by the very crisis that supposedly demonstrates Chinese ascendancy: strikes on Bandar Abbas threaten a central BRI corridor, and the Jerusalem Post noted the war “forces China to rethink the entire concept from expansion to resilience.” The yuan cannot function as a reserve currency while Beijing maintains capital controls. As the Texas National Security Review observed, “The United States today commands the world’s largest GDP, fields unmatched global lift and C4ISR capabilities, and anchors a sprawling lattice of treaty allies across Europe and Asia.”

Historical precedent also counsels caution. Predictions of American decline surged after Sputnik, Vietnam, the 1970s oil shocks, Japan’s rise in the 1980s, and the 2008 financial crisis. Each time, the US adapted. Paul Kennedy’s influential 1987 thesis of “imperial overstretch” was widely cited — until the Soviet Union collapsed and the American economy boomed through the 1990s. As one AEI analysis noted, declinists consistently make the error of “straight-lining” rivals’ growth curves indefinitely while assuming American weaknesses are permanent.

But there is a critical difference this time. Previous decline scares were driven by external competitive pressure — Japan outproducing, the Soviets outarming. **The 2026 crisis is substantially self-inflicted.** The United States chose to strike Iran. The administration chose not to plan for Hormuz closure, despite intelligence warnings. The president chose to tell allies to fend for themselves. The damage to American credibility is not being imposed from outside; it is being generated from within. And Gallup’s finding that China’s global approval rating (**36%**) now exceeds America’s (**31%**) — the largest gap in 20 years — suggests the reputational damage is registering worldwide.


## Neither Suez redux nor business as usual

The honest synthesis is that the Suez analogy captures something real but remains incomplete. Where it holds most powerfully is in the mechanism Fubini identified: **a superpower’s failure to guarantee free navigation on critical trade routes erodes the credibility that underpins its currency and its alliances.** The United States is demonstrating, in real time, that it either cannot or will not perform the function that justifies dollar primacy and American hegemonic leadership. That is genuinely consequential.

Where the analogy breaks down is in the absence of a successor. Britain’s decline had a clean handoff — American institutions, American capital, American military power were ready to fill every vacuum London left behind. China in 2026 has no such capacity. It cannot secure the Gulf militarily. It cannot offer an open, convertible currency. It cannot provide the legal infrastructure for international commerce. Its demographic and economic headwinds are severe. What China can do — and is doing — is position itself as the alternative for countries disillusioned with American reliability, building bilateral relationships, yuan-denominated trade agreements, and diplomatic credibility one crisis at a time.

The Iran war is best understood not as a single Suez-like inflection point but as **a powerful accelerant applied to trends that were already underway**: gradual de-dollarization, the energy transition toward technologies China dominates, the erosion of American alliance credibility, and the depletion of US military-industrial capacity. None of these trends began in February 2026. All of them have been measurably intensified by the crisis.

For the “Suez scenario” to fully play out, several things would need to happen: the strait would need to remain functionally closed or under Iranian control for months; the US would need to formally withdraw without securing reopening; China would need to be seen as instrumental in eventually brokering a resolution; and the yuan would need to gain meaningful traction in energy markets. Some of these conditions are already materializing. For the US to avoid it, the administration would need to either reopen the strait decisively — demonstrating that American power still underwrites global commerce — or orchestrate a diplomatic resolution that preserves American leadership credibility. The current trajectory, in which the White House appears ready to declare victory and leave the strait to others, satisfies neither condition.

The deepest lesson of Suez was not that Britain lost a battle. It was that Britain revealed it could no longer do what a superpower must do — and that once revealed, the revelation could not be unrevealed. The question for Washington is whether the Hormuz crisis has crossed that threshold. Five weeks in, with the strait still closed and the president telling the world to “go get your own oil,” the answer is not yet certain. But it is closer to yes than any previous moment in the post-1945 American order.
