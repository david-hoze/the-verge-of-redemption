# The Sound You Aren’t Hearing

[- - 

](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!MTwu!,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2Fb7a07d8c-37a3-4791-8c47-d2d4758ad603_600x399.jpeg)You’re tired. You’ve got something in you you can’t express in words. You feel like you’re struggling against life, wading against the current. You didn’t always feel like this. Or maybe you did, and you just didn’t have words for it yet.

Think back. You were seven, maybe eight. The classroom was bright. The teacher was talking. And something in you — something restless, something alive — was looking out the window. Not because you were stupid. Not because you didn’t care. But because whatever was happening out there — the bird on the wire, the clouds shifting, the way the light hit the puddle in the parking lot — felt more *real* than the worksheet in front of you.

And someone said: *pay attention.*

And you tried. You really did. But your attention wasn’t broken. It was *searching*. It was looking for something that mattered, something with texture and depth, something that engaged the part of you that was always on — the part that could sit for hours with a book you chose, or a project you cared about, or a problem that grabbed you by the collar and wouldn’t let go. You weren’t missing attention. You were missing a reason to give it.

But nobody said that. What they said was: something is wrong with you. You’re not focusing. You’re not meeting expectations. You’re falling behind.

And you believed them. Of course you believed them. You were eight.

You should know: you were not alone. Nearly one in eight American children now carries a diagnosis that says their brain doesn’t work properly. In some states, one in seven is on medication for it. But the youngest kids in any classroom — the ones born in August instead of September, who are simply younger than their peers by eleven months — are 34% more likely to get that label. Same brain. Different birthday. This clearly doesn’t reflect biology. It reflects a system that has a very narrow definition of “normal” and a very quick prescription for everyone who falls outside it.

You weren’t defective. None of us were. You were a child in a system that didn’t know what to do with the way you were made.

---[- - 

](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!-A6B!,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2F89bf8c0c-2fda-4a15-ae18-72d4fef7072a_1800x1200.jpeg)And then there were your parents.

This is the part that’s hard to talk about, because you love them. Or you want to love them. Or you did love them, once, before everything got complicated, before the distance set in, before you realized that the voice in your head that tells you you’re not good enough sounds a lot like one of theirs.

They didn’t mean to hurt you. Almost no parent means to. But they were tired too. They were swimming in the same current — working too much, worrying too much, carrying things they never learned to put down, because their parents never taught them how, because *their* parents didn’t either. The wound passes down. Not through cruelty. Through exhaustion. Through silence. Through the things that were never said at the dinner table because there was no dinner table, or because everyone was looking at a different screen, or because by the time your father sat down he had nothing left to give. If he was even there.

What you needed — what every child needs — was for someone to look you in the eye and say: *you are good. Not because of your grades. Not because of what you’ll become. You are good right now, as you are, because you exist.* You needed to be *seen*. Not evaluated. Not corrected. Not compared to your sister or your cousin or the kid down the street who made honor roll. Just seen.

And maybe that didn’t happen. Maybe what happened instead was the other thing — the subtle, constant signal that you weren’t quite right. Too loud. Too messy. Too sensitive. Too much. Or not enough — not focused enough, not disciplined enough, not successful enough to justify the space you took up in the world.

So you learned something that felt like survival but was actually a kind of slow poison: you learned to stop listening to yourself. You learned that your needs were inconvenient. That your feelings were too big. That your instincts about what you wanted — what you *really* wanted to do with your life — were impractical, irresponsible, childish. That the safest thing to do was to take the path someone else laid out for you and to bury the rest somewhere deep enough that it wouldn’t bother anyone.

You carried that into adulthood. Of course you did. Where else would it go?

---And now you’re here.

You’re at a desk, or behind a counter, or in a truck, or in a cubicle that smells like recycled air, and you are doing something that you are adequate at, maybe even good at, that has almost nothing to do with the thing that makes you come alive.

Maybe the thing is that you’ve always wanted to work with people who are struggling — really work with them, sit with them, build something that pulls them out of the hole — but you’re in marketing, because marketing pays the mortgage. And the mortgage exists because you bought the house you were supposed to buy, in the neighborhood you were supposed to live in, to project the life you were supposed to have.

Maybe you were the kid who took apart every appliance in the house and put it back together better. You had ideas — real ones, strange ones, the kind that wake you up at four in the morning — but somewhere between the idea and the world, there was a system. The academy wanted you to publish, not invent. The tech company wanted you to ship features on a sprint cycle, not spend a year on something that might not work. The investors wanted hockey-stick growth curves, not the slow, weird, unmarketable thing you actually believe in. So the ideas are still in your notebook. The notebook is in a drawer. The drawer hasn’t been opened in three years.

Maybe you were supposed to teach. Not the way schools teach — standing in front of rows, delivering content, preparing kids for tests — but really teach. Movement, art, music, the thing your body knows how to do that lights up every room you’re in when you let yourself do it. You were supposed to be a dance teacher. Or a woodworker. Or the person who runs the community garden. Or the one who sits with the elderly and listens — actually listens — because you have a gift for making people feel less alone. But you’re filing expense reports. Or answering phones. Or managing a team you didn’t ask to manage, building something you don’t believe in, because when you were twenty-two someone told you: *that’s not a real job. That won’t pay the bills. Be practical.* And you listened. Because you’d been listening to voices like that your whole life.

And now it’s Tuesday afternoon, and the thing you were supposed to do with your life is so far away you can’t even see it anymore. And the distance between who you are and who you’re pretending to be is the exact size and shape of your depression.

And your other struggles — the ones you’re ashamed of, the ones you don’t tell anyone about — those have the same shape too.

The overeating. The late-night scrolling that turns into something darker. The pornography. The extra drink that became the extra three. The weed. The shopping. The gambling. The whatever-it-is that you reach for at the end of the day when the emptiness gets loud enough that you need to drown it out.

You think that’s weakness. You think that’s a character flaw. You think: if I were stronger, more disciplined, a better person, I wouldn’t need this.

But you’re wrong. You’re not weak. You’re *starving*.

Not for food. Not for pleasure. Not for the thing you keep reaching for. You’re starving for the life you’re not living. The soul is hungry, and you’re feeding it substitutes — because the real meal, the thing that would actually satisfy you, feels impossible. So you eat instead. Or drink. Or scroll. Or numb. Not because you’re broken, but because a person who is spending all day doing something that has nothing to do with their purpose will come home with a void that demands to be filled. And if the real thing isn’t available — if you’ve locked it in a drawer and lost the key — the void will accept *anything*. Sugar. Pixels. Chemicals. Anything that provides thirty seconds of relief from the knowledge, buried but never quite gone, that you are wasting your life.

And this — this is the part where most people misunderstand what God has to say about it.

You’ve been told, probably, that religion’s view on sex and pleasure and indulgence is just: *don’t.* That it’s about restriction, control, suppression. That God is the great prohibitor, standing over you with a list of things you’re not allowed to enjoy, making life smaller and harder for reasons that seem arbitrary and punishing.

What if He’s saying something different: *My son, I put distractions in the world. Powerful ones. Beautiful ones. I made the substitutes feel real — because the test had to be real. And when you reach for them — the food you don’t need, the sex that leaves you emptier than before, the screen that promises connection and delivers nothing — I’m not angry at you. I’m not disgusted. I’m watching you from above and I’m trying to make you hear, as gently as I can: that’s not it. That’s not what you’re hungry for. You’re reaching in the wrong direction.*

That’s not oppression. That’s the most loving thing anyone has ever said to you.

The true meaning behind restraint — around food, around sex, around consumption — is not making your life smaller. It’s about keeping you from filling the hole with things that don’t fit, so that the hole stays open long enough for you to find the thing that does. That hunger is sacred. That ache is sacred. It is your soul telling you you haven’t found your purpose yet. And every substitute you pour into that space is just another layer of soundproofing between you and the voice that’s trying to guide you home.

You’re not lazy. You’re misaligned. A car with the parking brake on isn’t slow because the engine is weak. It’s slow because something is fighting against its own motion. Release the brake and the same engine that seemed broken is suddenly, effortlessly, powerful.

You know this is true because you’ve seen the proof in your own life. When you’re doing the thing — the real thing, the thing that lights you up — you don’t need the extra food. You forget to eat. You don’t reach for the drink. You don’t open the tab you’re ashamed of. Not because you’re exercising willpower. Because the hunger is *gone*. The void that demanded to be filled has been filled by the only thing that was ever meant to fill it: the purpose you were avoiding.

This is not a willpower problem. It is a direction problem. And it will not be solved by discipline, or diets, or accountability apps, or twelve-step programs — not ultimately. Those might manage the symptoms. But the symptom will keep finding new forms until the root is addressed. And the root is that you are not doing what you came here to do.

You should know: you’re not imagining this. You might think, “I’m the only one who doesn’t make it”. A Harvard study found that 58% of young adults say their lives lack meaning or purpose. Half. More than half. Those are just the ones admitting it. Incomes tripled over seven decades. But happiness stayed behind, even moving backwards. The U.S. Surgeon General declared loneliness a national epidemic — half of all American adults experience it. Not because people don’t have company. Because they don’t have *resonance*. They’re surrounded by people and systems that have nothing to do with who they actually are.

---Here is the thing nobody tells you, and it changes everything:

You are not depressed because something is wrong with you. You are depressed because something is *right* with you, and it has no place to go.

There’s a concept in Jewish thought — ancient, radical, and almost unbearably tender — that before you were born, your soul was given a specific mission. Not a generic one. Not “be a good person” or “follow the rules.” A *specific* one. Something only you can do, in the way only you can do it, in the place only you can do it. The Talmud teaches that before a child is born, an angel teaches it the entire Torah — and then, just before birth, the angel strikes the child above the lip, and the child forgets everything (Niddah 30b). Not to punish. Not to make life harder. But because the purpose of life is the *remembering*. The slow, sometimes agonizing process of discovering what you already know — what you already are.

Your restlessness is not a disorder. It is the remembering trying to happen.

That pull you feel — toward the thing you keep dismissing as impractical, as irresponsible, as too late — that pull is not a fantasy. It is the closest thing you have to a compass. And every time you override it — every time you say *not now*, *I can’t afford to*, *who am I to* — you push the mission a little further underground, and the depression gets a little heavier, and the tiredness gets a little harder to explain.

The Hebrew word for “soul” — *neshamah* — shares a root with the word *neshimah*, “breath.” Your purpose is as close to you as your breathing. You don’t have to go find it on a mountaintop. You don’t have to earn it. You just have to stop holding your breath.

---The hints have been coming your whole life. You’ve been dismissing them because the culture taught you to.

The time you stayed up until two in the morning building something — not for money, not for a deadline, not because anyone asked you to — and you forgot to eat, and you weren’t tired, and the hours disappeared, and you felt more like yourself than you had in months. That wasn’t a hobby. That was the mission, leaking through.

The conversation where you said the thing you didn’t plan to say — the honest thing, the thing that came from somewhere deeper than your social script — and the other person looked at you and said *I’ve never heard anyone put it that way before.* That wasn’t luck. That was your specific gift, showing its face for a moment before you pushed it back down.

The job you didn’t take. The one that paid less, or made no sense on paper, or would have required you to explain yourself to people who would never understand. The one you still think about, years later, in the shower, on the highway, in the minutes before sleep. That thought is not nostalgia. It’s a signal that hasn’t stopped transmitting, because the mission doesn’t give up on you just because you gave up on it.

The dream you had as a kid — the ridiculous one, the one you stopped telling people about because they smiled in that way that meant *that’s cute, now let’s talk about something realistic* — that dream was not a child’s fantasy. Children are closer to their mission than adults. They haven’t yet learned to be embarrassed by it. The dream you had at eleven might have been the most honest thing you’ve ever thought.

These hints are not abstract. They’re not mystical experiences reserved for saints. Most of the time, they don’t feel like God talking. They feel like an itch. Like a pang. Like the inexplicable urge to volunteer at the shelter, or to write the essay, or to learn the instrument, or to call the old friend, or to quit the job. They feel like your life, trying to steer you toward itself.

And every time you feel that pull and do nothing — because the mortgage, because the kids’ tuition, because *what would people think* — a small light goes out. Not permanently. It can always be relit. But a little dimmer each time. And the accumulation of all those dimmed lights is what the world calls depression, and what the soul calls exile.

There’s a passage in the Talmud (Sotah 35b) that captures this with frightening precision. Uzzah tried reaching out to steady the Ark and got struck down in a threshing floor. The threshing floor was called two names; in the book of Samuel, it’s called *Nachon* and in Chronicles, it’s called *Kidon*. The Gemara asks the obvious question: which one was it? Rabbi Yochanan answers: both. First it was *Kidon* — a spear — because the place struck like a weapon. Then it was *Nachon* — established, correct — because the crisis forced David to stop and realize a profound truth about the ark.

The suffering is *Kidon*. It feels like a spear, killing you slowly. But it is also *Nachon* — the signal that something in your life is going awry. The Ark, the Gemara teaches, *carried those who carried it*. It didn’t need Uzzah’s help. It didn’t need a wagon. When David tried to move it the wrong way — to control it, to haul it like cargo on a cart — Uzzah got punished. Not because God was cruel. Because the Ark was not meant to be carried that way. David had to stop pushing and start listening. The oxen stumbled, the cart shook, Uzzah grabbed — and it cost him everything.

These are the signs in your life. When things get crazy and you try to push through — push yourself, push your career, push reality into cooperating — you get hurt. That’s because you are carrying the ark the wrong way. Your true role, the one you were made for, is the one where you don’t need to force things. The Ark carries you, not the other way around.

---Let’s go back to the marriage. Because this is where it all converges.

You came into the relationship already unable to say what you need. Not because you’re selfish — because you were trained, from childhood, to believe that your needs don’t matter. That expressing them is weakness or neediness or too much. So when your partner asks *what’s wrong?* you say *nothing*, because that’s what you’ve always said, because the one time you tried to open up it came out wrong, or it started a fight, or they looked at you in a way that reminded you of the look your mother or father gave you when you were too much.

And the person across from you — they came in with their own version of the same wound. Maybe they were the child who was too emotional, too needy, too dramatic. Maybe they learned to swallow their needs too, or maybe they learned the opposite — that the only way to be heard in a family that didn’t listen was to push harder, to escalate, to turn up the volume until someone paid attention. Either way, you’re standing on opposite sides of the kitchen counter, and the two of you are speaking different languages that both translate to the same thing: *I don’t feel safe enough to be fully honest with you.*

But here’s the thing that makes it worse, the thing nobody talks about: you’re not just hiding your feelings. You’re hiding your *life*. The real one — the one where you come home drained not because work is hard, but because work is *wrong*. The one where you’re spending ten hours a day building someone else’s dream while yours dies quietly in that drawer. And you can’t say that, because if you did, the next question would be *so what are you going to do about it?* — and you don’t have an answer, and the not-having-an-answer feels like failure, and the failure feels like danger, because if you’re not providing, not performing, not holding it together, then what are you?

So your partner gets the exhausted version of you. The version that has nothing left. And they think the problem is the marriage, or the problem is them, or the problem is that you don’t love them enough. But the real problem is that you are living someone else’s life, and it is consuming everything you have, and by the time you walk through that door you are a shell of the person they married, and neither of you knows how to say so.

The Talmud records a teaching from Rabbi Shimon bar Yochai: *“The bad culture within a person’s home is harder than the war of Gog and Magog”* (Berachot 7b). Gog and Magog — the great apocalyptic war at the end of days. And Rashbi says: what’s happening in your living room is harder.

Not because you’re failing. But because you’re fighting the hardest battle there is — trying to be human in a system that has no interest in your humanity — and nobody told you that’s what you were doing.

---Here is what the world won’t say, because it has no framework to say it:

You have a role. A specific, irreplaceable role that the world needs and that only you can fill.

Not “find your passion” — that’s a bumper sticker. Not “follow your bliss” — that’s a luxury for people without rent. Something much more serious than that. The Jewish tradition teaches that every soul is sent into the world with a *tikkun* — a specific repair that it is here to make. A hole in the fabric of reality that is exactly your shape. And when you are living in alignment with that purpose — even imperfectly, even partially, even just one hour a week in the margins of a life that is otherwise given over to survival — something inside you comes to rest. Not because the struggle ends. But because the struggle *means something*.

The man who is supposed to be building an organization that feeds the hungry but is stuck in a sales job he hates — his depression is not chemical. It is directional. It is his soul telling him: *not this way. Turn.*

The woman who was born to teach children through movement and music but is filing insurance claims because someone told her that art doesn’t pay — her anxiety is not a disorder. It is a compass, spinning wildly because she is walking away from north.

The kid who keeps dropping out of every framework — school, college, the entry-level job, the career track — not because he’s lazy, but because none of them fit, because he was built for something the system doesn’t have a box for — he is not a failure. He is an instrument that hasn’t found its orchestra yet.

And this is why the hints matter. They are not generic. They are *specific to you*. The thing that lights you up is not random. The cause that breaks your heart is not accidental. The skill that comes so naturally you don’t even think of it as a skill — the thing people say *how do you do that?* and you shrug because it’s like asking how you breathe — *that* is the thread. Pull it. Follow it. Not because it will make you rich or famous or even comfortable, but because it is *yours*, and every day you spend ignoring it is a day the world goes without something it was supposed to receive from you.

---And now maybe you see why the tiredness won’t go away.

It’s not that you’re overworked. You might be, but that’s not the root. It’s that the work you’re doing doesn’t belong to you. It’s that you’re spending your energy — your *life* energy, the finite, unrepeatable hours of your existence — on something that is not your mission. And the body knows. The soul knows. Even when the mind has been trained to say *this is fine*, the deeper part of you is keeping score. And the score is: another day spent building someone else’s world. Another day your world went unbuilt.

The ancient sages saw this coming, too.

Eighteen hundred years ago, the Mishnah at the end of Tractate Sotah described a generation where truth would vanish from public life, where the cost of living would crush people even amid abundance, where children would feel like strangers to their parents — not out of rebellion, but because the chain of love had been broken. They called this generation the *Ikveta deMeshicha* — the footsteps of the Messiah. The generation just before the world is redeemed.

They were describing ours.

Not to frighten you. To tell you: *you are not alone.* The disorientation you feel, the sense that the world has gone sideways, that the systems aren’t working, that something fundamental is broken — half the country feels it. Half the world feels it. And slowly, painfully, like a person waking from a long sleep, the civilization that built these systems is beginning to realize that what it constructed was never designed for the human soul. The data is everywhere — the depression, the loneliness, the collapsing trust — and it all points in the same direction. Not toward despair. Toward the possibility that we are, at last, ready to build something different.

And the Mishnah’s conclusion — after describing everything that would go wrong — is not a lament. It is an *etzah*. A piece of advice. The most practical thing anyone has ever said to a generation that has lost its footing:

*“Upon whom can we rely? Only upon our Father in Heaven.”*

Get still. Get honest. Listen for the voice underneath the noise. Everything you need to know is already being said — you just haven’t been quiet enough to hear it.

---You’ve been getting hints your whole life. Let me say that again, because it matters:

*Your whole life.*

Not just in the big spiritual moments. Not just in the dramatic crossroads. In the small, ordinary, easy-to-miss moments that you write off as coincidence or mood or random preference.

The article that appeared in your feed that described, with uncanny accuracy, the exact thing you’d been thinking about for months. The stranger who said something at a dinner party that landed in your chest like a stone and wouldn’t leave. The wrong turn that took you past the building where the thing you’ve been dreaming about is already happening — and you slowed down, and you looked, and you kept driving.

The friend who called out of nowhere, the week you were ready to quit, and said *I had this weird feeling I should check on you.* The book that fell off the shelf. The sentence in the paragraph that might as well have had your name on it.

These are not coincidences. The Hebrew tradition calls it *hashgacha pratit* — personal providence. Not the grand, dramatic kind, where seas part and mountains burn. The quiet kind. The kind that works through the ordinary fabric of a Tuesday afternoon. The kind that arranges a conversation, a headline, a memory, a pang of longing — and waits for you to notice.

Most of the time, you don’t notice. Not because you’re oblivious. Because the culture you grew up in has inoculated you against exactly this kind of knowing. It has one word for intuition, instinct, gut feeling, calling, divine nudge — it calls all of it “subjective” and moves on. It trusts data. It trusts credentials. It trusts what can be measured. And it has built a world so noisy, so overstimulated, so relentlessly full of content and opinion and notification, that the still small voice doesn’t stand a chance.

And maybe the religious people you’ve met haven’t helped. Maybe the version of faith you were shown — judgmental, performative, rigid, more interested in compliance than compassion — felt like just another system telling you you weren’t good enough. Another voice saying: *you’re doing it wrong.* If that’s been your experience, I understand why you stopped listening.

But consider the possibility that what you rejected wasn’t God. It was a distortion of God, presented to you by people who were themselves wounded, who inherited forms without spirit, who mistook obedience for connection. The spark inside you — the one that knew, even as a child, that something deeper was real — that spark wasn’t wrong. It was looking for something genuine. And it couldn’t find it in a framework that felt like another cage.

The voice hasn’t stopped. It never stops. It just gets quieter, and you have to get stiller.

The prophet Elijah — the toughest, fiercest prophet who ever lived, the one who stood alone against an entire kingdom — heard God not in the earthquake, not in the fire, not in the wind. He heard Him in the *kol d’mama daka* — the still, small voice (I Kings 19:12). And he heard it only after the noise stopped.

You’ve been running a long time. The noise has been loud a long time. But the voice is still there. And it is still saying your name. And it is still — with infinite patience, with a tenderness that would break you if you let it in — trying to tell you what you came here to do.

---Let’s come back to the practical, because this isn’t only about the soul. The soul lives in a body, in a house, in an economy. And the economy is hard.

Maybe you haven’t even allowed yourself to dream, but if you have, then you know the math. You’ve done it a hundred times. If I leave this job and try the thing — the real thing, the thing I was meant for — the numbers don’t work. The rent. The insurance. The kids’ school. The debt. The math doesn’t work.

And you’re not wrong. The math often doesn’t work, at least not immediately, at least not the way the world measures it. The cost of living is crushing — and it’s not your imagination. The Mishnah predicted even this: in the generation before the redemption, *“the vine will yield abundantly, yet wine will be expensive.”* Abundance everywhere, affordability nowhere. The median home now costs five times the median income. Seventy-five percent of American households can’t afford a median-priced new home. You’re not failing to manage your money. You’re caught in a system that was never designed for a person trying to live a meaningful life. It was designed for a person trying to service debt.

So I’m not going to tell you to quit your job tomorrow. That might be reckless. That might be irresponsible. What I *am* going to tell you is this: the thing you’re dismissing as impossible might just be missequenced. You don’t have to leap off the cliff. You have to turn toward the edge and look. Take one step this week. One. The phone call you’ve been avoiding. The conversation with your partner where you say, *I need to tell you something about what I actually want.* The hour on Saturday morning where you do the thing — just the thing, nothing else, no monetization plan, no five-year strategy — and see how it feels to be alive again.

Because here is what happens when you take even one step toward the mission: the tiredness changes. Not because you’re less busy. Sometimes you’re more busy. But the energy shifts. It comes from a different source. The Zohar calls it a light that enters when a person aligns with their purpose — not metaphorically, but as a felt experience. You’ve had it. You know exactly what I’m talking about. The night you stayed up until two working on the thing you love and woke up less tired than after eight hours of sleep following a day at the job you hate. That wasn’t adrenaline. That was alignment.

---But you’re scared. Of course you are.

The money isn’t there. The plan isn’t there. The people around you don’t understand. Your parents think you’re being irresponsible. Your friends think you’re having a midlife crisis. Your own mind — trained by decades of *be practical, be realistic, don’t be foolish* — is screaming that this is a terrible idea. And the voice of the mission, the one that whispers *this is what you’re supposed to do*, is so much quieter than the voice of fear.

So here is the most practical piece of advice I can give you. It doesn’t require money. It doesn’t require a plan. It doesn’t require a synagogue or a church or a mosque or a priest or a rabbi or a guru or anyone’s permission. It requires five minutes and a willingness to feel ridiculous.

Talk to God.

Not in a formal way. Not with the right words. Not from a book. In your own words. Out loud, if you can — in your car, on a walk, in the shower, lying in bed at three in the morning when you can’t sleep anyway. Just talk.

And if you’re thinking *I don’t know what to say* — good. That’s the right starting place. Because God doesn’t want your eloquence. He doesn’t want your performance. He has the angels for that. What He wants — the only thing He wants — is your honesty.

There’s a story told about a Breslover Hasid who wanted to teach his young children this practice. He promised them each a candy if they would sit alone for a few minutes and talk to God. The children agreed. But then they asked: *What should we say to Him?*

The father smiled and said: *Say, “God, when is this hitbodedut going to end so I can get my candy?”*

That’s it. That’s a real prayer. Not because it’s holy-sounding — because it’s *true*. The child who says *I don’t want to be here, I just want the candy* is more honest before God than the adult who recites beautiful words and means none of them. And God — if the tradition is right about who He is — would rather have the child’s bored honesty than the adult’s polished performance. Every time.

So if all you can say is *I don’t know if You’re real, and I feel stupid doing this* — say that. If all you can say is *I’m angry at You for making my life this hard* — say that. If all you can say is *I don’t have words* — say that, and then sit in the silence, and see what happens. There is no wrong way to do this. There is only the doing.

And if you want words — if it helps to have a place to start — here are some. Not because they’re the right ones, but because sometimes you need someone to go first:

Say: *I think You gave me something to do, and I don’t know how to do it. I’m scared. The money doesn’t make sense. The people in my life don’t get it. I don’t even know if I’m right about what the mission is. But something in me won’t let go of it. And I’m asking You — if this is real, if this is what You put me here for — give me the courage to move toward it. Give me the wisdom to handle all the other parts of my life that don’t disappear just because I’ve found my purpose. The mortgage doesn’t disappear. The kids still need shoes. Give me a plan. Not the whole plan — just the next step. Show me the next step and give me the guts to take it.*

Say: *Let me trust myself. Let me trust that the instinct I’ve been burying since I was eight years old is not a fantasy — it’s the homing signal You planted in me. Let me trust that the longing is not weakness. That it’s the compass. That You made me this way on purpose.*

Say: *And the people who don’t believe in me — the ones who tell me I’m dreaming, who say it’ll never work, who look at me like I’m losing my mind — let me understand that they’re not the enemy. They’re scared too. They’re living inside the same system I’m trying to step out of, and my stepping out threatens everything they’ve built their lives around. Let me love them and not need their permission. Let me understand that the resistance, the disbelief, the hardship — it’s not proof that I’m wrong. It’s the test. It’s the part where I prove — to myself, to You, to the mission — that I mean it. That I’m not going to fold the first time it gets hard. Because it will get hard. And that I need to be the kind of person who does it anyway.*

That’s it. That’s the whole practice.

You don’t have to be religious. You don’t have to believe in any denomination or system or institution. You don’t have to set foot in a building. You just have to be willing to speak — honestly, clumsily, with no performance and no script — to the One who gave you the mission. And then listen. Not for a thunderclap. For the next hint. The nudge. The door that opens a crack. The conversation that lands differently than you expected. The idea that arrives in the shower.

The Hebrew tradition calls this *hitbodedut* — literally, “self-seclusion.” It’s the practice of speaking to God in your own language, from your own heart, about your own life. No liturgy. No intermediary. Just you and the Source, in a room, being honest. And the remarkable thing — the thing that no one can explain to you but that you will recognize the moment it happens — is that the conversation changes things. Not always dramatically. Sometimes it’s just that the fear loosens its grip by five percent. Sometimes it’s that the next step, which was invisible an hour ago, is suddenly obvious. Sometimes it’s that you feel, for the first time in years, that you’re not doing this alone.

Because you’re not. You never were.

---The prophet Malachi — the last prophet, the one who spoke the final words before the long silence — saw this. His closing prophecy, the last line of all prophecy in the Hebrew Bible, was not about nations or armies. It was about fathers and children:

*“And he will turn the hearts of fathers to their children, and the hearts of children to their fathers.”* (Malachi 3:24)

The turning of a heart. That’s it. That’s what stands between civilization and ruin. A parent deciding to put down the mask, sit on the floor next to their kid, and say: *tell me about your day. I’m listening. I’m not going anywhere.*

And maybe — maybe that parent can only do this after they’ve started to turn toward their own truth. Maybe the reason they couldn’t be present before is that they were too busy performing someone else’s life. And maybe the first act of turning — the turn that makes all the other turns possible — is the one where they stop pretending and start, terrified, to pursue the thing they were actually made for.

And here is what happens next — the part nobody tells you, because the culture can’t see it:

The parent who learns that they don’t have to perform — that they can be awkward, uncertain, weird, that they can stumble toward their purpose without having it all figured out — will let their child be that way too. The father won’t need the boy to be impressive, because he’s stopped needing to be impressive himself. The mother won’t need the girl to follow a safe path, because she’s learned, in her own bones, that the safe path was the dangerous one all along. Neither will look at their child’s strange obsession, impractical dream, way of being that doesn’t fit any box — and try to fix it. They’ll recognize it. They’ll see the mission in the child the way they finally learned to see it in themselves.

And something else happens — something quieter, and maybe more important than all of it:

They believe in the child. Not as a decision. Not as a parenting strategy. As a natural consequence of believing in themselves. A parent who knows — really knows, in their body, not just in their head — that *they* were placed here with a purpose, that *their* life matters, that the Creator doesn’t make accidents — that parent looks at their child and sees the same thing. Of *course* this boy has a mission. Of *course* this girl was made for something. How could it be otherwise? If I was worth creating, so was she. If my strange, impractical, doesn’t-fit-the-box purpose is real, then so is his.

And the child *feels* this. Children don’t listen to speeches about self-worth. They don’t absorb affirmations from a poster on a classroom wall. What they absorb is the thing the parent actually believes, transmitted through a thousand invisible signals — the tone of voice, the quality of attention, the look in the eye when the child says something odd or unexpected. A parent who secretly believes their own life is meaningless will radiate that, no matter how many times they tell the child *you can be anything you want.* But a parent who has found their own purpose — or is honestly, visibly, imperfectly fighting their way toward it — radiates something else entirely. The child doesn’t hear *you have a mission.* The child feels it. The way you feel warmth from a fire without anyone explaining combustion.

And the child who grows up with a parent like that — a parent who says, not in a speech but in the way they live, *you don’t have to be what the world expects; you have to be what you are* — that child will not spend thirty years burying the thing they were made for. That child will not learn that their instincts are wrong, that their longings are impractical, that the deepest parts of themselves are too much for the people who love them. That child will grow up knowing — not as an idea, but as a felt, bodily truth — that they are allowed to exist as themselves. That being strange is not a flaw. That the thing that makes them different might be the thing that makes them irreplaceable.

This is how the wound stops passing down. Not through better parenting techniques. Not through books or programs. Through a parent who has done the terrifying work of becoming real, and who offers that realness to their child as the greatest inheritance they will ever receive.

Remember the eight-year-old at the beginning of this essay? The one looking out the window, searching for something real? Imagine if the person sitting across from that child had said, not *pay attention*, but: *What are you looking at? Tell me. I want to see what you see.*

Everything would have been different. And it still can be — for the child sitting across from *you*.

---There is a teaching — it comes from the Zohar, the great mystical text — that the darkest moment of the night occurs immediately before dawn (Zohar II:46a). Not as a metaphor for optimism. As a description of how reality works. The depth of the darkness is itself the signal.

If that’s true, then the exhaustion you feel, the confusion, the sense that everything is wrong — that is not the end of the story. It is the last chapter before a new one begins.

But the new chapter doesn’t begin with the world changing. It begins with you.

It begins with one honest conversation. One step toward the thing you’ve been avoiding. One evening where you turn off the noise and sit with the silence long enough to hear what it’s been trying to tell you.

Maybe it’s been trying to tell you to start the organization. Maybe it’s been trying to tell you to leave the firm and teach. Maybe it’s been trying to tell you to pick up the instrument, or call the old friend, or write the thing, or move to the town, or forgive the person, or open the drawer where the notebook has been waiting.

Maybe it’s been trying to tell you that you’re not lost. You’re on the way home. And the fact that you feel lost — that the world feels wrong, that the answers you were given don’t match the questions you carry — is not evidence that you’re broken. It is evidence that you’re awake. In a world that is sleepwalking, the person who says *something is missing* is the one who is closest to finding it.

---There’s a passage in the Mishnah — the same one that describes all the signs of this era — and at the very end, after listing everything that will go wrong, it says:

*“V’al mi yesh lanu l’hisha’en? Al Avinu sheba’Shamayim.”*

*“Upon whom can we rely? Only upon our Father in Heaven.”*

Read that carefully. It is not saying: everything is lost. It is saying: *stop looking for permission from the world.* The school *won’t* see you. The system *won’t* validate you. The culture won’t hand you your purpose on a plate. Stop waiting for it. The only One you can trust with your mission is *you*, and the One who gave it to you.

Rabbi Nachman of Breslov taught: *“Echad haya Avraham”* — Abraham was one. One person, standing against an entire world. Everyone thought he was insane. He did it anyway. Not because he had a support system. Not because the numbers made sense. Because he heard the voice and he trusted it.

That is what’s being asked of you. Not to have it figured out. Not to have a plan. To hear the voice — your voice, God’s voice, they are closer than you think — and to trust it more than you trust the world that told you to sit down, get practical, and stop looking out that window.

That is the sound you aren’t hearing.

Or maybe — maybe you’ve been hearing it all along.
