# The only answer

*Field of Dreams (1989). A man (Kevin Costner) hears a voice no one else hears. It tells him to plough his cornfield and build a baseball diamond.She found the video at 1 a.m., the way you find everything at 1 a.m. — by not looking for it.*

She’d been scrolling for an hour, maybe two. First the news, which was unbearable. Then Instagram, which was worse — everyone performing their lives in high definition while hers felt like it was being shot on a broken phone. Then YouTube, where the algorithm fed her a man with a beard talking about faith.

She almost scrolled past. She was not religious. She was not even sure she was spiritual, whatever that meant. She was thirty-six, divorced, raising a boy who had stopped talking to her in full sentences sometime around his thirteenth birthday.

But something made her stop. Not what he was saying. How he was saying it. Like he meant it. Like he wasn’t performing. Like he was sitting across from her in her kitchen, not on a stage, not behind a microphone, not selling anything.

He said: *When everyone sounds right, what is left?*

She replayed it. She didn’t know why.

---His name was Rashid and he drove a cab in Manchester and he had opinions about everything.

He said: “My wife wants me to see a therapist. You know what a therapist costs? A hundred and twenty quid an hour. You know what I make in an hour? Eleven quid on a good day. So I need to drive for eleven hours to pay for one hour of someone asking me how I feel. I know how I feel. I feel like I’m driving a cab.”

His passenger, a quiet woman in her fifties who hadn’t said a word since Piccadilly, said: “So why does your wife want you to go?”

“Because I yell.”

“At her?”

“At everyone. At the news. At the other drivers. At my kids when they don’t listen. At myself, mostly, but she doesn’t know that part.”

The woman looked out the window. Then she said: “My husband used to yell. Then he stopped. That was worse.”

Rashid glanced at her in the mirror.

“When he yelled, at least I knew he was still in there,” she said. “When he stopped, it was like the house was full and empty at the same time.”

They drove in silence for a while. Then Rashid said, very quietly: “I don’t know what to do with myself. I used to know. I used to pray and it meant something. Now I say the words and I hear myself saying the words and there’s nothing behind them.”

“Maybe stop talking,” she said.

“What?”

“To Him. Maybe stop talking and listen.”

---The boy’s name was Nathan and he was fifteen and he knew everything.

He knew that religion was a control mechanism. He knew that capitalism was a scam. He knew that AI was going to replace every job his teachers were preparing him for, which made school a particularly elaborate form of torture. He knew that his mother watched videos of bearded men on her phone at night because he could hear it through the wall, and he knew this was embarrassing.

He knew everything except what to do with the feeling he got at three in the morning when the house was dark and his phone was dead and there was nothing between him and the silence.

He’d asked ChatGPT about it once. “I feel empty at night. What is this?” The answer was thorough. It mentioned existential anxiety, circadian rhythm disruption, adolescent identity formation, and the importance of sleep hygiene. It suggested journaling. It suggested meditation. It suggested speaking with a trusted adult.

It was the most useless thing he had ever read.

---Rashid tried what that passenger offered him. He stopped talking. He stopped praying for a week. He just — sat. In the morning, before anyone else was up, he sat in the kitchen and didn’t say anything. He was so used to the five prayers a day, but he decided that if it’s not working, he has to try something else. At last it’s cheaper than a therapist, he thought to himself.

Nothing happened for two weeks. But he stayed.

And one morning, The silence stopped being uncomfortable and started being something else. A memory surfaced. He was nine years old, drawing on his grandfather’s wall. Not scribbling. Drawing. A door, with light coming through it. His grandfather had painted over it the same afternoon. But Rashid remembered the feeling in his hand — the sureness of it, the way the pencil knew where to go before his mind did. That feeling had been asleep for thirty years.

The next morning he picked up a pencil and drew on the back of a receipt. A door. A terrible door. He drew it again on another receipt. Then on the back of his daughter’s homework, then on actual paper that he went out and bought during his lunch break. It was the first time in fifteen years he had bought something that wasn’t food, petrol, or something for the kids.

He didn’t quit driving. He couldn’t afford to quit. But he started painting at night after his kids went to sleep. Old things. Found things. A shutter he pulled off a skip on the high street. A chair leg. A piece of plywood that had been a shelf. He painted them colours he didn’t know he remembered — blues and oranges and a green that was the colour of the inside of a leaf.

His wife Amina watched him for a week without saying anything. Then she said: “You stopped yelling.”

He hadn’t noticed. But she was right. He was still driving a cab. He was still broke. But at night he was painting, and the painting was so obviously, stupidly, clearly what he was supposed to have been doing all along that he couldn’t believe he had missed it for thirty years. It had been sitting inside him like a door someone had painted over and he had never scraped back.

---Nathan found the video too. The same one. In some mysterious way, the algorithm decided to offer him the same video.

He watched it out of boredom. Then he watched it again out of something else.

The man was saying: *The professor and the dropout have the same search bar. So who do you trust?*

Nathan paused. He had never once, in fifteen years, thought about who he trusted. He’d thought about what was true, what was provable, what was logical. Trust was a different question. Trust was not about information. Trust was about — what? He didn’t have the word.

He went to his mother’s room. She was awake, phone in hand, the man’s voice still playing from the small speaker.

“You’re listening to this too?” he said.

She looked startled. Then embarrassed. Then something else — hopeful, maybe, in the way she used to look before the divorce, before the full-time job, before she forgot how.

“Nathan,” she said. “Are you mad at something?”

He sat down. “Mum, why did you leave Dad?”

She went still. He had never asked her this. In three years, not once. He had resented her for it, defended his father in arguments that were really about his own confusion, blamed her silently the way children do when the world cracks and someone has to be at fault.

She said: “I just knew.”

“That’s not an answer.”

“I know it’s not. But it’s the truth. Every person I asked told me something different. The solicitor said stay. My mother said go. My friend Helen said wait. I even asked an AI. I typed the whole situation into ChatGPT at four in the morning like I was confessing to a machine.”

“What did it say?”

“It said I should consider my values, assess the relationship dynamics, and consult a licensed professional. It said both options had merit.”

“Both options had merit.”

“Both options had merit.” She was quiet. Then she said: “Nathan, the smartest system in the world told me both options had merit. And maybe it was right. On paper, maybe both options had merit. But I knew. Not in my head — my head was a mess. My head said stay, my head said go, my head said both options had merit. But somewhere underneath all of that, I knew that my life was supposed to go a different way. Not because your father was bad. He wasn’t bad. But something in me knew — that this was not where I was meant to stay. That there was something I was supposed to become, and I couldn’t become it there.

Nathan paused for a while. He thought about that bearded man. He thought about trust. “You know Mum”, he finally said, “I think I understand.”

His mum smiled, surprised at his answer.

---She got into his cab on a Wednesday afternoon. Piccadilly to Didsbury. It was raining the way it always rains in Manchester — not dramatically, just persistently, like the sky had made a commitment.

Her name was Claire. His name was Rashid. She had a boy at home who was starting to talk to her again. He had a garage full of painted shutters that nobody had bought yet. Neither of them knew any of this about the other. She saw a Pakistani man behind the wheel. He saw a tired white woman with shopping bags. The city moved past the windows, grey and wet and ordinary.

She was on the phone when she got in, finishing a conversation with her mother. “I know, Mum. I know. But I can’t explain it any better than that. I just knew it was the right thing. No, I didn’t have — Mum, I have to go.” She hung up and exhaled.

Rashid glanced in the mirror. “Mothers,” he said.

She almost laughed. “She’s been asking me the same question for three years. Why did I leave my husband. I give her the same answer every time and she never accepts it.”

“What’s the answer?”

She looked up, surprised. She knew Pakistani people might look at her decision as another one of the British people’s weaknesses. That divorce was too easy in their culture. But she felt she’d answer anyway.

“That I just knew,” she finally said. “That I couldn’t explain it. That every expert, every friend, every — I even asked a computer, you know. An AI. It told me both options had merit.”

Rashid was quiet for a moment. Then he said decisively: “Both options had merit!”

“You’re laughing at me.”

“I’m not. I asked the same thing. Not about a marriage. About —” He stopped. He didn’t talk about this. Not to passengers. Not to anyone except Amina. But something about the rain, or the way she’d said *I just knew*, or the fact that she would be out of his cab in twelve minutes and he would never see her again — something made him continue. “I was driving this cab for fifteen years. Fifteen years. And I hated it. I didn’t know I hated it because I didn’t know there was something else. I thought this was it. You grow up, you get a job, you drive. My father had the shop. I had the cab. That’s how it works.”

“What changed?”

“I stopped praying. I started listening. I’m not hippie you know. But after two weeks of sitting there, I remembered something. I used to draw when I was nine, in my grandfather’s house in Lahore. I drew a door on his wall — a door with light coming through it. He painted over it the same day. And I forgot about it for thirty years.”

Claire was looking at him now. Not at her phone. Not out the window. At him.

“Now I paint at night,” he said. “After the kids sleep. Old things. Furniture off the street. Nobody buys them. My wife says *who buys a painted shutter?*“ He almost smiled. “She’s probably right. But it’s mine. It’s the first thing in my life that’s mine.”

The cab was stopped at a light. Rain on the windscreen. The wipers going back and forth.

Claire said: “Everyone told me I was mad to leave. My mother. My solicitor. My friends. On paper, it made no sense. He wasn’t cruel. He wasn’t unfaithful. He was — fine. Everything was fine. And I left anyway.”

Rashid turned around and looked at her. A Pakistani cab driver and a white British divorcée, sitting in traffic on the A34 in the rain, and for a moment neither of them was what the other one would have expected.

“That’s it,” he said. “That’s the thing.”

“What thing?”

“The knowing. Before you can explain it. Do you have a word for that in English?”.

“Well. You could say intuition. Or an inner feeling. But I don’t think we have a word for it really. It’s funny, my mum just asked me the same thing. I told her I just knew. She said that’s not an answer.”

“It’s the only answer.”

The light changed. He turned back and drove. “Your shutters. The ones nobody buys.”

“What about them?” Rashid asked.

“What colour are they?”

“Blue, mostly. And orange. And a green that — this is going to sound mad — a green I remember being the colour of the inside of a leaf. From when I was nine.”

“It doesn’t sound mad.”

“It does a bit.”

“Well, not as mad as leaving my husband for no apparent reason.”

He laughed. The cab pulled up to her street.

She paid. She opened the door. Then she stopped and said: “The shutters. Bring one to the farmers’ market on Lapwing Lane. Saturdays. My neighbour has a stall there. She sells — I don’t know, candles, things. She’d make space for you. People in Didsbury buy that sort of thing.”

“You think?”

She nodded.

She got out. He watched her walk to her door, shopping bags in one hand, phone in the other, a woman who looked from the outside like every other tired woman on every other Wednesday in the rain. And he thought: she left her whole life. And I’m leaving mine.

He drove home. He didn’t yell at a single driver the whole way.
