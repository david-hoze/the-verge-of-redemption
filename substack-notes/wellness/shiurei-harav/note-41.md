# The Jewel Overboard

A man was digging mud for a living when he found a diamond worth more than everything he'd earned in his life. Nobody in his town had that kind of money. They told him only buyers in London could afford it. So he went to the port - back then there were no tickets, you just showed up and found a captain willing to take you. He found one. The captain asked what business he had in London. The man showed him the diamond. The captain said: come aboard, you'll pay me when you sell it.

The captain was glad to have him. Ship captains are lonely men. Most of them drink because there's nothing else to do - the crew can't read, the sea doesn't talk back. But here was a man with a beard and bright eyes who could hold a conversation. So every day the captain came down to the man's cabin to talk. They became something like friends.

The man had a habit. During meals he would take the diamond out and set it on the table in front of him. Not to show it off - because looking at it made him happy. And he believed you should eat in joy, that food digested better when you were happy, and turned to poison when you ate in sadness. So he placed the diamond where he could see it and ate his meals in peace.

One night while the man slept, a servant came to clear the table. He gathered the tablecloth with the scraps and the crumbs and the diamond still sitting on it, and threw the whole thing over the railing into the sea.

The man woke up and understood exactly what had happened. And he understood something else: the diamond was his passage. Without it he had no money, no way to pay the captain, no reason to be on that ship. He was traveling for free now - and there is no such thing as traveling for free. The captain came to his cabin every single day. If the captain saw grief on his face, saw that the diamond was gone, he would send the man after it. That's how it worked. You lost the thing that bought your ticket, you went overboard after it.

So the man went to the mirror. He looked at his own face - the face of a man who had just lost everything - and he practiced smiling. He kept practicing until the smile stopped looking like a lie. Then he walked out and met the captain the way he always did. Calm. Happy. As if the diamond were still sitting on his table. He did this every day for the rest of the voyage.

That was how he survived. Not by retrieving what he lost. Not by finding another diamond. By refusing to let the loss show on his face - because the grief would have killed him faster than the loss itself. Sometimes the only thing keeping you alive is your ability to carry a catastrophe without wearing it. Not because it doesn't matter. Because the world doesn't give you time to fall apart.
