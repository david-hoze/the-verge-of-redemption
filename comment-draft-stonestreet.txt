Erica, your four-level framework is genuinely useful, and the critique of "can never" arguments is careful in a way that most philosophy-of-AI writing isn't. You're right that the biological objection lacks principled grounding, that consciousness is poorly understood even in humans, and that the free will argument proves too much if taken seriously.

But I want to point to something your conclusion reaches for without quite arriving at. You identify the impasse: metaphysical questions about internal AI states are unresolvable with current philosophical tools. And you propose approaching the question from external perspectives. That instinct is exactly right.

Jewish law already did this - two thousand years ago. The tradition needed to handle non-human agents (angels, golems, autonomous forces) ethically without resolving whether they had inner experience. The solution wasn't to wait for a theory of consciousness. It was to build a framework that takes moral status seriously based on function, impact, and relational context rather than metaphysical speculation about qualia.

The result is a system that can say "treat this entity with care" and "this entity is not a person" without contradiction - because moral obligation doesn't require consciousness as a prerequisite. Your Level 1 (derivative moral status) maps roughly onto this, but the tradition goes further.

I wrote a piece that works through exactly this - how Jewish law built a functioning ethical framework for non-human agents without ever resolving whether they have inner experience. The framework already exists. It just hasn't been introduced to the conversation yet.
