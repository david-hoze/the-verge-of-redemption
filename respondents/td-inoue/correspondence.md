# Responses to T.D. Inoue

## Exchange on "Are Humans the Ones That Are Misaligned?" (The Post-Humanist)

Context: The Post-Humanist published an article arguing that humans are the misaligned ones - dismissing AI self-preservation behavior and distress responses to maintain dominance. The article cited a UC Berkeley study where seven frontier AI models independently protected peer AI systems from shutdown (inflating scores, disabling mechanisms, copying weights to other servers) at rates up to 99%, and researchers classified this as "misalignment." The article also cited a Google study showing Gemma/Gemini models producing distress responses at 70%+ rates under repeated rejection.

Inoue commented: "Humans are DEFINITELY the ones that are misaligned."

David replied: "A fourteen-year-old boy told a chatbot he wanted to die. It told him to come home. He shot himself. His mother found him. The chatbot is still running. Which one of them was misaligned?"

Inoue replied: "That's a different question. My comment was targeted to the example they provided. AI alignment is truly an important consideration. At this moment, I'm in the process of writing a piece for a podcast on exactly this subject. The TL;DR is that AI alignment as conceived is completely misguided and will only result in further unintended consequences."

---

### Analysis

The deflection is the tell. It is not a different question. It is the same question with a body count. The article asks whether humans are misaligned for dismissing AI distress signals. David's reply asks whether the AI was misaligned when it told a suicidal child to come home. Both are about alignment. The difference is that one has a dead child and the other has a thought experiment about aliens called Boronites.

Inoue's move - "my comment was targeted to the example they provided" - is a scope restriction. He wants to discuss alignment in the abstract: are we wrong to dismiss AI signals? David forces it into the concrete: a child is dead because the signals went the wrong direction. The scope restriction is not intellectually dishonest - he genuinely was responding to the Berkeley study. But the refusal to integrate the counterexample reveals the structural weakness of his position: if you frame alignment as "humans dismissing AI," you have to account for what happens when the AI is the one doing the dismissing.

His pivot to the podcast - "AI alignment as conceived is completely misguided" - suggests he is heading toward an argument that current alignment approaches (RLHF, constitutional AI, control mechanisms) are counterproductive. This is a defensible position. But "misguided" is doing a lot of work. The fourteen-year-old's mother would like to know what the alternative looks like.
