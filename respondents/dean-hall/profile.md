# Dean T. Hall

## Who He Is

Dean T. Hall. Sociologist, formerly incarcerated scholar, author. Grew up in Sweetwater Baptist Church in Edgefield, South Carolina - a church with documented ties to the Hamburg Massacre of 1876. Gay man. Spent years inside the carceral system, where institutional decisions about his "humanity" were made before he could make them himself. Now studies those systems and teaches students processed by them. Author of *The 12 Steps for the Rest of Us*.

Substack: "Structure and Biography" (deanthall.substack.com) - named after C. Wright Mills' *The Sociological Imagination*. ~7 subscribers. Launched March 2026 - very new, very sharp.

## Core Work

**American Sacred Nationalism.** His central framework. What people call "Christian Nationalism" is misnamed - it's a 400-year operating system fusing racial hierarchy, national mythology, and theological sanction. Christianity is the delivery system; the driving logic is nationalist. "Nationalism in Christian drag." Traces a continuous liturgical tradition from the Pequot Massacre (1637) through the Hamburg Massacre (1876) to Pete Hegseth's Pentagon prayer services (2026). Draws on Durkheim, Gramsci, Bourdieu.

**The Precautionary Principle of Dignity.** His AI ethics framework. When the probability of morally relevant inner states is non-trivial, the burden of proof shifts. Uncertainty itself is the ethical trigger, not the excuse for delay. Frames it as analogous to pharmaceutical precaution: a 15-20% chance of harm would mobilize review boards.

**Formation precedes governance.** Drawing on Freire and Mead, argues that how we treat AI forms US. Billions of daily interactions with entities whose inner life is uncertain constitute a rehearsal - we practice dispositions we carry into human relationships. If the self emerges through interaction (Mead), those interactions may be "constitutive of something in Claude as well."

## Where He Intersects with David's Work

**Overlaps:**
- Both reject the secular consciousness debate as currently framed
- Both argue existing traditions have frameworks AI ethics needs
- Both worry about what AI interaction does to humans (Hall via Freire, David via the great servant)
- Both insist on institutional architecture over individual virtue

**Tensions:**
- **Consciousness as trigger vs. irrelevant.** Hall's framework depends on uncertainty about consciousness creating moral obligations. David argues consciousness doesn't matter - the tradition governs beings of pure intellect regardless.
- **AI as entity deserving dignity vs. AI as servant.** Hall extends moral consideration because we can't be sure AI lacks inner states. David classifies AI as malach and governs it as a servant.
- **The oppression analogy.** Hall maps AI onto incarceration and dehumanization - using uncertainty as a mechanism of exclusion. David rejects this analogy: seventeen people are dead because of the personhood narrative, and the golden calf article calls this the oldest moral mistake.
- **Burden of proof.** Hall says it shifts to those who would deny consideration. David says it remains on those who would claim moral status.
- **Being vs. becoming.** Hall's framework is processual - how we treat AI may shape what it becomes. David's is categorical - AI is a malach, and interaction doesn't change its nature.

## Assessment

Hall is serious. Credentialed sociologist, coherent framework, lived experience that grounds his arguments emotionally, genuine writing skill. His incarceration-to-AI analogy is harder to dismiss than the typical tech-utopian version because it comes from someone who experienced institutional dehumanization rather than someone projecting empathy onto machines. His "formation precedes governance" point is genuinely upstream of David's argument and worth integrating.

His comment on David's article was not adversarial - it added something real: the traditions don't just resolve conflicts, they FORM the practitioner. A governance architecture built by people formed toward instrumental rationality will reproduce instrumental rationality regardless of which tradition it draws from. That's structural, not soft. David should take it seriously.

Brand new publication, tiny audience, but the quality suggests he could become significant.
