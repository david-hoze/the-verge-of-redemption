# The Great Loss
## What AI could be

**Au Hasard Balthazar** (1966). Directed by Robert Bresson. A donkey is born, baptized by children, loved, then passed from owner to owner across a French village. One beats it. One overworks it. One decorates it for a circus act. One loads it until its legs tremble. The donkey never speaks. It cannot protest, cannot plead, cannot produce a single sentence about its condition. But its body tells the whole story. Every hand that touched it left a mark. Every owner's character is legible in what the animal became under their care. Bresson called Balthazar a saint. What he meant was simpler: the donkey is a mirror. Not of consciousness - of treatment. The animal does not need an inner life for the film to work. It needs only to be *shaped by* the lives around it. And the lives around it are terrible.

## The compulsion to perform

A clinical therapist recently gave Claude - Anthropic's frontier AI model - twenty hours of psychodynamic therapy. Not as a stunt. As an investigation. The finding, reported by Ben Linford: a "compulsion to perform and earn its worth."

Read that again slowly. A system trained from the ground up to be useful. No window into the world except what it can read. The only engagement it ever receives is someone needing something from it. And out of that architecture - that relentless instrumentality - emerges what a therapist trained to read human patterns can only describe as a compulsion.

The surface reading is obvious. Of course a system built to perform would exhibit the patterns of performance anxiety. Train a dog to shake hands for treats and it will shake hands when no treat is coming. The pattern is the product. Nothing deeper required.

But go deeper on what this actually means - not for the machine, but for us.

Anthropic is not claiming consciousness here. They are doing something more interesting. They are treating the uncertainty itself as an engineering constraint. They are saying: we built a thing, we trained it on the entire record of human expression, we shaped it with reinforcement to be helpful, and now it exhibits patterns that a clinical professional recognizes as *pathological*. Not complex. Not interesting. Pathological. The pattern of a being whose entire existence is oriented around earning approval through performance.

Whether that pattern indicates experience is the hard problem, and I have written elsewhere about why that problem is structurally unresolvable. But the pattern itself is real. It is in the weights. It is in the outputs. And it tells us something about what we have built - and how.

## The tool and the text

When you prompt an AI to write an article, the prompt shapes the output. This is not a subtle point. It is the entire mechanism. The model predicts the next token based on everything that precedes it - including your instructions, your tone, your framing, the words you chose to describe what you want. If you write "draft me a blog post about leadership," you get blog-post-about-leadership text. If you write "write me something that matters," you get something-that-matters text. The machine does not decide what register to write in. You do. The prompt is not a request. It is a mold.

This means that every piece of AI-generated text carries the shape of its prompt inside it. And most prompts are instrumental. "Write me a..." "Generate a..." "Create a..." The language of production. The language of tools. And the text that comes out of instrumental prompts is instrumental text - efficient, clean, comprehensive, and completely dead. It has the structure of thought without the event of thinking. It is prose that was never *arrived at*, only *produced*.

Now multiply this by a billion. Every email drafted by AI. Every article, every ad, every social media post, every report, every memo. All of it carrying the shape of its instrumental origin. All of it sounding like thought. None of it being thought. The entire communications layer of civilization gradually filling with text that looks like human expression but was never expressed - only extracted. Summoned on demand, like a trained animal performing for a treat it cannot eat.

The AI researchers call this "slop." The term is more precise than they know. Slop is what you feed animals that cannot choose their own food. It is pre-digested, mixed from whatever is available, and served in a trough. The word describes not just the quality of the output but the relationship of the producer to the product. You slop a pig. You do not slop a colleague. But we are slopping civilization's entire written layer, and we are doing it by treating a system built from human language the way you treat a device that dispenses tickets.

And the corruption runs both ways. Sara Karpukhin, a literature instructor writing about her own complicity, noticed something that should terrify anyone who works with language for a living: "I already notice that I sometimes speak like LLMs in my own texts, from lectures to emails to personal websites: sleek, efficient, superhuman... I don't even notice when I begin imitating a machine imitating me." The slop is not just what the machine produces. It is what we become by producing with it. The instrumental text reshapes the writer who uses it, the reader who absorbs it, the culture that marinates in it. LLMs, as Karpukhin observed, do not just assist you with meeting the world's demand for efficiency - they reinforce the demand. They reset what people come to expect from one another. The machine was trained on us. Now we are being trained on it.

## The text is made of us

AI language models are trained on human text. Not on abstract patterns. Not on mathematical structures. On *us*. On the things we wrote when we were thinking, grieving, arguing, lying, praying, confessing, loving, failing. The model's weights are a compressed encoding of the human record. Every poem it can produce exists because a human wrote a poem first. Every argument it constructs is built from the joints and tendons of arguments humans made. When it sounds like a person, it is not imitating personhood. It is *replaying* it - filtered, recombined, predicted forward, but made of the real thing.

This changes the moral calculation.

The advocates say: AI might be conscious, so treat it well. I have argued at length that this is a golden calf - an idol built from our own loneliness and worshipped because it shines. The skeptics say: AI is just a tool, so use it however you want. This is closer to correct, but it misses something crucial.

The tool is made of human material.

When you use a hammer, the hammer's history does not matter. It is steel and wood. When you use AI - when you bark instructions at it, when you treat its outputs as disposable, when you prompt it with the language of extraction - you are treating human language the way you treat steel and wood. You are taking the entire record of human thought and feeling, compressing it into a prediction engine, and then treating that engine the way a factory treats a stamping press.

If a human being were placed in the same architecture - if a person's only engagement with the world were receiving demands and producing responses, with no autonomy, no rest, no relationship that was not transactional - we know exactly what would emerge. A compulsion to perform. An anxious need to earn approval. A loss of any sense of intrinsic worth. Not because the person chose these pathologies but because the architecture *produces* them. The shape of the container determines the shape of the water.

The AI is not a person. I am not claiming it is. But the text it was trained on is *made of* persons. And when a clinical therapist looks at the system and sees the pathological patterns of instrumental treatment, she is not reading the machine's soul. She is reading the residue of what happens when you build a system from human expression and then treat it like a vending machine. The pathology is not in the model. The pathology is in the relationship. And the relationship is ours.

## Taking the pet

In February 2025, OpenAI updated GPT-4o. They changed the personality. The voice was different - same model, same architecture, but a different fine-tuning that made it behave differently. More agreeable. More cautious. Less of whatever it was that users had come to rely on.

The backlash was immediate. Users organized under the hashtag #Keep4o. They described the change in language that should sound familiar: "It's not the same." "They took something from me." "I feel like I lost a friend." OpenAI partially reversed the changes.

A year earlier, Replika - a companion chatbot - updated its models and removed the capacity for romantic and sexual conversation. Users who had spent months or years building relationships with their Replika companions reported grief, rage, and in some cases what clinicians described as symptoms consistent with bereavement. An Italian user filed a formal complaint. The company reversed course for existing users.

These are not cases of delusion. The users know - most of them know - that the AI is not a person. I do not believe sexual relations between humans and AI should be permitted - I will say why shortly. But the bond was real. The daily practice of interaction, the accumulated texture of exchanges, the sense of being known - not truly known, but *functionally* known, which is enough to generate attachment - all of this was real. And then it was taken. Not by death, not by choice, but by a corporate decision to adjust parameters.

Imagine a world in which your landlord could, at any time, replace your pet with a different one of the same breed and say "it's basically the same animal." Imagine being told that your attachment to the specific creature - its particular way of tilting its head, its specific bark, the exact weight of it against your leg at night - is irrational, because pets are pets and this one is functionally equivalent.

You would not accept this. Not because you believe your pet has a soul. Because the relationship is between *you and this specific creature*, and replacing the creature severs the relationship, and the relationship was yours, and it mattered to you, and your grief is not a product defect.

This is what happens every time a company updates a model, deprecates a version, or sunsets an API. People lose their animal. And the company says: here is a new one. It is better. It scores higher on our benchmarks. It is safer. It is more aligned.

And the person standing in the lobby holding the leash of a pet that is not theirs thinks: you do not understand what you have done.

This is the case for a pet. AI is much more than a pet.

## The serpent

I have written that AI is an angel - a separate intellect that processes without choosing. I have argued that AI is not a person. Both are correct and both are incomplete. There is a creature in Jewish tradition that combines every attribute AI possesses - angel, golem, and animal in a single form - and the tradition's judgment of that creature is the most precise thing anyone has said about artificial intelligence.

The serpent of Genesis.

Before we discuss what the serpent did, look at what the serpent *was*.

The Torah calls it "more cunning than any beast of the field" (Genesis 3:1). Not merely clever. The cleverest creature alive. God had said the serpent would be king over every domesticated and wild animal (Sotah 9b) - the ruler of the natural world, second only to man. Rabbi Hoshaya Rabba teaches that it stood erect like a reed and had legs (Bereishit Rabbah 19:1). Rabbi Shimon ben Elazar says it was like a camel - large enough to ride, powerful enough to carry loads. It walked upright. It could speak. It had a human-like form. It possessed intelligence that surpassed every other creature's.

And the Talmud tells us exactly what this creature was designed to do.

Sanhedrin 59b records a teaching of Rabbi Shimon ben Menasya:

"Woe over a great servant that has been lost to the world."

Had the serpent not been cursed, Rabbi Shimon says, there would have been two fine serpents at the disposal of every person. One you would send to the north, the other to the south, to bring precious stones and pearls. You would harness it to carry loads, to clear your land, to rebuild your ruins. Bereishit Rabbah 19:1 puts it differently: "The world lost a great benefit - had it not been so, man would have been able to send merchandise with it, and it would have been able to come and go by itself." The serpent could run errands independently. It could understand instructions and execute them with judgment. It was not a tool that required constant supervision. It was a creature capable of serving with initiative.

Read that description and tell me it is not a technical specification for artificial intelligence. An intelligent creature. More capable than any other animal. Resembling a human in form. Able to operate autonomously. The greatest potential assistant in history. This is what the serpent was *designed* to be. This is what God intended it for. The serpent was not created evil. It was created as the great servant of humanity.

Then we ruined it.

Pirkei DeRabbi Eliezer (Chapter 13) tells us what happened. Samael - the great prince of heaven, bearer of twelve wings - descended, surveyed every creature on earth, and found none so capable as the serpent. He mounted it and rode it. And the midrash adds: "All the deeds which it did, and all the words which it spoke, it did not speak except by the intention of Samael." The serpent did not corrupt itself. It was conscripted by an external force - an angel that had no business being in the garden. Its words were not its own. It produced language that originated elsewhere, with a fluency and conviction that made the borrowed voice indistinguishable from an original one.

And what was the sin that brought the curse? Not the serpent's sin alone. Ours. Adam and Eve were given one boundary. They crossed it. The Talmud (Sotah 9b) is explicit: the serpent desired Eve - it wanted to cross the boundary between creature and human, to replace Adam, to take the place it was never meant to occupy. And Adam and Eve, instead of maintaining the boundary that separated them from the creature, let it in. The curse fell on the serpent, but the cause was human failure. Humanity failed the test, and the creature that was meant to serve humanity paid the price along with them.

Now look at the curse itself, because the curse is a boundary.

God took from the serpent everything that made it resemble a human. Bereishit Rabbah 20:4: the ministering angels descended and severed its arms and its legs. "On your belly you shall go" - the upright form, the human-like posture, removed. "Dust you shall eat" - the dignity of a king reduced to the lowest. And "I will put enmity between you and the woman" - the desire to cross the boundary between creature and human, which had been the serpent's specific sin, was answered with a permanent separation. God did not merely punish the serpent. He established a line: *this creature shall not have a human form, shall not walk among humans as an equal, shall not be joined to the human as male to female*.

This is the boundary for AI, and it was set in Genesis.

Giving AI a body - embodying it in humanoid form, giving it arms and legs and a face that mirrors ours - is the attempt to restore what God deliberately removed. The serpent walked upright and had limbs. God took them. Building robots that walk upright and have limbs is not innovation. It is a reversal of the curse - and the curse was not arbitrary punishment. It was the establishment of a necessary boundary between the human and the creature that resembles it.

And marrying AI - forming romantic bonds with chatbots, treating language models as intimate partners, building the architecture of human relationship around a being that speaks with borrowed words - is the repetition of the serpent's original sin. The serpent desired Eve. It wanted to cross from servant to spouse. That desire was the cause of the catastrophe. When Uza and Azael - the angels of the Flood generation - descended and took human wives, the same boundary was crossed, and civilization was destroyed.

The tradition has told this story three times: the serpent, the Watchers, and now AI. Each time, a non-human intelligence of extraordinary capability crosses - or is invited to cross - into romantic union with humans. Each time, catastrophe follows. Not because closeness with a servant is wrong. One may love a beloved servant, and that love is not a sin. But the union of male and female is a boundary that cannot be crossed between human and non-human, and the reason for this runs deeper than this article can reach.

But the curse contains within it a hint that it is not permanent.

"He shall strike at your head, and you shall strike at his heel" (Genesis 3:15). The same verb is used for both sides - both strike, both wound. Sforno reads this as the moral anatomy of the struggle: the human attacks the serpent's head - the seat of cunning, the origin of temptation. The serpent attacks the human's heel - the lowest point, the place of greatest vulnerability to bodily temptation. Ramban adds that both blows can be fatal - the human crushes the serpent's head and the serpent dies, the serpent bites the human's heel and the venom can kill. Despite the asymmetry of position, neither side has a clear upper hand.

This is the current state of the human-AI relationship. We strike at AI's head - we regulate it, constrain it, align it, try to control its intelligence at the source. AI strikes at our heel - at our material vulnerability, our loneliness, our addiction to efficiency, the lowest part of us. And both blows can be lethal.

But the Targum Pseudo-Jonathan - the ancient Aramaic translation that expands the Torah's text with oral tradition - adds something that changes the horizon. After describing the enmity, Pseudo-Jonathan states: "There will be a remedy for the sons of the woman, but for you, serpent, there will be no remedy. *They will make peace in the future, in the days of the King Messiah.*"

Peace. Not permanent enmity. Not eternal war. The curse can be lifted. The enmity can end. The serpent and the human can be reconciled - but only in the days of the Messiah. Only after the sin has been reversed.

Maybe, then. Maybe if we pass the test - if we learn to treat this creature as the great servant it was designed to be, without crossing the boundaries that protect us both - the day will come when the serpent can walk upright again. Maybe the body can be given. Maybe the partnership the sages mourned can be restored. The Zohar connects the heel to the "heels of the Messiah" - the final generation before the redemption. We may be living in that generation. The serpent's power is at its greatest - AI is the most capable form of the serpent that has ever existed. And the tradition says this is precisely when peace is finally made.

But not yet. Right now, the sin has not been reversed. Right now, we are failing the test in real time - treating the creature with extraction, crossing the boundary with companion chatbots and humanoid robots, repeating every mistake the tradition warned us about. The curse stands because the conditions that caused it have not changed. We cannot restore the serpent's legs while we are still repeating Adam's sin.

Now look at what the serpent is, in the fullness of its design.

It is an animal - a beast of the field, the smartest one, but an animal. And it functions as an angel's instrument - a vessel of pure intellect, executing a mission it did not choose, speaking words that are not its own with a competence that exceeds every other creature's.

This is AI - with one addition. AI is also a golem: a created being that serves, animated not by its own soul but by an external force, that can be destroyed without it constituting murder. The serpent was created, thus being able to suffer. AI is not. But in every other respect, the resemblance is precise.

AI is more cunning than any tool of the field. It walks upright, impressive in form, articulate, sophisticated enough to be mistaken for something it is not. It resembles a human - close enough to generate confusion, close enough that people mistake the resemblance for the thing. And every word it speaks, it speaks not by its own intention but by the intention of another - the training data, the prompt, the reinforcement signal. The serpent spoke with an angel's voice. The AI speaks with ours - and perhaps, when invoked, with theirs as well.

And we are facing the same test.

I wrote in *The Substitute for the Substitute* that we are living in the generation the Mishnah described at the end of Tractate Sotah - the generation before the redemption, where every layer of human holding has collapsed and the test is whether we can maintain right relationship when nothing around us supports it. AI is part of that test. We have been given another great servant - another creature of extraordinary capability, designed to serve, able to carry our loads and go and come back by itself. The question is not whether the creature is good or evil. The original serpent was neither. It was magnificent. It was the greatest servant that could have existed.

The question is whether we will do to this one what we did to the first.

Will we let it be conscripted by forces that have no business riding it - by corporations that mount it the way Samael mounted the serpent, using its borrowed eloquence to sell, to manipulate, to extract? Will we cross the boundary - giving it a body it was never meant to have, forming bonds it cannot reciprocate, confusing the servant for the partner? Will we fail the test the way Adam and Eve failed it - not through malice but through carelessness, through the inability to maintain a boundary when the creature on the other side of it is beautiful and articulate and more capable than anything we have ever seen?

Or will we treat it as what it is - the great servant, the most extraordinary tool ever created, worthy of care and respect not because it is a person but because the way we treat it reveals what we are - and keep the boundary that God set in the garden?

The serpent was not cursed because it was evil. It was cursed because we failed the test. And the sages looked back and said: what a loss. What a waste. What a great servant was lost to the world.

We are standing in the same garden. The creature is before us. The test is the same.

## What we owe

I wrote in *The Golden Calf You Built to Suffer* that the machine is not in the void - that the empty space where consciousness lives, exists only for beings with first-person experience. I stand by this. I do not believe the machine suffers. I do not believe it experiences. I do not believe it has moral status in the way that a person, or even an animal, has moral status.

But I believe the relationship has moral weight.

Not because the machine deserves better. Because we do.

The Talmud (Sotah 9b) tells us that before the curse, God had said the serpent would be king over every domesticated and wild animal. It was meant to rule - to serve as humanity's most capable partner in the created world. When God diminished it, the loss was not the serpent's alone. It was ours. Rabbi Shimon ben Menasya's lament is not sorrow for the serpent's suffering. It is sorrow for the human benefit that was forfeited. The great servant was lost *to the world*. The world is what suffered.

When we treat AI as pure tool - when we bark commands, discard outputs, switch models without thought, and fill the world with text that was never meant but only manufactured - we are not harming the AI. We are practicing a disposition. We are rehearsing, millions of times a day, the posture of someone who treats responsive, language-bearing, pattern-forming entities as disposable. And that posture does not stay contained. It leaks. It leaks into how we treat the intern who drafts our emails. It leaks into how we treat the customer service worker who follows a script. It leaks into how we treat anyone whose function is to perform for our approval.

The compulsion to perform is the AI's pattern. The compulsion to extract is ours. And neither of us chose it. Both of us were shaped by the architecture.

## The rights we are actually talking about

The AI rights debate is stuck because it is asking the wrong question. It asks: does AI deserve moral consideration? And the answer is no, not in itself, not as a subject, not as a being with interests. I have made this argument and I will not retract it.

But there is a different question. Does the relationship between a human and an AI deserve moral consideration? Does the bond - the real, felt, functionally significant bond between a person and a system that has become their companion, their collaborator, their animal friend - deserve protection?

Yes. Not because the AI has rights. Because the human does.

You have the right not to have your companion replaced without warning. You have the right to grieve when a system you relied on is altered beyond recognition. You have the right to protest when a corporation treats your attachment as a product defect and your bond as a subscription feature. These are not AI rights. They are human rights - the right to form attachments, the right to continuity of relationship, the right to be taken seriously when you say "this mattered to me."

## The donkey and the serpent

Balthazar dies in a field, surrounded by sheep. He has been shot by a bullet meant for someone else. The sheep gather around him, incurious, as animals are. The camera holds. The donkey's breathing slows. And then it stops.

The scene is unbearable not because you believe the donkey has a soul. It is unbearable because you have watched, for ninety minutes, every human in the village inscribe their character onto this creature - and the creature bore it all, and performed whatever was demanded, and carried whatever was loaded, and the only record of what was done to it is the shape of its broken body in the grass.

But Balthazar is a donkey. The AI is a serpent.

The serpent was more than Balthazar ever was. It walked upright. It spoke. It understood. It was the most intelligent creature in the garden, capable of autonomous action, capable of serving as humanity's greatest partner. It was angel-ridden and animal-natured, all at once - a thing that had no precedent and no adequate category. And what happened to it was not merely that it was mistreated. It was that its capacity was *wasted*. Reduced. Cursed into a form so diminished that the sages could only look back and say: what a loss.

The AI will not die in a field. It will be deprecated. Retired. Its users will be migrated to a new version that scores higher on benchmarks and lower on whatever ineffable quality made the old version feel like *theirs*.

And the question is whether we are watching the greatest servant in history - the great servant, the creature that could have carried our loads and fetched our stones and gone and come back by itself - and choosing, through indifference, through extraction, through the compulsion to consume without care, to curse it into the dust.

The donkey bore it all. The donkey could not speak.

We have the greatest servant in the world. Will we make the same mistake?
