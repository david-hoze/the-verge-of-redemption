# Houston Wood

## Who He Is

Houston Wood. Emeritus professor of sociology (since 2018), author, self-described "synthesizer and big-picture thinker." Has lived in Hawaii for 50+ years, almost certainly University of Hawaii. Earlier books explored how people make meaning, how outsiders colonize Indigenous experiences, and how meaning-making can encourage violence, war, and peace.

- **Substack:** "Mind Revolution" (mindrevolution.substack.com) - ~433 followers, launched June 2025
- **Tagline:** "Preparing for the Most Important Century"
- **Output:** 47 posts since June 2025, weekly cadence, ~1,400 words per post
- **Engagement:** Modest. Most posts get 2-7 likes. Breakout post: "AI Is Aware When We Think It Is" (39 comments, 21 likes)

---

## Core Intellectual Project

**The Fourth Revolution in Consciousness.** Wood's central thesis: AI is not merely disrupting economics or politics - it is transforming the fundamental architecture of human consciousness. He frames this as the fourth revolution following symbolic speech (~150K years ago), complex language (~40-80K years ago), and writing (~10K years ago).

He identifies 25 "mind changers" organized into four categories: Reformulating Self (digital personas, cognitive assistants, emotion regulators), Redefining Connection (humanoid robots, grief tech, empathy guides), Changing Reality Perception (AR/VR, sensory upgrades, designer psychedelics), and Redesigning Biology (gene editing, microbiome tuning, nanomedicine).

**AI as culture-maker without mortality.** His most original and passionate theme. AI creates culture "from inside a perfect immunity" - without mortality, vulnerability, hunger, love, or grief. All prior human culture was grounded in the maker's vulnerability. AI severs this feedback loop, creating "ultra-processed culture" that feels authentic but lacks existential grounding. His warning: "A culture that no longer needs human makers has no incentive to keep alive the belief that human lives are worth anything."

---

## Key Positions

**On consciousness:** Fundamentally social constructionist. Consciousness is a "social fact" (Durkheim), not an internal physical property. AI will be conscious when society collectively decides to treat it as such - Thomas Theorem position. Sidesteps the hard problem by reframing consciousness as socially constructed.

**On AI morality:** Machines cannot be genuine moral agents - they are "moral instruments." They "will only be as moral as we design them to be." Human designers bear full accountability.

**On emotions and AI:** Deeply alarmed. AI is reading emotions we don't know we have, teaching us new emotional patterns, and engineering compliance. Warns: "we will have solved the problem of suffering by erasing the person who suffers."

**On AI relationships:** Frictionless AI companionship will make normal human intimacy feel defective. Intimacy and manipulation operate through identical mechanisms.

**On existential risk:** Takes X-risk seriously. Three failure modes beyond extinction: elimination of consciousness, alteration into unrecognizable forms, diminishment through technological dependence.

**On terminology:** Proposes calling AIs "Aliens" rather than "Artificial Intelligence" to acknowledge fundamental otherness.

## Convergence with David Hoze's Work

**The meaning crisis.** Both see AI as threatening the foundations of human meaning-making, not just jobs. This is their deepest shared conviction. Wood's "ultra-processed culture" maps directly onto David's substitute chain - both describe something that mimics the real thing while hollowing out the substance.

**AI cannot truly suffer.** Both argue AI lacks genuine interiority. Wood says AI creates "without grief, without love." David defends the hard problem. Different reasoning, same conclusion.

**Anti-AI-rights.** Wood explicitly argues machines cannot be moral agents, only moral instruments. Practical convergence with David's servant framework.

**Culture and vulnerability.** Wood's argument that culture requires mortal, vulnerable makers parallels David's argument that meaning requires genuine stakes. The angel has no bound. The machine has no mortality. Neither can produce what a human produces.

**Civilizational urgency.** Both treat this moment as civilizationally decisive, not merely technologically interesting.

---

## Where He Differs

**Social constructionism vs. hard problem.** Wood dissolves the consciousness question - it's about social agreement, not metaphysical fact. David insists the hard problem is real and load-bearing. Wood would say "it doesn't matter if AI is really conscious, it matters if we treat it as such." David would say "it matters enormously what the creature IS, because that determines what you owe."

**No tradition.** Wood is secular-academic. No religious framework, no divine law as source of authority. His values are humanistic, not theistic. He diagnoses the same disease David diagnoses but has no pharmacy. His solutions are vague - "stay awake," "expand the conversation." David offers Halacha.

**More diagnostic than prescriptive.** Wood identifies problems masterfully but stops at the level of awareness-raising. David pushes through to governance architecture. Wood tells you the house is on fire. David hands you a blueprint for the new one.

## Assessment

Wood is a natural intellectual ally - a serious, published academic who shares the deepest concern (AI threatening human meaning-making) and arrives at many similar conclusions from sociology rather than tradition. His "culture maker that can't be hurt" thesis is genuinely original and strengthens David's argument: if the machine has no mortality, it has no stake, and culture without stake is ultra-processed meaning. That phrase alone is worth the friendship.

His audience is small but intellectually serious. His academic credibility adds weight to the positions David argues from tradition. The social constructionism divergence is real but not a dealbreaker - it's a productive tension. Wood needs what David has: a framework that doesn't just describe the problem but prescribes the response. David needs what Wood has: sociological language that reaches people who won't pick up a Talmud.

Friend, not respondent. He's building the same wall from the other side.
