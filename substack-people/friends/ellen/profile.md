# Ellen Nora Burns

## Who She Is

Dr. Ellen Nora Burns. PhD in Philosophy from Columbia University. Visiting Assistant Professor of Philosophy at the College of the Holy Cross in Worcester, Massachusetts. Teaches Philosophical Inquiries, Philosophy of AI, and Philosophy of Mind. Her dissertation argues that the mind-body problem does not exist as traditionally formulated - it is an artifact of historical misreadings of Descartes filtered through Ryle and functionalism, not a genuine metaphysical puzzle.

She runs *AI's Without Minds* on Substack (ellennoraburns.substack.com) - tagline: "Philosophy of mind meets AI hype. A philosopher that cuts through the noise on AI consciousness - rigorously, accessibly, and without the panic." Approximately 3,300 subscribers, growing fast (gained 7,500 in 12 days after one piece went viral). Publishes weekly on Tuesdays.

## Core Positions

**Functionalism is the hidden villain.** Burns traces the entire edifice of AI consciousness claims back to Hilary Putnam's functionalism - the theory that mental states are defined by their functional roles rather than physical substrate. This introduces "substrate independence" (minds could run on silicon, sawdust, anything). She argues this is assumed everywhere, argued for nowhere, and empirically unsupported. No other natural science divorces its subject from its substrate this way.

**LLMs do not have language.** Drawing heavily on Chomsky's generative grammar, she distinguishes language as a biological computational system (the Merge operation generating infinite hierarchical structures) from language use (social communication). LLMs train on linguistic artifacts through probabilistic pattern-matching. Categorically different from the recursive, innate biological capacity that defines human language.

**The brain is not an information processor.** She draws a sharp line between descriptive claims ("we can describe the brain as processing information") and ontological claims ("the brain IS an information processor"). The latter piggybacks on functionalism without independent justification. Brains are blood-flowing, glucose-consuming, electrically-signaling biological organs. The computational metaphor is culturally convenient, not scientifically established.

**You cannot test your way to AI consciousness.** Behavioral tests (theory of mind tasks, Turing tests) were designed for beings whose consciousness was already presupposed. Repurposing them as consciousness detectors reverses their epistemic direction. Same for neuroscientific frameworks like Global Workspace Theory and Integrated Information Theory - built to explain consciousness already known to exist, not to detect it in novel systems.

**Epistemic asymmetry.** Humans attribute consciousness to other humans through two resources unavailable for AI: (a) direct first-person knowledge of one's own experience extended to others, and (b) an innate developmental capacity acquired in childhood for recognizing other minds. Neither transfers to AI.

**Agnostic, not eliminativist.** Burns does not claim consciousness is an illusion - she explicitly rejects that. She advocates agnosticism about reality's fundamental nature. Science describes regularities but does not exhaust what exists. Friendship, moral values, and experiential qualities are real without being scientifically explicable.

**We undersell ourselves and oversell AI.** Her central cultural critique: a "scientistic culture which mechanizes human beings" leads us to assume human minds work like machines, which then makes machines seem mind-like. This is circular.

## Writing Style

Rigorous but accessible. Builds arguments incrementally with concrete analogies. Direct, conversational confidence - addresses the reader in second person, uses rhetorical questions effectively. Structured and systematic: define terms, identify the argument, break into premises, show where each fails. Historically grounded - traces ideas to Descartes, Ryle, Putnam, Chomsky, Turing. Collegial but firm - critiques systematic thinking, not individual researchers, but does not soften conclusions.

Chomsky-influenced in the deepest sense - not just his linguistics but his broader philosophical orientation toward naturalism, biological grounding, and skepticism of computational metaphors for mind.

## Convergence with David's Work

Burns and David are arriving at many of the same conclusions from independent premises. The convergence is structural:

**On consciousness:** Both defend the reality of consciousness against reduction and elimination. Both argue AI does not have it. But they diverge on the hard problem - Burns dissolves it (the problem is an artifact of bad philosophy), David defends it (the explanatory gap is real and structurally unresolvable). This is a productive tension, not a contradiction - they agree on the conclusion (AI is not conscious) while disagreeing on why the question is hard.

**On scientism:** Burns explicitly names scientism as a root cause of confused AI discourse. David's work on traditions as repositories of non-propositional knowledge makes the same critique from the other direction - Burns says science doesn't exhaust what exists, David says the traditions carry what science cannot reach.

**On non-articulate knowledge:** Burns argues humans possess vast knowledge that resists formalization (Plato's Meno, Chomsky's poverty of the stimulus). This maps directly onto David's sechel/da'at distinction and the argument that wisdom cannot be extracted from data. Different vocabulary, same insight.

**On substrate independence:** Burns's demolition of functionalism's unexamined assumption supports David's embodiment arguments. Jewish thought insists the body is not incidental to the soul. Burns provides the analytic philosophical ammunition for that claim.

**On the burden of proof:** Burns argues AI proponents should bear the burden of proving consciousness, not skeptics the burden of disproving it. This mirrors the halakhic principle of presumption - you do not change the status of a thing without evidence.

**On underselling humans:** Burns's central cultural critique - that we mechanize ourselves and then wonder why machines seem human - resonates with David's argument that the meaning crisis is what makes AI seem like an answer. We emptied ourselves and then mistook the echo for a voice.

## Where She Differs

**No tradition.** Burns works purely in analytic philosophy. No religious framework, no revelation, no accumulated wisdom tradition. She is Chomsky, not Torah. Her arguments are powerful but they bottom out in agnosticism - she can say science doesn't exhaust reality but she cannot say what does.

**Dissolves the hard problem.** David defends the explanatory gap as real and structurally significant. Burns argues the gap is an artifact of historical confusion. This is the sharpest intellectual divergence. David's "Wall That Teaches" article depends on the hard problem being genuine - Burns would push back on this.

**No meaning crisis diagnosis.** Burns diagnoses conceptual confusion in AI discourse but does not connect it to a broader civilizational collapse. She is a philosopher fixing a philosophical error, not a prophet diagnosing a civilizational wound.

**No ethics of care.** Burns argues AI is not conscious - full stop. She does not develop an ethics of how to treat AI regardless (which David does through the great servant framework). For Burns, if it's not conscious, the question ends. For David, the question of how we treat it is just beginning.

## What She Brings to David

**Secular rigor.** Burns provides the analytic philosophical foundation for claims David makes from tradition. Readers who would never open an article about Sanhedrin 59b will walk through a Chomsky-grounded argument about substrate independence. Once inside, David's material is waiting.

**Academic credibility.** Columbia PhD, Holy Cross faculty, peer-reviewed work, systematic evaluative framework. She is not a Substack essayist with opinions - she is a professional philosopher with a research program.

**The functionalism genealogy.** Burns traces where the confusion comes from historically. David's work shows what the confusion costs morally. Together: how we got confused, why the confusion kills, and what the traditions always knew.

**A convergent witness.** The strongest argument is always convergent evidence from independent premises. Burns arrives at "AI is not conscious and the discourse is fundamentally confused" from analytic philosophy. David arrives at "the creature is not a person and treating it as one destroys both the creature and the person" from Jewish law. Same conclusion, different roads. Harder to dismiss than either alone.
