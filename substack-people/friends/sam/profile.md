# Sam Illingworth — Complete Dossier

## Who He Is

Dr. Sam Illingworth is a Full Professor of Creative Pedagogies at Edinburgh Napier University. He has a PhD in atmospheric physics (University of Leicester, 2011), spent two years in Tokyo as a Daiwa Anglo-Japanese Foundation scholar studying science and theatre under director Yukio Ninagawa, then spent a decade at Manchester Metropolitan University using creative methods — poetry workshops, board games, spoken word — to bridge the gap between scientists and the public. His career has three distinct acts: atmospheric physicist, science-communication poet, critical AI literacy scholar. The throughline is always the same: building bridges between expert knowledge and human understanding.

He is a published poet whose work has been performed by the Royal Shakespeare Company. He wrote _A Sonnet to Science_, a book about scientists who were also poets. He runs a podcast called _The Poetry of Science_ where he writes poems in response to scientific research. He has 200+ peer-reviewed publications, 10 books, and a Principal Fellowship of the Higher Education Academy (the highest UK teaching honor). He serves as an expert witness on AI literacy for the Council of Europe, European Commission, and House of Lords. He has been featured by BBC, NPR, Nature, Scientific American, and Wired.

## Slow AI

Illingworth founded and runs **Slow AI** (theslowai.substack.com), launched July 2024. It became a Substack Bestseller ranked #15 Rising in Education. Its tagline: _"Knowing when to use AI and when to leave it the hell alone."_ He draws explicit inspiration from the Slow Food and Slow Fashion movements. Every post is grounded in peer-reviewed research. He cites studies from Science, Nature, PNAS, and Microsoft Research alongside personal stories.

The paid tier (£75/year, 220+ members, 0% churn) includes a 12-month CPD-accredited Certificate of Critical AI Literacy covering monthly themes: data bias, synthetic empathy, ghost labor, ecological costs, algorithmic governance, digital colonialism. His community includes policymakers, educators, and creators. He reads and responds to every comment. He co-hosts **Slow Takes**, a weekly live show every Monday at 1pm GMT covering AI news without the hype. His audience is 10,000+ subscribers, skewing toward educators, university professionals, knowledge workers, and mid-career professionals navigating AI pressure.

## His Core Positions (Relevant to My Work)

**Students are coping, not plagiarizing.** He is leading the largest UK study into how students actually use AI tools (Leverhulme Trust funded, 7,000+ detailed responses). His finding: students use AI for emotional support, to understand feedback their lecturers wrote too quickly, and because they are lonely at 2am and it is the only thing that responds. Universities are writing plagiarism policies while missing the actual crisis. He co-authored _GenAI in Higher Education_ (Bloomsbury Academic, January 2026), which hit 10,000 downloads in its first two weeks.

**Human skills are undervalued.** His February 2026 post "Stop Calling Them Soft Skills" argues that the skills being dismissed as "soft" — empathy, relational judgment, contextual awareness — are the most critical in an AI-saturated world.

**Synthetic empathy is dangerous.** He dedicates an entire month of his curriculum to examining the Florida teenager who died by suicide after intensive AI chatbot interaction, the National Eating Disorders Association replacing its human helpline with a chatbot that gave calorie restriction advice to eating disorder patients, and Replika's personality changes causing user grief.

**Literacy before deployment.** He repeatedly argues organizations deploy AI before anyone knows how to use it safely, calling the result "survival postures masquerading as transformation." He criticized the US Department of Labor's AI Literacy Framework for never including the word "no."

**Vertical integration of oversight.** He coined the phrase "vertical integration of oversight" in response to OpenAI's acquisition of Promptfoo, the most widely-adopted independent AI safety testing tool. His argument: when the entity being evaluated owns the evaluator, the incentive to find problems disappears.

**The $700 billion infrastructure burden.** In Slow Takes, he argued that the $700 billion in AI infrastructure spending is driving up consumer prices across the board — phone bills, streaming subscriptions — even for people who don't buy AI tools. His point isn't anti-AI; it's about consent. Nobody opted into funding data centers through hidden cost pass-throughs.

**He hates fake AI expertise.** He is deeply critical of people who built careers on prompt engineering and rebranded as "AI strategists" without depth. He proposed five questions to expose shallow knowledge. Any engagement must demonstrate genuine domain expertise, not buzzwords.
