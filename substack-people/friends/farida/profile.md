# Farida

## Who She Is

Farida is an Egyptian Muslim woman from Ismailia, a city near the Sinai Peninsula — a place she describes as "where all the major prophets lived." She grew up in a deeply traditional Islamic environment. She eventually left organized religion, knowing she would be "scolded and called names" for doing so. She did not leave God. She left the institution that she felt had buried God's message under generations of human tradition.

She is David Hoze's co-author on an interfaith article exploring the shared moral core of Judaism, Christianity, and Islam. She is a spiritual rebel from within Islam — someone who holds God close while refusing the version of religion her culture handed her.

## Her Core Beliefs

**Religion has been corrupted by tradition.** Farida believes God's word — scripture — is perfect, timeless, and immortal. But over centuries, people have buried it under layers of radical tradition, cultural practice, and institutional rules. In her words: "it is unfortunately more tradition practised than religion… the divine part has gone or lost under generation of traditions."

**Only divine revelation counts as religion.** She draws a strict line: Judaism, Christianity, and Islam are religions because they carry scripture directly from God. Buddhism, Hinduism, and others she respects but categorizes as philosophies or doctrines, not divine revelation. This conviction is what led her to suggest narrowing the joint project with David to the three Abrahamic faiths.

**The power of words is sacred.** She connects words to creation itself — God's first act was a word ("Be," the Arabic _Kun_), and words carry that divine, immortal power. True words, she believes, move your soul before your mind even processes them.

**"Being new doesn't mean it's good."** This is her warning about AI and modern scriptural reinterpretation. God's word cannot be invented or improved upon — it can only be uncovered by stripping away the human narratives piled on top. She specifically cautions that AI-generated scriptural interpretation is unreliable because, in her view, the training data skews toward radical Islamic models.

**Organized religion felt like a club.** Join and conform, or leave. She chose to leave. Not out of indifference, but out of a refusal to let institutional loyalty replace genuine connection to God.

## Her Role in the Project

Farida co-authored with David an interfaith article titled _"The God Who Waits: Why the Creator Chose to Be Chosen."_ The piece argues that across Islam, Christianity, and Judaism, the same melody sounds: God, who could compel obedience, chooses instead to wait for a free heart. Islam calls this the trust of the _khalifah_. Judaism calls it owning the good rather than receiving _nahama dekisufa_ (bread of shame). Christianity calls it the living water that only quenches when freely chosen.

The article draws on Surah Al-Ma'un (107:1–7), the fierce Quranic warning against those who pray but refuse to help the orphan. It cites the hadith that none truly believes until he loves for his brother what he loves for himself. It places these alongside Isaiah's rejection of sacrifices from unjust hands, Hillel's one-foot summary of the Torah, and Jesus' words about knowing his disciples by their love.

The article also includes a section on disagreement — acknowledging that the moment a Muslim hears the name Jesus, something tightens, and the moment a Jew hears Muhammad, something closes. Farida and David wrote through this honestly, not pretending harmony but modeling what it looks like to hold your belief with your whole heart while listening with both ears.

Farida suggested the scope of the article. She brought the Islamic scholarship, the Quranic citations, and the perspective of someone who knows the tradition from the inside — its beauty and its failures. She also flagged theological sensitivities, such as the hadith "God created Adam in His image," which many Muslim scholars interpret differently than the Jewish and Christian parallel.

## Her Contribution to David's Broader Work

Farida is woven into the larger fabric of David's project in several ways:

**She validates the Judeo-Islamic convergence.** David's research documents deep structural parallels between Halacha and Sharia — case-based legal reasoning, graduated liability, affirmative duties, preserved minority opinions. Farida embodies this convergence personally: an Egyptian Muslim who finds common ground with a religious Jew in Jerusalem, not by diluting their traditions but by going deeper into them.

**She grounds the Watchers parallel.** David's article on the Watchers myth traces the 1 Enoch tradition of fallen angels teaching forbidden knowledge to its Islamic counterpart in Quran 2:102 (Harut and Marut). Farida's presence in the project gives this comparative work a living dimension — she is not a source being cited but a voice in the conversation.

**She represents the meaning crisis from the inside.** Farida didn't leave religion because she stopped believing. She left because the institution failed her — it demanded conformity instead of offering connection. Her story is a case study for the very crisis David's essays diagnose: that people are not abandoning God, they are abandoning institutions that have lost touch with what God actually asks.

**She is cited through the Exodus.** In the broader project conversations, Farida's voice is referenced as part of the assembling "symphony" — alongside Ent, Hawkeye, Caitlin, Sabrina, and others. She is part of the court that the Master of Prayer (in Nachman's tale) walks between towns to reassemble.

## What She Brings That David Doesn't Have

Farida sees religion from the exit. David sees it from the inside. He is a practicing religious Jew in Jerusalem; she is a woman who walked away from organized Islam in Egypt. Together they cover both perspectives: the one who stayed and the one who left, both still facing the same God. This is what gives their joint work its credibility. It is not two academics comparing notes. It is a Muslim and a Jew, in the middle of history's oldest conflict, choosing to write together about what they share.

She also brings a directness that sharpens the work. Her statement that religion has become "more tradition practised than religion" is not diplomatic. It is a diagnosis from someone who paid a social price for seeing it clearly.
