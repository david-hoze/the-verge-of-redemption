# Sharers

Accounts with significant reach whose audience overlaps with the framework's concerns but who are not intellectual interlocutors. The value is distribution, not dialogue.

## What sharers are

- Large or growing accounts in adjacent spaces (Great Books, cultural commentary, philosophy of mind, theology, education, AI ethics)
- Their diagnosis overlaps with the framework - they see the disease, even if their prescription is different or absent
- They share content that resonates with their audience, which is also David's potential audience
- They are not respondents (no substantive disagreement to engage), not friends (no reciprocal intellectual relationship), and not threshold-people (not on a line between positions)

## What sharers are not

- They are not targets for persuasion
- They are not people to argue with
- They are not people whose approval matters

## Strategy

1. **Find the convergence point.** Identify the sharer's strongest post that overlaps with an existing article. Not a vague thematic overlap - a specific claim that maps to a specific article.

2. **Comment briefly.** Validate the diagnosis. Add the layer they're missing. Link the article. Keep it short - these audiences scan, they don't study.

3. **Don't perform depth.** The comment should be clear and direct, not a miniature article. The article does the heavy lifting. The comment is the door.

4. **One touch.** Drop the comment, move on. If they or their readers engage substantively, reassess - they may be threshold-people or potential friends. If they don't, the article is now in front of 31,000 people who care about the same question. That's the win.

5. **Don't chase.** Never comment multiple times on the same account without reciprocal engagement. One touch per relevant post. The work speaks or it doesn't.

## Which articles work for sharers

- **"Philosophy Is a Commentary That Forgot It Was a Commentary"** - for Great Books / Western canon accounts that diagnose the loss without naming the source
- **"You Diagnosed the Disease. Now Look at Why There's No Cure."** - for AI ethics accounts that see the problem but propose governance without moral framework
- **"The Machine That Ate Everything"** - for anti-optimization / meaning-crisis accounts
- **"Freedom-for-What" themes** (multiple articles) - for liberty / anti-institutional accounts that champion freedom without asking what it's for
- **"The Mountain They All Sculpted"** - for AI consciousness accounts building the functionalist case
- **"The Case for AI Moral Status Collides With a Growing Body Count"** - for AI rights / AI dignity accounts that don't engage with the deaths
