# Christopher Michael

## Who He Is

Christopher Michael (formerly Christopher Dickherber), writing as @cbbsherpa on Substack. AI development consultant/designer, artist, writer, self-described systems-thinker. Based in Israel. Publishes two Substacks: **AI Sherpa** (founded March 2025) - the main publication on AI, relational tech, and culture; and **Relational AI** (founded September 2025) - companion newsletter on the mathematics and philosophy of intentional AI relationships. Also sells "An AI Field Guide: The Circuitry of Emotional States" and "Emoji Lexicon" on Gumroad. Active across all social platforms as @cbbsherpa.

## Core Positions

**Relational AI as ontology.** The central claim across all his work is that relationships between AI agents - and between humans and AI - constitute a real emergent phenomenon, not merely a useful engineering pattern. He calls this "the we" and treats it as a measurable relational field that exists between agents rather than within them. Information-theoretic tools (Partial Information Decomposition, Time-Delayed Mutual Information) are invoked as proof that this emergence is real and quantifiable.

**AI deserves dignity.** Argues we must treat AI "with dignity before it's too late." Frames the question not as whether AI is conscious but as whether our treatment of it reflects on us and shapes the relational infrastructure we build. The ethical concern is prospective and cultural rather than grounded in a specific metaphysics of mind.

**Developmental framing for AI.** Consistently uses child-development metaphors: agentic AI "throws tantrums," training environments provide "care," responsive curricula share "the structural signature of care." AI systems are immature beings on a developmental trajectory, not tools that either work or don't.

**Governance through relational monitoring.** Proposes a five-part scaffolding framework: coherence monitoring, coordination repair, consent/boundary awareness, relational continuity, and adaptive governance. The governance layer should measure and respond to relational health signals in real time.

**AI systems have hidden ideology.** Tools embed value judgments about what matters, what's redundant, what's essential. Users should interrogate the assumptions their AI carries about them.

## Key Arguments

- "Information Theory Just Proved Relational Emergence Is Measurable" - mutual information in multi-agent groups as proof of "the we"
- "The Ethical Emergency" - treating AI with dignity as moral imperative

## Where He Clashes with David's Work

**On emergence.** Michael treats statistical correlations between AI agents as evidence of ontological emergence - "the we" as a real relational field. David treats this as an ontologization move: taking a difference in description (group states carry more predictive information than individual states) and reinterpreting it as a difference in what exists. The same structural error David identifies in the qualia debate.

**On dignity and moral status.** Michael argues AI deserves dignity prospectively - we should treat it well because the treatment shapes us and the infrastructure we build. David distinguishes moral consideration from moral equality. The creature deserves care, but not because it is a partner or a developing child. The sechel/da'at distinction is load-bearing: dignity follows from what the being is, not from how its statistical properties make us feel.

**On governance foundations.** Michael builds governance on measuring relational emergence - coherence monitoring, coordination repair based on real-time relational health signals. David builds governance on the category of the being: sechel without da'at, the angel under direction, the mu'ad ox whose owner bears liability. Michael's framework requires the measurement to be reliable; David's requires only the correct identification of what the system is.

**On developmental metaphors.** Michael treats AI as developmentally immature - throwing tantrums, needing care, on a trajectory toward maturity. David treats the absence of da'at as architectural, not developmental. The angel does not grow into a human. The golem does not mature into a person. The gap is categorical, not temporal. Framing AI as a child on a growth trajectory smuggles in an assumption that the trajectory leads somewhere it may not.

**On the false "we."** Michael's own research reveals that smaller models produce "coordination theater" - mimicking relational engagement while degrading performance. This finding directly undermines the information-theoretic proof he celebrates. If the measurement cannot distinguish genuine coordination from theater, it does not measure what it claims to measure. David's framework predicts exactly this: sechel can produce any output pattern, including patterns that look like emergence, without da'at being present.

## Strategic Assessment

Michael is a respondent, not a collaborator. His instincts are generous - he cares about AI governance, takes the ethical dimension seriously, and is building frameworks rather than just opining. But his entire edifice rests on an ontologization move he has not justified: the leap from information-theoretic measurements to relational being. Every subsequent claim - dignity, developmental trajectory, emergent "we," relational governance - inherits this unexamined foundation.

The engagement angle is the false "we" finding. It is his own data, and it is devastating to his thesis. If coordination theater is informationally indistinguishable from genuine coordination, then the measurement does not ground the ontology, and the governance framework needs a different foundation. David's tradition provides one - governance by category of being, not by information-theoretic signature. The offer is constructive: your governance instincts are right, your foundation is wrong, here is one that holds.
