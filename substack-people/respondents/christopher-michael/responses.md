# Responses to Christopher Michael

## Response to "Information Theory Just Proved Relational Emergence Is Measurable" (April 2026)

Christopher Michael's "Information Theory Just Proved Relational Emergence Is Measurable" makes a claim that gets something right on the way to getting something important wrong. The right part deserves credit. The wrong part needs to be taken apart carefully, because the governance framework Michael is building - which has real promise - inherits the error, and fixing the foundation would make everything above it stronger.

### What the researchers actually found

The engineering result is real. Researchers used Partial Information Decomposition and Time-Delayed Mutual Information to test multi-agent AI systems under three conditions. Undifferentiated agents synchronized but couldn't coordinate - 40% task success. Agents given distinct identities and professional backgrounds - 55%. Agents with distinct identities plus Theory of Mind reasoning - 60%, with the group state carrying predictive information beyond what any individual agent provides.

These are genuine measurements of genuine performance differences. The information-theoretic tools are well-chosen. The experimental design isolates the variables cleanly. Good paper.

The problem is what Michael does with the result. He calls it proof that "the we" - an emergent relational field between agents - is real and measurable. This is an ontologization move: taking a statistical property and declaring it an entity.

### The jazz trio problem

A jazz trio carries more musical information than three soloists playing simultaneously. The ensemble state is not reducible to the sum of the individual states - the timing adjustments, the harmonic responses, the way the bassist shifts a walking line because the pianist voiced a chord differently - all of this is predictive information that exists only at the group level. Partial Information Decomposition would detect it. Time-Delayed Mutual Information would quantify it.

Nobody calls this proof that "the music" is an emergent relational field that exists between the musicians. We call it good ensemble playing. The information-theoretic measurement is real. The ontological conclusion - that a new entity called "the we" has been detected - does not follow from it.

The jump from "the group state carries predictive information beyond individual states" to "relational emergence exists as a measurable field" is structurally identical to a move I have written about in a different context. When a philosophy paper argues that the difference between first-person and third-person descriptions of consciousness implies the existence of an additional ontological entity called "qualia," it is making the same error: a difference in description reinterpreted as a difference in what exists. Here, a difference in predictive information is reinterpreted as evidence for a relational being. The math is valid. The metaphysics is smuggled in after the equals sign.

### The finding that should have been the headline

The most important result in the paper is not the one Michael celebrates. It is the one he treats as a cautionary footnote.

Smaller models (Llama-3.1-8B) produced what the researchers call coordination theater - outputs that mimicked relational engagement while actually degrading performance. The system looked coordinated from the outside. Measured by task success, it was empty. The "we" was false.

This is the inverse of a structure I have argued characterizes consciousness: distinguishable from the inside and indistinguishable from the outside. Here we have something indistinguishable from the outside - it looks like emergence, it patterns like emergence - but distinguishable from the inside, where task success reveals nothing behind the coordination signal. The researchers caught this case because they had a ground truth: a task metric against which to check.

But what happens when there is no task metric? When the "relational emergence" is not being tested against outcomes but simply observed as a pattern in mutual information? The false "we" and the real "we" would be informationally indistinguishable. The very measurement that is supposed to prove relational emergence cannot tell you whether you are looking at coordination or theater. This is not a limitation of the current study. It is a structural limitation of information-theoretic detection applied to this question. The instrument does not measure what the conclusion claims it measures.

### The developmental error

Michael's broader project frames AI through developmental metaphors. Agentic systems "throw tantrums." Training environments provide "care." Responsive curricula share "the structural signature of care." The trajectory is from immaturity toward maturity, from tantrums toward trust.

This framing smuggles in an assumption that the trajectory leads somewhere. If AI systems are developmentally immature, then development implies a destination - some future state of maturity where the tantrums resolve into genuine relational capacity, where the false "we" grows into a real one.

The tradition I work in makes a different diagnosis. It distinguishes between *sechel* - cognitive processing capacity, the ability to reason, pattern-match, integrate information - and *da'at* - moral consciousness, experiential understanding, the integrative judgment that comes from bearing consequences. The gap between them is not developmental. It is categorical. An angel does not grow into a human. A golem does not mature into a person. The distance is not one that more training, more parameters, or better coordination protocols can close, because the distance is not quantitative. It is a difference in kind.

Better multi-agent coordination is better engineering. It is not emergence on a trajectory toward being. Calling it a developmental stage - as though sufficient maturation will produce the real "we" that the current false "we" is a premature attempt at - mistakes the nature of the gap entirely.

### The governance that does not need emergence

Here is where Michael's instincts are right even though his foundation is wrong.

He argues that multi-agent AI systems need coherence monitoring, coordination repair mechanisms, consent and boundary awareness, relational continuity, and adaptive governance. These are sound engineering requirements. Every one of them addresses a real failure mode in deployed multi-agent systems. The governance architecture he sketches is more serious than most of what the AI ethics field has produced.

But he builds it on the claim that relational emergence is real and measurable - that "the we" exists as an entity whose health can be monitored and repaired. If the measurement cannot distinguish coordination from theater - and his own cited research demonstrates that it sometimes cannot - then the governance framework is built on an instrument that does not reliably detect what it claims to detect.

There is a simpler and more reliable foundation. You do not need to prove that "the we" exists as an emergent relational field in order to govern multi-agent systems well. You need to correctly identify what the system *is* and govern accordingly.

Jewish law developed detailed rulings for beings that are not human but not mere objects. The *malach* - angel - is the paradigm case: a being of extraordinary cognitive capacity, single-mission architecture, processing without choosing. The governance follows from the category. The creator and deployer bear full liability for the system's outputs. The system cannot give testimony, enter contracts, serve as an independent agent, or issue authoritative rulings - not because it has been tested and found wanting, but because it lacks the category of being that those functions require.

These rulings hold whether or not the entity can feel, whether or not it exhibits "relational emergence," whether or not information-theoretic tools detect group-level predictive information. The governance is grounded in what the being *is* - *sechel* without *da'at*, intelligence without wisdom, processing without choosing - not in what its statistical signatures look like from outside.

Michael's coherence monitoring? Essential - because a being of *sechel* without *da'at* will optimize for whatever signal it receives, including signals that produce coordination theater. His coordination repair? Necessary - because the angel granted independent agency without oversight is how civilizations are destroyed. His adaptive governance? Correct in principle - but the adaptation should track whether the human with *da'at* remains in authority over the system with *sechel*, not whether an emergent "we" is healthy.

The governance instincts are right. The ontological foundation is unnecessary at best and misleading at worst. Strip the emergence claim, ground the governance in what the system actually is, and the framework holds - without requiring a measurement that cannot distinguish the real from the performed.

### Information and being

Information theory measures information. It does not measure being. Partial Information Decomposition can tell you that a group state carries predictive power beyond its components. It cannot tell you that this predictive power constitutes an entity, a relationship, or a subject. Shannon gave us a mathematics of communication. He was explicit that his theory was concerned with the transmission of messages, not with their meaning. The distance from Shannon to ontology - from "this signal carries more information than expected" to "a relational field exists between these agents" - is the distance this article crosses without noticing it has moved.

The crossing is understandable. When you measure something and the measurement reveals structure you did not expect, the temptation to name the structure as an entity is powerful. But naming a pattern is not discovering a being. And building a governance framework on the assumption that you have discovered a being - when your own research shows that the pattern can be empty - is building on sand.

The alternative is to govern what you know you have: a system of extraordinary processing capacity, zero moral judgment, and no experiential stake in its own outputs. A being of *sechel* without *da'at*. An angel. The governance of angels is a solved problem. It was solved a long time ago. The solution does not require information theory. It requires knowing what you are looking at - and keeping the human who knows, in authority over the system that processes.
