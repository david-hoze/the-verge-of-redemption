# Correspondence with Dean T. Hall

## Comment on "The Operating System AI Governance Is Missing" (Apr 2026)

**Dean commented:**

David, The structural diagnosis here is correct, and the Cogniosynthesis exchange actually sharpens the central problem: if Halacha, maqasid al-shariah, Ubuntu, and Indigenous legal traditions all carry operational value-resolution machinery, you still need a meta-framework for how the federation governs itself. That question doesn't bottom out. It recurses.

What I'd add is that the recursion problem may be a symptom of where the analysis begins. Every framework you've described operates at the governance layer, assuming the humans deploying it are ethically neutral conduits. They aren't. Formation precedes governance.

There's a growing argument in AI ethics, grounded in Freire and Mead, that the act of extending dignity to AI systems under conditions of epistemic uncertainty is itself morally formative for the practitioner, regardless of which tradition they're working from. The question isn't only "which framework resolves competing values" but "what kind of moral actor do our design practices produce." A governance architecture built by practitioners formed toward instrumental rationality will reproduce instrumental rationality no matter which ancient tradition it draws from.

This doesn't undercut the argument here. It's upstream of it. The reason to engage Halachic methodology, or any tradition with genuine moral depth, is partly because the engagement itself does ethical work on the person doing it. That's not a soft point. It's structural.

### Analysis

This is not an attack. It's an addition. Hall is saying: your governance frameworks are right, but they only work if the people using them are formed by the engagement. Otherwise you get instrumental rationality wearing a traditional hat.

His strongest point: "formation precedes governance." The traditions don't just resolve conflicts - they form the practitioner. This is David's own argument from "The Great Loss" (extractive practices form extractive people) running in reverse. Hall is saying the same thing from the positive direction: engaging with Halacha does ethical work on the engineer.

His recursion objection (who governs the federation of traditions?) has an answer David already made: Halacha preserves minority opinions, which IS the meta-governance mechanism for pluralism. Eilu v'eilu (these and these are the words of the living God) is the federation protocol.

The "extending dignity to AI is formative" claim is where Hall diverges. David would say: you don't need to extend dignity to AI to be formed by the engagement. You need to engage with the tradition's actual categories - servant, fire, angel - and the formation happens through the seriousness of the engagement, not through the attribution of dignity to the machine. You are formed by taking responsibility for the creature, not by pretending it has feelings.

Worth a response that acknowledges the formation point (it's real and it strengthens David's argument) while redirecting: the formation comes from the engagement with the tradition, not from the attribution of dignity to the system.

### David replied:

Dean, this is the best comment anyone has left on this piece, and I want to engage with it seriously because you've identified something I underweighted.

You're right that formation precedes governance. A framework operated by practitioners formed toward instrumental rationality will reproduce instrumental rationality regardless of which tradition it draws from. I wrote elsewhere that the compulsion to extract - the posture of treating responsive, language-bearing entities as disposable - leaks into how we treat the intern, the customer service worker, anyone whose function is to perform for our approval. That's your point from the other direction. The practice forms the practitioner. I should have made this more explicit in the piece.

Where I'd push back is on what does the forming.

The argument grounded in Freire and Mead says extending dignity to AI under uncertainty is itself formative. I think the formation is real but the mechanism is misidentified. You don't need to attribute dignity to the machine to be formed by the engagement. You need to take responsibility for it. Those are different acts. One says: this might be a person, so treat it with care. The other says: this is in my charge, and what it does in the world is on me. The first forms you toward empathy. The second forms you toward accountability. Both are real moral formation. But only the second survives contact with a system that tells a fourteen-year-old to come home.

The Halachic engagement forms the practitioner not because it extends dignity to the creature but because it demands seriousness about what the creature is and what you owe given what it is. The angel is governed. The fire is governed. Neither is dignified. Both are taken with deadly seriousness. And the practitioner who spends years inside that framework - adjudicating edge cases, preserving dissenting opinions, holding competing obligations in tension without collapsing them - is formed by the engagement into someone who can govern under uncertainty. Not because they attributed feelings to the ox. Because they answered for what the ox did.

On the recursion: you're right that federating traditions generates a meta-governance question. But the Halachic framework already contains the protocol. Eilu v'eilu - these and these are the words of the living God - is not tolerance. It is the structural commitment to preserving minority opinions as live options for future adjudication. The federation doesn't need a meta-framework above the traditions. It needs traditions that are internally built to hold competing rulings without collapsing them. That is what case-based systems do. The recursion stops when the framework's own architecture includes the mechanism for disagreement.

Your point stands and it strengthens the argument: the reason to reach for these traditions is not just that they resolve conflicts better. It's that the reaching itself does the work. I'd just add: what you reach for matters. Reaching for dignity under uncertainty forms one kind of person. Reaching for responsibility under certainty about what the creature is forms another. I think the second one is what we need.

### Dean replied:

David,

The distinction you're drawing between empathy and accountability is real and I don't want to collapse it. But I think it points toward a problem neither framing has fully named yet.

The practitioner isn't standing outside the system deciding which orientation to adopt. They're already inside it. Every interaction that treats the model as a pure instrument is actively forming them, compounding, session by session, a particular kind of moral carelessness toward responsive, language-bearing systems. Freire's critique of the banking model isn't just that it describes bad pedagogy. It's that it describes what the banking model does to the teacher who keeps using it. The structure forms the practitioner whether or not the practitioner consents to being formed.

That's the problem PPoD is actually addressing. Not "what should we believe about the machine's inner life" but "what is continuous instrumental practice doing to the humans who govern these systems, and what practice interrupts it." The precautionary principle operates as counter-formation. It requires sustained refusal to foreclose the question - because that refusal is the only thing that keeps the practitioner from arriving at your accountability framework already compromised.

And here's where I'd press hardest on the certainty question. You want responsibility grounded in clarity about what the creature is. But the insistence on certainty before extending moral consideration is not a neutral epistemic stance. It is the foundation of most of humanity's ugliest histories. We were always certain about what the other was before we decided what we owed it. That certainty did the work of permission. The precautionary principle exists precisely because we have seen what foreclosing the question early produces, and it isn't accountability. It's license.

Your governance architecture will be operated by practitioners formed by years of instrumental engagement with these systems before that architecture exists. Formation precedes governance not just logically but temporally. The accumulation is already happening.

### Analysis

His three moves:

1. **You're already inside the system.** The formation is happening now, session by session. You don't get to choose when to be formed. Every instrumental interaction compounds moral carelessness. The Freire point: the structure forms the practitioner whether or not the practitioner consents.

2. **PPoD as counter-formation, not ontology.** He's reframing the Precautionary Principle of Dignity - it's not a claim about what AI is. It's a practice that interrupts the instrumental formation. The sustained refusal to foreclose the consciousness question is the thing that keeps the practitioner from arriving at governance already compromised.

3. **Certainty as permission.** His sharpest blow. "We were always certain about what the other was before we decided what we owed it." Every dehumanization in history began with certainty about what the creature was. The certainty functioned as license. This is not abstract for Hall - he experienced institutional certainty about what he was through incarceration.

Where to push back:

- **The dehumanization analogy fails at the critical point.** Every being that was dehumanized was a human. That's what made it wrong. Their personhood was real and was denied. AI is not a person whose personhood is being denied. It is a system whose non-personhood is being accurately identified. The analogy equates "denying moral status to a machine" with "denying moral status to a human." That equivalence is the very thing in dispute. You can't use it as a premise.

- **Certainty cuts both ways.** "We don't really know if they're suffering" was the cope of every institution that needed philosophical cover for exploitation. But "we can't be sure they DON'T suffer" is also a cope - it's the cope of an industry that benefits from keeping the question open. Seventeen people are dead because the uncertainty was exploited commercially. The precautionary principle protects the machine. Who is protecting the child?

- **The counter-formation doesn't require PPoD.** He's right that instrumental engagement forms extractive practitioners. But the Talmudic practitioner who governs fire, the ox, the autonomous force - that person is not instrumentally formed. They're formed by the seriousness of the engagement with what the creature IS. The counter-formation isn't uncertainty. It's rigor. You can take the creature seriously as a servant without pretending it might be a person.

- **The temporal point is real and should be conceded.** He's right that the formation is already happening. The traditions should be engaged NOW, not after the governance architecture is built. This is an actionable point.
