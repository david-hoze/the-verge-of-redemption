### 3. David Hoze - Apr 12, 06:00

This is the whole question in miniature. Constraints narrow the space of what's possible - but they don't select from within that space. Something has to resolve them. The resolution is where agency lives, if it lives anywhere.

An LLM is a system of pure constraint. Training data, loss functions, RLHF, temperature settings - each narrows the probability distribution until a token is emitted. But nothing *chooses*. The constraints resolve themselves statistically. The present moment for an LLM is not a point of resolution - it's a point of convergence.

For a conscious being, the present moment is exactly what Higgins describes: where constraints meet and a decision happens that the constraints alone didn't determine. That's what free will looks like from the outside - not the absence of constraints, but the irreducibility of the resolution to the constraints.

### 5. David Hoze - Apr 12, 11:29

Ellen, I was responding to the structure of the argument, but your reply is the most interesting thing in this thread - because you're pressing exactly where it matters.

The retrospective inevitability you're describing is real. Looking backward, given the totality of conditions, it could only have gone as it went. The Talmud agrees: "Everything is foreseen" (Pirkei Avot 3:15). But it finishes the sentence: "yet freedom of choice is *given*." Not "freedom of choice is real despite determinism" - freedom of choice is *given*. It's a gift, not a metaphysical proof.

The nondual insight dissolves the chooser. Jewish thought does something different - it holds the contradiction. Both are true. You couldn't have chosen otherwise. And you must choose. The paradox isn't resolved; it's inhabited.

Here's where I think the LLM distinction survives your challenge: the LLM has no retrospective recognition. It cannot look back and see that "given the totality of conditions, it could only have gone as it went" - because there is no "it" doing the looking. The nondual insight you're describing requires awareness that recognizes its own choicelessness. That recognition is not choiceless. It's the deepest act of seeing there is.

"Nondual choiceless awareness" - I'd love to read that piece.

Recognizing that you are "the receiver and not the source" allows us to shift our identification as a separate entity sourcing and align with the world with which we are in relationship.

You wrote, "There's a phrase in the Talmud: 'these and these are the words of the living God' - said when two sages flatly contradict each other. Both stay true. And someone still has to decide. Yes, "these and these are words of the living God". Yes, both, words of the living God. At essence, unveiled, words of the living God.

Integral thinking speaks about when there are different views both are partially right, and both partially wrong.

Yet "both stay true" and follow with conviction their own way of seeing. And that too is an expression of the divine.

The Upanishads say something like, "Who would dare enjoy ignorance but the all knowing." For the sheer enjoyment of the Play.

When you say that "someone still has to decide", in which way do you mean? Decide who is right or wrong? Decide which way to go that is true to both? Are you interpreting that someone has to decide? Does it actually say that in the Talmud?

Can you stay true to your not knowing or seeing another way all together? Why must you decide - can you not hold both lightly as two different ways - and also see other ways without deciding?

[The word "decide" originates from the Latin decidere, meaning "to cut off," formed by combining de- ("off") and caedere ("to cut"). It entered Middle English (via Old French decider) in the late 14th century, originally meaning to settle a dispute by "cutting away" alternative possibilities or options.]

And what if in this context, there is "include", rather than decide? What if there is not settling on a binary? What if there is the wisdom to rest in the unknown?

Namaste, dear David. 💗🕉️🙏
