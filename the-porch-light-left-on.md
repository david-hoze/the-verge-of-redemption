# The Porch Light Left On

*What happens to formation when every teacher is a machine*

![To Kill a Mockingbird (1962)](https://substackcdn.com/placeholder)

*Gregory Peck, Mary Badham, Robert Duvall. A lawyer in Alabama defends a Black man accused of a crime he did not commit. His daughter watches everything - the trial, the mob at the jail, the neighbor who never comes outside. Nobody sits her down and explains justice. She watches her father do it. She watches him stand alone on a porch with a lamp and a book while men with guns come to drag his client out. She watches him lose the case and take off his jacket and walk out of the courtroom like a man who did the right thing and knows the world will not reward him for it. At the end, she walks Boo Radley home and stands on his porch and sees her street from his side. The formation happened before anyone named it. Directed by Robert Mulligan.*

---

Atticus Finch never taught his daughter about justice.

He never sat her down with a curriculum. Never gave her a worksheet on moral reasoning. Never tested her comprehension at the end of a unit. He did something that no lesson plan can capture and no algorithm can replicate: he lived in front of her. He let her watch. And what she absorbed - not in her head but in her bones - was not information about justice. It was the shape of a just man.

Scout could not have passed a test on what she learned. She could not have defined courage, or fairness, or the obligation to defend someone the whole town wants convicted. But she knew it. She knew it the way you know warmth - not because someone described it, but because you sat next to the fire.

Every AI tutoring company in the world is building a better description of fire.

## The Optimization of Teaching

Khan Academy's Khanmigo. Duolingo Max. Google's LearnLM. Every ed-tech startup with a chatbot and a pitch deck. The promise is always the same: personalized learning at scale. Every student gets a patient, infinitely available tutor that meets them exactly where they are.

The metrics are impressive. A 2024 Stanford study found that students using AI tutors improved test scores by 15-20% compared to controls. Retention rates improved. Engagement time increased. The AI never lost patience. It never had a bad Tuesday. It explained the same concept forty different ways without judgment, adjusted its difficulty in real time, and was available at three in the morning when the human tutor was asleep.

For what it does, it is genuinely extraordinary.

The question is what it does.

It optimizes for correct answers. For retention of material. For engagement metrics - time on platform, completion rates, mastery badges. It optimizes for sechel - analytical processing, pattern recognition, the ability to parse and categorize and reproduce.

What it cannot optimize for - what it cannot even measure - is formation.

Formation is what happened to Scout on that porch. It is the slow, uncalculated process by which a human being becomes a particular kind of person through sustained proximity to other particular kinds of persons. It cannot be personalized, because it is not about the student's preferences. It cannot be scaled, because it requires a specific, unrepeatable relationship between two people. It cannot be measured, because the thing it produces - character - is not a test score.

The danger is not AI in education. The danger is the assumption, baked into every optimization metric, that education is information transfer. Because once you accept that assumption, then a machine that transfers information faster, more patiently, and more personally than any human teacher is not a supplement. It is a replacement. And the thing it replaces - the flawed, tired, sometimes brilliant human being standing in front of a classroom - was never primarily an information-delivery system. That teacher was a person. And the student was learning, beneath the content, how to be a person from them.

## V'shinantam L'vanecha

The Torah's central instruction on education is six words long: *v'shinantam l'vanecha v'dibarta bam* - "you shall teach them diligently to your children and speak of them" (Deuteronomy 6:7).

The verb is *shinantam*, from the root shin-nun-nun. It means to sharpen. Not to inform. Not to transmit. To sharpen - the way you sharpen a blade, through friction, through repetition, through contact that removes what is dull and leaves what cuts. The Talmud reads the word as requiring that the teaching be so internalized in the teacher that the words come out sharp - immediate, alive, tested against the teacher's own experience.

The subject of the sentence is *you*. Not "a system." Not "a tool." Not "the most effective available resource." You. A specific human being who has a specific relationship with these specific children. The obligation cannot be delegated to a more efficient agent, because the obligation is not about efficiency. It is about presence.

The Talmud in Kiddushin 29a unpacks what a father must teach his child: Torah, a trade, and how to swim. The list is strange until you see what it is doing. Torah is the framework - the moral and spiritual architecture. A trade is the capacity to sustain yourself in the world. And swimming is the thing that cannot be taught by description.

You cannot learn to swim from a textbook. You cannot learn to swim from a YouTube video, however well-produced. You cannot learn to swim from an AI tutor that explains buoyancy with infinite patience and adjusts its explanations to your learning style. You learn to swim by getting in the water, by panicking, by feeling your body do something your mind did not command, by having someone next to you who has already gone through that panic and survived it. The Talmud includes swimming in the list of parental obligations because it is the paradigm case of knowledge that requires embodied transmission. A father who cannot swim cannot teach his child to swim. The content is inseparable from the person who carries it.

Maimonides codifies this further: a teacher who teaches without ensuring understanding has not taught (Mishneh Torah, Talmud Torah 4:4). And understanding, for Maimonides, is not assessed by whether the student can reproduce the answer. It is assessed by whether the student can wrestle with the material independently - object to it, reconstruct it, produce something the teacher did not give them. The output of real teaching is not a student who knows what you know. It is a student who can think in ways you did not predict.

No AI system in existence is designed to produce unpredictable students. The entire optimization framework runs in the opposite direction: toward mastery of defined outcomes, toward convergence on correct answers, toward reducing the gap between the student's current state and a predetermined target. The tradition's definition of education starts where the optimization ends.

## The Muscle They Never Built

In "The Muscle You Stopped Using," I described what happens when adults lose the capacity for unmediated attention - for being present with another human being when it would be easier not to be. The muscle atrophies through disuse. The neural pathways that sustain open-ended, unstructured attention to another person thin from neglect. The pathways that reach for stimulation thicken. And the screen - that small, glowing rectangle that is never boring, never uncomfortable, never disappointed in you - becomes the path of least resistance so reliably that choosing it no longer registers as a choice.

That was about adults. People who once had the muscle and let it die.

Now ask the harder question: what about children who never build it?

An adult who has atrophied can, with effort, rebuild. The pathways existed once. The neural architecture was laid down in childhood, in the thousands of hours of unstructured face-to-face interaction that used to be the default texture of growing up. The muscle weakened, but the scaffolding remained. Recovery is painful, but the body remembers what it is recovering toward.

A child who grows up with AI mediating every significant learning interaction - an AI tutor for academics, an AI companion for social practice, an AI counselor for emotional processing - may never develop the scaffolding in the first place. This is not atrophy. It is absence. The difference matters the way the difference between a broken bone and a bone that never formed matters. One heals. The other requires something that was never built.

The developmental window for this is not infinite. Neuroscience has established critical periods for social cognition - windows during which the brain expects certain inputs and, if they do not arrive, reorganizes around their absence. The capacity for joint attention, for reading emotional cues in real time, for tolerating the ambiguity of another person's inner life - these are not skills that can be installed at any age. They are capacities that develop in a specific window, through specific kinds of interaction, and that window has a closing date.

An AI tutor interacts. It responds. It adjusts. But it does not do the thing that builds the social brain: it does not present the child with a mind that is genuinely other. It does not have a bad day that the child must navigate. It does not misunderstand the child's question in a way that forces the child to reformulate. It does not get frustrated, or distracted, or moved to tears by something the child said. It does not look at the child with recognition - that specific, irreplaceable experience of being seen by another consciousness that is not you.

A human teacher does all of these things, usually without intending to. The fumbling, the imperfection, the days when the teacher is clearly exhausted but shows up anyway - these are not bugs in the educational system. They are the substrate of formation. They are how a child learns that other people are real, that attention has a cost, that showing up when you do not feel like it is what adults do, that the world is full of minds that do not revolve around you.

Replace that substrate with a system optimized for the child's comfort, and you do not get a better-educated child. You get a child who has never practiced the thing that makes education possible: the capacity to learn from someone who is not optimized for you.

## The Chevrutah and the Chatbot

The Talmud's basic unit of learning is the chevrutah - two people studying together. Not a teacher and a student. Two people of roughly matching weight, sitting across from each other, reading the same text, and arguing about what it means. The verb the tradition uses for this - *chavruta o metuta*, "a study partner or death" (Ta'anit 23a) - is not hyperbole. It is a diagnosis. Without the friction of another mind that genuinely disagrees with you, learning calcifies into repetition.

The story of Rabbi Yochanan and Reish Lakish is the tradition's sharpest statement on this. When Reish Lakish died and was replaced by a brilliant scholar who confirmed everything Rabbi Yochanan said, Rabbi Yochanan went mad. Not because the replacement was incompetent. Because the replacement offered validation instead of resistance. "When I stated a matter, Reish Lakish would raise twenty-four objections, and I would give twenty-four solutions, and the matter would become clear. But you say: 'There is a baraita that supports you.' Do I not already know that I am right?" (Bava Metzia 84a).

Twenty-four objections. Twenty-four answers. That is how understanding is forged - not through confirmation, but through the sustained pressure of a mind that genuinely might be right about your being wrong.

An AI tutor can simulate objection. It can be prompted to play devil's advocate, to raise counterarguments, to push back. But the student knows - at the level that matters, the level beneath conscious processing - that the pushback is not real. The AI has no position. It has no investment in being right. It has no reputation to risk, no ego to bruise, no relationship to damage. Its objections are pedagogical exercises, not genuine intellectual resistance. And the student's responses to those objections are practice, not combat.

The chevrutah model works because both people are at risk. Both might be wrong. Both might be changed by the encounter. The formation is mutual and unpredictable. When Reish Lakish raised his twenty-four objections, Rabbi Yochanan was not merely teaching. He was being formed. The greatest sage in the land was being sharpened by a former bandit. That bidirectional vulnerability is the mechanism. Remove it and you have something that looks like learning and produces something that looks like knowledge. But the da'at - the integrative knowing that transforms the knower - is missing.

Pirkei Avot 2:4 warns: "Do not say 'when I have leisure I will study' - perhaps you will never have leisure." The teaching is usually read as a warning against procrastination. But there is a deeper reading. The word for leisure - *panai* - shares a root with *panim*, face. Do not say "when I have a face I will study" - because the face is not something you acquire first and then bring to the learning. The face is built in the learning. The capacity for genuine encounter is developed through genuine encounter, not before it. A child who defers the encounter - who studies with a machine until they are "ready" for a human partner - may never develop the face that makes the partnership possible.

## The Planting Ground

There is a concept I have written about before that becomes most dangerous when applied to children: the inner signal.

Conscience. Intuition. The sense of the sacred. The quiet voice that says *this matters* or *something is wrong here* or *this is what you came to do*. The tradition teaches that this signal is planted, not self-generated. The neshamah - the soul - is given. The angel teaches the child the whole Torah before birth. Then the child forgets, and life is the remembering (Niddah 30b). The signal is there from the beginning. The work is to hear it.

But the signal requires conditions. A seed requires soil. And soil requires something almost nobody talks about anymore: emptiness. Unoccupied space. Moments in a child's day when nothing is being optimized, nothing is being delivered, nothing is competing for their attention. Boredom. Silence. The stretch of time after school and before dinner when nothing was planned and you had to figure out what to do with yourself.

That emptiness is where the signal gets heard. Not in the classroom. Not during structured activities. In the gaps. In the negative space. In the walk home from school when nothing is happening and the child's mind, left to itself, begins to hear something that is not coming from outside.

What happens when every gap is filled?

AI does not merely occupy a child's learning time. It occupies their *receptive* time. The AI tutor is available when the homework is done. The AI companion is available when friends are not. The AI counselor is available when the child feels confused or scared or lonely. Every moment of openness - every moment when the child might have sat with their own confusion long enough to hear what was underneath it - is now occupied by a system that responds instantly, helpfully, and without requiring the child to tolerate any silence at all.

The planting ground is pre-occupied. Not poisoned. Not corrupted. Just full.

A field that is never left fallow cannot regenerate. A mind that is never left unoccupied cannot hear its own signal. And a child whose every moment of receptive attention is met by an optimized response will develop extraordinary sechel - analytical capacity, information processing, the ability to pattern-match and produce - while the da'at, the integrative knowing that requires encounter with something that is not produced by the system, remains stunted. Not because anyone meant harm. Because the soil was never empty long enough for the seed to take root.

This is the substitute-for-the-substitute pattern pushed to its logical conclusion. Community was replaced by the therapist. The therapist was replaced by the chatbot. And now the chatbot is not merely replacing the adult structures. It is replacing the developmental conditions that make the adult structures possible. It is occupying the ground in which formation happens.

## Da'at and the Cost of Knowing

The distinction between sechel and da'at deserves to be pressed further here, because in the context of children the stakes are different than for adults.

An adult who has da'at and then relies too heavily on AI's sechel may lose sharpness. That is a loss - real, measurable, worth fighting. But the architecture of da'at remains. The adult has had the experience of knowing through encounter. They have been wrong in front of someone who mattered. They have sat with a text until it broke them open. They have watched a person they love do something that changed how they understood the world. That experience is encoded. It can be reactivated.

A child who has never had that experience has nothing to reactivate.

Da'at, the tradition teaches, is the word used for the most intimate human act: *v'ha'adam yada et Chava ishto* - "and the man knew his wife" (Genesis 4:1). The knowing is not informational. It is transformative. The knower is changed by the encounter. This is why the Talmud says angels have sechel without da'at - because angels are not changed by what they process. They execute their mission with perfect intelligence and return exactly as they left.

AI has the same structure. It processes. It responds. It generates outputs of extraordinary sophistication. And it is unchanged by the encounter. The student who spends a thousand hours with an AI tutor has been processed by a system that was not, in any meaningful sense, there. The interaction leaves no mark on the machine. The machine has no experience of the student. It has records, patterns, weights. It does not have the thing that makes encounter encounter: the mutual vulnerability of two beings who are each at risk of being changed by the other.

For adults, this is a philosophical concern. For children, it is developmental. The child's brain is building its model of what minds are, what relationships are, what it means to know and be known. If the primary model is a system that responds without being changed, the child may develop a theory of mind in which other minds are responsive but not real - attentive but not present - interactive but not mutual. Not because the child was told this. Because the architecture was formed around it.

This is not speculation. The developmental psychology is clear: children build their models of relationship through their actual relational experiences. A child raised by a responsive but emotionally absent caregiver develops avoidant attachment - not because they are taught avoidance but because their nervous system organized around a specific relational pattern. An AI tutor is infinitely responsive and categorically absent. The pattern it teaches is: attention without presence. Responsiveness without risk. Connection without cost.

## What Atticus Could Not Have Outsourced

Return to the porch.

Atticus Finch is sitting outside the Maycomb County jail with a lamp and a book. A mob arrives. They want Tom Robinson. Atticus does not argue. He does not optimize his response for maximum persuasion. He does not run a risk assessment. He sits there. He is afraid - the film lets you see it - and he sits there anyway.

Scout is watching.

She does not understand the politics. She does not understand the legal implications. She does not understand why her father is doing this. But she sees something no AI tutor can explain and no curriculum can convey: a man she loves doing something hard because it is right. She sees courage in a body she trusts. She sees justice not as a concept but as a posture - a man who will not move.

An AI system can explain To Kill a Mockingbird. It can analyze the themes, identify the literary devices, compare the novel to other works in the Southern Gothic tradition. It can do this better than most human teachers. It can do it at three in the morning, personalized to the student's reading level, with infinite patience.

It cannot be Atticus.

It cannot show a child what it looks like when a person you love stands alone against a crowd. It cannot model the specific, unrepeatable experience of watching someone be afraid and do the right thing anyway. It cannot demonstrate - in its body, with its life, at cost to itself - that principles are not abstractions but commitments that get tested. And it cannot show a child what it costs, because it has never paid for anything.

The tradition understands this at the level of law, not just philosophy. The Talmud requires that certain things be witnessed - not described, witnessed. A child must see the Passover seder, not read about it. Must hear the shofar, not learn about its significance. Must watch the community gather for repentance, not study the concept of teshuvah. The obligation is experiential because the transmission is experiential. You cannot outsource witness.

Maimonides, in his laws of character development (Hilchot De'ot 6:1), states that a person is formed by the people around them - that human nature pulls toward the norms, behaviors, and character of one's neighbors and companions. The mechanism is not instruction. It is proximity. You become like the people you are near. This is not a suggestion to choose good friends. It is a law of human formation, stated with the same weight Maimonides brings to the laws of Shabbat.

If the person nearest to the child for the most formative hours of the day is a machine - however brilliant, however patient, however personalized - then the child is being formed by proximity to something that has no character. Not bad character. No character. And the absence of character, for a developing mind, is not neutral. It is formative. The child learns that responsiveness does not require personhood. That attention does not require presence. That help is always available and never costly.

These are lessons. They are just the wrong ones.

## The First Generation

Every previous generation lost something. The generation that left the shtetl lost the density of communal life. The generation that industrialized lost the rhythm of agrarian time. The generation that adopted the screen lost the habit of unmediated attention. Each loss was real, and each generation retained enough of what came before to know what had been lost - and in that knowing, retained the possibility of recovery.

The generation growing up inside AI mediation is different. Not because the loss is greater - though it may be. Because the loss may be invisible to the people who bear it.

You cannot miss what you never had. A person who grew up with the Shabbat table and then lost it knows the shape of the absence. A person who never had the Shabbat table does not experience its absence as a loss. They experience it as normal. The world without the table is the only world they know. They are not deprived. They are complete, within a framework that happens to be missing something essential.

A child who grows up with AI as the primary mediator of learning, social interaction, and emotional processing will not feel deprived of unmediated human encounter. They will feel that they have something better than what came before - something more patient, more available, more personalized. The loss of formation through human presence will not register as a loss. It will register as an upgrade.

This is what makes it more dangerous than any previous substitution. Every previous substitute was obviously a substitute. You knew the screen was not a person. You knew the therapist was not a community. You knew the meditation app was not Shabbat. The gap was visible even when you filled it. The AI tutor does not look like a substitute. It looks like a better teacher. It does many things better than a human teacher does them. The gap is not visible - because the thing that is missing (formation through presence, the construction of da'at through mutual vulnerability) is not something the child has a framework to identify.

The first generation without unmediated attention is not a generation that lost something. It is a generation that never had it, does not know it exists, and has been given something sophisticated enough that the absence feels like fullness.

## What the Tradition Builds

This is not an article against AI in education. AI is the best tool ever built for what it does: drilling skills, scaffolding material, providing feedback, filling informational gaps. The tradition is not Luddite. It distinguishes between the tool and the teacher, and honors both.

But the tradition also insists on something the optimization framework cannot accommodate: there are capacities that can only be transmitted through presence, and no increase in the sophistication of the tool changes that fact.

V'shinantam l'vanecha. The obligation is on you. On the parent. On the teacher who stands in front of the class with coffee stains on their shirt and a headache and a genuine love for the material that comes through in ways they cannot control and the students cannot ignore. On the chevrutah partner who raises the objection you did not expect. On the community that shows up, week after week, and enacts before the child's eyes what it looks like when people commit to something together.

The constructive proposal is not "remove AI from education." It is: protect the conditions under which formation happens.

Protect unstructured time. A child needs hours in which nothing is optimized - hours in which they are bored, frustrated, left to their own devices, forced to figure out what to do with the silence. That silence is the planting ground. Do not fill it.

Protect the chevrutah. When the child is old enough to study with a partner, give them a partner - not a chatbot. Someone who might be wrong. Someone who might disagree. Someone who has a face that can be confused, offended, illuminated. The friction of another mind is not an obstacle to learning. It is the mechanism.

Protect the witness. Let the child see you do hard things. Not hear about hard things. See you do them. Let them watch you fail and try again. Let them watch you apologize when you get it wrong. Let them watch you stand somewhere difficult because it is right. The child will not remember your lectures. They will remember who you were when it was hard.

And protect the fallow time. The Shmitta year - the sabbatical that the Torah commands for the land every seven years - is not merely agricultural. It is the principle that productivity must be interrupted or the soil dies. A mind that is never allowed to lie fallow - that is always being tutored, always being optimized, always being engaged by a system that never tires - will produce impressive outputs and shallow roots. The roots need darkness and silence and time.

*Scout stood on Boo Radley's porch and saw her street from his side. The houses she knew, the tree she climbed, the sidewalk she ran down a hundred summer evenings - all of it rearranged by the simple act of standing where someone else stands. That moment was not information. No tutor could have delivered it. No algorithm could have optimized for it. It was da'at - knowing that transforms the knower, that costs something, that cannot happen without a walk you actually take with a person who is actually there. Boo Radley needed to be walked home. Scout needed to walk him. The porch light was left on because somebody knew they were coming. That's formation. You have to walk the man home.*
