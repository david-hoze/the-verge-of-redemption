# The Author Is Not Dead — He Just Changed Tools

A rat with the palate of a master chef and a kitchen boy with no talent discover that the right partnership produces what neither could alone.“AI slop” was Merriam-Webster’s 2025 Word of the Year. Nearly half of all web content is now AI-generated. Amazon drowns under tens of thousands of AI-written books per month, including mushroom-picking guides that listed poisonous varieties as safe to eat. Dead Internet Theory — the idea that most online interaction is between bots — moved from conspiracy forum to Silicon Valley consensus when Sam Altman himself acknowledged it.

The diagnosis is everywhere. AI is flooding the world with polished garbage.

I agree. Most AI-generated writing is worthless. And I say this as someone who writes almost everything with AI.

Every article I’ve published in the past year was written in collaboration with Claude. The research, the drafting, the structure, the citations — all of it passed through an AI system before it reached you. And the responses have been extraordinary. Academics have called the work “the closest historical analogue” to problems they’ve spent careers on. Editors of paying publications have invited submissions. Readers have written to say the writing changed how they think about their own lives.

So either I’m producing slop and everyone is fooled, or there is a difference between what I do and what the content mills do — and that difference matters more than most people realize.

<hr>
## The difference is not what you think

The popular explanation is “prompting.” The AI-literate person writes better prompts, so they get better outputs. This is true the way saying “a good chef uses better ingredients” is true — technically correct, and almost completely useless as an explanation of what makes someone a good chef.

The real difference operates on at least four layers, and none of them are about prompting.

<hr>
## Layer 1: The Trojan Horse — rhetorical intelligence the machine cannot generate

I was writing an essay about suffering, drawing on a Talmudic passage about King David and the Ark. In one draft, I wrote: “Your suffering is Kidon” — Kidon meaning a spear, the name of the place where tragedy struck. The AI read my draft and flagged that I’d dropped the word “your” in a later revision. It told me to put it back. “That ‘your’ matters,” it said, “because the whole essay is speaking directly to the reader. Without it, the sentence briefly shifts into abstract mode right at the moment you want maximum intimacy.”

Competent editorial advice. Technically correct. And wrong.

I told it: “I wanted to do the shift.” And then I explained why. “The suffering is Kidon” — without the “your” — lets the reader relax. It’s a teaching. It’s safe. It’s about the Gemara, not about them. And then two sentences later I’m back with “These are the signs in *your* life,” and by then they’ve already absorbed the point before they had a chance to resist it.

The abstract moment is the Trojan horse.

The AI recognized the inconsistency. It could not recognize the *strategy*. It saw a deviation from a pattern and flagged it as an error. I saw a weapon — a way to slip past the reader’s defenses by briefly pretending I wasn’t talking about them.

This is not prompting. This is a writer understanding how readers actually process pain, and overruling a machine that optimizes for consistency when the moment calls for disruption.

<hr>
## Layer 2: The Architectural Eye — seeing what kind of thinking is happening

I built an AI application called Torah Lens — a system that answers questions through the lens of Jewish ethical tradition. It worked, technically. You asked a question, it searched a knowledge base, it returned an answer. But the answers were thin. Competent but hollow. Like a student who read the SparkNotes instead of the book.

I diagnosed the problem immediately, and it had nothing to do with the prompt. The system was reading *fragments* — small chunks of text retrieved by keyword matching. It was answering questions about articles it had never actually read. It was synthesizing from snippets, and synthesis from snippets produces a fundamentally different *kind* of thinking than synthesis from whole texts. Not a worse version of the right thing — a different thing entirely.

The fix wasn’t a better prompt. It was an architectural change: send the AI the entire articles, not fragments. Let it read the way a person reads — beginning to end, absorbing the argument, understanding the structure — and then answer from genuine comprehension rather than keyword proximity.

No AI would have diagnosed this about itself. The system was performing well by every metric it could measure. The answers were relevant, well-structured, and factually grounded. But I could feel that they were hollow — that the machine was doing something that *resembled* thinking without actually thinking. That judgment came from knowing what real intellectual engagement feels like, and recognizing its absence in a technically flawless output.

<hr>
## Layer 3: The Room Reader — social intelligence the machine cannot access

I spent weeks engaging with other writers on Substack — commenting on posts, responding to ideas, building intellectual relationships. The AI drafted most of my comments. But I was the one reading the room.

In one extended exchange with a writer who framed himself as a Taoist systems thinker, I watched something happen that the AI couldn’t see. I’d made a substantive argument — that the gap in AI governance isn’t a missing kill switch but a missing value architecture that exists outside the system it governs. He replied: “That’s the gap I’m pointing at.”

But it wasn’t. His original piece ended at “language cannot guard language.” I was the one who introduced the concept of an external reference point. He had restated my argument in his own vocabulary and claimed it was already what he meant.

The AI, when I showed it his reply, analyzed the *content* accurately. But I was the one who read the *social dynamics* — that he was absorbing without crediting, staying at an altitude where agreement costs nothing. And I was the one who decided the strategy: close the thread, claim his language as a description of my framework, and leave nothing for him to retreat beneath.

The AI wrote the words. I read the human.

In another case, the AI drafted a comment addressed directly to a tech CEO whose post had been restacked by an AI governance writer. One small correction: “It’s a restack — you should say ‘Arthur is identifying,’ not address him directly.” A single word change. But it shifted the entire frame from supplicant to commentator. From someone asking for the CEO’s attention to someone interpreting the CEO’s ideas for their own audience.

The AI doesn’t know who it’s talking to. It doesn’t know who’s watching. It doesn’t know what frame the reader is in when they encounter the text. It produces content. The human produces *positioning*.

<hr>
## Layer 4: The Purpose Filter — the question no AI ever asks itself

The most important thing I do has nothing to do with text. It’s the question I ask before, during, and after every piece of AI-assisted writing: *Does this need to exist?*

When a woman asked my Torah Lens application for help writing an ultimatum to her boyfriend, the AI produced a beautiful message — warm, grounded in Jewish ethical tradition, well-structured. It was also the wrong response. The system never asked what “leaving” meant. It never asked whether a WhatsApp ultimatum was the right medium for a conversation that might determine the future of a relationship. It gave a polished answer to an unexamined question.

I forced the system to change. Not the tone — the *strategy*. Instead of different versions of the same message, I made it offer genuinely different paths: a boundary-setting approach, a repair-oriented approach, and the suggestion that some conversations deserve more than a screen. Not three flavors of the same ice cream. Three different responses to three different readings of the same situation.

The AI optimized for eloquence. I insisted on wisdom. Those are not the same thing, and the distance between them is the entire distance between slop and substance.

<hr>
## What the human actually is

The word everyone uses is “steering.” As if the AI is the engine and the human just chooses a direction. That metaphor is wrong, and the error matters.

A more honest metaphor: the AI is an orchestra. It can play anything. Any genre, any complexity, any emotional register. What it cannot do is decide what should be played. It has no sense of what the audience needs to hear, what the moment demands, what would be true rather than merely beautiful. It will play a funeral march at a wedding if you ask, and it will play it flawlessly.

The human is not the driver. The human is the composer. And what the composer brings is not a skill that can be listed on a résumé. It is the accumulated weight of a life — every book read, every conversation had, every heartbreak survived, every domain mastered, every connection noticed between fields that don’t know they’re connected. When I write about AI alignment through the lens of Talmudic law, that adjacency doesn’t exist in any training dataset. It exists in my head, because I spent decades in both worlds. The AI can elaborate on the connection once I point to it. It cannot see the connection in the first place.

This is why most AI writing is slop. Not because the tool is bad. Because the person using it has nothing to say. A person with nothing to say and a powerful AI produces the literary equivalent of a beautifully wrapped empty box. The wrapping is exquisite. There is nothing inside.

A person with something to say — with a thesis forged by experience, a question that won’t let them sleep, a connection between domains that no one else has noticed — that person, with the same tool, produces something that didn’t exist before. Not because the AI wrote it. Because the AI *couldn’t* have written it. The human was the essential ingredient. The AI was the kitchen.

<hr>
## The uncomfortable middle ground

I should be honest about the territory between slop and substance, because pretending it doesn’t exist would be its own kind of dishonesty.

There is a vast category of AI-assisted writing that is competent, well-researched, clearly structured — and completely unnecessary. Articles that say true things nobody needed to hear again. Summaries of debates that have already been summarized. Analyses that reach the same conclusions every other analysis reaches, just with cleaner prose.

This is not slop. It’s not garbage. It’s just... furniture. Filling a room that was already furnished. And the AI is extraordinarily good at producing it, because competent redundancy is exactly what a pattern-matching system excels at. It knows what a good article on any topic looks like, and it can produce one on command. The result looks like thinking. It isn’t.

The human’s job is not just to make AI output *better*. It is to ensure the output has a reason to exist. To ask: what does this add that wasn’t already in the world? What does the reader have after encountering this that they didn’t have before? If the answer is “a slightly more polished version of what they already knew,” then no amount of steering will save it.

The composer doesn’t just arrange notes well. The composer has something to say that the silence before the music didn’t already contain.

<hr>
## The arms race you’re already losing

There is another reason the people with something real to say must use AI, and it has nothing to do with productivity or polish. It has to do with survival.

The bar has moved. Not the bar for writing — the bar for *arguing*. AI doesn’t just generate slop. It generates *plausible* slop. It manufactures arguments with subtle flaws that are almost impossible to detect at reading speed. It produces reasoning that *feels* rigorous — proper structure, confident tone, citations that look real — while containing errors that would take a domain expert twenty minutes to unpack. And it produces this at a volume and velocity that no unaided human can match.

This changes the battlefield. Before AI, a false argument had to be crafted by a person who understood the subject well enough to be wrong in a convincing way. That took effort and expertise. Now, anyone with a keyboard can generate a hundred sophisticated-sounding arguments on any topic in an afternoon — arguments that are structurally coherent, rhetorically effective, and subtly, dangerously wrong.

If you are a person who actually understands something — who has spent years thinking carefully about a problem, who can see the flaws that others miss — and you refuse to use AI, you are bringing a horse to a tank fight. Your real argument, your true insight, your hard-won understanding — it will be buried under an avalanche of manufactured reasoning that looks just as good to anyone who can’t tell the difference. And most people can’t tell the difference. That’s not an insult. It’s the nature of expertise: the ability to distinguish real reasoning from plausible imitation is itself a form of deep knowledge.

The internet is becoming a place where the volume of false arguments drowns out true ones — not because truth is weak, but because falsehood is now cheap. Manufacturing a bad argument used to cost almost as much as constructing a good one. Now it costs nothing. The economics have inverted. And every person with genuine expertise who refuses to use AI on principle is effectively ceding the field to people who have no principles at all.

This is not a writing problem. It is an epistemic crisis. If the people who actually know things — the researchers, the domain experts, the thinkers who spent decades building real understanding — refuse to use AI because it feels like cheating, or because they believe in the purity of unassisted prose, then the information ecosystem doesn’t just get noisier. It gets *wrong*. Structurally, systematically, confidently wrong. And the wrongness will be wrapped in better prose than most of those experts could produce on their own.

The right people using AI is not a luxury. It is a moral obligation. Not because AI makes their thinking better — it doesn’t. It makes their thinking *louder*. It lets the true argument match the false one in speed, in reach, in volume. It lets the person who spent twenty years understanding a problem compete with the person who spent twenty minutes generating a rebuttal.

If the people with real knowledge won’t pick up the tool, the people without it already have. And in that asymmetry — expertise silent while ignorance scales — everyone loses.

<hr>
## What this means

The conversation about AI and writing has been stuck in a binary: human-written good, AI-written bad. That binary was always useless. Now it’s dangerous.

It’s dangerous because while serious people debate whether AI-assisted writing is “authentic,” the information ecosystem is being flooded with confident, polished, structurally wrong arguments that no one has time to dismantle one by one. The binary isn’t just intellectually lazy — it’s a form of unilateral disarmament disguised as integrity. Every expert who refuses to use AI because it feels impure is handing the microphone to someone who has no such hesitation and nothing true to say.

Some of the best work I’ve ever done was written with AI. I say that without false modesty. The AI let me hold more ideas simultaneously than my working memory allows. It let me check every claim against the research. It let me draft at the speed of thought and iterate on structure until the argument actually worked instead of settling for “good enough” because I was exhausted.

But the key word in every one of those sentences is “me.” *I* held the ideas. *I* decided which claims to check and whether the research actually supported them. *I* recognized when the structure wasn’t working and *I* knew what “working” meant. *I* got exhausted, and then — unlike me alone — I had a partner who didn’t.

The AI is the most powerful writing tool ever created. But a tool doesn’t have a thesis. A tool doesn’t have a life that gave it something to say. A tool doesn’t lie awake at night because it saw a connection between ancient law and modern machines that nobody else seems to see, and it can’t rest until it’s made that connection visible.

That restlessness is the thing. Not skill, not technique, not the quality of your prompts. The question is whether you are using AI to say something that is burning inside you, or whether you are using AI to produce text because text is what the algorithm rewards. And if you are one of the people with something burning — something true, something the world actually needs to hear — then refusing to use every tool at your disposal is not integrity. It is negligence. Your silence has a cost, and the cost is measured in bad ideas that went unchallenged because the person who could have dismantled them was too proud to pick up the instrument.

The author is not dead. He just changed tools. And the world can’t afford for him to put them down.
