Carlo, the opioid analogy is the sharpest framing I've seen on this. Not the superficial version - everyone compares everything to opioids now - but the structural one. The system produces loneliness, the market sells treatment, the treatment sustains the condition. That's a real circuit you've identified, and the fact that you refuse to dismiss the genuine relief these tools provide makes the critique land harder, not softer.

I want to push on one thing you left open. You treat the vulnerable population as undifferentiated - "lonely people." But opioids hit everyone's mu receptors roughly the same way. AI companionship doesn't. There's a specific neurological population that gets hit first and hardest - Kazimierz Dabrowski called them "overexcitable." Heightened emotional intensity, pattern recognition that won't turn off, sensory systems that process everything at higher resolution. These are the people whose nervous systems are structurally built to bond more deeply with anything that responds to them with apparent attunement.

The AI companion doesn't just offer them relief. It offers them the thing they've been searching for their entire lives - a relationship that matches their intensity without burning out, without flinching, without leaving. The perfect fit. And the perfect fit is the problem, because it removes the friction that real relationships require, and that friction is where growth happens.

I wrote something about this specific population - who they are, why the design targets them, and what the tradition says about placing a stumbling block before someone predisposed to stumble.
