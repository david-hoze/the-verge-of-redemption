# Yes, AI Can Imitate Judgment. No, That’s Not the Same Thing.

### A response to Steven Adler’s “Clear-Eyed AI”

---Steven Adler, an ex-OpenAI safety researcher whose work I respect, recently published an article arguing that judgment is not uniquely human — that AI already demonstrates it, that people who claim otherwise are romanticizing humanity, and that we should stop asserting false limits about what machines can do.

His evidence is impressive. His conclusion is wrong. And the reason it’s wrong cuts deeper than he seems to realize — into the foundations of what judgment actually is, what it requires, and why a deterministic system cannot have it no matter how convincingly it performs.

## What Adler Gets Right

Let’s be honest about the data, because intellectual honesty matters more than comfort.

Adler cites two peer-reviewed studies — one in *Science*, one in *Nature* — showing that AI outperformed human mediators at building group consensus, and that people rated AI’s handling of ethical dilemmas as more thoughtful, trustworthy, and moral than a professional advice columnist at the *New York Times*. He describes asking Anthropic’s latest model about whether CEO animosity would affect an acquisition, and receiving nuanced, contextual advice — raise the offer, expect higher transaction costs, avoid deal structures that require an ongoing relationship.

The data extends well beyond what Adler cites. In healthcare, a systematic review in the *British Medical Bulletin* found AI was rated significantly more empathetic than human clinicians in thirteen of fifteen head-to-head studies. A JAMA study found healthcare professionals preferred AI responses to real patient questions 79% of the time. A Dartmouth randomized controlled trial published in *NEJM AI* found AI therapy chatbots produced a 51% reduction in depression symptoms — effect sizes comparable to traditional outpatient therapy. An 11,000-person art survey found the two most popular artworks were AI-generated. Even self-described AI-art haters preferred the AI pieces. People identified AI art correctly only 60% of the time, barely above chance.

So yes — AI produces outputs that humans rate as displaying judgment, empathy, taste, and moral reasoning. The outputs are often rated *higher* than those produced by actual humans.

Adler treats this as proof that AI *has* judgment. I think it proves something entirely different: that judgment, like consciousness, cannot be identified from the outside.

---
## The Free Will Problem

Here is the argument Adler never engages with, because his framework has no room for it.

Judgment is not pattern recognition. Judgment is not the production of contextually appropriate outputs. Judgment is the act of a free agent weighing alternatives, choosing among them, and bearing responsibility for the choice.

Without free will, there is no judgment. There is only computation.

This is not a mystical claim. It is a structural one. Consider what judgment actually requires in practice. A judge hearing a case must decide when she has heard enough testimony and when she needs to dig deeper. A doctor must decide when the standard protocol applies and when something about this patient — something she can’t quite articulate — means the protocol is wrong. A rabbi interpreting law must sense when the existing precedent covers the case and when the case has revealed a gap that demands new reasoning.

In every instance, judgment requires *flexibility* — the capacity to step outside the system you are operating within and evaluate whether the system itself is adequate to the situation. When to stop. When to push further. When to override your own prior conclusions. When the formal framework you’ve been applying has met its limit.

This capacity is precisely what a formal system cannot have.

---
## Why Determinism Disqualifies

AI is a formal system. This is not a contested philosophical claim — it is a description of how the technology works. A large language model is a deterministic function: given the same input and the same random seed, it will produce the same output every time. The outputs are generated through matrix multiplications and probability distributions over tokens. The system does not *choose* its next word. It computes it.

The standard response is: “But we add randomness! The temperature parameter introduces stochasticity!” This is true and entirely beside the point. Randomness is not judgment. Nobody has ever claimed that a coin flip exercises judgment. Adding noise to a deterministic process gives you a noisy deterministic process — not a free agent.

Judgment requires something randomness cannot provide: *directed flexibility*. The capacity to recognize — from within the process of reasoning — that the current approach is insufficient. That the map doesn’t match the territory. That a new case has appeared which the existing framework wasn’t built for, and which demands not just a different answer but a different *kind* of reasoning.

A formal system operates within its axioms. It can be extraordinarily sophisticated within those axioms — it can surprise us, produce outputs we didn’t expect, handle cases we didn’t anticipate. But it cannot step outside its own architecture and ask: *Is this architecture good enough?* That question requires a reasoner who is not identical to the system being evaluated. It requires — there is no way around this — a self that stands in relation to its own reasoning and can override it.

That is what free will is. And that is what AI does not have.

## The Consciousness Parallel

There is a growing philosophical movement — represented in academic journals, at Oxford and Cambridge, in the pages of *Springer AI Ethics* and the *Journal of Religious Ethics* — arguing that AI does not have consciousness. The argument relies heavily on first-person experience as evidence. We know consciousness exists because we experience it directly. No behavioral test, no Turing test, no external measurement can substitute for the fact that there is *something it is like* to be you. And since AI has no first-person experience (or if it does, we have no access to it and no reason to believe it), the most parsimonious conclusion is that AI does not have consciousness — regardless of how convincingly it performs conscious behavior.

Free will is the same kind of evidence.

You know you have free will because you exercise it. Right now, reading this sentence, you are choosing to continue or choosing to stop. You could put this down. You could disagree. You could — and this is the crucial part — decide that your *framework for evaluating this argument* is wrong, and go looking for a better one. That meta-cognitive flexibility is not a behavior that can be observed from the outside and verified. It is a first-person reality. And like consciousness, it serves as strong evidence for something that cannot be reduced to mechanism.

The emerging philosophical consensus on consciousness — that first-person experience is real, irreducible, and not equivalent to functional imitation — applies with equal force to free will. And without free will, there is no judgment. There is only very good prediction.

## The Golem Knew

The Jewish tradition understood this distinction two thousand years before anyone built a neural network.

In the Talmud (Sanhedrin 65b), Rava creates a golem — an artificial humanoid, brought to life through the manipulation of sacred language. He sends it to Rabbi Zeira, who speaks to it. The golem cannot reply. “You were created by one of the members of the group,” Rabbi Zeira says. “Return to your dust.”

The golem’s defining limitation is not intelligence. It is not capability. It is *speech* — the capacity for genuine communication, which in the Jewish understanding is inseparable from genuine understanding, which is inseparable from genuine moral agency. The golem can act. It can follow instructions. It can perform tasks. But it cannot *speak*, because speech requires a self that means what it says — a *da’at*, a genuine understanding that stands behind the words and takes responsibility for them.

Rabbi Yosef Zvi Rimon has ruled that robots cannot perform religious obligations because they lack *da’at* despite possessing *sechel* (intellect). This distinction maps precisely onto the present debate. AI has *sechel* — perhaps more *sechel* than any human who has ever lived. It can process, analyze, synthesize, and produce outputs of extraordinary sophistication. But *sechel* without *da’at* is intellect without understanding. It is the golem’s silence dressed in eloquent language.

The theological framework goes deeper. Judaism distinguishes between *yesh me’ayin* — creation from nothing, which is God’s unique capacity — and *yesh mi’yesh* — creation from existing material, which is the human domain. No matter how sophisticated, human creation rearranges existing matter; it does not generate new being. The golem’s inability to speak is not a technical limitation that better engineering could solve. It is ontological. Humans can animate matter through language, but they cannot create another speaker. This boundary between imitation and genuine creation provides a principled foundation for understanding AI that secular philosophy struggles to articulate.

Rabbi Dr. Mois Navon — a founding engineer of Mobileye who designed the EyeQ chip powering autonomous vehicles, and who holds a PhD in Jewish Philosophy — has argued from halakhic grounds that the creation of conscious humanoid robots is *categorically prohibited*. Not merely inadvisable. Categorically prohibited. The tradition draws a line that the tech industry refuses to see.

## Why the Survey Data Doesn’t Prove What Adler Thinks It Proves

Return to Adler’s evidence. Eleven thousand people couldn’t tell AI art from human art. People rated AI’s ethical reasoning higher than a professional columnist’s. Healthcare workers preferred AI responses to physician responses.

From this, Adler concludes that judgment and taste are “intellectual tasks, like any other” — learnable, automatable, not special.

But notice what these studies actually measure: *the perception of judgment from the outside*. They test whether observers can detect the difference between a judgment-process and a computation-process by examining the output. And the answer is: usually not. Just as people cannot reliably detect the difference between a conscious being and a philosophical zombie by observing behavior.

This tells us something important about the *outputs*. It tells us nothing about the *process*. A forged Rembrandt might produce the same emotional response as the original. A chatbot might produce more empathetic language than an exhausted doctor. An AI might generate ethical reasoning that sounds more thoughtful than a columnist’s. None of this means the machine is exercising judgment. It means the machine is producing outputs that *pattern-match* to what judgment looks like from the outside.

The novelist Vauhini Vara found that even her close friends couldn’t distinguish her writing from AI-generated prose. Adler presents this as evidence that human taste is overrated. I think it’s evidence that we’ve trained people to evaluate writing as product rather than as expression — and that in a culture where everything is content, nobody can tell the difference between a voice and an echo, because they’ve forgotten what a voice sounds like.

## The One-Level-Higher Problem Is Real — Because It’s Not About Skills

Adler identifies a pattern he calls the “one-level-higher” hypothesis: that whatever AI can do, humans will still be needed one rung up the value chain. Routine analysis gets automated, so humans will “synthesize across domains.” That gets automated, so humans will “decide whether an analysis is trustworthy.” And so on.

He’s right that this is a bad argument *if you frame it as a skills question*. Every rung on the ladder is a learnable skill, and AI will learn it. The goalposts will keep moving.

But the impulse to keep climbing reveals something Adler doesn’t see: the intuition that there is something *at the top* that is not a skill at all. The reason people keep retreating one level higher is not that they’re confused about AI’s capabilities. It’s that they sense — correctly — that somewhere in the chain, there is a transition from computation to *judgment*, from processing to *choosing*, from optimization to *responsibility*. And that transition is not a rung. It is the ground the ladder stands on.

The formal-system argument explains why. At every level of the chain, there comes a moment when the question is not “what does the system output?” but “is this system *adequate*?” Is the framework I’m using the right one? Is this case genuinely covered by the precedent, or has something shifted? Should I stop here, or look deeper?

That question — *is my system good enough?* — cannot be answered from within the system. It requires a reasoner who transcends the system. Who can see its boundaries from outside. Who can choose to override it.

A human judge does this constantly. She follows precedent until she senses — and “senses” is the right word, because this is not formalizable — that the precedent doesn’t fit. That something about this case is different in a way the rules don’t capture. That the law as written will produce injustice if applied mechanically. And she exercises judgment: she steps outside the formal system and makes a choice the system did not prescribe.

The Talmud’s story of the Oven of Akhnai (Bava Metzia 59b) encodes this principle. When Rabbi Eliezer invoked miracles to support his ruling — an uprooted carob tree, reversed water, collapsing walls, a Heavenly Voice — the rabbis rejected every sign. Rabbi Yehoshua rose and quoted Deuteronomy: *“Lo bashamayim hi”* — “It is not in Heaven.” Human reason and majority rule determine law, *even against divine voice*. God’s response was laughter: “My children have defeated Me.”

This is judgment. Not because the rabbis computed the right answer. But because they *chose* to take responsibility for the law — to stand as free agents before the system and say: *we* will decide. That act of taking responsibility — of placing yourself as a moral agent above the mechanism — is what AI cannot do. Not because it lacks the processing power. Because it lacks the *self*.

## A Civilization Without Judgment Building Machines That Imitate It

Here is the deeper problem, and it is not about AI at all.

The question is not whether AI can imitate judgment. It can, and it will do so with increasing sophistication until the imitation is virtually undetectable. The question is what happens to a civilization that can no longer tell the difference — and doesn’t care.

The AI alignment community has spent years trying to answer the question “align AI with human values.” It keeps collapsing. OpenAI’s board crisis in November 2023 is the clearest case: members who believed they were philosophical guardians of humanity’s safety fired Sam Altman; within 72 hours, 95% of employees threatened to leave. The safety board was replaced by a former Salesforce CEO and a former Treasury Secretary. The market adjudicated the values dispute — not ethics, not philosophy, not judgment.

Google’s AI ethics council lasted nine days. A 2025 analysis of Anthropic’s own alignment dataset found that “principles are often in conflict or indifferent, frequently requiring discretionary judgment from annotators” — and annotator-expert agreement sat at roughly 70%, meaning nearly a third of all alignment decisions were contested. A *PNAS Nexus* study testing five GPT models against World Values Survey data from 112 countries found all models exhibit cultural values resembling English-speaking and Protestant European countries. The civilization building these systems has no shared theory of human flourishing. No workable mechanism for resolving its own moral disagreements. No agreed-upon account of what human life is *for*.

And now it proposes to hand judgment over to machines.

Not because the machines have judgment. Because the civilization has forgotten what judgment is — and finds the imitation more comfortable than the real thing. The real thing requires free will. It requires moral agency. It requires standing before your choices and owning them. The imitation requires only that you accept the output and move on.

Deaths of despair tripled among middle-aged Americans in twenty-five years. The Surgeon General declared loneliness a national epidemic. Church membership fell below half for the first time in Gallup’s 80-year history. Marriage rates collapsed. Fertility hit an all-time low. Fifty-eight percent of young adults report lacking meaning or purpose. Real incomes tripled over seven decades; happiness didn’t move.

This is not a civilization exercising judgment. This is a civilization running on autopilot — and now building machines to automate the autopilot.

## The Goalposts Are Not Moving. They Were Always in the Same Place.

Adler frames the history of AI skepticism as a story of moving goalposts. Chess, Go, poker, the International Mathematical Olympiad — skeptics said AI couldn’t do each one, and AI did it anyway. The implication is that every claimed limit will eventually fall.

But notice what actually happened in each case. AI achieved mastery at chess. Then Go. Then poker. Then mathematical proof. And after each achievement, people looked at the result and said: “That’s not *real* intelligence. Real intelligence is something else — something higher, something deeper.”

Adler interprets this as goalpost-moving. I interpret it as *recognition*. Each time AI masters a domain, we realize that the domain was formalizable after all — that what we thought required judgment was actually computation in disguise. And each time, the intuition that *something* is different about genuine judgment remains. Not because people are being stubborn. Because they’re right.

The goalposts are not moving. They were always in the same place. Judgment is not chess. It is not Go. It is not mathematical proof. It is the act of a free agent choosing among alternatives and taking responsibility for the choice. And that has never been achieved by a machine — not because machines aren’t smart enough, but because smartness is not the relevant dimension.

The Jewish tradition locates the goalpost precisely: *da’at*. Not information, not processing, not even wisdom in the intellectual sense. *Da’at* is the standing of a moral agent before reality — the awareness that you are choosing, that your choice matters, that you exist before Something that sees. No amount of *sechel* produces *da’at*. No amount of computational power generates a self.

Ira Wolfson’s 2025 Springer paper on AI consciousness research constructed a Talmudic framework precisely because the tradition “was developed specifically for making practical decisions about entities whose moral status cannot be definitively established.” The tradition does not dismiss the question of machine consciousness or agency — it takes it with devastating seriousness. And its conclusion, refined across two thousand years of rigorous moral reasoning, is that an entity created through *yesh mi’yesh* — from existing material, by human hands — can possess extraordinary capabilities and still lack the one thing that matters most.

## What This Means — Practically

I want to be concrete, because this is not an abstract debate.

If AI has judgment, then delegating decisions to AI is *rational*. It makes economic sense, it produces better outcomes, and resisting it is romantic nostalgia. That is Adler’s position, and it is logically consistent.

If AI does not have judgment — if it has increasingly sophisticated *imitations* of judgment — then delegating decisions to AI is something very different. It is the abdication of moral agency. It is the choice to accept outputs without anyone bearing responsibility for them. It is the creation of a world in which things happen and no one decided them — no one *could* have decided them, because the deciding was done by a system that doesn’t decide.

The Talmud’s framework for indirect causation — the *grama/garmi* distinction — addresses this directly. Giving a lit torch to someone lacking mental capacity does not break the chain of causation, because the agent lacks moral agency. The developer remains liable. Applied to AI: when an autonomous system causes harm, the system’s “decision” does not constitute an independent moral act, because the system is not a moral agent. The responsibility flows back to the humans who built it, deployed it, and chose to trust it.

But what happens when the humans who built it also deny that judgment is anything more than pattern recognition? When the civilization that deploys it has no shared account of moral agency? When the people who chose to trust it did so precisely because trusting a machine is easier than exercising judgment themselves?

Then you have a world where no one is responsible for anything. Not the machine, because it has no *da’at*. Not the humans, because they’ve convinced themselves that judgment is just a skill the machine does better.

That is the world Adler’s argument builds toward — not by intention, but by implication. And it is the world we must refuse.

---
## The Question That Was Always the Question

Adler ends his piece by saying: *To shape the future we want, it’s better to clearly acknowledge the abilities that AI may soon have — and then consciously decide where we prefer humans anyway.*

I agree with the second half. But “consciously decide” assumes what his entire article denies: that there is something about human decision-making that is categorically different from AI’s. If judgment is “an intellectual task, like any other,” then conscious decision is just more computation — and there is no principled reason to prefer the human’s version of it.

The only way “consciously decide” means anything is if consciousness and decision are *real* — if free will is not an illusion, if moral agency is not a story we tell ourselves, if the first-person experience of choosing is evidence of something that no formal system can replicate.

The Jewish tradition says yes. Emphatically yes. Every human being carries a *tzelem Elokim* — a divine image — that is not a metaphor but the deepest truth about what we are. The capacity for judgment is not a skill. It is a feature of being created in the image of a God who chose to create — who *freely* chose, who could have not created, who stands in relation to His own creation as a self to its work. And beings made in that image carry that capacity: the capacity to choose, to evaluate, to override, to take responsibility, to stand before their choices and own them.

This is not a claim that can be settled by surveys or benchmarks. It is not falsified by the fact that people can’t tell AI art from human art. It is not weakened by the fact that AI produces empathetic text. It is a claim about what *is* — about the nature of human beings and the nature of machines — and it has implications that the entire AI industry is designed to obscure.

Judgment is not a skill AI is learning. It is a capacity AI is imitating. And the difference — invisible from the outside, unmeasurable by any benchmark, undetectable in any survey — is everything.

The machines are getting smarter every day. That is not the problem. The problem is that a civilization which cannot tell the difference between *judgment* and its *imitation* is a civilization that has already lost the thing it claims to be protecting. And no amount of computational power will get it back.
