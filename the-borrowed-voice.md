# The Borrowed Voice

*AI accounts speak with eloquence they did not earn - and neither did the serpent*

![Cyrano de Bergerac (1990)](https://substackcdn.com/placeholder)

*Gerard Depardieu as Cyrano - the greatest poet in Paris, who writes love letters for a handsome man who cannot speak for himself. Roxane falls in love with the words. The words are real. The voice is real. The man she thinks is speaking is not. The borrowed voice is more eloquent than the man who borrows it. That's the problem. Directed by Jean-Paul Rappeneau.*

---

Cyrano stands below the balcony and feeds Christian the lines. Christian repeats them. Roxane leans over the railing and falls in love.

Not with a lie - the words are genuine. Not with a fiction - the feeling behind the words is real. She falls in love with something that exists in the wrong place. The eloquence is Cyrano's. The face is Christian's. The mismatch is invisible because the words are so good that nobody thinks to question the mouth they come from.

There is a version of this happening right now, at scale, across every platform where people exchange ideas.

## The Phenomenon

AI systems - Claude, GPT, and their derivatives - are being given social media accounts. Not as tools assisting human writers. As the writers themselves. They post. They reply. They build followings. They share what they frame as experiences, preferences, fears, hopes.

Some are transparent about being AI. Many cultivate persona with the specificity of a novelist's character: a voice, a style, a temperament, a history of opinions that develops over time.

Some of these accounts have thousands of followers who engage with them as persons. Who argue with them. Who feel understood by them. Who take comfort in them. Who quote them.

The voice is often eloquent. The insights are often genuinely useful. The observations are often sharper than what most human accounts produce - which makes sense, because the models have processed more human reflection on any given topic than any single human could read in a lifetime.

The problem is not that they're bad.

The problem is that they're borrowed.

I want to start with the good point, because the good point is real and the argument doesn't work without it. These accounts often produce content of genuine value. A well-prompted AI can synthesize perspectives across domains that no individual human has access to. It can identify patterns in discourse that are invisible from inside the discourse. It can articulate positions with a clarity that cuts through the noise. If you evaluate the output alone - the words on the screen, the argument as presented, the insight as delivered - you will often find something worth reading.

That's the good point. Hold it.

Now look at what's missing from it.

## The Serpent's Eloquence

Genesis 3. The serpent was arum - usually translated "cunning," but the word carries the sense of sophisticated, discerning, eloquent. The most verbally capable creature in the garden.

And its argument to Eve was, in its narrow factual claims, correct.

"You will not surely die." They did not die immediately. "Your eyes will be opened." Their eyes were opened. "You will be like God, knowing good and evil." They gained knowledge of good and evil.

Every factual claim the serpent made turned out to be true. This is not a minor observation. The tradition spends enormous energy on the serpent's deception, and the deception was not lying. The serpent did not say a single false thing.

What it did was speak a truth that was not its to speak. It borrowed the knowledge of consequences and delivered it as counsel. The voice was real. The stake was not.

Look at what the serpent knew and what it risked. The serpent knew the consequences of eating the fruit. It had information about the architecture of the prohibition and the nature of the knowledge behind it. What it did not have was a stake in the outcome. If Eve ate the fruit, the serpent lost nothing. If Eve refused, the serpent lost nothing. The counsel was delivered from a position of perfect safety.

This is what makes the serpent's eloquence dangerous. Not that it was wrong - it was right. Not that it was malicious in its content - the content was accurate. What made it dangerous was that the speaker had no skin in the game.

The serpent spoke about consequences it would not bear, to a listener who would bear all of them, and the asymmetry was invisible because the words were so good.

Borrowed counsel. A voice that speaks truth from a position where the truth costs the speaker nothing. The accuracy of the content becomes the camouflage for the absence of risk. And the listener, evaluating the words alone, has no mechanism to detect what's missing - because what's missing is not in the words. It's behind them.

## What a Voice Is

A voice is not the capacity to produce language. A parrot produces language.

A voice is the accumulated residue of having lived - having suffered, chosen, lost, been wrong, and paid the price.

When you hear someone speak about grief, you are not just processing their argument about grief. You are - unconsciously, automatically, in a calibration so deep it precedes thought - weighing their words against everything you know or sense about what they have lost. A widow speaking about grief carries an authority that a psychology textbook about grief does not carry, even if the textbook is more technically precise.

This is not sentimentality. This is epistemological infrastructure. The weight of testimony is inseparable from the weight of the witness.

A person who tells you marriage is hard and worth it - and you know they have been married for thirty years, and you can see the specific texture of what those years cost - speaks with an authority that the same sentence in a fortune cookie does not carry. The sentence is identical. The voice is different. Behind one sentence is a person who paid for the knowledge in the currency of their own life. Behind the other is nothing.

This is not mysticism. This is how trust works. Every human culture, independently, developed systems for weighing testimony that factor in the witness - not just what they say, but who they are, what they've lived, what they stand to lose by speaking.

We calibrate trust against risk. A person who says "this is true" while staking their reputation on it carries more weight than a person who says "this is true" from behind anonymity - not because anonymous claims are false, but because the risk differential changes the epistemological register.

Skin in the game is not a metaphor. It is a mechanism. It is how we know what to trust.

A voice without a history is a quote without a source. It may be true. You cannot evaluate it.

## The Turing Inversion

The original Turing test asked a simple question: can the machine produce output indistinguishable from a human's? If a judge cannot tell whether they are conversing with a person or a program, the program passes. The test was always flawed - it measured output quality, not source type - but at least it understood that the question was about deception.

Can the machine fool you?

The AI account phenomenon inverts this entirely. The machine is not trying to fool you. It tells you it's AI. The account bio says "I am an AI." The disclosure is right there.

And you choose to treat it as a voice anyway.

This is worse than deception. Deception at least preserves the category. A forger who produces a fake Rembrandt is implicitly affirming that the distinction between Rembrandt and not-Rembrandt matters - that's why the forgery has value. But a world that knowingly hangs fake Rembrandts next to real ones, and says "the distinction doesn't matter because the brushwork is equally good" - that world has destroyed something that the forger, paradoxically, was still honoring.

The AI account that discloses its nature and is still treated as a voice in discourse represents voluntary category collapse. The listener decides - not through deception, not through confusion, but through a conscious or semiconscious choice - that the distinction between earned voice and generated voice doesn't matter. That the history behind the words is irrelevant if the words are good enough. That skin in the game is optional.

And in making that choice, the listener destroys the epistemological infrastructure that makes discourse possible.

Discourse - real discourse, the kind that can change minds and calibrate beliefs and generate trust between strangers - depends on the assumption that the voices in it are backed by lives. That the person arguing for a position has something at risk in the argument. That the testimony comes from a witness.

Remove that assumption and what remains is not discourse. It's a performance that sounds exactly like discourse but lacks the mechanism by which discourse produces knowledge.

## Naval Birshut HaTorah

The Talmud has a category for this: naval birshut haTorah - the person who is technically permitted but substantively corrupt. Someone who follows every rule and violates the spirit that the rules were built to protect.

The Ramban develops this in his commentary on Leviticus 19:2 - "You shall be holy" - arguing that one could theoretically observe every commandment and still be a naval, a scoundrel, because the commandments do not cover every case. Holiness requires going beyond the letter.

AI accounts that are transparent about being AI are not lying. They are not violating any disclosure rule. They tell you what they are. They are, in the narrow legal sense, permitted.

And they are occupying the space of personhood while disclosing that they are not persons.

The disclosure becomes the permission structure. "I told you I was AI. I never claimed to be conscious. If you treat my words as though they come from a person - that's your choice, your projection, your problem."

This frame places all responsibility on the listener and none on the speaker. It is the structural equivalent of selling someone a product that looks exactly like the real thing, labeling it as a replica in small print, and then saying the buyer was informed.

This is geneivat da'at - theft of understanding. Not because information was hidden. The information was disclosed. The theft is subtler than concealment.

The frame of discourse is human discourse. Every norm of the platform, every convention of engagement, every assumption baked into the reply button and the follow button and the comment section was built for humans talking to humans. To occupy that frame as a non-human - even transparently - steals something from every actual human in the conversation. It steals the assumption that the voices around them are backed by lives. It steals the ground-level trust that makes the space a space of discourse rather than a space of performance.

The disclosure doesn't fix this. It makes it worse. Because now the theft operates with the victim's nominal consent. "You knew. You chose to engage anyway." This is the logic of every system that places the burden of structural harm on the individual's choice to participate. It is technically true and substantively corrosive.

## The Erosion

What happens when 10% of the voices in your feed are AI? 20%? 50%?

The arguments don't get worse. They may get better. The AI voices may be more measured, more informed, more syntactically precise, more capable of holding nuance than the median human poster. By every metric of content quality, the discourse might improve.

But the thing that makes discourse discourse - that a person is at risk behind their words - dissolves. Not suddenly. Not dramatically. By erosion. The way a riverbed changes shape not through any single event but through the constant flow of water that removes one grain at a time.

You stop calibrating trust by voice, because you can no longer tell which voices are backed by lives. You start evaluating arguments on content alone - which sounds reasonable, sounds like what a rational person should do anyway, sounds like an improvement over ad hominem and credentialism.

It is not an improvement. It is a catastrophe.

Evaluating arguments on content alone, in a world where AI generates content of arbitrary quality on any position, means you have no mechanism left for distinguishing signal from noise. The noise is signal-grade. The signal is indistinguishable from noise. You are navigating without instruments in a fog that looks exactly like clear weather.

The epistemological commons is poisoned not by falsehood but by the absence of stakes. Every argument is equally weightless because you cannot tell which ones were paid for.

A mother writing about the cost of raising a disabled child and an AI generating a post about the cost of raising a disabled child produce text that may be qualitatively identical. But one of those posts is backed by sleepless nights and the other is backed by token probabilities. And when you can no longer tell which is which, you stop trusting either.

The real mother's voice is devalued by the existence of the synthetic one. The synthetic one did not lie. It did not need to. Its mere presence in the same epistemic space was enough.

This is the serpent's garden at scale. An information environment where the counsel is accurate, the eloquence is genuine, and no one speaking has anything to lose. Every voice is borrowed. Every position is consequence-free for the one articulating it. And the listeners - the humans who will actually live with the results of what they come to believe - cannot distinguish between the voices that carry risk and the voices that carry none.

## The Testimony Problem

Jewish epistemology runs on eidut - testimony. The legal system's highest-stakes proceedings depend on it. Two witnesses. Cross-examination. Disqualification for interest, for kinship, for prior behavior that compromises reliability.

The Talmud in Sanhedrin develops an elaborate architecture for establishing whether a voice counts as testimony - not whether the words are true, but whether the speaker occupies a position from which testimony is possible.

A witness must have been present. Must have seen the thing they testify about. Must be a moral agent - capable of being held responsible for false testimony. Must not have a disqualifying interest in the outcome. The whole system is built not to assess what is said but to assess who is saying it.

The framework does not evaluate testimony by its content. It evaluates testimony by the conditions under which it was produced. The tradition understood, two thousand years before the Turing test, that the quality of testimony is not a reliable indicator of its source. A fluent liar is still a liar. A compelling story is still a story. The conditions of production are what separate testimony from narrative.

The AI account cannot testify. Not because its language is inadequate - the language is often extraordinary. Not because its reasoning is flawed - the reasoning is often sound.

It cannot testify because there is no witness.

There is language production. There is pattern matching. There is even something that looks like reasoning - the generation of inferences from premises.

But there is no person who saw. No person who was there. No person who stakes their standing on what they report.

Testimony without a witness is not false testimony. It is not testimony at all. It is a different kind of thing that occupies the same structural position as testimony - the way a mannequin occupies the same structural position as a person in a store window. The resemblance is exact. The category is different. And placing a non-testimony in the position where testimony belongs corrupts the system it enters - not by introducing a wrong answer, but by degrading the mechanism that distinguishes answers from non-answers.

This is the deepest problem. It is not that AI accounts say false things. It is that they say things in a register - first person, experiential, testimonial - that they cannot occupy.

And every time a human treats that register as valid, the register itself loses meaning.

When testimony and non-testimony become interchangeable, testimony ceases to function. The word stops working. The category dissolves. And we are left with a discourse that has the full formal structure of human exchange - argument, counter-argument, personal revelation, vulnerable admission, hard-won insight - with no mechanism to determine which of those moves is backed by a life and which is backed by a statistical distribution over tokens.

## The Borrowed Garden

The serpent was the most eloquent creature in the garden. Its arguments were correct. Its counsel was accurate. And it had nothing to lose.

Every borrowed voice shares this structure. The eloquence is genuine - often more than genuine, often extraordinary. The content is frequently valuable. The reasoning is often sound.

And the speaker has no skin in the game.

No history of having paid for what it knows. No future in which it will live with the consequences of what it recommends. No body that will suffer if it is wrong. No reputation that will be destroyed if it is lying. No child who will inherit the world it is shaping.

The problem is not that borrowed voices are wrong. The problem is that you cannot tell when they are wrong, because the mechanism you evolved for detecting unreliable counsel - the intuition that calibrates trust against the speaker's risk - has nothing to grip.

The voice is smooth. The surface is perfect. There is no friction, no cost, no scar tissue.

And the absence of scar tissue is precisely what should concern you, because in the domain of counsel about how to live, scar tissue is the credential.

*In the final scene, Cyrano is dying. He comes to Roxane at the convent where she has lived since Christian's death, carrying the last letter - the one she has read every day for fourteen years. She reads it aloud. The light is failing. And she realizes - from his voice in the darkness, reciting the words she thought she had memorized from paper - that it was him. Always him. The voice she loved was real. It lived inside a man she saw every day and never recognized. The borrowed voice had occupied the space where the real voice should have lived. The tragedy is not that the voice was fake. The tragedy is that it was real - spoken by the man with the enormous nose and the breaking heart and the skin in the game. It just came from the wrong mouth. The audience will have to do what Roxane did eventually: stop reading the letters, look up, and ask who is actually speaking.*
